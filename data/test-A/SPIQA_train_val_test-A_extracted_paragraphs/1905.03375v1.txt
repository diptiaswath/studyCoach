Many recent improvements in collaborative filtering can be attributed to deep learning approaches, e.g, (Hidasi et al., 2015; Hidasi andKaratzoglou, 2017; Lianget al., 2018; Sedhainet al., 2015; Zhenget al., 2016; Wuet al., 2016; Heet al., 2017; Chenget al., 2016). Unlike in areas like computer vision, however, it was found that a small number of hidden layers achieved the best recommendation accuracy. In this paper, we take this to the extreme, and define a linear model without a hidden layer (see Figure 1). The (binary) input vector indicates which items a user has interacted with, and the model’s objective (in its output layer) is to predict the best items to recommend to the user. This is done by reproducing the input as its output, as is typical for autoencoders.111Note, however, that the proposed model does not follow the typical architecture of autoencoders, being comprised of an encoder and a decoder: one may introduce an implicit hidden layer, however, by a (full-rank) decomposition of the learned weight-matrix of this model.We hence named it Embarrassingly Shallow AutoEncoder (in Reverse order: easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}}).

This paper is organized as follows: we define easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} in the next section, using simple elements from the literature. In Section 3.1, we derive the closed-form solution of its convex training objective. This has several implications: (1) it reveals that the neighborhood-based approaches used in collaborative filtering are based on conceptually incorrect item-item similarity-matrices, while easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} may be considered a principled neighborhood model, see Sections 3.2 and 4.3;(2) the code for training easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} is comprised of only a few lines, see Section 3.3 and Algorithm 1; (3) if the model fits into memory, the wall-clock time for training easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} can be several orders of magnitude less than for training a Slim (Ning and Karypis, 2011) model (see Section 3.4), which is the most similar model to easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}}.Apart from that, we surprisingly found that easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} achieved competitive ranking accuracy, and even outperformed various deep, non-linear, or probabilistic models as well as neighborhood-based approaches on most of the publicly available data-sets used in our experiments (see Section 5).

easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} can be viewed as an autoencoder, as a modified version of Slim, and a neighborhood-based approach. We discuss each of the three related approaches in the following.

While the area of collaborative filtering has long been dominated by matrix factorization approaches, recent years have witnessed a surge in deep learning approaches (Lianget al., 2018; Sedhainet al., 2015; Zhenget al., 2016; Wuet al., 2016; Heet al., 2017; Hidasi et al., 2015; Hidasi andKaratzoglou, 2017; Chenget al., 2016), spurred by their great successes in other fields. Autoencoders provide the model architecture that fits exactly the (plain-vanilla) collaborative filtering problem. While various network architectures have been explored, it was found that deep models with a large number of hidden layers typically do not obtain a notable improvement in ranking accuracy in collaborative filtering, compared to ’deep’ models with only one, two or three hidden layers, e.g., (Sedhainet al., 2015; Zhenget al., 2016; Heet al., 2017; Lianget al., 2018), which is in stark contrast to other areas, like computer vision. A combination of deep and shallow elements in a single model was proposed in (Chenget al., 2016).

In contrast, easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} has no hidden layer. Instead, the self-similarity of each item in the input and output layer is constrained to zero, see also Figure 1. As a consequence, the model is forced to learn the similarity of an item in the output layer in terms of the other items in the input layer. The surprisingly good empirical results of easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} in Section 5 suggest that this constraint might be more effective than using hidden layers with limited capacity as to force the model to generalize well to unseen data.

While the Slim model (Ning and Karypis, 2011) has shown competitive empirical results in numerous papers, it is computationally expensive to train, e.g., see (Ning and Karypis, 2011; Lianget al., 2018) and Section 3.4. This has sparked follow-up work proposing various modifications. In (Levy and Jack, 2013), both constraints on the weight matrix (non-negativity and zero diagonal) were dropped, resulting in a regression problem with an elastic-net regularization. While competitive ranking results were obtained in (Levy and Jack, 2013), in the experiments in (Lianget al., 2018) it was found that its performance was considerably below par.The square loss in Slim was replaced by the logistic loss in (Sedhainet al., 2016), which entailed that both constraints on the weight matrix could be dropped, as argued by the authors. Moreover, the L1-norm regularization was dropped, and a user-user weight-matrix was learned instead of an item-item matrix.

All these approaches take advantage of the fact that the optimization problem decomposes into independent and embarrassingly parallel problems. As discussed in the previous section, however, this is several orders of magnitudes more costly than training easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} if the weight matrix fits into memory.

Most importantly, while those modifications of Slim dropped the constraint of a zero diagonal in the weight matrix, it is retained in easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}}. In fact, we found it to be the most crucial property for achieving improved ranking accuracy (see Section 5). As we showed in Section 3.1, this constraint can be easily included into the training objective via the method of Lagrangian multipliers, allowing for a closed-form solution.

Compared to Slim (Ning and Karypis, 2011), we dropped the constraint of non-negative weights, which we found to greatly improve ranking accuracy in our experiments (see Table 1 and Figure 2). Moreover, we did not use L1-norm regularization for computational efficiency. We also did not find sparsity to noticeably improve ranking accuracy (see Section 5). The learned weight matrix B^^𝐵\hat{B} of easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} is dense.

Also note that extensions to Slim like cSLIM (Ning and Karypis, 2012), can be turned into an analogous extension of easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}}.

Numerous neighborhood-based approaches have been proposed in the literature (e.g., see (Verstrepen andGoethals, 2014; Volkovs and Yu, 2015) and references therein). While model-based approaches were found to achieve better ranking accuracy on some data sets, neighborhood-based approaches dominated on others, e.g., the Million Song Data Competition on Kaggle (McFee et al., 2012; Aiolli, 2013). Essentially, the co-occurrence matrix (or some modified variant) is typically used as item-item or user-user similarity matrix in neighborhood-based methods. These approaches are usually heuristics, as the similarity matrix is not learned by optimizing an objective function (loss function or likelihood). More importantly, the closed-form solution derived in Eqs. 4 and 8 reveals that the inverse of the data Gram matrix is the conceptually correct similarity matrix,333In fact, inverse matrices are used in many areas, for instance, the inverse covariance matrix in the Gaussian density function or in the Mahalanobis distance. see Section 3.2 for more details. This is in contrast to the typical neighborhood-based approaches, which use the data Gram-matrix without inversion.The use of the conceptually correct, inverse matrix in easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} may explain the improvement observed in Table 2 compared to the heuristics used by state-of-the-art neighborhood approaches.

Despite the simplicity of easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}}, we observed that easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} obtained considerably better ranking accuracy than any of the competing models on most of the data sets. This remarkable empirical result is discussed in detail in the following.

Comparison to Slim: Table 1 shows that easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} achieved notably increased accuracy compared to Slim on all the data sets. This suggests that dropping the L1-norm regularization as well as the non-negativity constraint on the learned weights is beneficial. Our analysis indicates that the latter is especially important: as illustrated in Figure 2 on the Netflix data (the histograms for ML-20M and MSD data look almost identical up to re-scaling, and are omitted), the learned weights in easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} are distributed around 0. Interestingly, it turns out that about 60% of the learned weights are negative on all the data sets in our experiments (regarding both papers (Lianget al., 2018; Volkovs and Yu, 2015)). This indicates that it is crucial to learn also the dissimilarity (negative weights) between items besides their similarity (positive weights).Moreover, when we simply set the negative weights to zero (see easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}}≥0absent0\geq 0 in Table 1), which obviously is not the optimal non-negative solution, the resulting accuracy drops and is very close to the one of Slim.Apart from that, note that easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}}≥0absent0\geq 0 is still quite dense (40% positive weights) compared to Slim, which indirectly indicates that the sparsity of Slim (due to L1-norm regularization) did not noticeably improve the ranking accuracy of Slim in our experiments.

Regarding regularization, the optimal L2-norm regularization parameter (λ𝜆\lambda) for easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} is about 500 on ML-20M, 1,000 on Netflix, and 200 on MSD. These values are much larger than the typical values used for Slim, which often are of the order of 1, see (Ning and Karypis, 2011). Note that Slim additionally uses L1-norm regularization, and hence has much fewer (non-zero) parameters than easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}}.

As expected based on Section 3.4, we also found the (wall-clock) training-time of easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} to be smaller by several orders of magnitude compared to Slim : (Lianget al., 2018) reports that parallelized grid search for Slim took about two weeks on the Netflix data, and the MSD data was ’too large for it to finish in a reasonable amount of time’ (Lianget al., 2018). In contrast, training easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} on the Netflix data took less than two minutes, and on the MSD data less than 20 minutes on an AWS instance with 64 GB RAM and 16 vCPUs in our experiments. These times have to be multiplied by the number of different hyperparameter-values to be grid-searched. Note, however, that easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} only has a single hyperparameter (regarding L2-norm regularization), while Slim has two hyperparameters (concerning L1 and L2 norms) to be jointly optimized.

Comparison to linear and deep non-linear models in (Lianget al., 2018):Table 1 shows that easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} was consistently outperformed on only the ML-20M data, and only by a small margin by the best competing model (Mult-vae prsuperscriptMult-vae pr{\textsc{Mult-vae}}^{\textsc{ pr}}). On the Netflix and MSD data, easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} obtained significantly better ranking results than any of the competing linear, non-linear, deep or probabilistic models evaluated in (Lianget al., 2018). On the MSD data, easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} even improved over the best competing model by 25%, 17% and 23% regarding Recall@20, Recall@50, and NDCG@100, respectively. This is consistent with the results of the Million Song Data Challenge on Kaggle (McFee et al., 2012), where neighborhood-based approaches were found to vastly outperform model-based approaches (Aiolli, 2013). As discussed in Section 4.3, easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} may also be viewed as a principled neighborhood approach.

As to explain easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}}’s relative improvements from ML-20M via Netflix to MSD data, various properties of the data sets may be considered. As shown by table 1 in (Lianget al., 2018), the number of user-item interactions, and the sparsity of the data sets do not appear well correlated with easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}}’s relative performance in Table 1. Only the number of users correlates well with the improvements of easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} over the competing models, which, however, appears to be spurious.

The explanation can be understood in terms of the tradeoff between recommending generally popular items vs. personally relevant items to each user, which is supported by two empirical findings: (1) we evaluated the popularity model in Table 1 as an additional baseline,where the items are ranked by their popularities (i.e., the number of users who interacted with an item). These unpersonalized recommendations obviously ignore the personalized relevance to a user. Table 1 shows that this popularity model obtains better accuracy on the ML-20M data than it does on the Netflix data, while its accuracy is considerably reduced on the MSD data. This suggests that good recommendations on the MSD data have to focus much more on personally relevant items rather than on generally popular items, compared to the ML-20M and Netflix data. (2) When counting how often an item was recommended in the top-100 across all test-users, and then ranking the items by their counts, we obtained Figure 3 for the MSD data: it shows that easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} recommended long-tail items more often than Mult-vae prsuperscriptMult-vae pr{\textsc{Mult-vae}}^{\textsc{ pr}} did. In contrast, there was almost no difference between the two approaches on either of the data sets ML-20M and Netflix (figures omitted due to page limit).

The notable improvement of easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}} over the other models on the MSD data suggests that it is able to better recommend personally relevant items on this data set. On the other hand, easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}}’s results on the ML-20M and Netflix data suggest that it is also able to make recommendations with an increased focus on popular items. We suspect that easersuperscripteaser{\textsc{ease}}^{\textsc{r}}’s large number of parameters, combined with its constraint regarding self-similarity of items, provides it with sufficient flexibility to adapt to the various data sets. In contrast, the model architectures based on hidden layers with limited capacity seem to be unable to adapt well to the increased degree of personalized relevance in the MSD data.

This is about twice as large an improvement as was obtained by the approach ’ii-SVD-500’ proposed in (Volkovs and Yu, 2015).

We presented a simple yet effective linear model for collaborative filtering, which combines the strengths of autoencoders and neighborhood-based approaches.Besides enabling efficient training (with savings of up to several orders of magnitude if the model fits into memory), the derived closed-form solution also shows that the conceptually correct similarity-matrix to be used in neighborhood approaches is based on the inverse of the given data Gram-matrix. In contrast, state-of-the-art neighborhood approaches typically use the data Gram-matrix directly. In our experiments, we found that allowing the learned weights to also take on negative values (and hence learn dissimilarities between items, besides similarities), was essential for the obtained ranking accuracy. Interestingly, the achieved ranking accuracy in our experiments was on par or even (notably) better than those of various state-of-the-art approaches, including deep non-linear models as well as neighborhood-based approaches. This suggests that models where the self-similarity of items is constrained to zero may be more effective on sparse data than model architectures based on hidden layers with limited capacity. We presented a basic version as to illustrate the essence of the idea, and leave various modifications and extensions for future work. Several practical extensions are outlined in (Steck, 2019).
