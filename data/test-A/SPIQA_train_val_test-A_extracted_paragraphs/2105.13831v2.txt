Matrix sensing represents a paradigm in modern statistics [10, 32, 33], with applications ranging from image compression [2] to collaborative filtering [23] and dimensionality reduction [40], for instance. The goal is to recover a rank-rрқ‘ҹr matrix рқҗ—вӢҶвҲҲв„қnГ—nвҖІsuperscriptрқҗ—вӢҶsuperscriptв„қрқ‘ӣsuperscriptрқ‘ӣвҖІ\mathbf{X}^{\star}\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n^{\prime}} from a set of linear measurements yi=вҹЁрқҗҖi,рқҗ—вӢҶвҹ©subscriptрқ‘Ұрқ‘–subscriptрқҗҖрқ‘–superscriptрқҗ—вӢҶy_{i}=\langle\mathbf{A}_{i},\mathbf{X}^{\star}\rangle, i=1,вҖҰ,mрқ‘–1вҖҰрқ‘ҡi=1,\dots,m, where the sensing matrices рқҗҖiвҲҲв„қnГ—nвҖІsubscriptрқҗҖрқ‘–superscriptв„қрқ‘ӣsuperscriptрқ‘ӣвҖІ\mathbf{A}_{i}\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n^{\prime}} are observed. This formulation includes the problem of matrix completion, where a subset of the entries of the matrix рқҗ—вӢҶsuperscriptрқҗ—вӢҶ\mathbf{X}^{\star} is observed.

Most of the literature on matrix sensing is based on some form of explicit regularization or rank constraint to encourage or enforce low-rankness of the estimated matrix. A popular approach is based on minimizing the nuclear norm or on using explicit regularization techniques based on the nuclear norm, e.g.В [6, 18, 22, 29, 32, 33, 36]. Another popular approach is based on non-convex optimization and the low-rank factorization рқҗ—=рқҗ”рқҗ•вҠӨрқҗ—superscriptрқҗ”рқҗ•top\mathbf{X}=\mathbf{U}\mathbf{V}^{\top} with matrices рқҗ”вҲҲв„қnГ—rрқҗ”superscriptв„қрқ‘ӣрқ‘ҹ\mathbf{U}\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times r}, рқҗ•вҲҲв„қnвҖІГ—rрқҗ•superscriptв„қsuperscriptрқ‘ӣвҖІрқ‘ҹ\mathbf{V}\in\mathbb{R}^{n^{\prime}\times r}, where the factorization itself enforces the low-rankness of рқҗ—рқҗ—\mathbf{X}, e.g.В [9, 10, 19, 28, 27, 35, 37, 45].

The literature on implicit regularization for matrix sensing is more recent and less well developed. When рқҗ—вӢҶsuperscriptрқҗ—вӢҶ\mathbf{X}^{\star} is assumed to be a positive semidefinite matrix, it was first empirically observed in [16] that mininizing the unregularized empirical risk using vanilla gradient descent with parametrization рқҗ—=рқҗ”рқҗ”вҠӨрқҗ—superscriptрқҗ”рқҗ”top\mathbf{X}=\mathbf{U}\mathbf{U}^{\top} and random full-rank initialization close to zero yields a low nuclear norm solution, even when рқҗ”вҲҲв„қnГ—nрқҗ”superscriptв„қрқ‘ӣрқ‘ӣ\mathbf{U}\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}, i.e.В when no constraint on the rank of рқҗ—рқҗ—\mathbf{X} is enforced. It was later proved that, with this parametrization, gradient descent minimizes the nuclear norm under the assumption that the sensing matrices рқҗҖisubscriptрқҗҖрқ‘–\mathbf{A}_{i}вҖҷs commute [16], or when the sensing matrices satisfy a restricted isometry property [25]. Gradient descent with low-rank initialization within a specific вҖңcapture neighborhoodвҖқ has been studied in [11], which ensures that the iterates of the algorithm stay low-rank. When рқҗ—вӢҶsuperscriptрқҗ—вӢҶ\mathbf{X}^{\star} is a general rectangular matrix, implicit regularization in matrix sensing has been studied through the lenses of deep matrix factorization in [3, 15, 26, 31], and empirical and theoretical evidence is provided which suggests that a notion of rank-minimization is involved in the implicit bias of gradient descent. However, these works do not establish an explicit characterization of the limiting point of the optimization algorithm, e.g.В in the form of a quantity that is minimized among all minimizers of the empirical risk. In the special case of full-observation matrix sensing, gradient flow has been shown to learn solutions with gradually increasing rank [14].

Most theoretical results on implicit regularization in matrix sensing consider the continuous-time dynamics (i.e.В gradient flow), e.g.В [3, 11, 15, 16, 26, 31], or assume an infinitesimally small initialization, e.g.В [16, 26]. Notable exceptions are [14], which makes a commutativity assumption on the data matrices in discrete time, and [25], which assumes a restricted isometry property that leads to a suboptimal sample complexity when the sensing matrices belong to a general class of random matrices [33].In the context of linear neural networks, the dependence of the implicit bias of gradient descent on the initialization has been studied in [4, 30, 41, 44]. For instance, linear diagonal networks with shared weights were considered in [41], where it was shown that gradient descent minimizes a quantity which corresponds to the в„“1subscriptв„“1\ell_{1}-norm in the limit ОұвҶ’0вҶ’рқӣј0\alpha\rightarrow 0 and to the (weighted) в„“2subscriptв„“2\ell_{2}-norm in the limit ОұвҶ’вҲһвҶ’рқӣј\alpha\rightarrow\infty, where Оұрқӣј\alpha denotes the initialization size. This result was later generalized in [44] using a tensor formulation, which allows for architectures including linear diagonal networks and linear full-length convolutional networks. However, these results focus on vector-based notions of norm-like functions that do not capture matrix-based quantities typically of interest in matrix sensing.

We study the implicit bias of discrete-time mirror descent in matrix sensing in both the general case of rectangular matrices and the particular case of positive semidefinite matrices. Under the only assumption on the sensing matrices рқҗҖisubscriptрқҗҖрқ‘–\mathbf{A}_{i}вҖҷs that there exists a matrix achieving zero training error, we characterize the limiting point as the matrix that minimizes a quantity which interpolates between the nuclear norm and Frobenius norm, parametrized by the mirror map parameter, in the rectangular case; and which is a linear combination of the nuclear norm and the negative von Neumann entropy, parametrized by the initialization size, in the positive semidefinite case. Compared to results on implicit regularization for gradient descent, our framework for mirror descent is simple, and the same analysis yields results for both the case of general rectangular and positive semidefinite matrices.

In the general case of rectangular matrices, we show that mirror descent, initialized at zero and equipped with the spectral hypentropy mirror map [13] parametrized by ОІ>0рқӣҪ0\beta>0, among all global minimizers of the empirical risk, converges to a matrix that minimizes a quantity interpolating between the nuclear norm in the limit ОІвҶ’0вҶ’рқӣҪ0\beta\rightarrow 0 and the Frobenius norm in the limit ОІвҶ’вҲһвҶ’рқӣҪ\beta\rightarrow\infty. As a consequence, our result implies that, for ОІвҶ’0вҶ’рқӣҪ0\beta\rightarrow 0, mirror descent can recover a rank-rрқ‘ҹr matrix рқҗ—вӢҶsuperscriptрқҗ—вӢҶ\mathbf{X}^{\star} under the same set of assumptions that is sufficient for nuclear norm minimization to be successful, namely when the sensing matrices рқҗҖisubscriptрқҗҖрқ‘–\mathbf{A}_{i}вҖҷs satisfy the restricted isometry property with restricted isometry constant smaller than some absolute constant [33], or if рқҗ—вӢҶsuperscriptрқҗ—вӢҶ\mathbf{X}^{\star} satisfies an incoherence condition and rвҖӢ(n+nвҖІ)рқ‘ҹрқ‘ӣsuperscriptрқ‘ӣвҖІr(n+n^{\prime}) (modulo constants and logarithmic term) random entries of рқҗ—вӢҶsuperscriptрқҗ—вӢҶ\mathbf{X}^{\star} are observed [32]. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first recovery guarantee for an implicit regularization-based algorithm that does not explicitly enforce low-rankness in general rectangular matrix sensing.

In the particular case of positive semidefinite matrices, we can alternatively consider the spectral entropy mirror map and show that mirror descent, initialized at ОұвҖӢрқҗҲрқӣјрқҗҲ\alpha\mathbf{I} for any Оұ>0рқӣј0\alpha>0, converges to a positive semidefinite matrix that minimizes a linear combination of the nuclear norm and the negative von Neumann entropy, where the relative weights are controlled by the initialization size Оұрқӣј\alpha. While the limit ОұвҶ’0вҶ’рқӣј0\alpha\rightarrow 0 corresponds to minimizing the nuclear norm as in the case of rectangular matrices, the limit ОұвҶ’вҲһвҶ’рқӣј\alpha\rightarrow\infty corresponds to maximizing the nuclear norm. This also translates into guaranteed recovery of a low-rank matrix рқҗ—вӢҶsuperscriptрқҗ—вӢҶ\mathbf{X}^{\star} for ОұвҶ’0вҶ’рқӣј0\alpha\rightarrow 0 under the same assumptions that are sufficient for nuclear norm minimization [32, 33]. A comparable result for gradient descent with full-rank factorized parametrization has been established in [25] under a stronger assumption on the restricted isometry constant, which is assumed to be smaller than a quantity depending on both the rank and the condition number of the matrix рқҗ—вӢҶsuperscriptрқҗ—вӢҶ\mathbf{X}^{\star} and translates into a sub-optimal sample complexity.

We establish our results using a potential-based analysis for mirror descent in terms of the Bregman divergence, which provides an alternative proof technique to characterize the limiting point of mirror descent compared to the analysis based on KKT optimality conditions used in [17]. As a by-product, our proof of Theorem 1 yields an alternative proof of Theorem 1 in [17]. The advantage of our approach is that convergence of mirror descent does not need to be assumed a-priori and can instead be established using convexity of the empirical risk. The analysis in terms of the Bregman divergence is not limited to convex settings and has been applied to non-convex problems, e.g.В [42, 43, 46], and hence can be of more general interest to investigate the phenomenon of implicit bias.

In the case of square matrices, we show, assuming that the sensing matrices рқҗҖisubscriptрқҗҖрқ‘–\mathbf{A}_{i}вҖҷs are symmetric and commute, that gradient descent with full-rank parametrization рқҗ—=рқҗ”рқҗ”вҠӨвҲ’рқҗ•рқҗ•вҠӨрқҗ—superscriptрқҗ”рқҗ”topsuperscriptрқҗ•рқҗ•top\mathbf{X}=\mathbf{U}\mathbf{U}^{\top}-\mathbf{V}\mathbf{V}^{\top}, where рқҗ”,рқҗ•вҲҲв„қnГ—nрқҗ”рқҗ•superscriptв„қрқ‘ӣрқ‘ӣ\mathbf{U},\mathbf{V}\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}, is a first-order approximation to mirror descent equipped with the spectral hypentropy, where the initialization size corresponds to the mirror map parameter ОІрқӣҪ\beta. A similar connection between mirror descent and reparametrized gradient descent has been established in the vector-case [1, 13, 39, 42]. Similarly, we show that gradient descent with full-rank parametrization рқҗ—=рқҗ”рқҗ”вҠӨрқҗ—superscriptрқҗ”рқҗ”top\mathbf{X}=\mathbf{U}\mathbf{U}^{\top}, рқҗ”вҲҲв„қnГ—nрқҗ”superscriptв„қрқ‘ӣрқ‘ӣ\mathbf{U}\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}, is a first-order approximation to mirror descent equipped with the spectral entropy when the sensing matrices are symmetric and commute, thus recovering a generalization of Theorem 1 in [16] which holds for any positive initialization size Оұ>0рқӣј0\alpha>0 (rather than in the limit ОұвҶ’0вҶ’рқӣј0\alpha\rightarrow 0).

We present numerical simulations which suggest that, in some regimes, our results on the dependence on the initialization size Оұрқӣј\alpha for mirror descent might be indicative for the behavior of gradient descent, even when the sensing matrices рқҗҖisubscriptрқҗҖрқ‘–\mathbf{A}_{i}вҖҷs do not commute. More precisely, the final estimates of gradient descent and mirror descent closely track each other when the number of measurements is sufficiently large for nuclear norm minimization to recover a planted low-rank matrix, while gradient descent seems to put more emphasis on lowering the effective rank [34] at the expense of a higher nuclear norm when fewer measurements are available, which supports the empirical observations in [3, 26].

In this paper, we analyzed discrete-time mirror descent for matrix sensing, equipped with the spectral hypentropy mirror map in the case of general rectangular matrices and equipped with the spectral entropy mirror map in the particular case of positive semidefinite matrices. We showed that mirror descent minimizes a quantity that interpolates between the nuclear norm and Frobenius norm in the rectangular case, and is a linear combination of the nuclear norm and the negative von Neumann entropy in the positive semidefinite case. We used this result to show that mirror descent, a first-order algorithm minimizing the unregularized empirical risk that does not explicitly enforce low-rankness of its iterates, can recover a low-rank matrix рқҗ—вӢҶsuperscriptрқҗ—вӢҶ\mathbf{X}^{\star} under the same set of assumptions which is sufficient for nuclear norm minimization to recover рқҗ—вӢҶsuperscriptрқҗ—вӢҶ\mathbf{X}^{\star} [32, 33].

A downside of mirror descent compared to gradient descent with full-rank factorized parametrization, which is an alternative implicit regularization-based algorithm for matrix sensing, is its computational cost: the spectral hypentropy requires a singular value decomposition in each iteration, while the spectral entropy requires computing a matrix exponential in each iteration, see Appendix A.
