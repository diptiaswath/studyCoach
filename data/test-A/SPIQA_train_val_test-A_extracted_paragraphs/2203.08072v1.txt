Optimal control of complex, highвҖ“dimensional systems requires computationally expensive numerical methods for differential equations (Pytlak, 2006; Rao, 2009). Here, realвҖ“time and hardware constraints preclude the use of accurate and expensive methods, forcing instead the application of cheaper and less accurate algorithms. While the paradigm of optimal control has successfully been applied in various domains (Vadali etВ al., 1999; Lewis etВ al., 2012; Zhang etВ al., 2016), improving accuracy while satisfying computational budget constraints is still a great challenge (Ross & Fahroo, 2006; BaotiДҮ etВ al., 2008).To alleviate computational overheads, we detail a procedure for offline optimization and subsequent online application of hypersolvers (Poli etВ al., 2020a) to optimal control problems. These hybrid solvers achieve the accuracy of higherвҖ“order methods by augmenting numerical results of a base solver with a learning component trained to approximate local truncation residuals. When the cost of a single forwardвҖ“pass of the learning component is kept sufficiently small, hypersolvers improve the computationвҖ“accuracy Pareto front of lowвҖ“order explicit solvers (Butcher, 1997). However, direct application of hybrid solvers to controlled dynamical system involves learning truncation residuals on the higherвҖ“dimensional spaces of state and control inputs.To extend the range of applicability of hypersolvers to controlled dynamical systems, we propose two pretraining strategies designed to improve, in the set of admissible control inputs, on the average or worstвҖ“case hypersolver solution. With the proposed methodology, we empirically show that Pareto front improvements of hypersolvers hold even for optimal control tasks. In particular, we then carry out performance and generalization evaluations in direct and model predictive control tasks. Here, we confirm Pareto front improvements in terms of solution accuracy and subsequent control performance, leading to higher quality control policies and lower control losses. In highвҖ“dimensional regimes, we obtain the same control policy as the one obtained by accurate highвҖ“order solvers with more than 3Г—3\times speedup.

Also known in the literature as receding horizon control, Model Predictive Control (MPC) is a class of flexible control algorithms capable of taking into consideration constraints and nonlinearities (Mayne & Michalska, 1988; Garcia etВ al., 1989).MPC considers finite time windows which are then shifted forward in a receding manner. Hypersolvers are even more impactful in complex highвҖ“dimensional controlled systems.

This work is rooted in the broader literature on surrogate methods for speeding up simulations and solutions of dynamical systems (Grzeszczuk etВ al., 1998; James & Fatahalian, 2003; Gorissen etВ al., 2010). Differently from these approaches, we investigate a methodology to enable faster solution during a downstream, online optimization problem involving a potential mismatch compared to data seen during preвҖ“training. We achieve this through the application of the hypersolver (Poli etВ al., 2020a) paradigm.Modeling mismatches between approximate and nominal models is explored in (Saveriano etВ al., 2017) where residual dynamics are learned efficiently along with the control policy while (Fisac etВ al., 2018; Taylor etВ al., 2019) model systems uncertainties in the context of safetyвҖ“critical control. In contrast to previous work, we model uncertainties with the proposed multiвҖ“stage hypersolver approach by closely interacting with the underlying ODE base solvers and their residuals to improve solution accuracy.The synergy between machine learning and optimal control continues a long line of research on introducing neural networks in optimal control (Hunt etВ al., 1992), applied to modeling (Lin & Cunningham, 1995), identification (Chu etВ al., 1990) or parametrization of the controller itself (Lin etВ al., 1991). Existing surrogate methods for systems (Grzeszczuk etВ al., 1998; James & Fatahalian, 2003) pay a computational cost upfront to accelerate downstream simulation. However, ensuring transfer from offline optimization to the online setting is still an open problem. In our approach, we investigate several strategies for an accurate offlineвҖ“online transfer of a given hypersolver, depending on desiderata on its performance in terms of average residuals and error propagation on the online application. Beyond hypersolvers, our approach further leverages the latest advances in hardware and machine learning software (Paszke etВ al., 2019) by solving thousands of ODEs in parallel on graphics processing units (GPUs).

We presented a novel method for obtaining fast and accurate control policies. Hypersolver models were firstly preвҖ“trained on distributions of states and controllers to approximate higherвҖ“order residuals of base fixedвҖ“step ODE solvers. The obtained models were then employed to improve the accuracy of trajectory solutions over which control policies were optimized. We verified that our method shows consistent improvements in the accuracy of ODE solutions and thus on the quality of control policies optimized through numerical solutions of the system. We envision the proposed approach to benefit the control field and robotics in both simulated and potentially realвҖ“world environments by efficiently solving highвҖ“dimensional spaceвҖ“continuous problems.
