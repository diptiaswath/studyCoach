Inference aims at obtaining the posterior distribution pвҖӢ(Оё|X)рқ‘қconditionalрқңғрқ‘Ӣp(\theta|X) of latent model parameters Оёрқңғ\theta given the observed data Xрқ‘ӢX. In the context of Hierarchical Bayesian Models (HBM), pвҖӢ(Оё|X)рқ‘қconditionalрқңғрқ‘Ӣp(\theta|X) usually has no known analytical form, and can be of a complex shape -different from the priorвҖҷs (Gelman etВ al., 2004). Modern normalizing-flows based techniques -universal density estimators- can overcome this difficulty (Papamakarios etВ al., 2019a; Ambrogioni etВ al., 2021b). Yet, in setups such as neuroimaging, featuring HBMs representing large population studies (Kong etВ al., 2019; Bonkhoff etВ al., 2021), the dimensionality of Оёрқңғ\theta can go over the million. This high dimensionality hinders the usage of normalizing flows, since their parameterization usually scales quadratically with the size of the parameter space (e.g. Dinh etВ al., 2017; Papamakarios etВ al., 2018; Grathwohl etВ al., 2018). Population studies with large dimensional features are therefore inaccessible to off-the-shelf flow-based techniques and their superior expressivity. This can in turn lead to complex, problem-specific derivations: for instance Kong etВ al. (2019) rely on a manually-derived Expectation Maximization (EM) technique. Such an analytical complexity constitutes a strong barrier to entry, and limits the wide and fruitful usage of Bayesian modelling in fields such as neuroimaging. Our main aim is to meet that experimental need: how can we derive a technique both automatic and efficient in the context of very large, hierarchically-organised data?

Approximate inference features a large corpus of methods including Monte Carlo methods (Koller & Friedman, 2009) and Variational Auto Encoders (Zhang etВ al., 2019). We take particular inspiration from the field of Variational Inference (VI) (Blei etВ al., 2017), deemed to be most adapted to large parameter spaces. In VI, the experimenter posits a variational family рқ’¬рқ’¬\mathcal{Q} so as to approximate qвҖӢ(Оё)вүҲpвҖӢ(Оё|X)рқ‘һрқңғрқ‘қconditionalрқңғрқ‘Ӣq(\theta)\approx p(\theta|X). In practice, deriving an expressive, yet computationally attractive variational family can be challenging (Blei etВ al., 2017). This triggered a trend towards the derivation of automatic VI techniques (Kucukelbir etВ al., 2016; Ranganath etВ al., 2013; Ambrogioni etВ al., 2021b). We follow that logic and present a methodology that automatically derives a variational family рқ’¬рқ’¬\mathcal{Q}. In Fig.В 1, from the HBM on the left we derive automatically a neural network architecture on the right. We aim at deriving our variational family рқ’¬рқ’¬\mathcal{Q} in the context of amortized inference (Rezende & Mohamed, 2016; Cranmer etВ al., 2020).Amortization is usually obtained at the cost of an amortization gap from the true posterior, that accumulates on top of a approximation gap dependent on the expressivity of the variational family рқ’¬рқ’¬\mathcal{Q} (Cremer etВ al., 2018).However, once an initial training overhead has been вҖңpaid forвҖқ, amortization means that our technique can be applied to a any number of data points to perform inference in a few seconds.

Due to the very large parameter spaces presented above, our target applications arenвҖҷt amenable to the generic flow-based techniques described in Cranmer etВ al. (2020) or Ambrogioni etВ al. (2021b). We therefore differentiate ourselves in exploiting the invariance of the problem not only through the design of an adapted encoder, but down to the very architecture of our density estimator. Specifically, we focus on the inference problem for Hierarchical Bayesian Models (HBMs) (Gelman etВ al., 2004; Rodrigues etВ al., 2021). The idea to condition the architecture of a density estimator by an analysis of the dependency structure of an HBM has been studied in (Wehenkel & Louppe, 2020; Weilbach etВ al., 2020), in the form of the masking of a single normalizing flow. With Ambrogioni etВ al. (2021b), we instead share the idea to combine multiple separate flows. More generally, our static analysis of a generative model can be associated with structured VI (Hoffman & Blei, 2014; Ambrogioni etВ al., 2021a; b). Yet our working principles are rather orthogonal: structured VI usually aims at exploiting model structure to augment the expressivity of a variational family, whereas we aim at reducing its parameterization.

Our objective is therefore to derive an automatic methodology that takes as input a generative HBM and generates a dual variational family able to perform amortized parameter inference. This variational family exploits the exchangeability in the HBM to reduce its parameterization by orders of magnitude compared to generic methods (Papamakarios etВ al., 2019b; Greenberg etВ al., 2019; Ambrogioni etВ al., 2021b). Consequently, our method can be applied in the context of large, pyramidally-structured data, a challenging setup inaccessible to existing flow-based methods and their superior expressivity. We apply our method to such a large pyramidal setup in the context of neuroimaging (sectionВ 3.5), but demonstrate the benefit of our method beyond that scope. Our general scheme is visible in Fig.В 1, a figure that we will explain throughout the course of the next section.

We are interested in experimental setups modelled using plate-enriched Hierarchical Bayesian Models (HBMs) (Kong etВ al., 2019; Bonkhoff etВ al., 2021). These models feature independent sampling from a common conditional distribution at multiple levels, translating the graphical notion of plates (Gilks etВ al., 1994). This nested structure, combined with large measurements -such as the ones in fMRI- can result in massive latent parameter spaces. For instance the population study in Kong etВ al. (2019) features multiple subjects, with multiple measures per subject, and multiple brain vertices per measure, for a latent space of around 0.40.40.4 million parameters. Our method aims at performing inference in the context of those large plate-enriched HBMs.

In this section, we derive our main methodological contribution. We aim at obtaining posterior distributions for a generative model of pyramidal structure. Baseline choice In our experiments we use as baselines: Mean Field VI (MF-VI) (Blei etВ al., 2017) is a common-practice method; (Sequential) Neural Posterior Estimation (NPE-C, SNPE-C) (Greenberg etВ al., 2019) is a structure-unaware, likelihood-free method: SNPE-C results from the sequential -and no longer amortized- usage of NPE-C; Total Latent Space Flow (TLSF) (Rezende & Mohamed, 2016) is a reverse-KL counterpoint to SNPE-C: both fit a single normalizing flow to the entirety of the latent parameter space but SNPE-C uses a forward KL loss while TLSF uses a reverse KL loss; Cascading Flows (CF) (Ambrogioni etВ al., 2021b) is a structure-aware, prior-aware method: CF-A is our main point of comparison in this section. For relevant methods, the suffix -(N)A designates the (non) amortized implementation. More details related to the choice and implementation of those baselines can be found in our supplemental material.

Exploiting structure in inference In the SBI and VAE setups, data structure can be exploited through learnable data embedders (Zhang etВ al., 2019; Radev etВ al., 2020). We go one step beyond and also use the problem structure to shape our density estimator: we factorize the parameter space of a problem into smaller components, and share network parameterization across tasks we know to be equivalent (see sectionВ 2.2 and 3.3). In essence, we construct our architecture not based on a ground HBM graph, but onto its template, a principle that could be generalized to other types of templates, such as temporal models (Koller & Friedman, 2009).Contrary to the notion of black box, we argue that experimenters oftentimes can identify properties such as exchangeability in their experiments (Gelman etВ al., 2004). As our experiments illustrate (sectionВ 3.4, sectionВ 3.3), there is much value in exploiting this structure. Beyond the sole notion of plates, a static analysis of a forward model could automatically identify other desirable properties that could be then leveraged for efficient inference. This concept points towards fruitful connections to be made with the field of lifted inference (Broeck etВ al., 2021; Chen etВ al., 2020).

Mean-Field approximation A limitation in our work is that our posterior distribution is akin to a mean field approximation (Blei etВ al., 2017): with the current design, no statistical dependencies can be modelled between the RV blocks over which we fit normalizing flows (see sectionВ 2.3). Regrouping RV templates, we could model more dependencies at a given hierarchy. On the contrary, our method prevents the direct modelling of dependencies between ground RVs corresponding to repeated instances of the same template. Those dependencies can arise as part of inference (Webb etВ al., 2018).We made the choice of the Mean Field approximation to streamline our contribution, and allow for a clear delineation of the advantages of our methods, not tying them up to a method augmenting a variational family with statistical dependencies, an open research subject (Ambrogioni etВ al., 2021b; Weilbach etВ al., 2020). Though computationally attractive, the mean field approximation nonetheless limits the expressivity of our variational family (Ranganath etВ al., 2016; Hoffman & Blei, 2014). We ponder the possibility to leverage VI architectures such as the one derived by Ranganath etВ al. (2016); Ambrogioni etВ al. (2021b) and their augmented variational objectives for structured populations of normalizing flows such as ours.

Conclusion For the delineated yet expressive class of pyramidal Bayesian models, we have introduced a potent, automatically derived architecture able to perform amortized parameter inference. Through a Hierarchical Encoder, our method conditions a network of normalizing flows that stands as a variational family dual to the forward HBM. To demonstrate the expressivity and scalability of our method, we successfully applied it to a challenging neuroimaging setup. Our work stands as an original attempt to leverage exchangeability in a generative model.
