Deep Learning revolutionized Machine Learning by improving the accuracy by dozens of percents for fundamental tasks in Natural Language Processing (NLP) through learning representations of a natural language via a deep neural network (Mikolov et al., 2013; Radford et al., 2018; Le and Mikolov, 2014; Peters et al., 2018; Radford et al., 2019). Lately, it was shown that there is no need to train those networks from scratch each time we receive a new task/data, but to fine-tune a full pre-trained model on the specific task (Dai and Le, 2015; Radford et al., 2018; Devlin et al., 2019).However, in many cases, those networks are extremely large compared to classical machine learning models.For example, both BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) and XLNet (Yang et al., 2019) have more than 110110110 million parameters, and RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019b) consists of more than 125125125 million parameters.Such large networks have two main drawbacks: (i) they use too much storage, e.g. memory or disk space, which may be infeasible for small IoT devices, smartphones, or when a personalized network is needed for each user/object/task, and (ii) classification may take too much time, especially for real-time applications such as NLP tasks: speech recognition, translation or speech-to-text.

To this end, many papers suggested different techniques to compress large NLP networks, e.g., by low-rank factorization (Wang et al., 2019; Lan et al., 2019), pruning (McCarley, 2019; Michel et al., 2019; Fan et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2019; Gordon et al., 2020), quantization (Zafrir et al., 2019; Shen et al., 2020), weight sharing (Lan et al., 2019), and knowledge distillation (Sanh et al., 2019; Tang et al., 2019; Mukherjee and Awadallah, 2019; Liu et al., 2019a; Sun et al., 2019; Jiao et al., 2019); see more example papers and a comparison table in Gordon (2019) for compressing the BERT model. There is no consensus on which approach should be used in what contexts. However, in the context of compressing the embedding layer, the most common approach is low-rank factorization as in Lan et al. (2019), and it may be combined with other techniques such as quantization and pruning.

In this work, we suggest a novel low-rank factorization technique for compressing the embedding layer of a given model. This is motivated by the fact that in many networks, the embedding layer accounts for 20%−40%percent20percent4020\%-40\% of the network size. Our approach - MESSI: Multiple (parallel) Estimated SVDs for Smaller Intralayers - achieves a better accuracy for the same compression rate compared to the known standard matrix factorization. To present it, we first describe an embedding layer, the known technique for compressing it, and the geometric assumptions underlying this technique. Then, we give our approach followed by geometric intuition, and detailed explanation about the motivation and the architecture changes. Finally, we report our experimental results that demonstrate the strong performance of our technique.

The embedding layer aims to represent each word from a vocabulary by a real-valued vector that reflects the word’s semantic and syntactic information that can be extracted from the language.One can think of the embedding layer as a simple matrix multiplication as follows. The layer receives a standard vector x∈ℝn𝑥superscriptℝ𝑛x\in\mathbb{R}^{n} (a row of the identity matrix, exactly one non-zero entry, usually called one-hot vector) that represents a word in the vocabulary, it multiplies x𝑥x by a matrix AT∈ℝd×nsuperscript𝐴𝑇superscriptℝ𝑑𝑛A^{T}\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times n} to obtain the corresponding d𝑑d-dimensional word embedding vector y=AT​x𝑦superscript𝐴𝑇𝑥y=A^{T}x, which is the row in A𝐴A that corresponds to the non-zero entry of x𝑥x. The embedding layer has n𝑛n input neurons, and the output has d𝑑d neurons. The n​d𝑛𝑑nd edges between the input and output neurons define the matrix A∈ℝn×d𝐴superscriptℝ𝑛𝑑A\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d}. Here, the entry in the i𝑖ith row and j𝑗jth column of A𝐴A is the weight of the edge between the i𝑖ith input neuron to the j𝑗jth output neuron; see Figure. 1.

A common approach for compressing an embedding layer is to compute the j𝑗j-rank approximation Aj∈ℝn×dsubscript𝐴𝑗superscriptℝ𝑛𝑑A_{j}\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d} of the corresponding matrix A𝐴A via SVD (Singular Value Decomposition; see e.g., Lan et al. (2019); Yu et al. (2017) and Acharya et al. (2019)), factor Ajsubscript𝐴𝑗A_{j} into two smaller matrices U∈ℝn×j𝑈superscriptℝ𝑛𝑗U\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times j} and V∈ℝj×d𝑉superscriptℝ𝑗𝑑V\in\mathbb{R}^{j\times d} (i.e. Aj=U​Vsubscript𝐴𝑗𝑈𝑉A_{j}=UV), and replace the original embedding layer that corresponds to A𝐴A by a pair of layers that correspond to U𝑈U and V𝑉V.The number of parameters is then reduced to j​(n+d)𝑗𝑛𝑑j(n+d). Moreover, computing the output takes O​(j​(n+d))𝑂𝑗𝑛𝑑O(j(n+d)) time, compared to the O​(n​d)𝑂𝑛𝑑O(nd) time for computing AT​xsuperscript𝐴𝑇𝑥A^{T}x.As above, we continue to use Ajsubscript𝐴𝑗A_{j} to refer to a rank-j𝑗j approximation of a matrix A𝐴A.

The layers that correspond to the matrices U𝑈U and V𝑉V above are sometimes used only as initial seeds for a training process that is called fine tuning. Here, the training data is fed into the network, and the error is measured with respect to the final classification. Hence, the structure of the data remains the same but the edges are updated in each iteration to give a better accuracy.

The embedding layer can be encoded into a matrix A∈ℝn×d𝐴superscriptℝ𝑛𝑑A\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d} as explained above. Hence, each of the n𝑛n rows of A𝐴A corresponds to a point (vector) in ℝdsuperscriptℝ𝑑\mathbb{R}^{d}, and the j𝑗j-rank approximation Aj∈ℝn×dsubscript𝐴𝑗superscriptℝ𝑛𝑑A_{j}\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d} represents the projection on the j𝑗j-dimensional subspace that minimizes the sum of squared distances (“errors”) to the points. Projecting these points onto any j𝑗j-dimensional subspace of ℝdsuperscriptℝ𝑑\mathbb{R}^{d} would allow us to encode every point only via its j𝑗j-coordinates on this subspace, and store only n​j𝑛𝑗nj entries instead of the original n​d𝑛𝑑nd entries of A𝐴A. This is the matrix U∈ℝn×j𝑈superscriptℝ𝑛𝑗U\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times j}, where each row encodes the corresponding row in A𝐴A by its j𝑗j-coordinates on this subspace. The subspace itself can be represented by its basis of j𝑗j d𝑑d-dimensional vectors (j​d𝑗𝑑jd entries), which is the column space of a matrix VT∈ℝd×jsuperscript𝑉𝑇superscriptℝ𝑑𝑗V^{T}\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times j}. Figure 2 illustrates the small pair of layers that corresponds to U𝑈U and V𝑉V, those layers are a compression for the original big layer that corresponds to A𝐴A.

However, our goal is not only to compress the network or matrix, but also to approximate the original matrix operator A𝐴A. To this end, among all the possible j𝑗j-subspaces of ℝdsuperscriptℝ𝑑\mathbb{R}^{d}, we may be interested in the j𝑗j-subspace that minimizes the sum of squared distances to the points, i.e., the sum of squared projected errors. This subspace can be computed easily via SVD. The corresponding projections of the rows of A𝐴A on this subspace are the rows of the j𝑗j-rank matrix Ajsubscript𝐴𝑗A_{j}.

The hidden or statistical assumption in this model is that the rows of the matrix A𝐴A (that represents the embedding layer) were actually generated by adding i.i.d. Gaussian noise to each point in a set of n𝑛n points on a j𝑗j-dimensional subspace, that is spanned by what are called latent variables or factors. Given only the resulting matrix A𝐴A, the j𝑗j-subspace that maximizes the likelihood (probability) of generating the original points is spanned by the j𝑗j largest singular vectors of A𝐴A.

Even if we accept the assumption of Gaussian noise, e.g. due to simplicity of computations or the law of large numbers, it is not intuitively clear why we should assume that the rows of A𝐴A were sampled from a single distribution. Natural questions that arise are:(i)Can we get smaller and/or more accurate models in real-world networks by assuming multiple instead of a single generating distribution (i.e. multiple subspaces)?(ii)Can we efficiently compute the corresponding factorizations and represent them as part of a network ?

We answer the above open questions by suggesting the following contributions.In short, the answers are:(i)In all the real-world networks that we tested, it is almost always better to assume k≥2𝑘2k\geq 2 distributions rather than a single one that generated the data. It is better in the sense that the resulting accuracy of the network is better compared to k=1𝑘1k=1 (SVD) for the same compression rate.(ii)While approximating the global minimum is Max-SNP-Hard, our experiments show that we can efficiently compute many local minima and take the smallest one. We then explain how to encode the result back into the network. This is by suggesting a new embedding layer architecture that we call MESSI (Multiple (parallel) Estimated SVDs for Smaller Intralayers); see Figure 3. Extensive experimental results show significant improvement.

Our technique also constructs the matrix A∈ℝn×d𝐴superscriptℝ𝑛𝑑A\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d} from a given embedding layer. However, inspired by the geometric intuition from the previous section, we suggest to approximate the n𝑛n rows of A𝐴A by clustering them to k≥2𝑘2k\geq 2 subspaces instead of one. More precisely, given an integer k≥1𝑘1k\geq 1 we aim to compute a set of k𝑘k subspaces in ℝdsuperscriptℝ𝑑\mathbb{R}^{d}, each of dimension j𝑗j, that will minimize the sum over every squared distance of every point (row in A𝐴A) to its nearest subspace. This can be considered as a combination of j𝑗j-rank or j𝑗j-subspace approximation, as defined above, and k𝑘k-means clustering. In the k𝑘k-means clustering problem we wish to approximate n𝑛n points by k𝑘k center points that minimizes the sum over squared distance between every point to its nearest center. In our case, the k𝑘k centers points are replaced by k𝑘k subspaces, each of dimension j𝑗j. In computational geometry, this type of problem is called projective clustering; see Figure 4.

The result of the above technique is a set of k𝑘k matrices Aj1,⋯,Ajksuperscriptsubscript𝐴𝑗1⋯superscriptsubscript𝐴𝑗𝑘A_{j}^{1},\cdots,A_{j}^{k}, each of rank j𝑗j and dimension ni×dsubscript𝑛𝑖𝑑n_{i}\times d where the i𝑖ith matrix corresponds to the cluster of nisubscript𝑛𝑖n_{i} points that were projected on the i𝑖ith j𝑗j-dimensional subspace. Each of those matrices can be factored into two smaller matrices (due to its low rank), i.e., for every i∈{1,⋯,k}𝑖1⋯𝑘i\in\left\{1,\cdots,k\right\}, we have Aji=Ui​Visubscriptsuperscript𝐴𝑖𝑗superscript𝑈𝑖superscript𝑉𝑖A^{i}_{j}=U^{i}V^{i}, where Ui∈ℝni×jsuperscript𝑈𝑖superscriptℝsubscript𝑛𝑖𝑗U^{i}\in\mathbb{R}^{n_{i}\times j}, and Vi∈ℝj×dsuperscript𝑉𝑖superscriptℝ𝑗𝑑V^{i}\in\mathbb{R}^{j\times d}. To plug these matrices as part of the final network instead of the embedded layer, we suggest to encode these matrices via k𝑘k parallel sub-layers as described in what follows and illustrated in Figure 3.

Our suggested architecture can be generalized and extended to support many other optimization functions that may be relevant for different types of datasets, tasks or applications besides NLP.

For simplicity, our suggested approach aims to minimize sum of squared distances to k𝑘k subspaces. However, it can be easily applied also to sum of distances from the points to the subspace. In this case, we aim to compute the maximum-likelihood of the generating subspaces assuming a Laplacian instead of Gaussian distribution. More generally, we may want to minimize the sum over every distance to the power of q>0𝑞0q>0., i.e., we take the q𝑞q-norm ∥e​r​r∥qsubscriptdelimited-∥∥𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑞\left\lVert err\right\rVert_{q} where e​r​r𝑒𝑟𝑟err is the distance between a point to its projection on its closest subspace.

Even for k=1𝑘1k=1 recent results of Tukan et al. (2020b) show improvement over SVD.

Observe that given the optimal subpaces, the system architecture in these cases remains the same as ours in Figure 3, and a local minimum can still be obtained by the suggested algorithm. The only difference is that the SVD computation of the optimal subspace for a cluster of points (k=1𝑘1k=1) should be replaced by more involved approximation algorithm for computing the subspace that minimizes sum over distances to the power of q𝑞q; see e.g. Tukan et al. (2020b); Clarkson and Woodruff (2015).

Non-uniform dimensions. In this paper we assume that k𝑘k subspaces approximate the input points, and each subspace has dimension exactly j𝑗j, where j,k≥1𝑗𝑘1j,k\geq 1 are given integers. A better strategy is to allow each subspace to have a different dimension, jisubscript𝑗𝑖j_{i} for every i∈{1,⋯,k}𝑖1⋯𝑘i\in\left\{1,\cdots,k\right\}, or add a constraint only on the sum j1+⋯+jksubscript𝑗1⋯subscript𝑗𝑘j_{1}+\cdots+j_{k} of dimensions. Similarly, the number k𝑘k may be tuned as in our experimental results. Using this approach we can improve the accuracy and enjoy the same compression rate. This search or parameter tuning, however, might increase the computation time of the compressed network. It also implies layers of different sizes (for each subspace) in Figure 3.

Dictionary Learning. Our approach of projective clustering is strongly related to Dictionary Learning (Tosic and Frossard, 2011; Mairal et al., 2009). Here, the input is a matrix A∈ℝn×d𝐴superscriptℝ𝑛𝑑A\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d} and the output is a “dictionary” VT∈ℝd×jsuperscript𝑉𝑇superscriptℝ𝑑𝑗V^{T}\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times j} and projections or atoms which are the rows of U∈ℝn×j𝑈superscriptℝ𝑛𝑗U\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times j} that minimize ∥A−U​V∥delimited-∥∥𝐴𝑈𝑉\left\lVert A-UV\right\rVert under some norm. It is easy to prove that U​V𝑈𝑉UV is simply the j𝑗j-rank approximation of A𝐴A, as explained in Section 1. However, if we have additional constraints, such as that every row of U𝑈U should have, say, only k=1𝑘1k=1 non-zero entries, then geometrically the columns of VTsuperscript𝑉𝑇V^{T} are the j𝑗j lines that intersects the origin and minimize the sum of distances to the points. For k>1𝑘1k>1 every point is projected onto the subspace that minimizes its distance and is spanned by k𝑘k columns of VTsuperscript𝑉𝑇V^{T}.

Coresets. Coresets are a useful tool, especially in projective clustering, to reduce the size of the input (compress it in some sense) while preserving the optimal solution or even the sum of distances to any set of k𝑘k subspaces. However, we are not aware of any efficient implementations and the dependency on d𝑑d and k𝑘k is usually exponential as in Edwards and Varadarajan (2005). A natural open problem is to compute more efficient and practical coresets for projective clustering.
