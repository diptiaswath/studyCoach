Linear regression, or ℓ2subscriptℓ2\ell_{2} least-square problem is ubiquitous in numerical linear algebra, scientific computing and machine learning. Given a tall skinny matrix A∈ℝn×d𝐴superscriptℝ𝑛𝑑A\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d} and a label vector b∈ℝn𝑏superscriptℝ𝑛b\in\mathbb{R}^{n}, the goal is to (approximately) compute an optimal solution x′superscript𝑥′x^{\prime} that minimizes the ℓ2subscriptℓ2\ell_{2} loss ‖A​x−b‖2subscriptnorm𝐴𝑥𝑏2\|Ax-b\|_{2}. Unfortunately, modern machine learning emphasizes more and more on large, complex, and nonlinear models such as deep neural networks, thus linear regression becomes less appealing as a model. However, it is still a very important subroutine in many deep learning and optimization frameworks, especially second-order method for training neural networks [4, 32] or convex optimization [19, 20, 31, 15]. In these applications, one typically seeks forward error guarantee, i.e., how close is the approximate solution x′superscript𝑥′x^{\prime} to the optimal solution x∗superscript𝑥x^{*}. This makes their analysis particularly hard to generalize to other dense sketching matrices beyond SRHT.

In this work, we present a novel framework for analyzing the ℓ∞subscriptℓ\ell_{\infty} guarantee induced by SRHT and more generally, a large class of dense sketching matrices. Our analysis is arguably much simpler than [22], and it exposes the fundamental structure of sketching matrices that provides ℓ∞subscriptℓ\ell_{\infty} guarantee: if any two columns of the sketching matrix have a small inner product with high probability, then ℓ∞subscriptℓ\ell_{\infty} guarantee can be preserved. We then prove that the small pairwise column inner product is also closely related to the Oblivious Coordinate-wise Embedding (OCE) property introduced in [31]. More concretely, for any two fixed vectors g,h∈ℝn𝑔ℎsuperscriptℝ𝑛g,h\in\mathbb{R}^{n}, we say the sketching matrix is (β,δ,n)𝛽𝛿𝑛(\beta,\delta,n)-OCE if |⟨S​g,S​h⟩−⟨g,h⟩|≤βm⋅‖g‖2​‖h‖2𝑆𝑔𝑆ℎ𝑔ℎ⋅𝛽𝑚subscriptnorm𝑔2subscriptnormℎ2|\langle Sg,Sh\rangle-\langle g,h\rangle|\leq\frac{\beta}{\sqrt{m}}\cdot\|g\|_{2}\|h\|_{2} holds with probability at least 1−δ1𝛿1-\delta. This property has previously been leveraged for approximating matrix-vector product between a dynamically-changing projection matrix and an online sequence of vectors for the fast linear program and empirical risk minimization algorithms [20, 15, 31, 23] as these algorithms need ℓ∞subscriptℓ\ell_{\infty} bound on the matrix-vector product. One common theme shared by those applications and ℓ∞subscriptℓ\ell_{\infty} guarantee is to use dense sketching matrices, such as random Gaussian, the Alon-Matias-Szegedy sketch (AMS, [2]) or SRHT. This is in drastic contrast with the trending direction for using sparse matrices such as Count Sketch [5, 8] and OSNAP [21, 6], as they can be applied in (nearly) input sparsity time.

In recent years, sketches that can be applied to the tensor product of matrices/vectors have gained popularity [3, 27, 10, 28, 9, 1, 35, 26, 36, 30, 29, 24] as they can speed up optimization tasks and large-scale kernel learning. We show that dense sketches for degree-2 tensors also provide ℓ∞subscriptℓ\ell_{\infty} guarantee.

In this paper, we study the sketching-based regression algorithm with an ℓ∞subscriptℓ\ell_{\infty} guarantee. We show that SRHT with m=ϵ−2​d​log3⁡(n/δ)𝑚superscriptitalic-ϵ2𝑑superscript3𝑛𝛿m=\epsilon^{-2}d\log^{3}(n/\delta) rows provides the desired ℓ∞subscriptℓ\ell_{\infty} guarantee solution, improving upon the ϵ−2​d1+γsuperscriptitalic-ϵ2superscript𝑑1𝛾\epsilon^{-2}d^{1+\gamma} rows for γ=log⁡log⁡nlog⁡d𝛾𝑛𝑑\gamma=\sqrt{\frac{\log\log n}{\log d}} of [22]. This is nearly-optimal up to logarithmic factors. Our proof adapts the oblivious coordinate-wise embedding property introduced in [31] in a novel way. We also greatly extends the reach of ℓ∞subscriptℓ\ell_{\infty} guarantee to degree-2 Kronecker product regression via TensorSRHT matrix.

In addition, we introduce the SRCT and TensorSRCT matrices. These matrices can be applied in a fashion similar to SRHT, and they have similar OCE behaviors as SRHT.

Our result provides an elegant way to integrate fast, sketching-based regression solver for optimization process, in particular second-order methods. The regression problem per iteration can be solved in time nearly-linear in the input size, and the ℓ∞subscriptℓ\ell_{\infty} guarantee comes in handy when analyzing convergence with approximate step. It also gives improved generalization bound on approximate regression via SRHT [22].
