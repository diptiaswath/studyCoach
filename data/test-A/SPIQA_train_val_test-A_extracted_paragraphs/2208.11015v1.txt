Community detection (Lancichinetti and Fortunato, 2009; Papadopoulos et al., 2012; Xie et al., 2013; Cao et al., 2018; Kang et al., 2020; Ding et al., 2021) is an indispensable task in network analyses to understand the fundamental features of networks. Community detection algorithms should be designed by taking into account the inherently overlapping nature since community memberships in social networks are allowed to overlap as nodes belong to multiple clusters at once (Shchur and Günnemann, 2019; Yang and Leskovec, 2012, 2013; Yang et al., 2013; Ye et al., 2018; Tran et al., 2021a).

Nevertheless, the underlying true network is not initially known in various domains of real-world network applications (Valente and Pumpuang, 2007). Thus, existing downstream graph mining approaches are often ineffective without retrieving relationships among nodes in a network, which is very labor-intensive. To overcome this problem, exploratory influence maximization was introduced along with a solution to perform information retrieval over unknown networks by querying individual nodes to explore their neighborhoods, while aiming at discovering the set of seed nodes leading to the maximum influence spread (Wilder et al., 2018a, b; Kamarthi et al., 2020; Tran et al., 2021b). However, the aforementioned studies focused on solving the problem of influence maximization; the research related to community detection via exploratory learning has been largely underexplored yet.

In social networks, node metadata can often be retrieved from a variety of sources such as user-created content over given (social) networks (Leroy et al., 2010) or even be inferred from users’ behavior (Kosinski et al., 2013), thereby making them more readily available than the relationship between individuals. In this paper, motivated by the fact that collecting such node metadata is relatively easy-to-collect and cost-effective (Leroy et al., 2010), we present META-CODE, a novel end-to-end framework for overlapping community detection in networks with unknown topology via exploratory learning that retrieves information from both node queries and node metadata. As the first step of META-CODE, we infer the network structure by using both the multi-assignment clustering (MAC) (Streich et al., 2009) and the community-affiliation graph model (AGM) (Yang and Leskovec, 2012). Then, META-CODE performs the following two steps iteratively: we generate node-level community-affiliation embeddings based on graph neural networks (GNNs) trained by our new loss function, which is established for preserving structural and attribute information; and, for network exploration, we identify the next node to query based on the discovered community affiliations.

Through comprehensive experiments using real-world datasets, we demonstrate that META-CODE consistently outperforms benchmark methods for overlapping community detection. We also validate the effectiveness of both our network inference module and our training model in GNNs. Moreover, the proposed node query strategy in META-CODE shows faster network exploration than competing sampling strategies, thus potentially leading to superior performance on community detection.

Datasets. We conduct our experiments on two Facebook datasets (Leskovec and Mcauley, 2012), which are ego-networks collected from Facebook social networks, and one large network dataset, Engineering, which is a co-authorship network constructed from the Microsoft Academic Graph (Microsoft, 2015) where communities correspond to research areas and node metadata are based on keywords of the papers by each author222Although there are other Facebook and large co-authorship networks, we have not further adopted them since they exhibit similar tendencies to those of the above three datasets under consideration..

Performance metrics. We quantify the degree of agreement between the ground truth communities and the detected communities by adopting the normalized mutual information (NMI) and the F1subscript𝐹1F_{1} score. We note that the two metrics are in a range of [0,1]01[0,1], and higher values represent better performance.

Benchmark methods. We present three benchmark methods for overlapping community detection: BIGCLAM (Yang and Leskovec, 2013) learns the affiliation matrix using a block coordinate gradient ascent algorithm; vGraph (Sun et al., 2019) jointly performs community detection and node representation learning based on a generative model; and NOCD (Shchur and Günnemann, 2019) discovers overlapping communities via GNNs using the network structure and node metadata. Since the above benchmark methods were originally developed for fully observable networks, we need to first infer the network structure. To this end, we employ two sampling strategies for network exploration via node queries: random sampling (RS) and depth-first search (DFS)333Although other sophisticated sampling strategies such as biased random walks can also be employed, they were not employed in our study since they reveal similar tendencies to those of RS and DFS.. Similarly as in META-CODE, we iteratively perform community detection alongside the explored network via sampling given a budget of node queries. We implement all these benchmark methods through the parameter settings mentioned in the original articles.

Experimental setup. In our experiments, we adopt GCN (Kipf and Welling, 2017) with 2 layers as a popular GNN architecture to extract the community-affiliation embeddings. We train our GNN model using Adam optimizer (Kingma and Ba, 2015) with a learning rate of 0.001. All models are implemented via a geometric learning library, named PyTorch Geometric (Fey and Lenssen, 2019). We tune the hyperparameter η𝜂\eta within a designated range η∈[1.0,2.0]𝜂1.02.0\eta\in[1.0,2.0], which leads to substantial gains over the case of η=0𝜂0\eta=0 corresponding to the GNN model trained in the sense of preserving the structural information only. We introduced META-CODE, a novel end-to-end solution for overlapping community detection in networks with unknown topology. Our META-CODE method was designed in such a way of leveraging limited node queries and node metadata to iteratively generate GNN-based embeddings and identify queried nodes after initial network inference. Using three real-world datasets, we demonstrated (a) the superiority of META-CODE over competitors with substantial gains up to 23.56%percent23.5623.56\% and (b) the effectiveness of key components in META-CODE such as the initial network inference module and the network exploration module via node queries.
