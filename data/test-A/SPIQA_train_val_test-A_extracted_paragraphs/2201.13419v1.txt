In this paper, we consider the fundamental problem of agnostically learning homogeneous halfspaces. Specifically, we assume there is an unknown distribution P𝑃P over ℝd×{−1,+1}superscriptℝ𝑑11\mathbb{R}^{d}\times\{-1,+1\} to which we have access in the form of independent and identically distributed samples drawn from P𝑃P. Our goal is to compete with a homogeneous linear classifier u¯¯𝑢\bar{u} (i.e. one that predicts the label sign​(⟨u¯,x⟩)sign¯𝑢𝑥\mathrm{sign}(\langle\bar{u},x\rangle) for input x𝑥x) that achieves the optimal zero-one risk of OPT>0OPT0\mathrm{OPT}>0 over P𝑃P.Alternatively, we can think that the labels of the examples are first generated by u¯¯𝑢\bar{u}, andthen an OPTOPT\mathrm{OPT} fraction of the labels are adversarially corrupted.

There have been many algorithmic and hardness results on this topic, seeSection 1.1 for a discussion. A very natural heuristic for solving the problem is to use logistic regression. However, the analysis oflogistic regression for this problem isstill largely incomplete, even though it is one of the most fundamentalalgorithms in machine learning.One reason for this is that it can return extremely poor solutions in the worst case:Ben-David et al. (2012) showed that the minimizer of thelogistic risk may attain a zero-one risk as bad as 1−OPT1OPT1-\mathrm{OPT}on an adversarially-constructed distribution.

As a result, much attention has been devoted to certain “well-behaved”distributions, for which much better results can be obtained.However, even when the marginal distribution on the feature space, Pxsubscript𝑃𝑥P_{x}, is assumed to be isotropic log-concave, in recent work Diakonikolas et al. (2020d) proved an Ω~​(OPT)~ΩOPT\widetilde{\Omega}\mathinner{\bigl{(}\mathrm{OPT}\bigr{)}} lower bound on the zero-one risk for any convex surrogate including logistic regression.On the positive side, in another recent work, Frei et al. (2021b) proved that vanilla gradient descent on the logistic risk can attain a zero-one risk ofO~​(OPT)~𝑂OPT\widetilde{O}\mathinner{\bigl{(}\sqrt{\mathrm{OPT}}\bigr{)}}, as long as Pxsubscript𝑃𝑥P_{x} satisfies some specificwell-behaved-ness conditions.(For the details of such conditions, see Sections 1.1 and 3.)

The above results still leave a big gap between the upper and the lower bounds, raising the question of identifying the fundamental limits of logistic regression for this problem.In this work we study this question and develop the following set of results.

In Section 2, we construct a distribution Q𝑄Q over ℝ2×{−1,1}superscriptℝ211\mathbb{R}^{2}\times\{-1,1\}, and prove a lower bound for logistic regressionthat matches the upper bound in (Frei et al., 2021b), thereby closing the gap in recent works (Diakonikolas et al., 2020d; Frei et al., 2021b).Specifically, the marginal distribution Qxsubscript𝑄𝑥Q_{x} is isotropic and bounded,and satisfies all the well-behaved-ness conditions from the aforementioned papers,but the global minimizer of the logistic risk on Q𝑄Qonly attains Ω​(OPT)ΩOPT\Omega\mathinner{\bigl{(}\sqrt{\mathrm{OPT}}\bigr{)}} zero-one risk on Q𝑄Q.

The lower bound mentioned above shows that one needs to make additional assumptions to prove better bounds.In Section 3, we show that by making a radial Lipschitzness assumption in addition to well-behaved-ness, it is indeed possible to achieve the near-optimalO~​(OPT)~𝑂OPT\widetilde{O}(\mathrm{OPT}) zero-one risk via logistic regression.In particular, our upper bound result holds if the projection of Pxsubscript𝑃𝑥P_{x} onto anytwo-dimensional subspace has Lipschitz continuous densities. Moreover, our upper bound analysis is versatile:it can recover theO~​(OPT)~𝑂OPT\widetilde{O}\mathinner{\bigl{(}\sqrt{\mathrm{OPT}}\bigr{)}} guarantee for general well-behaveddistributions shown by Frei et al. (2021b),and it also works for the hinge loss, which motivates a simple and efficient two-phase algorithm, as described next.

Motivated by our analysis, in Section 4, we describe a simple two-phase algorithm that achieves O~​(OPT)~𝑂OPT\widetilde{O}\mathinner{\bigl{(}{\mathrm{OPT}}\bigr{)}} error for general well-behaved distributions, without assuming radial Lipschitzness. Thus, we show that the cost of avoiding the radial Lipschitzness condition is simply an additional convex loss minimization. Our two-phase algorithm involveslogistic regression followed by stochastic gradient descent with the hinge loss(i.e., the perceptron algorithm) with a restricted domain and warm start.For general well-behaved distributions, the first phase can only achieve anO~​(OPT)~𝑂OPT\widetilde{O}\mathinner{\bigl{(}\sqrt{\mathrm{OPT}}\bigr{)}} guarantee, however we show that the secondphase can boost the upper bound to O~​(OPT)~𝑂OPT\widetilde{O}(\mathrm{OPT}).

The problem of agnostic learning of halfspaces has a long and rich history Kearns et al. (1994). Here we survey the results most relevant to our work. It is well known that in the distribution independent setting, even weak agnostic learning is computationally hard (Feldman et al., 2006; Guruswami and Raghavendra, 2009; Daniely, 2016). As a result most algorithmic results have been obtained under assumptions on the marginal distribution Pxsubscript𝑃𝑥P_{x} over the examples.

The work of Kalai et al. (2008) designed algorithms that achieve OPT+ϵOPTitalic-ϵ\mathrm{OPT}+\epsilon error for any ϵ>0italic-ϵ0\epsilon>0 in time dpoly​(1ϵ)superscript𝑑poly1italic-ϵd^{\text{poly}(\frac{1}{\epsilon})} for isotropic log-concave densities and for the uniform distribution over the hypercube.
