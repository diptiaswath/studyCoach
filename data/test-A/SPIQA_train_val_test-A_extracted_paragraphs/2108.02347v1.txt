Transformers [vaswani2017attention] have achieved state-of-the-art performance in sequence processing tasks, including machine translation and language modeling [vaswani2017attention, al2019character, dai2019transformer, baevski2018adaptive, williams-etal-2018-broad, devlin2018bert, NEURIPS2020_1457c0d6]. Also, transformers can effectively transfer knowledge from a pre-trained model to tasks with limited supervision [radford2018improving, radford2019language, devlin2018bert, yang2019xlnet, liu2019roberta]. Transformers rely on the attention mechanism and particularly self-attention [cho-etal-2014-learning, parikh-etal-2016-decomposable, DBLP:journals/corr/LinFSYXZB17], an inductive bias that connects each token in the input through a relevance weighted basis of every other token, as a fundamental building block for their modeling [bahdanau2014neural, vaswani2017attention, kim2017structured]. Moreover, it has been argued that the flexibility in capturing diverse syntactic and semantic relationships [tenney-etal-2019-bert, vig-belinkov-2019-analyzing, clark-etal-2019-bert, voita-etal-2019-analyzing, hewitt-liang-2019-designing] and the capacity of the attention mechanism [tenney-etal-2019-bert] are critical components for the success of transformers.

The self-attention mechanism is used to learn long-range dependencies while enabling parallel processing of the input sequence. Also, the computational complexities of computing the matrix-matrix products 𝑸​𝑲⊤𝑸superscript𝑲top{\bm{Q}}{\bm{K}}^{\top} and 𝑨​𝑽𝑨𝑽{\bm{A}}{\bm{V}} are both 𝒪​(N2)𝒪superscript𝑁2\mathcal{O}(N^{2}). These limitations impede the application of transformers to many important settings that involve very long sequences [j.2018generating, huang2018music, pmlr-v80-parmar18a]. When applying self-attention for long sequence modeling, we have to limit the context window to a reasonable size to make it computationally feasible, limiting the effectiveness of learning long-term dependencies. Efficient transformer models have been proposed, including leveraging sparse and low-rank attention. Many of the existing efficient transformers gain computational and memory efficiency at the cost of significant accuracy degradation.

Leveraging the idea of the fast multipole method (FMM) [greengard1987fast], we propose a class of efficient and flexible transformer, namely FMMformers, to boost the performance of efficient transformers. At the core of FMMformers is to replace the self-attention 𝑽^=𝑨​𝑽^𝑽𝑨𝑽\hat{{\bm{V}}}={\bm{A}}{\bm{V}} in (1) with the following matrix-matrix product𝑽^:=(𝑫+𝑳)​𝑽,assign^𝑽𝑫𝑳𝑽\hat{{\bm{V}}}:=({\bm{D}}+{\bm{L}}){\bm{V}},(2)where 𝑫𝑫{\bm{D}} is a banded matrix with bandwidth k≪Nmuch-less-than𝑘𝑁k\ll N and 𝑳𝑳{\bm{L}} is a low-rank matrix of rank r≪Nmuch-less-than𝑟𝑁r\ll N. In practice, we normalize matrix 𝑫+𝑳𝑫𝑳{\bm{D}}+{\bm{L}} such that the sum of each row is 111; for the sake of presentation, we ignore this normalization step below. Both 𝑫​𝑽𝑫𝑽{\bm{D}}{\bm{V}} and 𝑳​𝑽𝑳𝑽{\bm{L}}{\bm{V}} can be computed with linear computational and memory complexity; they model the near-field and far-field attention, respectively. FMMformers are flexible in designing the sparse banded matrix and the low-rank matrix for modeling near-field and far-field attention. In particular, we can control the bandwidth of the banded matrix 𝑫𝑫{\bm{D}} and the rank of the low-rank matrix 𝑳𝑳{\bm{L}} for expressivity and efficiency tradeoff. In addition to the efficiency and flexibility, FMMformers gain significant accuracy improvement over linear transformers and can even outperform the standard transformer in terms of accuracy. FMM is a numerical method that was originally developed to speed up the calculation of long-range forces in the n𝑛n-body problem [greengard1987fast] and has been regarded as one of the top 101010 algorithms in scientific computing in the 202020th century [cipra2000best]. The key idea is that the far-field interaction can be well approximated by separable low-rank matrices while the near-field interaction can be calculated directly. In this section, we numerically verify the efficiency of FMMformers and empirically analyze the effects of near-field and far-field attention on various benchmarks, including synthetic sequence copy (Section 4.1), Long Range Arena (LRA) (Section 4.2), and language modeling (Section 4.3). We aim to show that: (i) FMMformers are efficient in both computational time and memory footprint. (ii) Multiple kernels benefit learning of the far-field attention. (iii) Blending near-field attention with far-field attention can boost the performance of linear transformers. Throughout this section, we compare FMMformers with linear transformers (linear, r=1𝑟1r=1 in (10)), standard softmax transformers (softmax), and softmax transformers that use a banded attention matrix of bandwidth k𝑘k (bandk). All experiments are conducted on a server with 4 NVIDIA 3090TI GPUs.

We first consider a synthetic copy task with various sequence lengths, including 128, 256, and 512. In this task, the model has to duplicate a sequence of symbols. Each training and test sample is a sequence of maximum length 128/256/512 with ten different symbols separated by a dedicated separator symbol. We train all transformers for this task using the same setting as in [katharopoulos2020transformers].

We first compare FMMformers, obtained by blending the linear transformer with a banded attention matrix of bandwidths 10, 20, and 30, respectively. Figure 4 shows that for shorter sequences of length 128, all transformers reach similar loss; the standard softmax transformer converges much faster than the linear transformer while blending the linear transformers with near-field attention can improve training. Moreover, the benefits of near-field attention become more significant as the sequence length increases.

After observing that the linear transformer performs poorly as the sequence length increases, we consider augmenting the linear transformer with multiple feature maps; in particular, we consider the three feature maps mentioned above, i.e., ϕ1​(𝒙)=elu​(𝒙)+1,ϕ2​(𝒙)=elu​(−𝒙)+1formulae-sequencesubscriptitalic-ϕ1𝒙elu𝒙1subscriptitalic-ϕ2𝒙elu𝒙1\phi_{1}({\bm{x}})={\rm elu}({\bm{x}})+1,\phi_{2}({\bm{x}})={\rm elu}(-{\bm{x}})+1, and ϕ3​(𝒙)=tanh​(𝒙)subscriptitalic-ϕ3𝒙tanh𝒙\phi_{3}({\bm{x}})={\rm tanh}({\bm{x}}). In this experiment, we evaluate our model on tasks that involve longer sequence lengths in the Long Range Arena benchmark [tay2021long]. Datasets and metrics. We consider all five tasks in the LRA benchmark, including Listops [nangia-bowman-2018-listops], byte-level IMDb reviews text classification [maas-etal-2011-learning], byte-level document retrieval [radev2013acl], CIFAR-10 image classification on sequences of pixels [krizhevsky2009learning], and Pathfinder [linsley2018learn]. These tasks involve long sequences of length 2​K2𝐾2K, 4​K4𝐾4K, 4​K4𝐾4K, 1​K1𝐾1K, and 1​K1𝐾1K, respectively. We follow the setup/evaluation protocol in [tay2021long] and report the test accuracy for individual task and the average result across all tasks.

Results. We summarize our results in Table 1. Like in the copy task, we observe that adding near-field attention modeled by banded attention matrices improves the performance of linear transformers. More interestingly, using bandwidth 5 already yields good results across all LRA tasks while significantly reducing the computational and memory cost of calculating the attention matrix. For example, in the byte-level document retrieval [radev2013acl] task, a banded matrix with bandwidth 5 only accounts for 0.125% of the corresponding full attention matrix. The FMMformer with 1 kernel (blending a banded matrix of bandwidth 5 with the linear transformer using feature map ϕ1​(𝒙)subscriptitalic-ϕ1𝒙\phi_{1}({\bm{x}})) outperforms the linear transformer and yields similar or better results than the standard softmax transformer in all tasks. Furthermore, the FMMformer with 2 kernels (blending a banded attention matrix of bandwidth 5 with the linear transformer using feature maps ϕ1​(𝒙)subscriptitalic-ϕ1𝒙\phi_{1}({\bm{x}}) and ϕ2​(𝒙)subscriptitalic-ϕ2𝒙\phi_{2}({\bm{x}})) further improves the FMMformer with 1 kernel, justifying the need of better low-rank approximation for the far-field attention. Across tasks, the FMMformer obtains the best average accuracy. Also, it is worth noting that tasks in the LRA benchmark cover different data modalities include text and images. Good performance of the FMMformer on these tasks demonstrates that the advantages of our model over the linear and standard transformers are consistent across data modalities.

Experiments on the copy task in Section 4.1 illustrate the effect of combining near-field and far-field attention. Results on the LRA benchmark in Section 4.2 show the ability of our FMMformer to capture very long-term dependency and extend to different data modalities. Now our goal is to confirm the advantage of the FMMformer on a large-scale application. We consider the word-level language modeling task on WikiText-103 [DBLP:conf/iclr/MerityX0S17].

Datasets and metrics. WikiText-103 consists of articles from Wikipedia and is a dataset with long contextual dependencies. The training set is made up of about 28​K28𝐾28K articles containing 103​M103𝑀103M running words; this corresponds to text blocks of about 3600 words. The validation and test sets are composed of 218​K218𝐾218K and 246​K246𝐾246K running words, respectively. Each of them contains 606060 articles and about 268​K268𝐾268K words. Our experiment follows the standard setting [DBLP:conf/iclr/MerityX0S17, schlag2021linear] and split the training data into L𝐿L-word independent long segments. For evaluation, we use a batch size of 1, and go through the text sequence with a sliding window of size L𝐿L. We consider only the last position for computing perplexity (PPL) except in the first segment, where all positions are evaluated as in [al2019character, schlag2021linear].

Consistent with previous experiments, the FMMformer outperforms the linear transformer.
