Learning-to-rank is a central problem in a range of applications including web search, recommendation systems, and question answering. The task is to learn a function that, conditioned on some context, arranges a set of items into an ordered list so as to maximize a given metric. In this work, without loss of generality, we take search as an example where a set of documents (items) are ranked by their relevance to a query (context).

Rather than directly working with permutations, learning-to-rank methods typically approach the ranking problem as one of вҖңscore and sort.вҖқ The objective is then to learn a вҖңscoringвҖқ function to estimate query-document relevance. Subsequently, they sort documents in decreasing relevance to form a ranked list. Ideally, the resulting ranked list should maximize a ranking metric.

Popular ranking metrics are instances of the general class of conditional linear rank statisticsВ (ClГ©menГ§on and Vayatis, 2008) that summarize the Receiver Operator Characteristic (ROC) curve. Of particular interest are the ranking statistics that care mostly about the leftmost portion of the ROC curve, corresponding to the top of the ranked list. Mean Reciprocal Rank and Normalized Discounted Cumulative GainВ (JГӨrvelin and KekГӨlГӨinen, 2002) are two such metrics that are widely used in information retrieval.

Ranking metrics, as functions of learning-to-rank scores, are flat almost everywhere; a small perturbation of scores is unlikely to lead to a change in the metric. This property poses a challenge for gradient-based optimization algorithms, making a direct optimization of ranking metrics over a complex hypothesis space infeasible. Addressing this challenge has been the focus of a large body of researchВ (Liu, 2009), with most considering smooth loss functions as surrogates to metrics.

The majority of existing loss functionsВ (CaoetВ al., 2007; Burges etВ al., 2005; Burges, 2010; XiaetВ al., 2008; Joachims, 2006), however, are only loosely related to ranking metrics such as NDCG. ListNetВ (CaoetВ al., 2007), as an example, projects labels and scores onto the probability simplex and minimizes the cross-entropy between the resulting distributions. LambdaMARTВ (Burges, 2010; WuetВ al., 2010) (denoted as О»вҖӢmartрқңҶmart\lambda\textsc{mart}) forgoes the loss function altogether and heuristically formulates the gradients.

The heuristic nature of learning-to-rank loss functions and a lack of theoretical justification for their use have hindered progress in the field. While О»вҖӢmartрқңҶmart\lambda\textsc{mart} remains the state-of-the-art method to date, the fact that its loss function, presumed to be smooth, is unknown makes a theoretical analysis of the algorithm difficult. Empirical improvements over existing methods remain marginal for similar reasons.

In this work, we are motivated to help close the gap above. To that end, we present a construction of the cross-entropy loss which we dub xendcgsubscriptxendcg\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}}, that is only slightly different from the ListNet loss, but that enjoys strong theoretical properties. In particular, we prove that our construction is a convex bound on NDCG, thereby lending credence to its optimization for the purpose of learning ranking functions. Furthermore, we show that the generalization error of xendcgsubscriptxendcg\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}} compares favorably with that of О»вҖӢmartрқңҶmart\lambda\textsc{mart}вҖҷs. Experiments on benchmark learning-to-rank datasets further reveal the empirical superiority of our proposed method. We anticipate the theoretical soundness of our method and its strong connection to ranking metrics enable future research and progress.

Our contributions can be summarized as follows:вҖўWe present a cross entropy-based loss function, dubbed xendcgsubscriptxendcg\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}}, for learning-to-rank and prove that it is a convex bound on negative (translated and log-transformed) mean NDCG;вҖўWe compare model complexity between О»вҖӢmartрқңҶmart\lambda\textsc{mart} and xendcgsubscriptxendcg\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}};вҖўWe formulate an approximation to the inverse Hessian for xendcgsubscriptxendcg\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}} for optimization with second-order methods; and,вҖўWe optimize xendcgsubscriptxendcg\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}} to learn Gradient Boosted Regression Trees (denoted by xendcgвҖӢmartsubscriptxendcgmart\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}}\textsc{mart}) and compare its performance and robustness with О»вҖӢmartрқңҶmart\lambda\textsc{mart} on benchmark learning-to-rank datasets through extensive experiments.

A large class of learning-to-rank methods attempt to optimize pairwise misranking errorвҖ”a popular ranking statistic in many prioritization problemsвҖ”by learning to correctly classify pairwise preferences. Examples include RankSVMВ (Joachims, 2006) and AdaRankВ (Xu and Li, 2007) which learn margin classifiers, RankNetВ (Burges etВ al., 2005) which optimizes a probabilistic loss function, and the P-Norm Push methodВ (Rudin, 2009) which extends the problem to settings where we mostly care about the top of the ranked list. While the so-called вҖңpairwiseвҖқ methods typically optimize convex upper-bounds of the misranking error, direct optimization methods based on mathematical programming have also been proposedВ (Rudin and Wang, 2018) albeit for linear hypothesis spaces.

Pairwise learning-to-rank methods, while generally effective, optimize loss functions that are misaligned with more complex ranking statistics such as Expected Reciprocal RankВ (Chapelle etВ al., 2009) or NDCGВ (JГӨrvelin and KekГӨlГӨinen, 2002). This discrepancy has given rise to the so-called вҖңlistwiseвҖқ methods, where the loss function under optimization is defined over the entire list of items, not just pairs.

Listwise learning-to-rank methods either derive a smooth approximation to ranking metrics or use heuristics to construct smooth surrogate loss functions. Algorithms that represent the first class are SoftRankВ (TayloretВ al., 2008) which takes every score to be the mean of a Gaussian distribution, and ApproxNDCGВ (Qin etВ al., 2010) which approximates the indicator functionвҖ”used in the computation of ranks given scoresвҖ”with a generalized sigmoid.

The other class of listwise learning-to-rank methods include ListMLEВ (XiaetВ al., 2008), ListNetВ (CaoetВ al., 2007), and О»вҖӢmartрқңҶmart\lambda\textsc{mart}В (WuetВ al., 2010; Burges, 2010). ListMLE maximizes the log-likelihood based on the Plackett-Luce probabilistic model, a loss function that is disconnected from ranking metrics. ListNet minimizes the cross-entropy between the ground-truth and score distributions. Though a recent workВ (BruchetВ al., 2019a) establishes a link between the ListNet loss function and NDCG under strict conditionsвҖ”requiring binary relevance labelsвҖ”in a general setting, its loss is only loosely related to ranking metrics.

О»вҖӢmartрқңҶmart\lambda\textsc{mart} is a gradient boosting machineВ (Friedman, 2001) that forgoes the loss function altogether and, instead, directly designs the gradients of its unknown loss function using heuristics. While a recent workВ (Wang etВ al., 2018) claims to have found О»вҖӢmartрқңҶmart\lambda\textsc{mart}вҖҷs loss function, it overlooks an important detail: The reported loss function inВ (Wang etВ al., 2018) is not differentiable.

There is abundant evidence to suggest listwise methods are empirically superior to pairwise methods where MRR, ERR, or NDCG determines ranking qualityВ (Wang etВ al., 2018; BruchetВ al., 2019b; Liu, 2009). However, unlike pairwise methods, listwise algorithms remain theoretically ill-understood. Past studies have examined the generalization error bounds for existing surrogate loss functionsВ (Tewari andChaudhuri, 2015; Chapelle and Wu, 2010; LanetВ al., 2009), but little attention has been paid to the validity of such functions which could shed light on their empirical performance.

While establishing that в„’в„’\mathcal{L} bounds the NDCG loss is a necessary property in a surrogate, it is not sufficient. As an example, the constant function в„’вҖӢ(f)=2в„’рқ‘“2\mathcal{L}(f)=2 bounds the NDCG loss, but optimizing it does not lead to an optimal fвҲ—superscriptрқ‘“вҲ—f^{\ast}. This is where the notion of Fisher consistency becomes critical: In summary, a loss function is consistent with an evaluation measure, if the optimal solution to the loss function is also an optimal solution of the evaluation measure.

The cross entropy function that is at the heart of ListNet and our proposed method was shown to be consistent with the 0вҲ’1010-1 ranking loss inВ (XiaetВ al., 2008). In general, however, the loss is not consistent with NDCGВ (RavikumaretВ al., 2011). But under special conditions that are common in information retrieval, cross entropy (and as a result xendcgsubscriptxendcg\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}}) become NDCG-consistent.

We omit a complete proof due to space constraints, but note that the above is a trivial consequence of the conditions. We are largely interested in a comparison of (a) the overall performance of ListNet, О»вҖӢmartрқңҶmart\lambda\textsc{mart}, and xendcgsubscriptxendcg\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}} on benchmark learning-to-rank datasets, and (b) their robustness to various types and degrees of noise as a proxy to complexity. In this section, we describe our experimental setup and report our empirical findings.

It is important to note that there is an extensive list of published workВ (BruchetВ al., 2019b; Zhuang etВ al., 2020; Pasumarthi etВ al., 2019; BruchetВ al., 2020) that compare learning-to-rank algorithms on benchmark datasets we use in this work. We rely on prior research and do not include methods that have been shown repeatedly to be weaker than О»вҖӢmartрқңҶmart\lambda\textsc{mart}, including BoltzRankВ (Volkovs and Zemel, [n.d.]), ListMLEВ (XiaetВ al., 2008), Position-Aware ListMLEВ (LanetВ al., 2014), SoftRankВ (TayloretВ al., 2008), ApproxNDCGВ (BruchetВ al., 2019b; Qin etВ al., 2010), or other direct optimization methodsВ (MetzleretВ al., 2005; XuetВ al., 2008).

We conduct experiments on two publicly available benchmark datasets: MSLR Web30KВ (Qin and Liu, 2013) and Yahoo! Learning to Rank Challenge Set 1В (Chapelle andChang, 2011). Web30K contains roughly 30,000 examples, with an average of 120 documents per example. Documents are represented by 136 numeric features. Yahoo! also has about the same number of examples but with an average of 24 documents per example, each represented by 519 features. Documents in both datasets are labeled with graded relevance from 0 to 4 with larger labels indicating a higher relevance.

From each dataset, we sample training (60%), validation (20%), and test (20%) examples, and train and compare models on the resulting splits. We repeat this procedure 100 times and obtain mean NDCG at different rank cutoffs for each trial. We subsequently compare the ranking quality between pairs of models and determine statistical significance of differences using a paired t-test.

During evaluation, we discard examples with no relevant documents. There are 982 and 1,135 such examples in the Web30K and Yahoo! datasets. The reason for ignoring these examples during evaluation is that their ranking quality can be arbitrarily 0 or 1, which only skews the average.

We train О»вҖӢmartрқңҶmart\lambda\textsc{mart} models using LightGBMВ (Ke etВ al., 2017). The hyperparameters are guided by previous workВ (Ke etВ al., 2017; Wang etВ al., 2018; BruchetВ al., 2019a). For Web30K, max_bin is 255, learning_rate is 0.02, num_leaves is 400, min_data_in_leaf is 50, min_sum_hessian_in_leaf is set to 0, ПғрқңҺ\sigma is 1, and lambdamart_norm is set to false. For Yahoo!, num_leaves is 200 and min_data_in_leaf is 100. We use NDCG@5 to select the best models on validation sets and fix early stopping round to 50 up to 500 trees.

In SectionВ 5.4, we examined the behavior of О»вҖӢmartрқңҶmart\lambda\textsc{mart} and xendcgвҖӢmartsubscriptxendcgmart\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}}\textsc{mart} in the presence of noise on datasets with explicit relevance judgments. In this section, we provide an analysis of the robustness of the two algorithms on a simulated click dataset where noise occurs more naturally (e.g., where a user clicks a non-relevant document by accident).

We follow the procedure proposed inВ (JoachimsetВ al., 2017) to simulate a user in the cascade click modelВ (CraswelletВ al., 2008). In the cascade click model, when presented with a ranked list of documents, a user scans the list sequentially from the top and clicks a document according to a click probability distributionвҖ”the probability that a document is clicked given its relevance label. We assume the user is persistent in that they continue to examine the list until either a document is clicked or they reach the end of the list.

We construct click datasets as follows. We first create training and validation splits using the procedure of SectionВ 5.1. Given a training (or validation) example (рқ’ҷ,рқ’ҡ)рқ’ҷрқ’ҡ(\bm{x},\bm{y}) consisting of mрқ‘ҡm documents and relevance labels, we shuffle its elements and sequentially scan the resulting list to produce clicks using the cascade click model. We stop at the first occurrence of a click and return the list up to the first click as an вҖңimpression.вҖқ We create 10 impressions per training example to form our click dataset. Finally, we train ranking models on the click dataset and evaluate on the original (non-click) test set. We repeat this experiment 20 times and measure mean NDCG.

In this work, we presented a novel вҖңlistwiseвҖқ learning-to-rank loss function, xendcgsubscriptxendcg\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}}, that, unlike existing methods bounds NDCGвҖ”a popular ranking metricвҖ”in a general setting. We contrasted our proposed loss function with О»вҖӢmartрқңҶmart\lambda\textsc{mart} and showed its superior theoretical properties. In particular, we showed that the loss function optimized by О»вҖӢmartрқңҶmart\lambda\textsc{mart} (if it exists), has a higher complexity with a Lipschitz constant that is a function of the number of documents, mрқ‘ҡm. In contrast, the complexity of xendcgsubscriptxendcg\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}} is invariant to mрқ‘ҡm.

Furthermore, we proposed a model that optimizes xendcgsubscriptxendcg\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}} to learn an ensemble of gradient-boosted decision trees which we refer to as xendcgвҖӢmartsubscriptxendcgmart\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}}\textsc{mart}. Through extensive experiments on two benchmark learning-to-rank datasets, we demonstrated that our proposed method performs better than ListNet and О»вҖӢmartрқңҶmart\lambda\textsc{mart} in terms of quality and robustness. We showed that, xendcgвҖӢmartsubscriptxendcgmart\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}}\textsc{mart} is less sensitive to the number of documents and is more robust in the presence of noise. Finally, our experiments suggest that the performance gap between xendcgвҖӢmartsubscriptxendcgmart\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}}\textsc{mart} and О»вҖӢmartрқңҶmart\lambda\textsc{mart} widens if we constrain the size of the learned ensemble. Better performance with fewer trees is important for latency-sensitive applications.

As a future direction, we are interested in an examination of the bound and its effect on the convergence and consistency of xendcgsubscriptxendcg\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}}. Furthermore, given its robustness to label noise (implicit and explicit), we are also interested in studying xendcgsubscriptxendcg\textsc{xe}_{\textsc{ndcg}} in an online learning setting.
