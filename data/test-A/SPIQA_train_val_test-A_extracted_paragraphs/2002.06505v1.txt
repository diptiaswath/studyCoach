A standard (feedforward) neural network with nрқ‘ӣn input units, mрқ‘ҡm output units, and with one or more hidden layers, refers to a computational model рқ’©рқ’©\mathcal{N} that can compute a certain class of functions ПҒ:в„қnвҶ’в„қm:рқңҢвҶ’superscriptв„қрқ‘ӣsuperscriptв„қрқ‘ҡ\rho:\mathbb{R}^{n}\to\mathbb{R}^{m}, where ПҒ=ПҒWрқңҢsubscriptрқңҢрқ‘Ҡ\rho=\rho_{W} is parametrized by Wрқ‘ҠW (called the weights of рқ’©рқ’©\mathcal{N}). Implicitly, the definition of ПҒрқңҢ\rho depends on a choice of some fixed function Пғ:в„қвҶ’в„қ:рқңҺвҶ’в„қв„қ\sigma:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}, called the activation function of рқ’©рқ’©\mathcal{N}. Typically, ПғрқңҺ\sigma is assumed to be continuous, and historically, the earliest commonly used activation functions were sigmoidal.

A key fundamental result justifying the use of sigmoidal activation functions was due to Cybenko (1989), Hornik etВ al. (1989), and Funahashi (1989), who independently proved the first version of what is now famously called the universal approximation theorem. This first version says that if ПғрқңҺ\sigma is sigmoidal, then a standard neural network with one hidden layer would be able to uniformly approximate any continuous function f:XвҶ’в„қm:рқ‘“вҶ’рқ‘Ӣsuperscriptв„қрқ‘ҡf:X\to\mathbb{R}^{m} whose domain XвҠҶв„қnрқ‘Ӣsuperscriptв„қрқ‘ӣX\subseteq\mathbb{R}^{n} is compact. Hornik (1991) extended the theorem to the case when ПғрқңҺ\sigma is any continuous bounded non-constant activation function. Subsequently, Leshno etВ al. (1993) proved that for the class of continuous activation functions, a standard neural network with one hidden layer is able to uniformly approximate any continuous function f:XвҶ’в„қm:рқ‘“вҶ’рқ‘Ӣsuperscriptв„қрқ‘ҡf:X\to\mathbb{R}^{m} on any compact XвҠҶв„қnрқ‘Ӣsuperscriptв„қрқ‘ӣX\subseteq\mathbb{R}^{n}, if and only if ПғрқңҺ\sigma is non-polynomial.

Although a single hidden layer is sufficient for the uniform approximation property (UAP) to hold, the number of hidden units required could be arbitrarily large. Given a subclass в„ұв„ұ\mathcal{F} of real-valued continuous functions on a compact set XвҠҶв„қnрқ‘Ӣsuperscriptв„қрқ‘ӣX\subseteq\mathbb{R}^{n}, a fixed activation function ПғрқңҺ\sigma, and some Оө>0рқңҖ0\varepsilon>0, let N=NвҖӢ(в„ұ,Пғ,Оө)рқ‘Ғрқ‘Ғв„ұрқңҺрқңҖN=N(\mathcal{F},\sigma,\varepsilon) be the minimum number of hidden units required for a single-hidden-layer neural network to be able to uniformly approximate every fвҲҲв„ұрқ‘“в„ұf\in\mathcal{F} within an approximation error threshold of ОөрқңҖ\varepsilon. If ПғрқңҺ\sigma is the rectified linear unit (ReLU) xвҶҰmaxвҒЎ(0,x)maps-toрқ‘Ҙ0рқ‘Ҙx\mapsto\max(0,x), then Nрқ‘ҒN is at least О©вҖӢ(1Оө)О©1рқңҖ\Omega(\frac{1}{\smash{\sqrt{\varepsilon}}}) when в„ұв„ұ\mathcal{F} is the class of C2superscriptрқҗ¶2C^{2} non-linear functionsВ (Yarotsky, 2017), or the class of strongly convex differentiable functionsВ (Liang & Srikant, 2016); see also (Arora etВ al., 2018). If ПғрқңҺ\sigma is any smooth non-polynomial function, then Nрқ‘ҒN is at most рқ’ӘвҖӢ(ОөвҲ’n)рқ’ӘsuperscriptрқңҖрқ‘ӣ\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon^{-n}) for the class of C1superscriptрқҗ¶1C^{1} functions with bounded Sobolev norm (Mhaskar, 1996); cf. (Pinkus, 1999, Thm. 6.8), (Maiorov & Pinkus, 1999). As a key highlight of this paper, we show that if ПғрқңҺ\sigma is an arbitrary continuous non-polynomial function, then Nрқ‘ҒN is at most рқ’ӘвҖӢ(ОөвҲ’n)рқ’ӘsuperscriptрқңҖрқ‘ӣ\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon^{-n}) for the entire class of continuous functions. In fact, we give an explicit upper bound for Nрқ‘ҒN in terms of ОөрқңҖ\varepsilon and the modulus of continuity of fрқ‘“f, so better bounds could be obtained for certain subclasses в„ұв„ұ\mathcal{F}, which we discuss further in Section 4. Furthermore, even for the wider class в„ұв„ұ\mathcal{F} of all continuous functions f:XвҶ’в„қm:рқ‘“вҶ’рқ‘Ӣsuperscriptв„қрқ‘ҡf:X\to\mathbb{R}^{m}, the bound is still рқ’ӘвҖӢ(ОөвҲ’n)рқ’ӘsuperscriptрқңҖрқ‘ӣ\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon^{-n}), independent of mрқ‘ҡm.

To prove this bound, we shall give a direct algebraic proof of the universal approximation theorem, in its general version as stated by Leshno etВ al. (1993) (i.e. ПғрқңҺ\sigma is continuous and non-polynomial). An important advantage of our algebraic approach is that we are able to glean additional information on sufficient conditions that would imply the UAP. Another key highlight we have is that if в„ұв„ұ\mathcal{F} is the subclass of polynomial functions f:XвҶ’в„қm:рқ‘“вҶ’рқ‘Ӣsuperscriptв„қрқ‘ҡf:X\to\mathbb{R}^{m} with total degree at most dрқ‘‘d for each coordinate function, then (n+dd)binomialрқ‘ӣрқ‘‘рқ‘‘\binom{n+d}{d} hidden units would suffice. In particular, notice that our bound NвүӨ(n+dd)рқ‘Ғbinomialрқ‘ӣрқ‘‘рқ‘‘N\leq\binom{n+d}{d} does not depend on the approximation error threshold ОөрқңҖ\varepsilon or the output dimension mрқ‘ҡm.

We shall also show that the UAP holds even under strong conditions on the weights. Given any Оҙ>0рқӣҝ0\delta>0, we can always choose the non-bias weights in the last layer to have small magnitudes no larger than Оҙрқӣҝ\delta. Furthermore, we show that there exists some О»>0рқңҶ0\lambda>0 (depending only on ПғрқңҺ\sigma and the function fрқ‘“f to be approximated), such that the non-bias weights in the first layer can always be chosen to have magnitudes greater than О»рқңҶ\lambda. Even with these seemingly strong restrictions on the weights, we show that the UAP still holds. Thus, our main results can be collectively interpreted as a quantitative refinement of the universal approximation theorem, with extensions to restricted weight values.

An interesting consequence of Theorem 3.3 is the following: The freezing of lower layers of a neural network, even in the extreme case that all frozen layers are randomly initialized and the last layer is the only вҖңnon-frozenвҖқ layer, does not necessarily reduce the representability of the resulting model. Specifically, in the single-hidden-layer case, we have shown that if the non-bias weights in the first layer are fixed and randomly chosen from some suitable fixed range, then the UAP holds with probability 111, provided that there are sufficiently many hidden units. Of course, this representability does not reveal anything about the learnability of such a model. In practice, layers are already pre-trained before being frozen. It would be interesting to understand quantitatively the difference between having pre-trained frozen layers and having randomly initialized frozen layers.

Theorem 3.3 can be viewed as a result on random features, which were formally studied in relation to kernel methods (Rahimi & Recht, 2007). In the case of ReLU activation functions, Sun etВ al. (2019) proved an analog of Theorem 3.3 for the approximation of functions in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space; cf. (Rahimi & Recht, 2008). For a good discussion on the role of random features in the representability of neural networks, see (Yehudai & Shamir, 2019).

The UAP is also studied in other contexts, most notably in relation to the depth and width of neural networks. Lu etВ al. (2017) proved the UAP for neural networks with hidden layers of bounded width, under the assumption that ReLU is used as the activation function. Soon after, Hanin (2017) strengthened the bounded-width UAP result by considering the approximation of continuous convex functions. Recently, the role of depth in the expressive power of neural networks has gathered much interestВ (Delalleau & Bengio, 2011; Eldan & Shamir, 2016; Mhaskar etВ al., 2017; MontГәfar etВ al., 2014; Telgarsky, 2016). We do not address depth in this paper, but we believe it is possible that our results can be applied iteratively to deeper neural networks, perhaps in particular for the approximation of compositional functions; cf. Theorem 5.6 is rather general, and could potentially be used to prove analogs of the universal approximation theorem for other classes of neural networks, such as convolutional neural networks and recurrent neural networks. In particular, finding a single suitable set of weights (as a representative of the infinitely many possible sets of weights in the given class of neural networks), with the property that its corresponding вҖңnon-bias Vandermonde matrixвҖқ (see Definition 5.5) is non-singular, would serve as a straightforward criterion for showing that the UAP holds for the given class of neural networks (with certain weight constraints). We formulated this criterion to be as general as we could, with the hope that it would applicable to future classes of вҖңneural-likeвҖқ networks.

We believe our algebraic approach could be emulated to eventually yield a unified understanding of how depth, width, constraints on weights, and other architectural choices, would influence the approximation capabilities of arbitrary neural networks.

Finally, we end our paper with an open-ended question. The proofs of our results in Section 5 seem to suggest that non-bias weights and bias weights play very different roles. We could impose very strong restrictions on the non-bias weights and still have the UAP. What about the bias weights?
