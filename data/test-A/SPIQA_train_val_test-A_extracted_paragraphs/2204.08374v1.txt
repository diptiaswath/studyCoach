In a nutshell, dynamical systems are mathematical models of movement in space over time.The interaction between space and time is a fundamental aspect of reality, making such models ubiquitous in many scientific disciplines, ranging from physics to economics.Computer science is no exception, which should be unsurprising given the temporal aspect of processes and the deep connections between topology and computation, as demonstrated by abstract models of computation such as the well-known Scott domains [23].

There are many recent examples from pure and applied work in computer science involving dynamical systems.Lin and Antsaklis [20] use hybrid dynamical systems in the research of artificial intelligence and computer-aided verification.Brunton and Kutz [5] purposed approaching data-related problems through dynamical systems, and Weinan [26] suggested modelling nonlinear functions implemented in machine learning using dynamical systems.Mortveit and Reidys’s [21] sequential dynamical systems generalise cellular automata and provide a framework for studying dynamical processes in graphs.Dynamical systems are also found in their linear form in the shape of Markov chains, linear recurrence sequences and linear differential equations.It is therefore not surprising that connections have been established between dynamical systems and algorithms.Such links can be found for example in the work of Hanrot, Pujol and Stehlé [14], and in the work of Chu [7].This list is by no means exhaustive.

The applications above warrant the need for an effective formal reasoning framework about topological dynamics, i.e. the action of a (typically continuous) function on a topological space.Modal logic was first suggested to serve that purpose in the 1990s by Artemov et al. [2], who envisioned dynamic topological logic as a bimodal logic for reasoning about topological dynamics.They defined the logic 𝐒𝟒𝐂𝐒𝟒𝐂\bf S4C and showed that it possesses desirable properties such as a natural axiomatisation and the finite model property.Kremer and Mints [16] suggested that including a third modality, ‘henceforth’ from linear temporal logic (𝐋𝐓𝐋𝐋𝐓𝐋\mathbf{LTL}), would lead to a logic powerful enough to reason about the asymptotic behaviour of dynamical systems, possibly leading to applications in automated theorem proving.They dubbed the resulting system dynamic topological logic (𝐃𝐓𝐋𝐃𝐓𝐋\bf DTL).They proposed a natural axiomatisation for 𝐃𝐓𝐋𝐃𝐓𝐋\bf DTL and conjectured it to be sound and complete for the class of dynamical systems.

However, the situation turned out to be much more intricate than that of 𝐒𝟒𝐂𝐒𝟒𝐂\bf S4C.While completeness for Kremer and Mints’ calculus has yet to be shown, Fernández-Duque proposed an extension of 𝐃𝐓𝐋𝐃𝐓𝐋\bf DTL, denoted 𝐃𝐓𝐋∗superscript𝐃𝐓𝐋{\bf DTL}^{*}, which enriches the original language with topological fixed points known as tangled operators.He proved that 𝐃𝐓𝐋∗superscript𝐃𝐓𝐋{\bf DTL}^{*} has a natural axiomatisation [10]. Later, Fernández-Duque showed that Kremer and Mints’ axiomatic system is incomplete; in fact, 𝐃𝐓𝐋𝐃𝐓𝐋\bf DTL is not finitely axiomatisable [11].

At this point the status of the problem of axiomatising 𝐃𝐓𝐋𝐃𝐓𝐋\bf DTL in the original trimodal language becomes subtle:one must search for a non-finite axiomatisation, which is nevertheless ‘natural’ in some sense.Chopoghloo and Moniri [6] proposed an infinitary axiomatisation for 𝐃𝐓𝐋𝐃𝐓𝐋\bf DTL; the axioms and rules are fairly standard, but the ω𝜔\omega-rule, which has infinitely many premises, is allowed.Thus proofs are infinite objects, unlike the case for 𝐃𝐓𝐋∗superscript𝐃𝐓𝐋{\bf DTL}^{*}, which has infinitely many axioms but finite individual proofs.To what extent this solves the problem of axiomatising the original trimodal logic is subject to debate.

In this paper we follow a different route and instead restrict our attention to a specific class of dynamical systems, namely, those based on scattered spaces.Scattered spaces are topological spaces where every non-empty subspace has an isolated point.They have gathered attention lately in the context of computational logic, as they may be used to model provability in formal theories [1], leading to applications in characterising their provably total computable functions [4]. Modal logic on scattered spaces enjoys definable fixed points [22], connecting it to the topological μ𝜇\mu-calculus [3].The latter is particularly relevant to us, as the expressive power gained by topological fixed points, including the tangled operators of 𝐃𝐓𝐋∗superscript𝐃𝐓𝐋\mathbf{DTL}^{*}, is absent in this setting.As the logic of scattered spaces is the Gödel-Löb modal logic 𝐆𝐋𝐆𝐋\bf GL, we refer to the dynamic topological logic of scattered spaces as dynamic Gödel-Löb logic (𝐃𝐆𝐋𝐃𝐆𝐋\bf DGL).Moreover, we base our semantics on the Cantor derivative rather than the topological closure, since the former is known to be more expressive [18].

Our goal is to demonstrate that the standard finite axiomatisation of 𝐃𝐆𝐋𝐃𝐆𝐋\bf DGL is sound and complete, leading to the first complete trimodal dynamic topological logic, as well as the first such logic combining the Cantor derivative with the infinitary ‘henceforth’ from 𝐋𝐓𝐋𝐋𝐓𝐋\bf LTL.By the ‘standard axioms’ we refer to the combination of the well-known axiomatisation of 𝐆𝐋𝐆𝐋\bf GL with 𝐋𝐓𝐋𝐋𝐓𝐋\bf LTL axioms for the tenses and (🌑​p∧🌑​□​p)→□​🌑​p→🌑𝑝🌑□𝑝□🌑𝑝(\newmoon p\wedge\newmoon\Box p)\to\Box\newmoon p – a variant of the continuity axiom of Artemov et al. adapted for the Cantor derivative.The proof of completeness employs various advanced techniques from modal logic, including an application of Kruskal’s theorem in the spirit of the work of Gabelaia et al. [13].

We have exhibited the first finitely axiomatisable dynamic topological logic in the original trimodal language.The techniques employed here can be applied to related logics which may or may not be topologically inspired, including expanding products of modal logics.111See Gabelaia, Kurucz, Wolter and Zakharyaschev [13] for the connection between expanding products and dynamic topological logic.In particular, dynamic Grzegorczyk logic (𝐃𝐆𝐫𝐳𝐃𝐆𝐫𝐳\mathbf{DGrz}) could be treated in the same fashion, where ◆◆\lozenge is interpreted as closure rather than Cantor derivative. Note, however, that the Cantor derivative can define the topological closure, so completeness for 𝐃𝐆𝐫𝐳𝐃𝐆𝐫𝐳\mathbf{DGrz} should also follow from embedding it into 𝐃𝐆𝐋𝐃𝐆𝐋\mathbf{DGL} using proof-translation techniques.

In fact, tangle-free logics may be applicable to a wider class of topological spaces by modifying the underlying Boolean algebra.Instead of considering the powerset of X𝑋X, one may work on sub-algebras (i.e., regular open or closed sets [15]).In this setting, the tangled operators could also be trivialised, eliminating the need for such operators without restricting the class of topological spaces at one’s disposal.

Finally, there is the question of axiomatising the dynamic topological logic of Aleksandroff spaces.Chopoghloo and Moniri [6] provided an infinitary proof system for this class, and the results of Fernández-Duque [11] apply in this setting as well and rule out a finite axiomatisation.However, it is possible that a natural, finitary proof system can be found in this setting (albeit with infinitely many axioms).
