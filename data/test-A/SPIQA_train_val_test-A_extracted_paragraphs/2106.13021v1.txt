We consider the classic problem of online prediction with expertadviceВ [32] in a non-stationary environment.In this model natureВ sequentially generates outcomes whichlearnerВ attempts to predict. Before making each prediction,learnerВ listens to a set of nрқ‘ӣn experts who each make theirown predictions. LearnerВ bases its prediction on the advice of theexperts. After the prediction is made and the true outcome is revealedby nature, the accuracies of learnerвҖҷs prediction and the expertpredictions are measured by a loss function. LearnerВ receivesinformation on all expert losses on each trial. We make nodistributional assumptions about the outcomes generated, indeednatureВ may be assumed to be adversarial. The goal of learnerВ is topredict well relative to a predetermined comparison class ofpredictors, in this case the set of experts themselves. Unlike thestandard regret model, where learnerвҖҷs performance is compared to thesingle best predictor in hindsight, our aim is for learnerВ topredict well relative to a sequence of comparison predictors. That is,вҖңswitchesвҖқ occur in the data sequence and different experts areassumed to predict well at different times.

In this work our focus is on the case when this sequence consists of afew unique predictors relative to the number of switches.Thus most switches return to a previously вҖңgoodвҖқ expert, and alearnerВ that can exploit this fact by вҖңrememberingвҖқ the past can adaptmore quickly than a learnerВ who has no memory and must re-learn theexperts after every switch.The problem of switching with memory in online learning is part of amuch broader and fundamental problem in machine learning: how a systemcan adapt to new information yet retain knowledge of the past.This is an area of research in many fields, including for example,catastrophic forgetting in artificial neuralnetworksВ [10, 33].

In this paper we present an рқ’ӘвҖӢ(n)рқ’Әрқ‘ӣ\mathcal{O}(n)-time per trial projection-based algorithmfor which we prove the best known regret bound for tracking expertswith memory. Our projection-based algorithm is intimately related toa more traditional вҖңweight-sharingвҖқ algorithm, which we show is a new method for Mixing PastPosteriors (MPP)В [4]. We show thatsurprisingly this method corresponds to the algorithm with theprevious best known regret bound for this problemВ [26].We also give an efficient рқ’ӘвҖӢ(n)рқ’Әрқ‘ӣ\mathcal{O}(n)-time algorithm forcomputing exact relative entropy projection onto a simplex withnon-uniform (lower) box constraints.Finally, we provide a guarantee which favors projection-based updatesover weight-sharing updates when updating weights may incur costs.
