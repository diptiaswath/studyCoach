Learning from demonstrations is an important paradigm to train AI agents (?;В ?;В ?;В ?).Ideally, one would like to harness as much cheaply available (and relevant) demonstrator data as possible.But major issues arise when there are differences between the sensors of demonstrator, us and agent we train.When ignoring such issues, or addressing them in a naive way, wrong and potentially harmful conclusions can result: about demonstratorвЂ™s behavior and the demonstratorвЂ™s actionsвЂ™ effects on the environment.

In this paper, we address learning from demonstrations (LfD) under sensor-shift, i.e., when there are differences between (a) the demonstratorвЂ™s own sensory input, (b) our sensors that observe the demonstrator and (c) the sensory input of the agent we train.Specifically, we consider two closely related вЂњsubtasksвЂқ of LfD: (1) inferring the effect of the demonstratorвЂ™s decisions (as in Example 1) and (2) imitating the demonstrator.

Our approach is based on causal models (?;В ?;В ?), which allow us to generalize from data beyond i.i.d.В settings.The idea is that, while some modular causal mechanisms that govern the data vary (the sensors), other mechanisms are invariant (e.g., the action-effect).

Our main contributions are:вЂұWe rigorously analyze, on the population-level, to what extent the relevant underlying mechanisms (the action-effect and the demonstrator policy) can be identified and transferred from the available observations together with prior knowledge of sensor characteristics (Sections 5, 6.1, 6.2 and 7.1). And we propose algorithms to calculate them (Algorithms 1 and 2).вЂұWe introduce several proxy methods (SectionsВ 6.3 and 7.2) which are easier to calculate, estimate from finite data and interpret than the exact solutions, alongside theoretical bounds on their closeness to the exact ones (PropositionsВ 2, 4 and 5). (Proofs are in the supplement111The supplement can be found at вЂњhttps://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.3549981вЂқ. of this paper.) вЂұWe conduct experiments to validate our two main methods on simulated and semi-real world highway drone data used for autonomous driving (Section 8).

Learning from demonstrations (LfD) (?) is a broad area, with two concrete tasks being the ones we also consider in this paper: (1) inferring the effect of action on outcome given observation (we call it вЂњaction-effectвЂқ in our a-temporal framework, while in the language of (?) this is called the вЂњsystem modelвЂқ or вЂњworld dynamicsвЂқ), and (2) imitation learning (see next paragraph).Generally in LfD, the problem that sensors differ between demonstrator, observer and target AI agent has been considered (?;В ?;В ?).In the language of (?), this is described as the вЂњrecording mappingвЂқ or вЂњembodiment mappingвЂқ not being the identity. However, we are not aware of any treatment of this problem which is as systematic and general as ours in terms of guarantees on exact and approximate identifiability. Instead, approaches are practically-focused, tailored to specific, say, robot tasks (?;В ?).

Within LfD, imitation learning means learning to perform a task from expert demonstrations (?;В ?).There are two main approaches to address this problem: behavioral cloning (?), which we are focusing on,and inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) (?;В ?).

The problem of bounding as well as transferring and integrating causal relations across different domains has been studied by (?;В ?;В ?). But all this work does not consider the training of AI agents.Within causal modelling, maybe closest related to our paper are (?;В ?;В ?;В ?), who also study the integration of data from heterogeneous settings for training agents (often with latent confounders and from a multi-armed bandit perspective).

For example, (?) tackle the problem of transferring knowledge across bandit agents in settings where causal effects cannot be identified by standard learning techniques. Their approach consists of two steps: (1) deriving bounds over the effects of selecting arms and (2) incorporating these bounds to search for more promising actions.However, when bounding the causal effect, they focus on binary variables, while we consider arbitrary finite as well as continuous ranges (which are highly relevant in practice) and they do not focus on general sensor-shifts.

The authors of (?) study вЂњcausal confusionвЂқ in causal-model-free imitation learning. There, additional observations can lead to worse performance due to the mechanism (policy) that generates them differing between demonstrations and target environment.However, in their model they assume that both the demonstrator and the imitator have (at least) the same observations.This is not always the case, and therefore our treatment allows the observations to differ.

Sensor-shift is a significant problem in learning from demonstrations. In this work, we proposed a principled and general framework to address it, based on causal modeling.We developed novel algorithms that uniquely identify or constrain/approximate the relevant causal effects, and established theoretical guarantees.The take away message is that the relevant causal relationships may still be identifiable, even if the demonstrator, spectator and target agent have different sensors.
