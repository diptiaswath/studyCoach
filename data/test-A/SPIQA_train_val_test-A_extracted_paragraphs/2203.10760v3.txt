Graphs are ubiquitous in real-world applications. It is often of great value to find cohesive subgraphs from a graph in both theoretical research and practical applications. A classic model of cohesive subgraph called clique, where vertices are pairwise connected, has attracted much attention in recent decades. Many algorithms of enumerating all maximal cliques in a graph were developed, such as the classic Bron-Kerbosch algorithm (Bron and Kerbosch, 1973) and its pivot-based variants (Tomitaet al., 2006; Eppsteinet al., 2013; Naudé, 2016), the output-sensitive algorithms (Changet al., 2013; Conteet al., 2016a), the parallel algorithms (Conte et al., 2016b; Segundoet al., 2018; Weiet al., 2021), and so on.

Real-world networks are often noisy or faulty (Conte et al., 2018; Zhouet al., 2020). It may be overly restrictive to find cohesive subgraphs when using the notion of clique. Thus, a set of relaxed clique models has been proposed to overcome this issue (Pattilloet al., 2013). One of an interesting relaxed clique models is the k𝑘k-plex which was first introduced in (Seidman andFoster, 1978). In particular, given a graph G𝐺G, a vertex set C𝐶C is a k𝑘k-plex if every vertex has a degree at least |C|−k𝐶𝑘|C|-k within the subgraph of G𝐺G induced by C𝐶C. A k𝑘k-plex C𝐶C is said to be maximal if there does not exist any other k𝑘k-plex that contains C𝐶C. The problem of enumerating all maximal k𝑘k-plexes from a graph has been widely studied in the literature (Wu and Pei, 2007; Berlowitzet al., 2015; Wanget al., 2017; Conte et al., 2018; Zhouet al., 2020), which often arises in a large number of real-world applications, such as community detection in social networks (Balasundaramet al., 2011), identifying protein complexes in protein-protein interaction networks (Luoet al., 2009), and serving as an alternative to cliques in biochemistry (Doyle et al., 2005).

However, the number of k𝑘k-plexes in a real-world graph is often exponential to the size of the graph. It is well-known that the problem of enumerating all maximal k𝑘k-plexes is NP-hard (Balasundaramet al., 2011), resulting in that many existing algorithms for computing maximal k𝑘k-plexes can only deal with very small graphs as reported in (Conte et al., 2017, 2018). Since small-size k𝑘k-plexes are often no practical use in real-world applications, it is more desirable to compute all relatively-large maximal k𝑘k-plexes with size no less than a given parameter q𝑞q (e.g., q>10𝑞10q>10) (Conte et al., 2017). To enumerate all relatively-large maximal k𝑘k-plexes, Conte et al. (Conte et al., 2017) first developed an efficient algorithm using clique and k𝑘k-core (Seidman, 1983) to reduce the search space. Then, Conte et al.  (Conte et al., 2018) further proposed an improved algorithm with an effective pivoting technique which was originally used in many maximal clique enumeration algorithms (Bron and Kerbosch, 1973; Eppsteinet al., 2013). However, the worst-case time complexity of these two algorithms is O​(n2​2n)𝑂superscript𝑛2superscript2𝑛O(n^{2}2^{n}). More recently, a theoretically faster branch-and-bound algorithm was proposed by Zhou et al. (Zhouet al., 2020), which was the first algorithm with the worst-case time complexity lower than O​(n2​2n)𝑂superscript𝑛2superscript2𝑛O(n^{2}2^{n}). Nevertheless, when setting k≥3𝑘3k\geq 3, all existing algorithms often require several hours to enumerate all relatively-large maximal k𝑘k-plexes on middle-size real-world graphs. Moreover, to our knowledge, only the algorithm proposed in (Conte et al., 2018) supports parallelism. However, as shown in our experiments, such a parallel algorithm is still very hard to handle large graphs even with 20 CPU cores. This motivates us to develop an efficient, parallel and scalable approach to enumerate all relatively-large maximal k𝑘k-plexes on large real-world graphs.

To achieve this goal, we first develop two novel upper-bounding techniques to reduce the search space, and then present a branch-and-bound enumeration algorithm based on a novel pivot re-selection technique. To further improve the scalability, we also devise a parallel version of our enumeration algorithm. In summary, we make the following contributions.

Novel upper bounds. We propose two novel upper bounds for the k𝑘k-plexes that contain a set C𝐶C of vertices. The key idea of our upper bounds is based on the principle that any possible maximal k𝑘k-plex C′superscript𝐶′C^{\prime} containing C𝐶C can not break the rule that every vertex in C𝐶C must have at least |C′|−ksuperscript𝐶′𝑘|C^{\prime}|-k neighbors in C′superscript𝐶′C^{\prime}. We show that with the help of our upper bounds, many unnecessary computations can be pruned in linear time.

Efficient algorithms. We develop a new branch-and-bound algorithm to enumerate all maximal k𝑘k-plexes with size no less than q𝑞q. Specifically, in our algorithm, we first propose a novel pivot re-selection technique to reduce the search space, and then apply the proposed upper-bounding techniques to further prune unpromising branches. Theoretically, we prove that the worst-case time complexity of our algorithm is bounded by O​(n2​γkn)𝑂superscript𝑛2superscriptsubscript𝛾𝑘𝑛O(n^{2}\gamma_{k}^{n}), where γksubscript𝛾𝑘\gamma_{k} is the branching factor of the algorithm with respect to k𝑘k which is strictly smaller than 222. Finally, an efficient parallel algorithm is further designed to process large real-world graphs.

Extensive experiments. We conduct extensive experiments on real-world massive graphs to test the efficiency of our algorithm. The experiments show that our sequential algorithm can achieve up to 2×2\times to 100×100\times speedup over the state-of-the-art sequential algorithm on most benchmark graphs. The results also show that the speedup ratio of our parallel algorithm is almost linearly w.r.t. (with respect to) the number of threads, indicating the high scalability of the proposed parallel algorithm. For example, on the enwiki-2021 graph (with more than 300 million edges), when k=2𝑘2k=2 and q=50𝑞50q=50, our parallel algorithm only takes 71.2 seconds to enumerating all desired k𝑘k-plexes using 20 threads, while the state-of-the-art parallel algorithm cannot terminate the computation within 24 hours under the same parameter settings. Here we propose a simple but effective parallelization strategy for our enumeration algorithm when running on the multi-core machines. To achieve this, we need to split the computations into multiple independent sub-tasks. In Algorithm 2, we can see that the branches that enumerate k𝑘k-plexes containing the particular set S𝑆S are completely independent. So, a general parallel computation scheme is to divide the entire task into n𝑛n sub-tasks, and then we dynamically assign these sub-tasks to each thread to complete the computations (i.e., run lines 2-6 of Algorithm 2 in parallel). However, the computational workloads of some threads may be very large, leading to an inefficient parallel algorithm. The main reason for this is that the computational cost of each branch 𝖡𝗋𝖺𝗇𝖼𝗁​(S,C,X,q,k)𝖡𝗋𝖺𝗇𝖼𝗁𝑆𝐶𝑋𝑞𝑘{\mathsf{Branch}}(S,C,X,q,k) is very different, which is mainly determined by the size of the set C𝐶C and the internal structural of the subgraph G​(S∪C)𝐺𝑆𝐶G(S\cup C). Thus, in this paper, we develop an alternative solution to achieve a better load balancing. The details are as follows.

In our implementation, we first make use of the above-mentioned general scheme to start the parallel computation. Then, during the branching calculations, the algorithm also monitors the work status of each thread. If the algorithm finds that a thread to be idle, some threads would divide the task being executed into two sub-tasks. The first sub-task is the sub-branch that computes the maximal k𝑘k-plexes containing the pivot vertex, and the other sub-task is to compute the maximal k𝑘k-plexes excluding the pivot vertex. Note that these two sub-tasks are also independent of each other. Thus, the newly generated sub-task can be safely assigned to the idle thread. This task division scheme is performed iteratively until all threads finished the computations. As shown in the experiments, such a parallel algorithm can achieve a very good speedup ratio over our sequential algorithm.

Maximal clique enumeration. Enumerating all maximal cliques from a graph is a fundamental problem in graph analysis. As shown in (Moon and Moser, 1965), the number of maximal cliques could be exponential w.r.t. the graph size , i.e., its number is O​(3n/3)𝑂superscript3𝑛3O(3^{n/3}) in the worst case. However, the number of cliques in real-world graphs are often much smaller than such a worst-case bound. Many practical algorithms, including the classic Bron-Kerbosch algorithm (Bron and Kerbosch, 1973) and its pivot-based variants (Tomitaet al., 2006; Eppsteinet al., 2013; Naudé, 2016), work well on real-world graphs. (Conte et al., 2016b; Segundoet al., 2018) developed parallel pivot-based maximal clique enumeration algorithms to handle large datasets. There also exist many output-sensitive maximal clique enumeration algorithms (Makino and Uno, 2004; Changet al., 2013; Conteet al., 2016a) which can achieve the polynomial-delay time complexity.

Maximal relaxed clique enumeration. Since the constraint of clique is often very strictly for real-world community detection related applications, there exist many relaxed clique models including k𝑘k-plex (Pattilloet al., 2013), s𝑠s-defective cliques (Pattilloet al., 2013), r𝑟r-clique (Behar and Cohen, 2018), γ𝛾\gamma-quasi-clique (Peiet al., 2005; Liu and Wong, 2008), and so on. To enumerate all these relaxed cliques, many practical algorithms have been proposed in recent years.
