Value-based methods are widely used in control, planning, and reinforcement learning (Gorodetsky etВ al., 2018; Alora etВ al., 2016; Mnih etВ al., 2015). To solve a Markov Decision Process (MDP), one common method is value iteration, which finds the optimal value function. This process can be done by iteratively computing and updating the state-action value function, represented by QвҖӢ(s,a)рқ‘„рқ‘ рқ‘ҺQ(s,a)В (i.e., the Qрқ‘„Q-value function). In simple cases with small state and action spaces, value iteration can be ideal for efficient and accurate planning. However, for modern MDPs, the data that encodes the value function usually lies in thousands or millions of dimensionsВ (Gorodetsky etВ al., 2018; 2019), including images in deep reinforcement learning (Mnih etВ al., 2015; Tassa etВ al., 2018). These practical constraints significantly hamper the efficiency and applicability of the vanilla value iteration.

Yet, the Qрқ‘„Q-value function is intrinsically induced by the underlying system dynamics. These dynamics are likely to possess some structured forms in various settings, such as being governed by partial differential equations.In addition, states and actions may also contain latent features (e.g., similar states could have similar optimal actions). Thus, it is reasonable to expect the structured dynamic to impose a structure on the Qрқ‘„Q-value. Since the Qрқ‘„Q function can be treated as a giant matrix, with rows as states and columns as actions, a structured Qрқ‘„Q function naturally translates to a structured Qрқ‘„Q matrix.

In this work, we explore the low-rank structures. To check whether low-rank Qрқ‘„Q matrices are common, we examine the benchmark Atari games, as well as 4 classical stochastic control tasks. As we demonstrate in SectionsВ 3 and 4, more than 40 out of 57 Atari games and all 4 control tasks exhibit low-rank Qрқ‘„Q matrices. This leads us to a natural question: How do we leverage the low-rank structure in Qрқ‘„Q matrices to allow value-based techniques to achieve better performance on вҖңlow-rankвҖқ tasks?

We propose a generic framework that allows for exploiting the low-rank structure in both classical planning and modern deep RL. Our scheme leverages Matrix Estimation (ME), a theoretically guaranteed framework for recovering low-rank matrices from noisy or incomplete measurementsВ (Chen & Chi, 2018).In particular, for classical control tasks, we propose Structured Value-based Planning (SVP). For the Qрқ‘„Q matrix of dimension |рқ’®|Г—|рқ’ң|рқ’®рқ’ң|\mathcal{S}|\times|\mathcal{A}|, at each value iteration, SVP randomly updates a small portion of the QвҖӢ(s,a)рқ‘„рқ‘ рқ‘ҺQ(s,a) and employs ME to reconstruct the remaining elements.We show that planning problems can greatly benefit from such a scheme, where fewer samples (only sample around 20% of (s,a)рқ‘ рқ‘Һ(s,a) pairs at each iteration) can achieve almost the same performance as the optimal policy.

For more advanced deep RL tasks, we extend our intuition and propose Structured Value-based Deep RL (SV-RL), applicable for deep Qрқ‘„Q-value based methods such as DQNВ (Mnih etВ al., 2015). Here, instead of the full Qрқ‘„Q matrix, SV-RL naturally focuses on the вҖңsub-matrixвҖқ, corresponding to the sampled batch of states at the current iteration.For each sampled Qрқ‘„Q matrix, we again apply ME to represent the deep Qрқ‘„Q learning target in a structured way, which poses a low rank regularization on this вҖңsub-matrixвҖқ throughout the training process, and hence eventually the Qрқ‘„Q-networkвҖҷs predictions. Intuitively, as learning a deep RL policy is often noisy with high variance, if the task possesses a low-rank property, this scheme will give a clear guidance on the learning space during training, after which a better policy can be anticipated.We confirm that SV-RL indeed can improve the performance of various value-based methods on вҖңlow-rankвҖқ Atari games: SV-RL consistently achieves higher scores on those games. Interestingly, for complex, вҖңhigh-rankвҖқ games, SV-RL performs comparably. ME naturally seeks solutions that balance low rank and a small reconstruction error (cf. Section 3.1). Such a balance on reconstruction error helps to maintain or only slightly degrade the performance for вҖңhigh-rankвҖқ situation.We summarize our contributions as follows:вҖўWe are the first to propose a framework that leverages matrix estimation as a general scheme to exploit the low-rank structures, from planning to deep reinforcement learning.вҖўWe demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach on classical stochastic control tasks, where the low-rank structure allows for efficient planning with less computation.вҖўWe extend our scheme to deep RL, which is naturally applicable for value-based techniques. Across a variety of methods, such as DQN, double DQN, and dueling DQN, experimental results on all Atari games show that SV-RL can consistently improve the performance of value-based methods, achieving higher scores for tasks when low-rank structures are confirmed to exist.

Efficient Planning via Online Reconstruction with Matrix EstimationThe previous results motivate us to exploit the structure in value function for efficient planning. The idea is simple:

If the eventual matrix is low-rank, why not enforcing such a structure throughout the iterations?

In other words, with the existence of a global structure, we should be capable of exploiting it during intermediate updates and possibly also regularizing the results to be in the same low-rank space. In particular, at each iteration, instead of every (s,a)рқ‘ рқ‘Һ(s,a) pair (i.e., Eq.В (1)), we would like to only calculate Q(t+1)superscriptрқ‘„рқ‘Ў1Q^{(t+1)} for some (s,a)рқ‘ рқ‘Һ(s,a) pairs and then exploit the low-rank structure to recover the whole Q(t+1)superscriptрқ‘„рқ‘Ў1Q^{(t+1)} matrix. So far, our focus has been on tabular MDPs where value iteration can be applied straightforwardly. However, the idea of exploiting structure is much more powerful: we propose a natural extension of our approach to deep RL. Our scheme again intends to exploit and regularize structures in the Qрқ‘„Q-value function with ME. As such, it can be seamlessly incorporated into value-based RL techniques that include a Qрқ‘„Q-network. We demonstrate this on Atari games, across various value-based RL techniques.

Before diving into deep RL, let us step back and review the process we took to develop our intuition.Previously, we start by treating the Qрқ‘„Q-value as a matrix. To exploit the structure, we first verify that certain MDPs have essentially a low-rank QвҲ—superscriptрқ‘„Q^{*}. We argue that if this is indeed the case, then enforcing the low-rank structures throughout the iterations, by leveraging ME, should lead to better algorithms.

A naive extension of the above reasoning to deep RL immediately fails. In particular, with images as states, the state space is effectively infinitely large, leading to a tall Qрқ‘„Q matrix with numerous number of rows (states). Verifying the low-rank structure for deep RL hence seems intractable. However, by definition, if a large matrix is low-rank, then almost any row is a linear combination of some other rows. That is, if we sample a small batch of the rows, the resulting matrix is most likely low-rank as well. To probe the structure of the deep Qрқ‘„Q function, it is, therefore, natural to understand the rank of a randomly sampled batch of states.In deep RL, our target for exploring structures is no longer the optimal QвҲ—superscriptрқ‘„Q^{*}, which is never available. In fact, like SVP, the natural objective should be the converged values of the underlying algorithm, which in вҖңdeepвҖқ scenarios, are the eventually learned Qрқ‘„Q function.

With the above discussions, we now provide evidence for the low-rank structure of learned Qрқ‘„Q function on some Atari games. We train standard DQN on 444 games, with a batch size of 323232. To be consistent, the 444 games all have 181818 actions. After the training process, we randomly sample a batch of 323232 states, evaluate with the learned Qрқ‘„Q network and finally synthesize them into a matrix. That is, a 32Г—18321832\times 18 data matrix with rows the batch of states, the columns the actions, and the entries the values from the learned Qрқ‘„Q network. Note that the rank of such a matrix is at most 181818. The above process is repeated for 10,000 times, and the histogram and empirical CDF of the approximate rank is plotted in Fig. 4. Apparently, there is a strong evidence supporting a highly structured low-rank Qрқ‘„Q function for those games вҖ“ the approximate ranks are uniformly small; in most cases, they are around or smaller than 3.

Having demonstrated the low-rank structure within some deep RL tasks, we naturally seek approaches that exploit the structure during the training process. We extend the same intuitions here: if eventually, the learned Qрқ‘„Q function is of low rank, then enforcing/regularizing the low rank structure for each iteration of the learning process should similarly lead to efficient learning and potentially better performance. In deep RL, each iteration of the learning process is naturally the SGD step where one would update the Qрқ‘„Q network. Correspondingly, this suggests us to harness the structure within the batch of states. Following our previous success, we leverage ME to achieve this task.

We now formally describe our approach, referred as structured value-based RL (SV-RL). It exploits the structure for the sampled batch at each SGD step, and can be easily incorporated into any Qрқ‘„Q-value based RL methods that update the Qрқ‘„Q network via a similar step as in Qрқ‘„Q-learning. In particular, Qрқ‘„Q-value based methods have a common model update step via SGD, and we only exploit structure of the sampled batch at this step вҖ“ the other details pertained to each specific method are left intact.

Further ObservationsInterestingly however, the performance gains vary from games to games. Specifically, the majority of the games can have benefits, with few games performing similarly or slightly worse. Such observation motivates us to further diagnose SV-RL in the next section.

So far, we have demonstrated that games which possess structured Qрқ‘„Q-value functions can consistently benefit from SV-RL. Obviously however, not all tasks in deep RL would possess such structures. As such, we seek to diagnose and further interpret our approach at scale.

DiagnosisWe select 444 representative examples (with 181818 actions) from all tested games, in which SV-RL performs better on two tasksВ (i.e., Frostbite and Krull), slightly better on one taskВ (i.e., Alien), and slightly worse on the otherВ (i.e., Seaquest). The intuitions we developed in SectionВ 4 incentivize us to further check the approximate rank of each game.As shown in Fig.В 7, in the two better cases, both games are verified to be approximately low-rankВ (вҲј2similar-toabsent2\sim 2), while the approximate rank in Alien is moderately highВ (вҲј5similar-toabsent5\sim 5), and even higher in SeaquestВ (вҲј10similar-toabsent10\sim 10).

Consistent InterpretationsAs our approach is designed to exploit structures, we would expect to attribute the differences in performance across games to the вҖңstrengthвҖқ of their structured properties.Games with strong low-rank structures tend to have larger improvements with SV-RL (Fig.В 7(a) and 7(b)), while moderate approximate rank tends to induce small improvements (Fig.В 7(c)), and high approximate rank may induce similar or slightly worse performances (Fig.В 7(d)).The empirical results are well aligned with our arguments: if the Qрқ‘„Q-function for the task contains low-rank structure, SV-RL can exploit such structure for better efficiency and performance; if not, SV-RL may introduce slight or no improvements over the vanilla algorithms. As mentioned, the ME solutions balance being low rank and having small reconstruction error, which helps to ensure a reasonable or only slightly degraded performance, even for вҖңhigh rankвҖқ games.We further observe consistent results on ranks vs. improvement across different games and RL techniques in AppendixВ E, verifying our arguments.

Structures in Value FunctionRecent work in the control community starts to explore the structure of value function in control/planning tasksВ (Ong, 2015; Alora etВ al., 2016; Gorodetsky etВ al., 2015; 2018). These work focuses on decomposing the value function and subsequently operating on the reduced-order space.In spirit, we also explore the low-rank structure in value function. The central difference is that instead of decomposition, we focus on вҖңcompletionвҖқ. We seek to efficiently operate on the original space by looking at few elements and then leveraging the structure to infer the rest, which allows us to extend our approach to modern deep RL.In addition, while there are few attempts for basis function learning in high dimensional RLВ (Liang etВ al., 2016), functions are hard to generate in many cases and approaches based on basis functions typically do not get the same performance as DQN, and do not generalize well.In contrast, we provide a principled and systematic method, which can be applied to any framework that employs value-based methods or sub-modules. Finally, we remark that at a higher level, there are studies exploiting different structures within a task to design effective algorithms, such as exploring the low-dimensional system dynamics in predictive state representationВ (Singh etВ al., 2012) or exploring the so called low вҖңBellman rankвҖқ propertyВ (Jiang etВ al., 2017). We provide additional discussions in Appendix F.

Value-based Deep RLDeep RL has emerged as a promising technique, highlighted by key successes such as solving Atari games viaQрқ‘„Q-learningВ (Mnih etВ al., 2015) and combining with Monte Carlo tree searchВ (Browne etВ al., 2012; Shah etВ al., 2019) tomaster Go, Chess and ShogiВ (Silver etВ al., 2017a; b). Methods based on learning value functions are fundamental components in deep RL, exemplified by the deep Qрқ‘„Q-networkВ (Mnih etВ al., 2013; 2015).Over the years, there has been a large body of literature on its variants, such as double DQNВ (VanВ Hasselt etВ al., 2016), dueling DQNВ (Wang etВ al., 2015), IQNВ (Dabney etВ al., 2018) and other techniques that aim to improve explorationВ (Osband etВ al., 2016; Ostrovski etВ al., 2017).Our approach focuses on general value-based RL methods, rather than specific algorithms. As long as the method has a similar model update step as in Qрқ‘„Q-learning, our approach can leverage the structure to help with the task. We empirically demonstrate that deep RL tasks that have structured value functions indeed benefit from our scheme.

Matrix EstimationME is the primary tool we leverage to exploit the low-rank structure in value functions.Early work in the field is primarily motivated by recommendation systems.Since then, thetechniques have been widely studied and applied to different domainsВ (Abbe & Sandon, 2015; Borgs etВ al., 2017), and recently even in robust deep learningВ (Yang etВ al., 2019). The field is relatively mature, with extensive algorithms and provable recovery guarantees for structured matrixВ (Davenport & Romberg, 2016; Chen & Chi, 2018). Because of the strong promise, we view ME as a principled reconstruction oracle to exploit the low-rank structures within matrices.

We investigated the structures in value function, and proposed a complete framework to understand, validate, and leverage such structures in various tasks, from planning to deep reinforcement learning. The proposed SVP and SV-RL algorithms harness the strong low-rank structures in the Qрқ‘„Q function, showing consistent benefits for both planning tasks and value-based deep reinforcement learning techniques. Extensive experiments validated the significance of the proposed schemes, which can be easily embedded into existing planning and RL frameworks for further improvements.
