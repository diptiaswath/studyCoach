In the widely studied paradigm of ontology-mediated querying, adatabase query is enriched with an ontology that provides domainknowledge as well as additional vocabulary for queryformulation Bienvenu et al. (2013b); Calvanese et al. (2009). Weconsider ontologies formulated in description logics (DLs) of the DL-Lite family and queries that are ℰ​ℒ​ℐℰℒℐ{\cal E\!\!\>LI} queries (ELIQs) or, in other words,tree-shaped unary conjunctive queries (CQs). DL-Lite is a prominent choicefor the ontology language as it underpins the OWL 2 QL profile of theOWL ontology language OWL Working Group (2009). Likewise, ELIQs are aprominent choice for the query language as they are computationallyvery well-behaved: without an ontology, they can be evaluated inpolynomial time in combined complexity, in contrast toNP-completeness for unrestricted CQs. Moreover, in the form ofℰ​ℒ​ℐℰℒℐ{\cal E\!\!\>LI} concepts they are a central building block of ontologies inseveral dialects of DL-Lite and beyond.

The aim of this paper is to study the related topics of computingleast general generalizations (LGGs) of ELIQs under DL-Lite ontologies andlearning ELIQs under DL-Lite ontologies in Angluin’s framework of exactlearningAngluin (1987a, b).Computing generalizations is a natural operation in query engineeringthat plays a crucial role in learning logical formulasPlotkin (1970); Muggleton (1991), in particular in exact learningten Cate andDalmau (2021). Exact learning, in turn, is concernedwith constructing queries and ontologies. This can be challenging andcostly, especially when logic expertise and domain knowledge are notin the same hands. Aiming at such cases, exact learning provides asystematic protocol for query engineering in which a learner interactsin a game-like fashion with an oracle, which may be a domain expert.

In exact learning, the learner and the oracle know and agree on theontology 𝒪𝒪\mathcal{O}, and they also agree on the target query qTsubscript𝑞𝑇q_{T} to use onlyconcept and role names from 𝒪𝒪\mathcal{O}.The learner may ask membership queries where theyproduce an ABox 𝒜𝒜\mathcal{A} and a candidate answer a𝑎a and ask whether𝒜,𝒪⊧qT​(a)models𝒜𝒪subscript𝑞𝑇𝑎\mathcal{A},\mathcal{O}\models q_{T}(a), that is, whether a𝑎a is an answer to qTsubscript𝑞𝑇q_{T}w.r.t. 𝒪𝒪\mathcal{O} on 𝒜𝒜\mathcal{A}. We consider as ontology languages the DLs DL-LiteℋsuperscriptDL-Liteℋ\textsl{DL-Lite}^{\mathcal{H}} and DL-LiteℱsuperscriptDL-Liteℱ\textsl{DL-Lite}^{\mathcal{F}}, equipped withrole inclusions (also known as role hierarchies) andfunctional roles, respectively. Both dialects admit conceptand role disjointness constraints and ℰ​ℒ​ℐℰℒℐ{\cal E\!\!\>LI} concepts on the right-handside of concept inclusions Calvanese et al. (2007); Kikot et al. (2011). Weshow that DL-LiteℋsuperscriptDL-Liteℋ\textsl{DL-Lite}^{\mathcal{H}} admits polynomial frontiers that can be computed inpolynomial time, and that DL-LiteℱsuperscriptDL-Liteℱ\textsl{DL-Lite}^{\mathcal{F}} does not even admit finitefrontiers. We then introduce a fragment DL-Liteℱ−superscriptDL-Litelimit-fromℱ\textsl{DL-Lite}^{\mathcal{F}-} of DL-LiteℱsuperscriptDL-Liteℱ\textsl{DL-Lite}^{\mathcal{F}} thatrestricts the use of inverse functional roles on the right-hand sideof concept inclusions and show that it is as well-behaved asDL-LiteℋsuperscriptDL-Liteℋ\textsl{DL-Lite}^{\mathcal{H}}. Both frontier constructions require a rather subtleanalysis. We then consider in detail the application of our results in thecontext of exact learning and show that ELIQs can be learned inpolynomial time w.r.t. ontologies 𝒪𝒪\mathcal{O} formulated in DL-LiteℋsuperscriptDL-Liteℋ\textsl{DL-Lite}^{\mathcal{H}} orDL-Liteℱ−superscriptDL-Litelimit-fromℱ\textsl{DL-Lite}^{\mathcal{F}-}. We now turn to non-learnability results. Without aseed CQ, ontologies with concept disjointness constraints are notlearnable using only polynomially many membership queries.A disjointness ontology is a DL-Liteℋ​ℱsuperscriptDL-Liteℋℱ\textsl{DL-Lite}^{\mathcal{H}\mathcal{F}} ontology that only consistsof concept disjointness constraints.

We next show that when we drop the syntactic restriction fromDL-Liteℱ−superscriptDL-Litelimit-fromℱ\textsl{DL-Lite}^{\mathcal{F}-}, then ELIQs are no longer learnable at all using onlymembership queries. Note that this is not a direct consequence ofTheorem 3 as there could be an alternativeapproach that does not use frontiers.

A natural next step for future work is to generalize the resultspresented in this paper to DL-Liteℋ​ℱsuperscriptDL-Liteℋℱ\textsl{DL-Lite}^{\mathcal{H}\mathcal{F}}, adopting the same syntacticrestriction that we have adopted for DL-LiteℱsuperscriptDL-Liteℱ\textsl{DL-Lite}^{\mathcal{F}}, and additionally requiringthat functional roles have no proper subroles. The latter serves tocontrol the interaction between functional roles and roleinclusions. Even with this restriction, however, that interaction isvery subtle and the frontier construction becomes significantlymore complex.Other interesting questions are whether ELIQs can be learned inpolynomial time w.r.t. DL-Litehorn ontologies and whether CQs canbe learned w.r.t. DL-Litecore ontologies when both membership andequivalence queries are admitted.
