Domain Adaptation (DA) is all about training a model in a labelled source domain S𝑆S (e.g., an autopilot trained in daytime), and the model is expected to generalize in a target domain T𝑇T (e.g., the autopilot deployed in nighttime), where T𝑇T and S𝑆S has a significant domain shift (e.g., day vs. night) [43].To illustrate the shift and generalization, we introduce the classic notion of causal representation learning [22, 51]:any sample is generated by 𝐱=Φ​(𝐜,𝐞)𝐱Φ𝐜𝐞\mathbf{x}=\Phi(\mathbf{c},\mathbf{e}), where ΦΦ\Phi is the generator, 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} is the causal feature determining the domain-invariant class identity (e.g., road lane), and 𝐞𝐞\mathbf{e} is the environmental feature (e.g., lighting conditions).As the environment is domain-specific, the domain shift 𝐞S≠𝐞Tsubscript𝐞𝑆subscript𝐞𝑇\mathbf{e}_{S}\neq\mathbf{e}_{T} results in 𝐱S≠𝐱Tsubscript𝐱𝑆subscript𝐱𝑇\mathbf{x}_{S}\neq\mathbf{x}_{T}, challenging the pursuit of a domain-invariant class model.However, if a model disentangles the causal feature by transforming each 𝐱=𝐜𝐱𝐜\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{c}, it can generalize under arbitrary shift in 𝐞𝐞\mathbf{e}.In Figure 1, even though 𝐞T=“colorful”subscript𝐞𝑇“colorful”\mathbf{e}_{T}=\textrm{``colorful''} is significantly different from 𝐞S=“red”subscript𝐞𝑆“red”\mathbf{e}_{S}=\textrm{``red''}, thanks to the non-discriminative “red” color in S𝑆S, the model still easily disentangles 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} from Φ​(𝐜,𝐞S)Φ𝐜subscript𝐞𝑆\Phi(\mathbf{c},\mathbf{e}_{S}) and thus generalizes to digits of any color.

Unfortunately, in general, disentangling 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} by using only S𝑆S is impossible as 𝐞Ssubscript𝐞𝑆\mathbf{e}_{S} can be also discriminative, i.e., spuriously correlated with 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c}.In Figure 2, the color 𝐞Ssubscript𝐞𝑆\mathbf{e}_{S} is correlated with the shape 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c}, i.e., 𝐜1=subscript𝐜1absent\mathbf{c}_{1}=“0” and 𝐜2=subscript𝐜2absent\mathbf{c}_{2}=“1” in S𝑆S tend to have 𝐞1=darksubscript𝐞1dark\mathbf{e}_{1}=\mathrm{dark} and 𝐞2=lightsubscript𝐞2light\mathbf{e}_{2}=\mathrm{light} digit colors, respectively.As both 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} and 𝐞Ssubscript𝐞𝑆\mathbf{e}_{S} are discriminative, a classifier trained in S𝑆S will inevitably capture both (see the red line in Figure 2).This leads to poor generalization when the correlation between 𝐞Tsubscript𝐞𝑇\mathbf{e}_{T} and 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} is different, i.e., the colors of 𝐜1=subscript𝐜1absent\mathbf{c}_{1}=“0” and 𝐜2=subscript𝐜2absent\mathbf{c}_{2}=“1” in T𝑇T tend to be 𝐞2=lightsubscript𝐞2light\mathbf{e}_{2}=\mathrm{light} and 𝐞1=darksubscript𝐞1dark\mathbf{e}_{1}=\mathrm{dark} instead.

To avoid spurious correlations between 𝐞Ssubscript𝐞𝑆\mathbf{e}_{S} and 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c}, a practical setting called Unsupervised DA (UDA) introduces additional information about T𝑇T through a set of unlabeled samples [17, 37, 14].There are two main lineups:1) Domain-alignment methods [60, 36, 12] learn the common feature space of S𝑆S and T𝑇T that minimizes the classification error in S𝑆S.In Figure 2, their goal is to unify “0”s and “1”s in S𝑆S and T𝑇T.However, they suffer from the well-known misalignment issue [33, 73], e.g., collapsing “0”s and ”1”s in T𝑇T to “1”s and “0”s in S𝑆S respectively also satisfies the training objective, but it generalizes poorly to T𝑇T.2) Self-training methods [74, 40, 54] use a classifier trained in S𝑆S to pseudo-label samples in T𝑇T, and jointly train the model with the labels in S𝑆S and confident pseudo-labels in T𝑇T.Yet, the pseudo-labels can be unreliable even with expensive threshold tweaking [34, 31], e.g., the ✗ areas in Figure 2 have confident but wrong pseudo-labels.In fact, the recent WILDS 2.0 benchmark [49] on real-world UDA tasks shows that bothlineups even under-perform an embarrassingly naive baseline: directly train in S𝑆S and test in T𝑇T.

Notably, although both methods respect the idiosyncrasies in S𝑆S (by minimizing the classification error), they fail to account for the inherent distribution in T𝑇T, e.g., in Figure 2, even though T𝑇T is unlabelled, we can still identify the two sample clusters in T𝑇T (enclosed by the blue circles), which provide additional supervision for the classifier trained in S𝑆S. In particular, the classifier (red line) in Figure 2 breaks up the two clusters in T𝑇T, showing that the correlation between color 𝐞Ssubscript𝐞𝑆\mathbf{e}_{S} and 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} in S𝑆S is inconsistent with the clusters affected by 𝐞Tsubscript𝐞𝑇\mathbf{e}_{T} in T𝑇T, which implies that color 𝐞𝐞\mathbf{e} is the environmental feature.Hence, to make the U in UDA matter, we aim to learn a classifier that is consistent with classification in S𝑆S and clustering in T𝑇T:

Consistency.We use the Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) loss to encourage the prediction similarity of each sample pair from the same class of S𝑆S or cluster of in T𝑇T, and penalize the pairs from different ones.In Figure 2, the red and blue lines denote the classifiers that minimize the BCE loss in S𝑆S and T𝑇T, respectively. By minimizing the combined loss, we learn the classifier consistent with the sample distribution in both domains (the upright black line), which predicts solely based on the causal feature 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} (i.e., disentangling 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} from Φ​(𝐜,𝐞)Φ𝐜𝐞\Phi(\mathbf{c},\mathbf{e})).

Invariance. However, enforcing consistency is a tug-of-war between S𝑆S and T𝑇T, and the BCE loss in one domain can dominate the other to cause failures.Without loss of generality, we consider the case where S𝑆S dominates in Figure 2.Due to the strong correlation between 𝐞Ssubscript𝐞𝑆\mathbf{e}_{S} and 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c}, the classifier that minimizes the combined losses of BCE (black line) deviates from the desired upright position as in Figure 2.To tackle this, we aim to learn an invariant classifier [1] that is simultaneously optimal w.r.t. the BCE loss in S𝑆S and T𝑇T.In Figure 2, for each of the two losses, we color the candidate regions where the classifier is optimal (i.e., gradients ≈0absent0\approx 0) with red and blue, respectively.In particular, by learning an invariant classifier lying in the intersection (purple region), we successfully recover the desired one as in Figure 2, even when one domain dominates.

Overall, we term our approach as Invariant CONsistency learning (ICON). Our contributions:•ICON is a novel UDA method that can be plugged into different self-training baselines. It is simple to implement (Section 2), and disentangles the causal feature 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} from Φ​(𝐜,𝐞)Φ𝐜𝐞\Phi(\mathbf{c},\mathbf{e}) that generalizes to T𝑇T with a theoretical guarantee (Section 3).•ICON significantly improves the current state-of-the-art on classic UDA benchmarks [31, 6] with ResNet-50 [21] as the feature backbone: 75.8% (+2.6%) accuracy averaging over the 12 tasks in Office-Home [62], and 87.4% (+3.7%) mean-class accuracy in VisDA-2017 [45] (Section 5).•Notably, as of NeurIPS 2023, ICON dominates the leaderboard of the challenging WILDS 2.0 UDA benchmark [49] (https://wilds.stanford.edu/leaderboard/), which includes 8 large-scale classification, regression and detection tasks on image, text and graph data (Section 5).

Preliminaries. We adopt the group-theoretic definition of disentanglement [22, 64] to justify ICON. We start by introducing some basic concepts in group theory. 𝒢𝒢\mathcal{G} is a group of semantics that generate data by group action, e.g., a “turn darker” element g∈𝒢𝑔𝒢g\in\mathcal{G} transforms 𝐱𝐱\mathbf{x} from white “0” to g∘𝐱𝑔𝐱g\circ\mathbf{x} as grey “0” (bottom-left to top-left in Figure 4b). A sketch of theoretical analysis is given below and interested readers are encouraged to read the full proof in Appendix.

Definition (Generalization). Feature space 𝒳𝒳\mathcal{X} generalizes under the direct product decomposition 𝒢=𝒢/ℋ×ℋ𝒢𝒢ℋℋ\mathcal{G}=\mathcal{G}/\mathcal{H}\times\mathcal{H}, if 𝒳𝒳\mathcal{X} has a non-trivial 𝒢/ℋ𝒢ℋ\mathcal{G}/\mathcal{H}-representation, i.e., the action of each h∈ℋℎℋh\in\mathcal{H} corresponds to a trivial linear map in 𝒳𝒳\mathcal{X} (i.e., identity map), and the action of each g∈𝒢/ℋ𝑔𝒢ℋg\in\mathcal{G}/\mathcal{H} corresponds to a non-trivial one.

This definition formalizes the notion of transforming each sample Φ​(𝐜,𝐞)Φ𝐜𝐞\Phi(\mathbf{c},\mathbf{e}) to the causal feature 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c}. Subgroup ℋ⊂𝒢ℋ𝒢\mathcal{H}\subset\mathcal{G} and quotient group 𝒢/ℋ𝒢ℋ\mathcal{G}/\mathcal{H} transform the environmental feature 𝐞𝐞\mathbf{e} and the causal 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c}, respectively.If 𝒳𝒳\mathcal{X} has a non-trivial 𝒢/ℋ𝒢ℋ\mathcal{G}/\mathcal{H}-representation, samples in a class are transformed to the same feature 𝐜∈𝒳𝐜𝒳\mathbf{c}\in\mathcal{X} regardless of 𝐞𝐞\mathbf{e} (i.e., representation of 𝒢/ℋ𝒢ℋ\mathcal{G}/\mathcal{H}), and different classes have different 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} (i.e., non-trivial). This definition is indeed in accordance with the common view of good features [64], i.e., they enable zero-shot generalization as 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} is class-unique, and they are robust to the domain shift by discarding the environmental features 𝐞𝐞\mathbf{e} from Φ​(𝐜,𝐞)Φ𝐜𝐞\Phi(\mathbf{c},\mathbf{e}). To achieve generalization, we need the following assumptions in ICON.

Assumption 1 states that classes in T𝑇T are separated by clusters. This corresponds to the classic clustering assumption, a necessary assumption for learning with unlabeled data [61].Figure 4a shows a failure case of Assumption 1, where the intra-class distance (i.e., dotted double-arrow) is much larger than the inter-class one (i.e., solid double-arrow) in T𝑇T, i.e., shape 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} is less discriminative than color 𝐞Tsubscript𝐞𝑇\mathbf{e}_{T}. Hence the clusters in T𝑇T are based on 𝐞𝐞\mathbf{e}, causing ICON to capture the wrong invariance.To help fulfill the assumption, UDA methods (and SSL in general) commonly leverage feature pre-training and data augmentations. We also specify the number of clusters as the class numbers in T𝑇T, which is a necessary condition of this assumption.

Assumption 2 states that 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} is the only invariance between S𝑆S and T𝑇T.Figure 4b illustrates a failure case, where there exist two invariant classifiers: both the vertical one based on shape 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} and the horizontal one based on color 𝐞𝐞\mathbf{e} separate the classes in S𝑆S and clusters in T𝑇T.Yet as T𝑇T is unlabelled, there is no way for the model to determine which one is based on 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c}.In practice, this assumption can be met by collecting more diverse unlabelled samples in T𝑇T (e.g., darker “0”s and lighter “1”s in Figure 4b), or introducing additional priors on what features should correspond to the causal one.

Theorem. When the above assumptions hold, ICON (optimizing Eq. (3)) learns a backbone θ𝜃\theta mapping to a feature space 𝒳𝒳\mathcal{X} that generalizes under 𝒢/ℋ×ℋ𝒢ℋℋ\mathcal{G}/\mathcal{H}\times\mathcal{H}. In particular, the learned f𝑓f is the optimal classifier in S𝑆S and T𝑇T.

By transforming each sample Φ​(𝐜,𝐞)Φ𝐜𝐞\Phi(\mathbf{c},\mathbf{e}) in S𝑆S and T𝑇T to 𝐜∈𝒳𝐜𝒳\mathbf{c}\in\mathcal{X}, the classifier easily associate each 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} to its corresponding class label using the labelled S𝑆S to reach optimal.

Domain-Alignment methods [15, 4, 59] map samples in S𝑆S and T𝑇T to a common feature space where they become indistinguishable.Existing works either minimize the discrepancy between the sample distributions of S𝑆S and T𝑇T [38, 2, 29], or learn features that deceive a domain discriminator [71, 65, 35]. However, they suffers from the misalignment issue under large domain shift from S𝑆S to T𝑇T [73, 33], such as shift in the support of the sample distribution [25] (e.g., Figure 2).

Self-Training methods [19, 3, 55] are the mainstream in semi-supervised learning (SSL), and recently became a promising alternative in UDA, which focuses on generating accurate T𝑇T pseudo-labels. This lineup explores different classifier design, such as k-NN [53], a teacher-student network [13] or an exponential moving average model [32]. Yet the pseudo-labels are error-prune due to the spurious correlations in S𝑆S, hence the model performance is still inconsistent [34].

Alleviating Spurious Correlation has been studied in several existing works of UDA [58, 34, 31]. They train a T𝑇T-specific classifier to generate pseudo-labels [58, 31]. However, their T𝑇T-classifiers are still initialized based on a classifier trained in S𝑆S, hence the spurious correlation in S𝑆S are introduced to the T𝑇T-classifiers right in the beginning. On the contrary, our ICON learns a cluster head in T𝑇T without supervision in S𝑆S, which is solely for discovering the sample clusters in T𝑇T. Hence, the spurious correlation in S𝑆S inconsistent with the clusters in T𝑇T can be removed by ICON (Section 5).

Graph-based SSL methods [18, 44, 24] share a similar spirit with ICON, where they learn a classifier using the labeled samples that is consistent with the unlabelled sample similarities. However, they lack the invariant constraint in ICON, which can cause the failure cases (e.g., Figure 2).

This is because ICON works by learning the causal feature 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} and discarding the environmental feature 𝐞𝐞\mathbf{e}, and the theory holds as long as the definition of 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} and 𝐞𝐞\mathbf{e} is consistent across the training and test data, e.g., iWildCam is about animal classification across the labeled, unlabeled, and test data.

Feature Backbone. We used the followings pre-trained on ImageNet [48]: ResNet-50 [21] on Office-Home, VisDA-2017 and iWildCAM, DenseNet-121 [23] on FMoW and Faster-RCNN [16] on GlobalWheat. We used DistilBERT [50] with pre-trained weights from the Transformers library on CivilComments and Amazon. On Camelyon17, we used DenseNet-121 [23] pre-trained by the self-supervised SwAV [5] with the training data in S𝑆S and T𝑇T. On PovertyMap and OGB-MolPCBA with no pre-training available, we used multi-spectral ResNet-18 [21] and graph isomorphism network [68] trained with the labelled samples in the source domain S𝑆S, respectively.

Other Details. We aim to provide a simple yet effective UDA method that works across data modalities, hence we did not use tricks tailored for a specific modality (e.g., image), such as mixup [70] or extensive augmentations (16 are used in [13]). On classic UDA datasets, we used entropy minimization and SAM optimizer following CST [34]. On VisDA-2017, iWildCam, Camelyon17 and FMoW, we pre-processed the features with UMAP [39] (output dimension as 50) and EqInv [63], which helps fulfill Assumption 1 (i.e., highlight causal feature to improve clustering).ICON is also resource efficient—all our experiments can run on a single NVIDIA 2080Ti GPU.

Failure Cases. ICON performance is relatively weak on PovertyMap and OGB-MolPCBA with two possible reasons: 1) no pre-training available, the backbone is trained only on S𝑆S and hence biased in the beginning, and 2) the ground-truth number of classes in T𝑇T is not well-defined in both datasets, hence Assumption 1 in Section 3 may fail.

Components. Recall that the main components of ICON include: 1) Consistency loss (CON) that leverages the supervision from the labels in S𝑆S and clusters in T𝑇T; 2) the use of invariance constraint (INV) in Eq. (3); 2) clustering in T𝑇T with #clusters==#classes. Their ablations are included in Table 4.From the first three lines, we observe that adding each component leads to consistent performance increase on top of the self-training baseline (FixMatch), which validates their effectiveness. From the next two lines, when #clusters≠\neq#classes, the model performs poorly, e.g., using one cluster in Figure 2 (i.e., 0.5×0.5\times#classes), the invariant classifier needs to predict the two red circles in S𝑆S as dissimilar (two classes) and the two blue ones in T𝑇T as similar (one cluster), which in fact corresponds to the erroneous red line (i.e., predictions in T𝑇T are similarly ambiguous).In the last line, we tried k-means for clustering instead of rank-statistics, leading to decreased performance. We postulate that rank-statistics is better because its online clustering provides up-to-date cluster assignments. In the future, we will try other more recent online clustering methods, e.g., an optimal transport-based method [5].Overall, the empirical findings validate the importance of the three components, which are consistent with the theoretical analysis in Section 3.

We presented a novel UDA method called Invariant CONsistency learning (ICON) that removes the spurious correlation specific to the source domain S𝑆S to generalize in the target domain T𝑇T.ICON pursues an invariant classifier that is simultaneously consistent with the labels in S𝑆S and the clusters in T𝑇T, hence removing the spurious correlation inconsistent in T𝑇T.We show that ICON achieves generalization in T𝑇T with a theoretical guarantee.Our theory is verified by extensive evaluations on both the classic UDA benchmarks and the challenging WILDS 2.0 benchmark, where ICON outperforms all the conventional methods. We will seek more principled pre-training paradigms that disentangle the causal features and relax the assumption of knowing the class numbers in T𝑇T to improve regression tasks.

Limitation. Our approach is based on the assumptions in Section 4, i.e., the classes in T𝑇T are separated by clusters, and there is enough diversity in T𝑇T such that causal feature 𝐜𝐜\mathbf{c} is the only invariance.Assumption 1 is essentially the clustering assumption, which is a necessary assumption for learning with unlabeled data [61]. In fact, UDA methods (and semi-supervised learning in general) commonly leverage feature pre-training and data augmentations to help fulfill the assumptions. One can also use additional pre-trained knowledge from foundation models, or deploy improved clustering algorithm (e.g., an optimal transport-based method [5]).Assumption 2 is necessary to rule out corner cases, e.g., in Figure 4b, without additional prior, there is no way for any UDA method to tell if each one of the blue cluster (unlabeled) should be pseudo-labeled as "0" or "1". In practice, these corner cases can be avoided by collecting diverse unlabeled data, which is generally abundant, e.g., collecting unlabeled street images from a camera-equipped vehicle cruise.

Broader Impact. Our work aims to capture the causal feature in the unsupervised domain adaptation setting. In particular, we significantly improve the state-of-the-art on WILDS 2.0 benchmark, which includes practical tasks that benefit wildlife preservation, tumour classification in healthcare, remote sensing, vision in agriculture, etc. The pursue of causal feature can produce more robust, transparent and explainable models, broadening the applicability of ML models and promoting fairness in AI.
