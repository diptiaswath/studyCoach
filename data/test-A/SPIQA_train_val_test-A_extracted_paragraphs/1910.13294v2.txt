Recent advances in NLP problems benefit from deep neural models to achieve significant performance gains.However, the usages of complex neural models bring the cost of interpretability, limiting their applications to domains requiring explainable decisions, e.g. the medical domain.

Addressing the interpretability issue requires the neural models self-explainable while maintaining predictive accuracy.In the NLP field, previous research mainly follows two paradigms. The first is to design the neural architectures to make themselves more explainable, such as module networks andreas2016learning; andreas2016neural; johnson2017inferring.However, such approaches require heavy architecture engineering, which takes a long way to be deployed to various NLP applications.Moreover, the performance of the new architectures is usually not guaranteed to be close to previous state-of-the-art results.Therefore, many studiesВ lei2016rationalizing; li2016understanding; chen2018learning adopted the second way:using the state-of-the-art architectures from prior work as-is, and elaborating on how the decisions are made regarding the inputs, i.e. rationales for the neural predictions.

The second line of research, rationalization, makes it easy to generate explanable results on any existing architectures for new applicationsВ chen2018learning.Specifically, these models consist of two agents, where the first agent learns to extract subsets from the input texts as rationales; and the second one has a same state-of-the-art neural architecture for the target task, but with the rationales as model inputs.However, in practice with limited supervised signals (e.g. usually end-task labels only), these methods may suffer from two problems, namely degeneration of rationale selection and downgrade of predictive performance.

The problem of degeneration refers to the cases where the generated rationales are not meaningful but are still sufficient for accurate predictions.Such cases happen because of the collaborative game formulation of the rationalization algorithms.Such cases happen because of the collaborative game formulation of the rationalization algorithms, which is an ill-defined training objective for rationales.

The collaborative game for rationalization converges if the two agents agree with a communication channel. Therefore, if an extractor agent is powerful enough to accurately guess the task label, it can just extract separable patterns for different labels. This leaves an easy problem for the predictor, no matter whether the patterns can represent the original inputs or not.Table 1 gives degenerating examples when the model from lei2016rationalizing is trained on the sentiment classification of beer appearance reviews (see the experiment section for details). It can be clearly seen that the selected rationales by previous workВ lei2016rationalizingare not representative of the labels.However, a collaborative predictor could work well with them.For example, these rationales consist of descriptions of colors, which are not as discriminative as descriptions of heads and lacings.For enhancing the negative sentiment prediction in the second example, it generates patterns вЂњno в‹Їв‹Ї\cdots no в‹Їв‹Ї\cdotsвЂќ.When the word no is only generated with negative examples, it becomes a strong sentiment indicator for the predictor even without any contents associated.111We tuned the regularizer of continuity (see the experiment section for the details) but the algorithm still generates two non-continuous words вЂњnoвЂќ.

The problem of performance downgrade refers to the cases where significant performance gaps exist between the predictions with only rationales and with the full original inputs.The performance downgrade violates the requirement of interpretability since good explanations should be sufficient for maintaining the predictive accuracy.

Note that the performance downgrade is measured on the development set.Therefore there are two different main reasons for the downgrade in the existing collaborative framework. First, the extractors may fail to disentangle the information about the correct label from the original inputs, which is more likely to happen when the domain of prediction is complex (e.g. large number of class labels as in Section LABEL:).Second, the extractors may fall into the degeneration mode, thus the extracted rationales, although predictive during training, fail to generalize to the development set.This situation is more likely to happen when the supervised signals are coarse-grained (e.g. the binary classification case), as confirmed by the example in Table 1, where the existing rationalization approach results in poor accuracy (76.4%). By comparison, our proposed method, with the same architectures of the extractor and the predictor, could achieve significantly higher accuracy (80.4%) with further inductive bias on the rationale selection.

The above problems happen because previous models are usually trained with the final task objectives without any supervision on rationales. Therefore there is no sufficient inductive bias distinguishing good rationales from bad ones.To address these problems, we propose a new rationalization framework with contributions in two folds:

в€™в€™\bullet First, we propose a three-player framework for rationalization.Besides the two agents playing the collaborative game as in previous works, we add the third discriminator agent.This new agent uses the texts not selected by the extractor agent, i.e. the texts not considered as rationales, to predict the task label.During training, the extractor agent plays a minimax game with this discriminator agent, trying to make it fail when predicting without the rationales.Intuitively, this third agent alleviates the degeneration problem because when degeneration happens, the left texts are usually sufficient for making accurate predictions. Such a result makes the extractor agent loses in the minimax game and pushes it to update the extraction strategy.

в€™в€™\bullet Second, we propose an introspective extractor. The new extractor is aware of the label that would be predicted with the full text, and use this knowledge to force the extraction of rationales to be correspondent to the label. In other words, this extractor introspects the reason for a non-interpretable base model to make a prediction and extracts the clue rationales accordingly, thus the predictive accuracy can be easily guaranteed.

Together with the two ideas, our framework pushes the extractor to generate sufficient and non-degenerated rationales.We also formally prove that the equilibrium of the three-player game guarantees the axiomatic properties of rationales.Experiments confirm the effectiveness of our proposed rationalization framework.

We construct three text classification tasks, including two sentiment classification tasks from the BeerAdvocate222www.beeradvocate.com review dataset mcauley2012learning333http://snap.stanford.edu/data/web-BeerAdvocate.html, and a more complex relation classification task from SemEval 2010 Task 8В hendrickx2009semeval.We show examples of the above tasks in Table 2.

This is the same data used inВ lei2016rationalizing. Each review evaluates multiple aspects of a kind of beer, including appearance, smell, palate and the overall evaluation. For each aspect, a rating score в€€\in [0,1] is labeled. We use a threshold of 0.6 to create balanced binary classification tasks on each aspect.

The advantage of this dataset is that it enables automatic evaluation of rationale extraction:the dataset provides sentence-level annotations on about 1,000 reviews, where for each aspect a sentence is annotated if it covers the aspect.We use this set to evaluate the extracted rationales for each aspect separately. Since the average ratio of annotated rationales is about 18% of the whole reviews, we make all the models extract on average 10% of words and evaluate the precision; and 20% of words for evaluating the recall.

Note that in this task, the sentences/phrases describing the target aspect is sufficient to predict the aspect sentiment polarity, and they have a high chance to overlap with the human-annotated rationales on the test set.Therefore the extractor can succeed as long as it could distinguish phrases related to different aspects, even when the extractions are restricted to 10% of the words.As a result, we consider it an easier task comparing the following two tasks.

This task is also built on the BeerAdvocate dataset. Noticing that some reviews have clear patterns indicating which sentence is talking about a specific aspect. For example, a sentence starting with вЂњappearance:вЂќ or вЂњa:вЂќ is likely to be a review on the appearance aspect.We extract such sentences for the appearance aspect to form a new sentence-level classification task, where the class label is still the polarity of the aspect from the original review.444The single-aspect dataset will be released upon publication. This newly constructed task is obviously more challenging compared to the multi-aspect one, since the extractor cannot complete the task by only guessing the topic; also it is in a finer-grained level because the required rationales are short phrases. Since there is no human annotations, we conduct human evaluate over the extracted rationales.

To show the generalizability of our proposed approaches to other NLP applications, we further evaluate our rationalization approach on the SemEval 2010 Task 8 dataset hendrickx2009semeval.Given two target entities in a sentence, the goal is to classify the relation type (w/ direction) between two entities, or predict None if there is no relation.The relation classification task is different from the above two because it requires the sentence encoding to be aware of the two target entities.To achieve this, we follow the usage of bidirectional LSTM for relation classification in nguyen2015combining, but without applying their rich human-crafted features. The only minimal feature we have is appending to each word a position vector indicating its relative positions regarding to the two entities (the words in the entity e1subscriptрќ‘’1e_{1}/e2subscriptрќ‘’2e_{2} has a relative position of 0 regarding to e1subscriptрќ‘’1e_{1}/e2subscriptрќ‘’2e_{2}). The positions are mapped to their (learnable) embedding vectors that are fed into the LSTM encoder together with word embeddings in each agent of a rationalization model.

Similar to the single-aspect beer review, this is also a fine-grained rationalization task.However the setting here is also significantly different from the other two tasks since the it has a much larger number of class labels.This could help us better investigate the problem of degeneration since more distinguishable training signals may alleviate the problem. On the other hand, as discussed in the introduction, the more complicated prediction space may introduce additional difficulty to avoid performance downgrade вЂ“ if the rationalization framework is not powerful enough, the extractor could generate rationales with mixed indicators that relate to irrelevant class labels thus hurts the predictive accuracy.

For both the generators and the two predictors, we use bidirectional LSTMs. In the introspection module, the Classifier has the same architecture as the predictors.We use the REINFORCE algorithm to train all the modules in an end-to-end manner. We have an exploration rate of 0.05.

The hyper-parameters are tuned on the development sets according to only predictive accuracy. In other words, all the models are tuned without seeing any rationale annotations.

From a passage contain reviews regarding multiple aspects, the task is to select the rationale that relates to the appearance aspect and make the sentiment prediction.We evaluate the methods in two settings, where all the methods are required to extract about 10% and 20% of the words from the passages respectively.

We first report the predictive accuracy of the extracted rationales on predicting the sentiments.

When only 10% of the words are used, the performance of lei2016rationalizing has a big gap compared to the accuracy when the whole passage is used (82.05 v.s. 87.59), which shows a significant performance downgrade. When this model is trained with our three-player game, the accuracy is slightly improved, mainly because that the three-player game forced the rationales capture more information related to the class labels.By comparison, our introspection models are able to maintain the accuracy with little performance drop. Similar observation can be found when 20% of the words are highlighted as rationales.

Moreover, when a classifier is trained with randomly highlighted rationales, it performs significantly worse even compared to lei2016rationalizing in both settings (i.e., 65.70 v.s. 82.05 for 10% highlighting, 72.31 v.s. 83.88 for 20%).This confirms that extracting concise and sufficient rationales is not a trivial task and lei2016rationalizing is a strong baseline.

In summary, our three-player framework consistently improves the quality of extracted rationales on both the model from lei2016rationalizing and the introspection model.The introspection model, when trained with only collaborative game, suffers little performance drop but serious degeneration as expected.When trained with the three-player framework, the introspection model manages to maintain both the predictive accuracy and the quality of rationalization.
