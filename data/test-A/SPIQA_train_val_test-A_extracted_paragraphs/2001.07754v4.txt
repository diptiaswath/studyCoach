Despite prominent standardization efforts such as OWL, a large varietyof description logics (DLs) continues to be used as ontologylanguages. In fact, ontology designers choose a DL suitable for theirpurposes based on many factors including expressive power,computational properties, and tool supportBaader et al. (2017). Since ontology engineeringfrequently involves (partial) reuse of existing ontologies, thisraises the problem of converting an ontology written in some source DLℒSsubscriptℒ𝑆\mathcal{L}_{S} into a desired target DL ℒTsubscriptℒ𝑇\mathcal{L}_{T}. A particularly importantcase is ontology approximation where ℒTsubscriptℒ𝑇\mathcal{L}_{T} is a fragment ofℒSsubscriptℒ𝑆\mathcal{L}_{S}, studied for example inPan and Thomas (2007); Ren et al. (2010); Botoeva et al. (2010); Carral et al. (2014); Zhou et al. (2015); Bötcher et al. (2019).

In practice, ontology approximation is often done in an ad hoc way bydropping all statements from the source ontology 𝒪Ssubscript𝒪𝑆\mathcal{O}_{S} that are notexpressible in ℒTsubscriptℒ𝑇\mathcal{L}_{T}, or at least the inexpressible parts of suchstatements. It is well-known that this results in incompleteapproximations, that is, there will be knowledge in 𝒪Ssubscript𝒪𝑆\mathcal{O}_{S} thatcould be expressed in ℒTsubscriptℒ𝑇\mathcal{L}_{T}, but is not contained in the resultingapproximated ontology. The degree and nature of the resultingincompleteness is typically neither understood nor analyzed. Onereason for this unsatisfactory situation might be the fact that it isby no means easy to construct complete approximations and, even worse,finite complete approximations are not guaranteed to exist. This wasstudied in depth in Bötcher et al. (2019) whereontologies formulated in expressive Horn DLs such asHorn-𝒮​ℋ​ℐ​ℱ𝒮ℋℐℱ\mathcal{SHIF} and ℰ​ℒ​ℐℰℒℐ\mathcal{ELI} are approximated intractable Horn DLs such as ℰ​ℒℰℒ{\cal EL}. For example, it is shown there thatfinite complete ℰ​ℒ​ℐℰℒℐ{\cal ELI}-to-ℰ​ℒℰℒ{\cal EL} approximations do not exist even inextremely simple cases including those occurring inpractice. The authors then lay out a newresearch program for ontology approximation that consists in mappingout the structure of complete (infinite) ontology approximations as atool for guiding informed decisions when constructing incomplete(finite) approximations in practice, and also to enable a betterunderstanding of the degree and nature of incompleteness.

In this paper, we consider ℒSsubscriptℒ𝑆\mathcal{L}_{S}-to-ℒTsubscriptℒ𝑇\mathcal{L}_{T} ontology approximationwhere ℒSsubscriptℒ𝑆\mathcal{L}_{S} is a non-Horn DL such as 𝒜​ℒ​𝒞𝒜ℒ𝒞\mathcal{ALC} and ℒTsubscriptℒ𝑇\mathcal{L}_{T} is atractable Horn DL such as ℰ​ℒℰℒ{\cal EL}. Arguably, these are extremely naturalcases of ontology approximation given that Horn vs. non-Horn isnowadays the most important classification criterion for DLsBaader et al. (2017). Non-Horn DLs include expressivefeatures such as negation and disjunction and require ‘reasoning bycases’ which is computationally costly, but also have considerablyhigher expressive power than Horn DLs. Horn DLs, in contrast, enjoyfavourable properties such as the existence of universal models and of‘consequence-based’ reasoning algorithms that avoid reasoning by casesCucala et al. (2019). Despite being natural, however,non-Horn-to-Horn approximation turns out to be a challengingendeavour.

We start with the fundamental case of ℰ​ℒ​𝒰ℰℒ𝒰{\cal ELU}-to-ℰ​ℒℰℒ{\cal EL} approximation. Given an ℰ​ℒ​𝒰ℰℒ𝒰{\cal ELU} ontology 𝒪Ssubscript𝒪𝑆\mathcal{O}_{S}, we aim to find a(potentially infinite) ℰ​ℒℰℒ{\cal EL} ontology 𝒪Tsubscript𝒪𝑇\mathcal{O}_{T} such that for all ℰ​ℒℰℒ{\cal EL} concepts C,D𝐶𝐷C,D in the signature of 𝒪Ssubscript𝒪𝑆\mathcal{O}_{S}, 𝒪S⊧C⊑Dmodelssubscript𝒪𝑆𝐶square-image-of-or-equals𝐷\mathcal{O}_{S}\models C\sqsubseteq D iff 𝒪T⊧C⊑Dmodelssubscript𝒪𝑇𝐶square-image-of-or-equals𝐷\mathcal{O}_{T}\models C\sqsubseteq D.

The last two lines of 𝒪Tsubscript𝒪𝑇\mathcal{O}_{T} illustrate that ℰ​ℒℰℒ{\cal EL} consequences of ℰ​ℒ​𝒰ℰℒ𝒰{\cal ELU} ontologies can be rather non-obvious.

We first prove that finite approximations need not exist in theℰ​ℒ​𝒰ℰℒ𝒰{\cal ELU}-to-ℰ​ℒℰℒ{\cal EL} case and that depth bounded approximations may benon-elementary in size. Our main result is then a concreteapproximation scheme that makes explicit the structure of completeinfinite approximations and aims to keep as much structure of thesource ontology as possible.An interesting and, given theresults in Bötcher et al. (2019), surprising feature ofour scheme is that it can be expected to often deliver finiteapproximations in practical cases. We perform a case study based on theManchester ontology corpus that confirm this expectation. We alsoshow that if 𝒪Ssubscript𝒪𝑆\mathcal{O}_{S} is an acyclic ℰ​ℒ​𝒰ℰℒ𝒰{\cal ELU} ontology, then a finite ℰ​ℒℰℒ{\cal EL} approximation always exists (though it need not be acyclic). The finiteapproximations that we obtain are too large to be directly used inpractice. Nevertheless, we view our results as positive andbelieve that in practice approximations of reasonable sizeoften exist, as in Example 1. A ‘push buttontechnology’ for constructing them, however, is outside of the scope ofthis paper.

We then proceed to the cases of ℰ​ℒ​𝒰⊥ℰℒsubscript𝒰bottom{\cal ELU}_{\bot}-to-ℰ​ℒ⊥ℰsubscriptℒbottom{\cal EL}_{\bot} and𝒜​ℒ​𝒞𝒜ℒ𝒞\mathcal{ALC}-to-ℰ​ℒ⊥ℰsubscriptℒbottom{\cal EL}_{\bot} approximations which turn out to be closelyrelated to each other. They also turn out to be significantlydifferent from the ℰ​ℒ​𝒰ℰℒ𝒰{\cal ELU}-to-ℰ​ℒℰℒ{\cal EL} case in that finite approximations donot exist in extremely simple (and practical) cases, much like in theHorn approximation cases studied inBötcher et al. (2019). Also, finite approximations ofacyclic ontologies are no longer guaranteed to exist. While this isnot good news, it is remarkable that the addition of the⊥bottom\bot symbol has such a dramatic effect. We again provide an(infinite) approximation scheme.

Finally, we propose a notion of approximation that is tailored towardsapplications in ontology-mediated queryingCalvanese et al. (2009)and show that it is intimately related to the subsumption-based approximations thatwe had studied before. Remarkably, if we concentrate on atomicqueries (AQs), then we obtain finite approximations even in the𝒜​ℒ​𝒞𝒜ℒ𝒞\mathcal{ALC}-to-ℰ​ℒ⊥ℰsubscriptℒbottom{\cal EL}_{\bot} case. Compared to the related work presented inKaminski et al. (2016), we do not require thepreservation of all query answers, but only of a maximal subsetthereof, and our method is applicable to all ontologies formulated inthe source DL chosen rather than to a syntactically restricted class. Wealso observe an interesting application to the rewritability ofontology-mediated queries.
