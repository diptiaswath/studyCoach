Due to the rapid growth of user historical behaviors, it becomes an essential problem to build an effective recommendation model to help users to find their desired items from a huge number of candidates.Classical recommendation methods, including collaborative filtering based models (BaietВ al., 2017; Koren, 2008; HeetВ al., 2017) and factorization machine based models (Rendle, 2010; Cheng etВ al., 2016; JuanetВ al., 2016), have mainly focused on modeling userвҖҷs general interests to find her favorite products; while less exploring the userвҖҷs demands with an aspect of time.As stated in (BaietВ al., 2019; ZhuetВ al., 2017; WuetВ al., 2017), time is definitely an important factor that can significantly influence userвҖҷs demands and result in periodical user behaviors.Therefore, a branch of recent attempts are proposed to capture userвҖҷs sequential patterns through either memory networks (Ren etВ al., 2019), recurrent neural networks (WuetВ al., 2017; ZhuetВ al., 2017), or temporal point processes (Du etВ al., 2016; BaietВ al., 2019).However, most existing approaches can only be applied for user behavior data with length scaling up to hundreds due to the computation and storage limitations in real online system (Zhou etВ al., 2019, 2018; Pi etВ al., 2020; QinetВ al., 2020).

To tackle this issue, we consider combining it with recently proposed search-based models (Pi etВ al., 2020; QinetВ al., 2020), whose key idea is to first search the effective information from the userвҖҷs historical records to capture specific interests of the user in terms of different candidate items, which are then used to make the final prediction.However, itвҖҷs non-trivial to do that due to the following challenges:вҖў(C1) How to incorporate the userвҖҷs sequential patterns into these search-based models?Existing search-based methods (Pi etВ al., 2020; QinetВ al., 2020)overlook userвҖҷs sequential patterns (i.e., the effect of time factor).As a consequence,when a teen has purchased a lipstick, these methods are likely to recommend the same or similar products beforeshe gets tired of or runs out of the purchased one.Hence, itвҖҷs essential to take the time information into account, as the userвҖҷs demands are highly time-sensitive.вҖў(C2) How to leverage the label information (i.e., user feedbacks) from historical data in the recommendation model?The principal way to use the user historical feedbacks is to treat this feedbacks as the label to supervise the model.However, as discussed in (WangetВ al., 2021; ShietВ al., 2020), combining the information from both label and feature as the input to train the model can significantly improve its performance.As directly mixing up all this information will definitely lead to the label leakage issue, then how to smartly enrich the model with the label information needsto investigate.вҖў(C3) How to design a learning algorithm to simultaneously train a search-based model and a prediction model in an end-to-end fashion?Previous attemptseither manually design a mixed loss function (Pi etВ al., 2020) or apply a reinforcement learning (RL) (QinetВ al., 2020) in training.As the performance of the former one largely relies on the loss design and hyper-parameter tuning, and the latter one usually suffers from the sample inefficiency of the RL algorithm, the training algorithm design also is another significant challenge.

In this paper, we propose a novel sequential recommendation framework named Search-based Time-Aware Recommendation (STARec) which captures userвҖҷs evolving demands over time through a unified search-based time-aware model.

Concretely, noticing thatcategory plays an essential role in search models (Pi etВ al., 2020),we first construct an embedding vector for each category.We then search and retrieve items either by a hard-search strategy based on category IDs or a soft-search strategy based on the similarities between their category embeddings.The intuition of using category for search and retrieval is straightforward.Taking Figure 1(a) as an instance, the motivation of the teen u1subscriptрқ‘ў1u_{1} buying the lipstick i1subscriptрқ‘–1i_{1} can either lie in that she is running out of her old lipstick i2subscriptрқ‘–2i_{2}, or that she needs an accessory for her new purchases (e.g., lip gloss i4subscriptрқ‘–4i_{4}), but not likely due to her purchased iPhone i3subscriptрқ‘–3i_{3}.Note that our search-based module using category embeddings instead of item embeddings would make the whole framework much easier to train.We also design a novel adaptive search mechanism that can gradually transfer from the hard-search strategy to the soft-search one when the embedding vectors are well-tuned.

In order to mine the hidden time-aware patterns, we then mix up the retrieved items together with recent browsed ones and feed them into a time-aware sequential network that considers not only the sequential orders but also their time intervals.Besides the userвҖҷs own histories, we also attempt to enrich the model by similar usersвҖҷ historical records.As shown in Figure 1(b), when recommending i1subscriptрқ‘–1i_{1} to u1subscriptрқ‘ў1u_{1}, we argue that referring to similar historical records from similar users such as u2subscriptрқ‘ў2u_{2} would be helpful; while the histories of dissimilar users such as u3subscriptрқ‘ў3u_{3} would be noise.This user similarity can be either softly estimated through the inner-product of their embedding vectors or can be hardly measured by counting the number of purchased items with the same category with i1subscriptрқ‘–1i_{1}.

Different from current prevailing methods using userвҖҷs feedbacks (e.g., click, purchase) only as the supervision signals.As Figure 1(c) shows, we propose to involve the userвҖҷs previous feedbacks as input, where the label of the target item is set as a randomized value.We call this technique label trick.Its intuition is straightforward that if a user finds her desired items, itвҖҷs unlikely for her to click or purchase other similar items.

In summary, the contributions of the paper are three-fold:вҖўWe propose a novel framework named STARec, which captures the userвҖҷs time-sensitive evolving demands via combining a search-based module and a time-aware module.вҖўWe propose to involve the userвҖҷs previous feedbacks as input and reveal that this label information can improve the performance.вҖўWe design a new adaptive search mechanism, which gradually transfers from the hard-search strategy to the soft one.

We conduct extensive experiments on three industrial datasets, and experimental results exhibit that the superiority of STARec over the state-of-art methods.We successfully deploy STARec in two main item recommendation scenarios in Company X, and share our hands-on experience and discuss the potential extensions to ranking tasks in the appendix.

Search-based Recommendation Model.Classical recommendation methods are proposed to recommend desired items to users based on rich user-item interaction histories either in tabular format (Rendle, 2010; KorenetВ al., 2009; GuoetВ al., 2017; JuanetВ al., 2016), sequence structure (Zhou etВ al., 2019, 2018; BaietВ al., 2019), or graph structure (Jin etВ al., 2020; WangetВ al., 2019).However, as stated in (QinetВ al., 2020), since the users are accumulating more and more behavioral data nowadays, itвҖҷs non-trivial to train the model from the whole user logs due to the limitations from the online computations.One feasible solution is only to focus on userвҖҷs recent records and generate personalized recommendations based on a short sequence instead of a long history (Hidasi etВ al., 2015; Tan etВ al., 2016; Quadrana etВ al., 2017; RuoccoetВ al., 2017; DonkersetВ al., 2017; ChatzisetВ al., 2017; PeietВ al., 2017).However, as recently proposed works (QinetВ al., 2020; Pi etВ al., 2020) suggest, these methods are not able to encode the periodicity or long-term dependency, which leads to sub-optimal solutions.Based on this observation, QinetВ al. (2020); Pi etВ al. (2020) further propose to build a search model either following a hard-search or soft-search strategy over the whole behavioral history.In this way, they can use those relevant items instead of the whole set of user-browsed items to efficiently learn a recommendation method.Unfortunately, these existing search-based methods overlook effects from time intervals among userвҖҷs behaviors and thus can not fully use userвҖҷs browsing sequence.

Time-aware (Sequential) Model.Recent studies (Hosseini etВ al., 2018; ZhuetВ al., 2017) have paid attention on leveraging the time intervals among userвҖҷs behaviors to better capture userвҖҷs preferences, for which traditional sequential architectures (Hidasi etВ al., 2015; Beutel etВ al., 2018; Hidasi andKaratzoglou, 2018; Jing and Smola, 2017; LiuetВ al., 2016b) are insufficient.One direction (ZhaoetВ al., 2018; LiuetВ al., 2016a; ZhuetВ al., 2017) is to develop the specific time gates to control the short-term and long-term interest updates.For example, ZhaoetВ al. (2018) introduces a distance gate based on a recurrent network to control the short-term and long-term point-of-interest updates.Another way (DaietВ al., 2016; Hosseini etВ al., 2018; Mei and Eisner, 2017; VassГёy etВ al., 2019; BaietВ al., 2019) to integrate time interval information is to formulate the userвҖҷs sequential histories by a point process, in which the discrete events in userвҖҷs histories can be modeled in continuous time.For example, Mei and Eisner (2017) proposes a neural hawkes process model which allows past events to influence the future prediction in a complex and realistic fashion.We argue that despite computation cost and time complexity, directly feeding long sequential user behavioral data into these methods will bring much more noise which makes it nearly impractical to capture the rich sequential patterns in the user logs.

In this paper, we combine the advantages from both search-based and time-aware models to efficiently retrieve relevant items and mine sequential patterns in an end-to-end way.Our paper is also related to the label trick proposed in (WangetВ al., 2021; ShietВ al., 2020) based graph structure.Instead, our work focuses on the label usage in the sequence cases, which, notably, is also different from the masking technique in existing sequential models such as BERT (DevlinetВ al., 2018) performing on the feature dimension instead of the label dimension.

In order to verify the effectiveness of STARec in real-world applications, we deploy our method in two main item recommendation scenarios (called вҖңGuess You LikeвҖқ and вҖңInformation FlowвҖқ) in Company X, a main-stream bank company.This App has millions of daily active users who create billions of user logs every day in the form of implicit feedbacks such as click behavior.Please refer to discussions on deployment in AppendixВ C.

For the offline experiment, we use a daily updated dataset collected from September 11th, 2021 to October 10th, 2021 for training and evaluation.Concretely, for each user, we use the last 31 user behaviors as the test set and use the rest records as the training set.The task is CTR prediction, and the overall performance is shown in Table 2.From Table 2, we see that STARec achieves 13.8% and 8.9% improvement over DIN (Zhou etВ al., 2018), 10.7% and 7.8% improvement over DIEN (Zhou etВ al., 2019) on AUC metric in вҖңGuess You LikeвҖқ and вҖңInformation FlowвҖқ scenarios respectively.

For the online experiment, we conduct A/B testing in two recommendation scenarios in Company XвҖҷs online App, comparing the proposed model STARec with the current production baseline methods DIN and DIEN.The whole online experiment lasts a week, from October 14, 2021 to October 21, 2021.In the вҖңGuess You LikeвҖқ scenario, 24.1% and 26.68% of the users are presented with the recommendation by DIN and DIEN, respectively, while 24.2% of the users are presented with the recommendation by STARec.And, in вҖңInformation FlowвҖқ, 25.4% and 24.8% of the users are presented with the recommendation by DIN and DIEN respectively; while 24.5% of the users are presented with the recommendation by STARec.We examine CTR metric defined as CTR=#вҖӢclicks#вҖӢimpressionsCTR#clicks#impressions\text{CTR}=\frac{\#\text{clicks}}{\#\text{impressions}} where #clicks and #impressions are the number of clicks and impressions.We report the average results in Table 2 and depict daily improvement of STARec over DIEN in Figure 7 in the вҖңGuess You LikeвҖқ scenario.From the table, we can see that STARec performs better in вҖңGuess You LikeвҖқ than вҖңInformation FlowвҖқ.One reason is that usersвҖҷ browsing lengths in вҖңInformation FlowвҖқ are much smaller than the lengths in вҖңGuess You LikeвҖқ, which limits the performance of our search-based and time-aware modules.Another reason would be that compared to вҖңGuess You LikeвҖқ, items in вҖңInformation FlowвҖқ are much more diverse, which includes shopping coupons and cinema tickets, besides items in вҖңGuess You LikeвҖқ, making searching for relevant items much harder.From the figure, we can see the CTR improvements are rather stable where the improvement of STARec fluctuates in the range of 2% to 13%.

In this paper, we propose a novel search-based time-aware model named STARec, where we design an adaptive search-based module to retrieve relevant items and then feed this information into a time-aware (sequential) module to capture user evolving interests.We also design a novel label trick that allows the model to use userвҖҷs previous feedbacks as the input, and reveal that this label information can significantly improve the performance.For future work, we plan to further deploy search-based models in other real-world scenarios with sequential data.
