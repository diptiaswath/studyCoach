The field of sparse recovery deals with the problem of recovering an (approximately) sparse vector xūĚĎ•x from only few linear measurements, presented by an underdetermined system of linear equations of the form y=A‚ÄčxūĚĎ¶ūĚźīūĚĎ•y=Ax. One approach to solve this problem is to consider the ‚Ąď0subscript‚Ąď0\ell_{0}-minimization under linear constraints, which is NP-hard in general [natarajan, davis_mallat_avellaneda].For computational reasons, instead of ‚Ąď0subscript‚Ąď0\ell_{0}-minimization, it is common practice to consider its convex relaxationminx‚ąą‚ĄĚN‚Äč‚ÄĖx‚ÄĖ1subject to‚ÄčA‚Äčx=y,subscriptūĚĎ•superscript‚ĄĚūĚĎĀsubscriptnormūĚĎ•1subject toūĚźīūĚĎ•ūĚĎ¶\min_{x\in\mathbb{R}^{N}}||x||_{1}\qquad\text{subject to}\ Ax=y,(P1subscriptūĚĎÉ1P_{1})

where A‚ąą‚ĄĚm√óNūĚźīsuperscript‚ĄĚūĚĎöūĚĎĀA\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times N}, b‚ąą‚ĄĚmūĚĎŹsuperscript‚ĄĚūĚĎöb\in\mathbb{R}^{m} are given, which is referred to as ‚Ąď1subscript‚Ąď1\ell_{1}(-norm) minimization [TaylorBankMcCoy79, claerbout_muir, DonohoLogan92] or basis pursuit [chen_donoho, ChenDonohoSaunders01] in the literature.

Unlike ‚Ąď0subscript‚Ąď0\ell_{0}-minimization, the optimization program (P1subscriptūĚĎÉ1P_{1}) is computationally tractable in general, and a close relationship of their minimizers has been recognized and well-studied in the theory of compressive sensing [CT06, CRT06b, donoho, FoucartRauhut13]. In statistics and machine learning, an unconstrained variant of (P1subscriptūĚĎÉ1P_{1}), often called LASSO, amounts to the most well-studied tractable estimator for variable selection in high-dimensional inference [tibshirani1996regression, hastie2019statistical, meinshausen2006high]. ‚Ąď1subscript‚Ąď1\ell_{1}-minimization has many other applications and it was even called the modern least squares [candes2008enhancing].

The tractability of (P1subscriptūĚĎÉ1P_{1}) becomes evident from the fact that it can be reformulated as a linear program [tillmann2015equivalence]. However, as many problems of interest in applications are high-dimensional and therefore challenging for standard linear programming methods, many specialized solvers for (P1subscriptūĚĎÉ1P_{1}) have been proposed, such as the Homotopy Method [donoho_tsaig], primal-dual methods [chambolle2011first, molinari2021iterative], Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers [boyd_admm], Bregman iterative regularization [yin_osher_goldfarb_darbon] and Semismooth Newton Augmented Lagragian Methods [li_sun_toh] and Iteratively Reweighted Least Squares (IRLS), the latter of which is in the focus of this paper.

Iteratively Reweighted Least Squares corresponds to a family of algorithms that minimizes non-smooth objective functions by solving a sequence of quadratic problems, with its idea going back to a method proposed by Weiszfeld for the Fermat-Weber problem [weiszfeld, back_sabach].A variety of different problems such as robust regression in statistics [holland_welsch, bhaskar_govind_prateek_purushottam], total variation regularization in image processing [GemanReynolds92, NikolavaNg05, allain_idier_goussard], joint learning of neural networks [zhang2019joint], robust subspace recovery [lerman2018fast] and the recovery of low-rank matrices [mohan_fazel, fornasier_rauhut_ward, kummerle_sigl, KM21] can be solved efficiently by IRLS in practice, as it relies on simple linear algebra to solve the linear systems arising from the quadratic problems at each iteration, without the need of a careful initialization or intricate parameter tuning. On the other hand, the analysis of IRLS methods is typically challenging: General convergence results are often weak, and stronger convergence results are only available in particular cases; see Section¬†2.3 for more details.

IRLS for sparse recovery. In the sparse recovery context, the first variants of IRLS were introduced in [rao_kreutz-delgado, gorodnitsky_rao] for the ‚Ąďpsubscript‚ĄďūĚĎĚ\ell_{p}-quasinorm minimization problem (Pp)subscriptūĚĎÉūĚĎĚ(P_{p}) with 0<p‚Č§10ūĚĎĚ10<p\leq 1 that is similar to (P1subscriptūĚĎÉ1P_{1}), but with ‚ÄĖx‚ÄĖpsubscriptnormūĚĎ•ūĚĎĚ\|x\|_{p} instead of ‚ÄĖx‚ÄĖ1subscriptnormūĚĎ•1\|x\|_{1} as an objective.In [chartrand_yin], modifications of the method of [rao_kreutz-delgado, gorodnitsky_rao] using specific smoothing parameter update rules were observed to exhibit excellent numerical performance for solving (Pp)subscriptūĚĎÉūĚĎĚ(P_{p}), retrieving the underlying sparse vector when most of the methods fail. A useful fact is that IRLS is one of the few methods (ADMM being the other one [boyd_admm]) that provides a framework to solve both constrained and unconstrained formulations of ‚Ąďpsubscript‚ĄďūĚĎĚ\ell_{p}-minimization problems.

A major step forward in the theoretical understanding of IRLS was achieved in [Daubechies10], where the authors showed that a variant of IRLS for (P1subscriptūĚĎÉ1P_{1}) converges globally to an ‚Ąď1subscript‚Ąď1\ell_{1}-minimizer if the measurement operator AūĚźīA fulfills the null space property of sufficient order, which essentially ensures that an ‚Ąď1subscript‚Ąď1\ell_{1}-minimizer is actually sparse. However, since this proof relies on the existence of a convergent subsequence, their proof does not reveal any rate for global convergence. The analysis of [Daubechies10] provides, furthermore, a locally linear convergence rate, but this local linear rate has the drawback that it only applies if the support of the true signal has been discovered, which is arguably the difficult part of ‚Ąď0subscript‚Ąď0\ell_{0}-minimization‚ÄĒcf. Proposition¬†3.1 below and Section¬†4.1.

As mentioned in the introduction, IRLS has a long history and has appeared under different names within different communities, e.g., similar algorithms are usually called half-quadratic algorithms in image processing [idier, allain_idier_goussard] and the KacŐÜanov method in numerical PDEs [diening_fornasier_tomasi_wank]. Probably the most common usage of IRLS has been in robust regression [holland_welsch, green1984iteratively], c.f. [burrus] for a survey that also covers applications in approximation theory. For pūĚĎĚp-norm regression, [adil_peng_sachdeva] proposed a version of IRLS for which convergence results for p‚ąą[2,‚ąě)ūĚĎĚ2p\in[2,\infty) were established, solving a problem that was open for over thirty years. Also, for robust regression, by using an ‚Ąď1subscript‚Ąď1\ell_{1}-objective on the residual, [bhaskar_govind_prateek_purushottam] showed recently global convergence of IRLS with a linear rate, with high probability for sub-Gaussian data. We note that our proof strategy is different from the one of [bhaskar_govind_prateek_purushottam] due to a structural difference of (P1subscriptūĚĎÉ1P_{1}) from robust regression.

In [ochs_dosovitskiy_brox_pock], the authors provide a general framework for formulating IRLS algorithms for the optimization of a quite general class of non-convex and non-smooth functions, however, without updated smoothing. They use techniques developed in [attouch_bolte_svaiter] to show convergence of the sequence of iterates to a critical point under the Kurdyka-ŇĀojasiewicz property [Bolte2007lojasiewicz]. However, no results about convergence rates were presented.

For the sparse recovery problem, the topic discussed in this paper, the references [lai_yangyang_wotao, fornasier_peter_rauhut_worm, voronin_daubechies] analyzed IRLS for an unconstrained version of (P1subscriptūĚĎÉ1P_{1}), which is usually a preferable formulation if the measurements are corrupted by noise. Additionally, the work [fornasier_peter_rauhut_worm] addressed the question of how to solve the successive quadratic optimization problems. The authors developed a theory that shows, under the NSP, how accurately the quadratic subproblems need to be solved via the conjugate gradient method in order to preserve the convergence results established in [Daubechies10].

Finally, for the related problems of low-rank matrix recovery and completion, IRLS strategies have emerged as one of the most successful methods in terms of data-efficiency and scalability [fornasier_rauhut_ward, mohan_fazel, kummerle_sigl, KM21].

While we were writing this paper, the manuscript [2021-Poon-noncvxpro] appeared providing new insights about IRLS. It describes a surprisingly simple reparametrization of the IRLS formulation for ‚Ąďpsubscript‚ĄďūĚĎĚ\ell_{p}-minimization (with p‚ąą(2/3,1)ūĚĎĚ231p\in(2/3,1)) that leads to a smooth bilevel optimization problem without any spurious minima, i.e., the stationary points of this new formulation are either global minima or strict saddles. It is an interesting future direction to explore the connection between this new approach and our global convergence theory.

As discussed in Section¬†2.2, the main theoretical advancements for IRLS for the sparse recovery problem were achieved in the work [Daubechies10].
