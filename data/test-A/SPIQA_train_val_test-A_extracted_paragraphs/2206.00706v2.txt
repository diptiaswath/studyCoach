Concentration of measure inequalities for sums of independent random variables are the most fundamental analysis tools in statistics and many other domains (Boucheron etВ al., 2013). Their history stretches almost a century back, and inequalities such as HoeffdingвҖҷs (Hoeffding, 1963) and BernsteinвҖҷs (Bernstein, 1946) are the main work horses of learning theory.

For binary random variables, one of the tightest concentration of measure inequalities is the klkl\operatorname{kl} inequality (Maurer, 2004; Langford, 2005; Foong etВ al., 2021, 2022), which is based on combinatorial properties of a sum of nрқ‘ӣn independent random variables.111The Binomial tail bound is slightly tighter, but it does not extend to the PAC-Bayes setting (Langford, 2005). Our split-klkl\operatorname{kl} approach can be directly applied to obtain a вҖңsplit-Binomial-tailвҖқ inequality. However, while being extremely tight for binary random variables and applicable to any bounded random variables, the klkl\operatorname{kl} inequality is not necessarily a good choice for sums of bounded random variables that can take more than two values. In the latter case, the Empirical Bernstein (Mnih etВ al., 2008; Audibert etВ al., 2009; Maurer and Pontil, 2009) and the Unexpected Bernstein (Cesa-Bianchi etВ al., 2007; Mhammedi etВ al., 2019) inequalities can be significantly tighter due to their ability to exploit low variance, as shown by Tolstikhin and Seldin (2013). However, the Empirical and Unexpected Bernstein inequalities are loose for binary random variables (Tolstikhin and Seldin, 2013).

The challenge of exploiting low variance and, at the same time, matching the tightness of the klkl\operatorname{kl} inequality if a distribution happens to be close to binary, was faced by multiple prior works (Tolstikhin and Seldin, 2013; Mhammedi etВ al., 2019; Wu etВ al., 2021), but remained an open question. We resolve this question for the case of ternary random variables. Such random variables appear in a variety of applications, and we illustrate two of them. One is a study of excess losses, which are differences between the zero-one losses of a prediction rule hв„Һh and a reference prediction rule hвҲ—superscriptв„Һh^{*}, Z=в„“вҖӢ(hвҖӢ(X),Y)вҲ’в„“вҖӢ(hвҲ—вҖӢ(X),Y)вҲҲ{вҲ’1,0,1}рқ‘Қв„“в„Һрқ‘Ӣрқ‘Ңв„“superscriptв„Һрқ‘Ӣрқ‘Ң101Z=\ell(h(X),Y)-\ell(h^{*}(X),Y)\in\{-1,0,1\}. Mhammedi etВ al. (2019) have applied the PAC-Bayes-Unexpected-Bernstein bound to excess losses in order to improve generalization bounds for classification. Another example of ternary random variables is the tandem loss with an offset, defined by в„“ОұвҖӢ(hвҖӢ(X),hвҖІвҖӢ(X),Y)=(в„“вҖӢ(hвҖӢ(X),Y)вҲ’Оұ)вҖӢ(в„“вҖӢ(hвҖІвҖӢ(X),Y)вҲ’Оұ)вҲҲ{Оұ2,вҲ’ОұвҖӢ(1вҲ’Оұ),(1вҲ’Оұ)2}subscriptв„“рқӣјв„Һрқ‘Ӣsuperscriptв„ҺвҖІрқ‘Ӣрқ‘Ңв„“в„Һрқ‘Ӣрқ‘Ңрқӣјв„“superscriptв„ҺвҖІрқ‘Ӣрқ‘Ңрқӣјsuperscriptрқӣј2рқӣј1рқӣјsuperscript1рқӣј2\ell_{\alpha}(h(X),h^{\prime}(X),Y)=(\ell(h(X),Y)-\alpha)(\ell(h^{\prime}(X),Y)-\alpha)\in\left\{\alpha^{2},-\alpha(1-\alpha),(1-\alpha)^{2}\right\}. We present an extensive set of experiments, where we first compare the klkl\operatorname{kl}, Empirical Bernstein, Unexpected Bernstein, and split-klkl\operatorname{kl} inequalities applied to (individual) sums of independent random variables in simulated data, and then compare the PAC-Bayes-klkl\operatorname{kl}, PAC-Bayes-Unexpected-Bersnstein, PAC-Bayes-split-klkl\operatorname{kl}, and, in some of the setups, PAC-Bayes-Empirical-Bennett, for several prediction models on several UCI datasets. In particular, we evaluate the bounds in the linear classification setup studied by Mhammedi etВ al. (2019) and in the weighted majority prediction setup studied by Wu etВ al. (2021). To the best of our knowledge, this is also the first time when the Empirical Bernstein and the Unexpected Bernstein inequalities are directly compared, with and without the PAC-Bayesian extension. In AppendixВ A.2 we also show that an inequality introduced by Cesa-Bianchi etВ al. (2007) yields a relaxation of the Unexpected Bernstein inequality by Mhammedi etВ al. (2019).

We have presented the split-klkl\operatorname{kl} and PAC-Bayes-split-klkl\operatorname{kl} inequalities. The inequalities answer a long-standing open question on how to exploit the structure of ternary random variables in order to provide tight concentration bounds. The proposed split-klkl\operatorname{kl} and PAC-Bayes-split-klkl\operatorname{kl} inequalities are as tight for ternary random variables, as the klkl\operatorname{kl} and PAC-Bayes-klkl\operatorname{kl} inequalities are tight for binary random variables.

In our empirical evaluation the split-klkl\operatorname{kl} inequality was always competitive with the klkl\operatorname{kl} and Unexpected Bernstein inequalities and outperformed both in certain regimes, whereas Empirical Bernstein typically lagged behind. In our experiments in the PAC-Bayesian setting the PAC-Bayes-split-klkl\operatorname{kl} was always comparable to PAC-Bayes-Unexpected-Bernstein, whereas PAC-Bayes-Empirical-Bennett most often lagged behind. The first two inequalities were usually comparable to PAC-Bayes-klkl\operatorname{kl}, although in some cases the attempt to exploit low variance did not pay off and PAC-Bayes-klkl\operatorname{kl} outperformed, which is also the trend observed earlier by Mhammedi etВ al. (2019). To the best of our knowledge, this is the first time when the various approaches to exploitation of low variance were directly compared, and the proposed split-klkl\operatorname{kl} emerged as a clear winner in the basic setting, whereas in the PAC-Bayes setting in our experiments the PAC-Bayes-Unexpected-Bernstein and PAC-Bayes-split-klkl\operatorname{kl} were comparable, and preferable over PAC-Bayes-Empirical-Bernstein and PAC-Bayes-Empirical-Bennett.
