Diffusion on graphs is ubiquitous in various domains owing to its generality in representing complex dynamics among interconnected objects. It appears in numerous high-impact applications, ranging from the study of residential segregation in socioeconomics (Schelling, 1978) and activation cascading in neuroscience (Avena-Koenigsberger etВ al., 2018), to the modeling of disease contagion in epidemiology (Klovdahl, 1985) and malware spreading in cybersecurity (Wang etВ al., 2009). At the core of these applications are the complete diffusion histories of the underlying diffusion process, which could be exploited to identify dynamical patterns (Coleman etВ al., 1957), reflect on precaution actions (Chen etВ al., 2015), forecast intervention effects (Valente, 2005), etc.

Despite their importance, complete diffusion histories are rarely available in real-world applications because diffusion may not be noticed in early stages, collecting diffusion histories may incur unaffordable costs, and/or tracing node states may raise privacy concerns (Sefer and Kingsford, 2016; Chen etВ al., 2019). It is thus highly desirable to develop learning-based methods to automatically reconstruct diffusion histories from limited observations. However, diffusion history reconstruction faces critical challenges. (i) Ill-posed inverse problem.Since different histories can result in the same observation, it is difficult to distinguish which history is preferred. Hence, it is crucial to design a formulation with desired inductive bias. (ii) Explosive search space. The number of possible histories grows exponentially with the number of nodes, so history estimation is an extremely high-dimensional combinatorial problem. (iii) Scarcity of training data. Conventional methods for time series imputation such as supervised learning (e.g., (Cao etВ al., 2018; Jing etВ al., 2021; Cini etВ al., 2022; Marisca etВ al., 2022)) require training data to learn from, but true diffusion histories are rarely available. Thus, they turnout to be inefficacious or even inapplicable for this problem.

Compared to extensive research on forward problems on diffusion (e.g., node immunization (Hayashi etВ al., 2004); see Sec.В 6 for a survey), few works exist for diffusion history reconstruction. The sparse literature on diffusion history reconstruction is exclusively based on the maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) formulation (Sefer and Kingsford, 2016; Chen etВ al., 2019), which relies on two assumptions: (1) knowing true diffusion parameters and/or (2) knowing partial diffusion histories. However, neither diffusion parameters or partial diffusion history is available in many real-world applications. For example, when an epidemic is noticed for the first time, we might only know who are currently infected but have no historical infection information (e.g., who the patient zero is, when outbreaks happen,and where super-spreaders locate).

To address these limitations, we study a novel and more realistic setting: reconstructing a complete Diffusion history from A single SnapsHot (DASH). Importantly, we remove both assumptions of existing works. That is, we do not require knowing true diffusion parameters, and we only have access to the current snapshot. 

Under such a realistic setting, we start with theoretical analyses revealing a fundamental limitation of the MLE formulation for the DASH problem, which motivates us to propose a novel barycenter formulation that addresses the limitation. We then develop an effective solver named DIffusion hiTting Times with Optimal proposal (DITTO) for the barycenter formulation. Our method is unsupervised and thus does not require real diffusion histories as training data, which is desirable due to the scarcity of training data. Extensive experiments demonstrate the efficacy and the scalability of our method DITTO. The main contributions of this paper are:

Furthermore, its NP-hardness implies that estimation error of diffusion parameters is unavoidable. In this section, we theoretically reveal a fundamental limitation of the MLE formulation for diffusion history reconstruction. In SectionВ 3.1, we show that estimation error of diffusion parameters is unavoidable due to the NP-hardness of diffusion parameter estimation. Then in SectionВ 3.2, we prove that the MLE formulation for diffusion history reconstruction is sensitive to estimation error of diffusion parameters. We prove that unfortunately, the MLE formulation is sensitive to estimation error of diffusion parameters when diffusion parameters are small, as is stated in TheoremВ 3.

TheoremВ 3 shows that the relative error of the likelihood induced by estimation error of diffusion parameters is inversely proportional to true diffusion parameters. The conditions nIRвҖӢ(рқ’ҡT)>nIRвҖӢ(рқ’ҡ0)superscriptрқ‘ӣIRsubscriptрқ’ҡрқ‘Үsuperscriptрқ‘ӣIRsubscriptрқ’ҡ0n^{\texttt{I}\texttt{R}}(\boldsymbol{y}_{T})>n^{\texttt{I}\texttt{R}}(\boldsymbol{y}_{0}) and nRвҖӢ(рқ’ҡT)>nRвҖӢ(рқ’ҡ0)superscriptрқ‘ӣRsubscriptрқ’ҡрқ‘Үsuperscriptрқ‘ӣRsubscriptрқ’ҡ0n^{\texttt{R}}(\boldsymbol{y}_{T})>n^{\texttt{R}}(\boldsymbol{y}_{0}) mean that infection and recovery do happen during the timespan Tрқ‘ҮT of interest, which is almost always the case in practice. Hence, the conditions are quite mild and realistic.

Infection and recovery rates in many real-world data are small (OвҖҷBrien etВ al., 1987; Goh and Xiao, 2009; Vanderpump, 2011). Hence, the likelihood under estimated diffusion parameters рқң·^^рқң·\widehat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} has a large relative error and is ill-conditioned. Moreover, since the error of the likelihood is proportional to the likelihood itself, the MLE history under estimated рқң·^^рқң·\widehat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} may have larger decrease in likelihood than other histories and thus may not be the MLE history under true diffusion parameters. Therefore, sensitivity is indeed a fundamental limitation of the MLE formulation in practice.

To address this limitation, we instead solve the DASH problem from a new perspective and propose a so-called barycenter formulation utilizing posterior expected hitting times, which, as we will prove, is stable against estimation error of diffusion parameters.

In this section, we propose a method called DIffusion hiTting Times with Optimal proposal (DITTO) for solving the DASH problem. In Sec.В 4.1, we employ mean-field approximation to estimate diffusion parameters. In Sec.В 4.2, we propose the barycenter formulation that is provably stable, and reduce the DASH problem to estimating the posterior expected hitting times. In Sec.В 4.3, we propose to use an unsupervised graph neural network to learn an optimal proposal in MetropolisвҖ“Hastings Markov chain Monte Carlo (MвҖ“H MCMC) algorithm to estimate the posterior expected hitting times. The overall procedure of DITTO is presented in AlgorithmВ 1.

Diffusion on graphs are deterministic or stochastic processes where information or entities on nodes transmit through edges (Kermack and McKendrick, 1927; Granovetter, 1978; Kempe etВ al., 2003; Aron and Schwartz, 1984; Watts, 2002; Ruan etВ al., 2015; Lee etВ al., 2017; TГ¶rГ¶k and KertГ©sz, 2017). In this section, we review related work on graph diffusion, which can be grouped into forward and inverse problems.

Forward problems on graph diffusion.The vast majority of research on diffusion or dynamic graphs (Fu and He, 2021; Fu etВ al., 2022) are devoted to forward problems. Pioneering works derive epidemic thresholds for random graphs from probabilistic perspectives (Bikhchandani etВ al., 1992) or for arbitrary graphs from spectral perspectives (Wang etВ al., 2003; Ganesh etВ al., 2005; Valler etВ al., 2011; Prakash etВ al., 2012). Later, observational studies investigate influence patterns of diffusion processes (Goldenberg etВ al., 2001; Briesemeister etВ al., 2003; Leskovec etВ al., 2007; Habiba and Berger-Wolf, 2011). On the algorithmic side, researchers have made tremendous effort to diffusion-related optimization problems, such as influence maximization (Goldenberg etВ al., 2001; Richardson and Domingos, 2002; Kempe etВ al., 2003; Chen etВ al., 2009; Datta etВ al., 2010; Chen etВ al., 2010, 2011; Goyal etВ al., 2011) and node immunization (Hayashi etВ al., 2004; Kempe etВ al., 2005; Tong etВ al., 2010; Prakash etВ al., 2010; Tuli etВ al., 2012). Recently, differential equations of graph diffusion has also been applied to the design of graph convolutional networks to alleviate oversmoothing (Klicpera etВ al., 2019; Chamberlain etВ al., 2021; Zhao etВ al., 2021; Wang etВ al., 2021; Chen etВ al., 2022; Thorpe etВ al., 2022).

Inverse problems on graph diffusion.Compared with forward problems on graph diffusion, the inverse problems are in general more difficult due to the challenge of ill-posedness. The inverse problems split into two categories by whether diffusion histories are known. In the one category where diffusion histories are known, the problems are relatively more tractable because the search space is smaller. These problems include estimating diffusion parameters (Gruhl etВ al., 2004; Myers and Leskovec, 2010; Gomez-Rodriguez etВ al., 2012; Zong etВ al., 2012; Gardner etВ al., 2014; Song etВ al., 2017), recovering graph topology (Gruhl etВ al., 2004; Myers and Leskovec, 2010; Gomez-Rodriguez etВ al., 2012; Zong etВ al., 2012; Gardner etВ al., 2014), and inferring diffusion paths (Abrahao etВ al., 2013; Fajardo and Gardner, 2013; Rozenshtein etВ al., 2016; Song etВ al., 2017; Sun etВ al., 2017; Dawkins etВ al., 2021).

The other category where diffusion histories are unknown is much less studied. In this category, the problems are often harder because the number of possible histories is explosively large. Among them, most works focus on the diffusion source localization problem.Only recently has research emerged on the even harder problem diffusion history reconstruction. (1) Diffusion source localization. The source localization problem aims to find the source nodes of diffusion. Early works focus on designing localization algorithms based on graph theory and network science (Lappas etВ al., 2010; Shah and Zaman, 2011, 2012; Farajtabar etВ al., 2015; Zhu etВ al., 2016; Zhu and Ying, 2016; Zhu etВ al., 2017; Feizi etВ al., 2018; Zhu and Ying, 2014; Wang etВ al., 2017). These methods may not generalize well to various diffusion models. Later works propose data-driven methods that utilize graph neural networks to learn to identify sources from data (Dong etВ al., 2019; Wang etВ al., 2022; Ling etВ al., 2022). (2) Diffusion history reconstruction. Compared with source localization, diffusion history reconstruction is even harder because the search space of possible histories is larger. Existing methods for diffusion history reconstruction are exclusively based on the MLE formulation, including DHREC (Sefer and Kingsford, 2016), CRI (Chen etВ al., 2016), and SSR (Chen etВ al., 2019). These methods assume that true diffusion parameters (Sefer and Kingsford, 2016; Chen etВ al., 2019) and/or partial diffusion histories are known (Chen etВ al., 2019), or cannot reconstruct a complete diffusion history (Chen etВ al., 2016). Meanwhile, diffusion history reconstruction can be alternatively formulated as a time series imputation problem. State-of-the-art methods include BRITS (Cao etВ al., 2018) for multivariate time series, and GRIN (Cini etВ al., 2022) and SPIN (Marisca etВ al., 2022) for graph time series. They are all supervised and thus suffer from the scarcity of training data of real diffusion histories. Furthermore, since the true diffusion model is unknown for real-world diffusion, it is difficult to synthesize training data that follow the same distribution as the true diffusion model. Therefore, they have limited applicability in practice.

In this work, we have studied a challenging problem: reconstructing diffusion history from a single snapshot.To address the sensitivity of the MLE formulation,we have proposed a barycenter formulation that is provably stable against the estimation error of diffusion parameters. We have further developed an effective solver named DITTO for the barycenter formulation, which is based on MetropolisвҖ“Hastings MCMC with a learned optimal proposal. Our method is unsupervised, which is desirable in practice due to the scarcity of training data. Extensive experiments have shown that DITTO consistently achieve strong performance for both synthetic and real-world diffusion.
