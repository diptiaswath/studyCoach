Conformal prediction (see Shafer and Vovk (2008); Angelopoulos and Bates (2021) for excellent surveys) has seen a surge of activity in recent years. One large category of recent work has been the development of sophisticated non-conformity scores that yield compelling empirical coverage in various settings when paired with split conformal prediction (Papadopoulos etВ al., 2002; Lei etВ al., 2018). This includes Romano etВ al. (2019) who give a nonconformity score based on quantile regression, Angelopoulos etВ al. (2020) and Romano etВ al. (2020b) who give nonconformity scores designed for classification, and Hoff (2021) who gives a nonconformity score that leads to Bayes optimal coverage when data is drawn from the assumed prior distribution. This line of work is complementary to ours: we give algorithms that can be used as drop-in replacements for split conformal prediction, and can make use of any of these nonconformity scores.

Another line of work has focused on giving group conditional guarantees. Romano etВ al. (2020a) note the need for group-conditional guarantees with respect to demographic groups when fairness is a concern, and propose separately calibrating on each group in settings in which the groups are disjoint. FoygelВ Barber etВ al. (2020) consider the case of intersecting groups, and give an algorithm that separately calibrates on each group, and then uses the most conservative group-wise threshold when faced with examples that are members of multiple groups вҖ” the result is that this method systematically over-covers. The kind of вҖңmultivalidвҖқ prediction sets that we study here were first proposed by Jung etВ al. (2021) in the special case of prediction intervals: but the algorithm given by Jung etВ al. (2021), based on calibrating to moments of the label distribution and using ChebyshevвҖҷs inequality, also generally leads to over-coverage. Gupta etВ al. (2022) gave a theoretical derivation of an algorithm to obtain tight multivalid prediction intervals in the sequential adversarial setting, and Bastani etВ al. (2022) gave a practical algorithm to obtain tight multivalid coverage using prediction sets generated from any non-conformity score, also in the sequential adversarial setting. Although the sequential setting is more difficult in the sense that it makes no distributional assumptions, it also requires that labels be available after predictions are made at test time, in contrast to the batch setting that we study, in which labels are not available. Gupta etВ al. (2022) give an online-to-batch reduction that requires running the sequential algorithm on a large calibration set, saving the algorithmвҖҷs internal state at each iteration, and then deploying a randomized predictor that randomizes over the internal state of the algorithm across all rounds of training. This is generally impractical; in contrast we give a direct, simple, deterministic predictor in the batch setting.

Our algorithms can be viewed as learning quantile multiaccurate predictors and quantile multicalibrated predictors respectively вҖ” by analogy to multiaccuracy Kim etВ al. (2019) and multicalibration HГ©bert-Johnson etВ al. (2018b) which are defined with respect to means instead of quantiles. Their analysis is similar, but with pinball loss playing the role played by squared error in (mean) multicalibration. This requires analyzing the evolution of pinball loss under Lipschitzness assumptions on the underlying distribution, which is a complication that does not arise for means. More generally, our goals for obtaining group conditional guarantees for intersecting groups emerge from the literature on вҖңmultigroupвҖқ fairness вҖ” see e.g. (Kearns etВ al., 2018; HГ©bert-Johnson etВ al., 2018b; Kearns etВ al., 2019; Rothblum and Yona, 2021; Dwork etВ al., 2019; Globus-Harris etВ al., 2022)
