The question of how information spreads through networks has been studied in various research disciplines. In computer science, the so-called influence maximization (IM) paradigm has attracted a lot of attention in the last two decades. The IM problem can be stated as follows. Given a social network G=(V,E)𝐺𝑉𝐸G=(V,E) in which information spreads according to some probabilistic model, target a set of at most k𝑘k seed nodes S⊆V𝑆𝑉S\subseteq V in such a way that σ​(S)𝜎𝑆\sigma(S), the expect number of nodes that receive the information, is maximized [KKT15].

As online social networks play an essential role in how we acquire information nowadays and as access to information has an important impact on our lives, more recently researchers have started to study the IM framework in the presence of fairness concerns as well. Fairness may be understood w.r.t. individuals or communities, the former being the special case of the latter with singleton communities. Generally, in such works, for a community C⊆V𝐶𝑉C\subseteq V, one considers the average probability σC​(S)subscript𝜎𝐶𝑆\sigma_{C}(S) of nodes in C𝐶C to be reached from S𝑆S, also called the community coverage of C𝐶C. Then the concern is to choose S𝑆S in such a way that some fairness criteria on the communities is maximized. The probably most commonly used one is the maxmin (or maximin) criterion. In the most basic setting, studied, e.g., by Fish et al. [FBdb+19], the goal is to find a seed set S⊆V𝑆𝑉S\subseteq V of size at most k𝑘k such that the minimum probability that nodes are reached minv∈V⁡σv​(S)subscript𝑣𝑉subscript𝜎𝑣𝑆\min_{v\in V}\sigma_{v}(S) is maximized.

Several articles have been published in this scope and they have shown certain success in finding seeding strategies that lead to fairer outcomes. Nevertheless, they are all based on the assumption that the information spreading entity, i.e., the agent choosing S𝑆S, has an interest in spreading information in a fair way. This assumption is however rather unrealistic in the real word. Information spreading agents may be, and probably mostly are, purely efficiency-oriented and not particularly interested in choosing fair seeding strategies.

In this work we take a different approach to fairness. We do not rely on the good will of the information spreading entity, but instead modify the underlying social network in such a way as to make efficiency-oriented information spreading automatically fair. The modification of the network may be done by the network owner or any other entity interested in guaranteeing fairness. While different ways of modifying the network are perceivable, we choose the possibly most natural one – we improve the network’s connectivity by adding links. Here, we take the rather realistic approach to assume the information spreading entity to be indifferent rather than adversarial towards fairness.

We formalize this problem as follows. Given a social network G=(V,E)𝐺𝑉𝐸G=(V,E), we want to add at most b𝑏b non-edges F⊆E¯=V2∖E𝐹¯𝐸superscript𝑉2𝐸F\subseteq\bar{E}=V^{2}\setminus E to G𝐺G in such a way that the minimum community coverage is maximized when information is spread in G′=(V,E∪F)superscript𝐺′𝑉𝐸𝐹G^{\prime}=(V,E\cup F) using a purely efficiency oriented seeding strategy, i.e., a seed set S𝑆S of size k𝑘k that maximizes the spread in G′superscript𝐺′G^{\prime}, we measure this using the function σ​(⋅,F)𝜎⋅𝐹\sigma(\cdot,F). We call this the FIMALsubscriptFIMAL\operatorname{FIM}_{\operatorname{AL}} problem – fair influence maximization by adding links. We study the complexity of solving FIMALsubscriptFIMAL\operatorname{FIM}_{\operatorname{AL}} (Section 3) and provide plenty of evidence that solving FIMALsubscriptFIMAL\operatorname{FIM}_{\operatorname{AL}} is challenging, both exactly and approximately. Maybe most importantly, we show that it is unlikely to be able to find an α𝛼\alpha-approximation to the optimal solution, for any α∈(0,1]𝛼01\alpha\in(0,1], even when having access to an oracle that solves an NPNP\operatorname{NP}-complete problem. We thus turn to study a second problem (Section 4) that is possibly practically better motivated in the first place – the FIMALgsuperscriptsubscriptFIMALg\operatorname{FIM}_{\operatorname{AL}}^{\operatorname{g}} problem: Here instead of assuming that the efficiency oriented entity uses maximizing sets to spread information, we assume it to employ the greedy algorithm. This is a quite realistic assumption as the problem of finding a maximizing set is NPNP\operatorname{NP}-hard, while the greedy algorithm can be used in order to obtain a 1−1/e−ε11𝑒𝜀1-1/e-\varepsilon-approximation for any ε∈(0,1)𝜀01\varepsilon\in(0,1) w.h.p. in poly⁡(n,ε−1)poly𝑛superscript𝜀1\operatorname{poly}(n,\varepsilon^{-1}) time, i.e, polynomial time in n=|V|𝑛𝑉n=|V| and ε−1superscript𝜀1\varepsilon^{-1}. Even more, this approximation guarantee is essentially optimal [KKT15]. Multiple implementations of the greedy algorithm for IM exist (e.g., [TXS14, TSX15]) and they have been shown to be extremely efficient in practice. We observe that, in contrast to FIMALsubscriptFIMAL\operatorname{FIM}_{\operatorname{AL}}, the FIMALgsuperscriptsubscriptFIMALg\operatorname{FIM}_{\operatorname{AL}}^{\operatorname{g}} problem is polynomial time solvable when b𝑏b is a constant – exactly in the (unrealistic) case of deterministic instances and up to an arbitrarily small additive error in the probabilistic case. While this highlights the difference between the two problems, the proposed algorithm is essentially a brute-force algorithm and is thus not promising in practice. We complement the finding of this algorithm for the special case of constant k𝑘k with a lower bound showing that it is NPNP\operatorname{NP}-hard to provide any approximation algorithm. We then propose a set of algorithms for FIMALgsuperscriptsubscriptFIMALg\operatorname{FIM}_{\operatorname{AL}}^{\operatorname{g}} and evaluate them against each other in a first experiment in Section 5. We then take the best performing algorithm for FIMALgsuperscriptsubscriptFIMALg\operatorname{FIM}_{\operatorname{AL}}^{\operatorname{g}} and, in a second experiment, compare the resulting fairness (i.e., fairness achieved by the greedy algorithm after adding the proposed non-edges to the graph) with competitor algorithms that choose seeds as to optimize fairness. We observe that already after adding very few edges to graphs with thousands of nodes, the fairness achieved by our algorithm outperforms the fairness achieved by the fairness-tailored algorithms. Maybe surprisingly, this even holds for algorithms that optimize ex-ante fairness.

There is a rich set of related works in this area, so we are able to summarize only the results most related to our work. Fish et al. [FBdb+19] were the first to study the maximin criterion in influence maximization w.r.t. single nodes. Tsang et al. [TWR+19] study the maximin criterion w.r.t. groups. Becker et al. [BDGG22] also consider the maximin criterion for groups, but allow probabilistic seeding strategies. Stoica and Chaintreau [SC19] analyze the fairness achieved by standard algorithms for influence maximization.

There are several works in which the authors add links to the network, however they do so with a different objective. Both Castiglioni, Ferraioli,and Gatti [CFG20] and Corò, D’Angelo, and Velaj [CDV21] study the problem of adding edges to a graph in order to maximize the influence from a given seed set in different models of diffusion.Castiglioni et al. [CFG20] prove that, for the independent cascade model,it is NPNP\operatorname{NP}-hard to approximate the problem to within any constant factor. Corò et al. [CDV21] study the problem with the goal of adding a limited number of edges to a given set of seed nodes. They considered the linear threshold model and proposed a constant approximation algorithm.D’Angelo, Severini, and Velaj [DSV19] study the problem of adding a set of edges incident to a given seed set with the same aim. In a setting, where the cost of adding each edge is 1, the authors showed that it is NP-hard to approximate the problem within a factor better than 1−1/(2​e)112𝑒1-1/(2e), and they proposed an algorithm with an approximation factor of 1−1/e11𝑒1-1/e for the independent cascade model. We studied two optimization problems with the goal of adding links to a social network such as to make purely efficiency-oriented information spreading automatically fair. In the first problem FIMALsubscriptFIMAL\operatorname{FIM}_{\operatorname{AL}}, our goal is to add at most b𝑏b non-edges F𝐹F to the graph such that the minimum community coverage σC​(S,F)subscript𝜎𝐶𝑆𝐹\sigma_{C}(S,F) is maximized w.r.t. maximizing sets S𝑆S of size at most k𝑘k to spread information.We showed several hardness and hardness of approximation results for FIMALsubscriptFIMAL\operatorname{FIM}_{\operatorname{AL}}. Maybe most importantly, the decision version of FIMALsubscriptFIMAL\operatorname{FIM}_{\operatorname{AL}} is Σ2psuperscriptsubscriptΣ2𝑝\Sigma_{2}^{p}-hard even in the deterministic case and remains NPNP\operatorname{NP}-hard even if b=1𝑏1b=1 or k=1𝑘1k=1 (in the latter case even to approximate within any factor). We thus proposed to study a second optimization problem FIMALgsuperscriptsubscriptFIMALg\operatorname{FIM}_{\operatorname{AL}}^{\operatorname{g}} that entails to add at most b𝑏b non-edges F𝐹F to the graph such that the minimum expected community coverage is maximized when information is spread using the greedy algorithm for influence maximization. We then proposed several heuristics for FIMALgsuperscriptsubscriptFIMALg\operatorname{FIM}_{\operatorname{AL}}^{\operatorname{g}} and evaluated them in an experimental study. Lastly, we conducted an experiment showing that the greedy algorithm for IM achieves similar or even better levels of fairness than fairness-tailored algorithms already after adding a few edges proposed by our algorithm.
