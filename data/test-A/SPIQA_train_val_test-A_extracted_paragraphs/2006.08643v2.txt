Machine learning models are commonly trained with L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} regularization.This involves adding the term 12вҖӢО»вҖӢвҖ–ОёвҖ–2212рқңҶsuperscriptsubscriptnormрқңғ22\frac{1}{2}\lambda\|\theta\|_{2}^{2} to the loss function, where Оёрқңғ\theta is the vector of model parameters and О»рқңҶ\lambda is a hyperparameter.In some cases, the theoretical motivation for using this type of regularization is clear.For example, in the context of linear regression, L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} regularization increases the bias of the learned parameters while reducing their variance across instantiations of the training data; in other words, it is a manifestation of the bias-variance tradeoff.In statistical learning theory, a вҖңhardвҖқ variant of L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} regularization, in which one imposes the constraint вҖ–ОёвҖ–2вүӨПөsubscriptnormрқңғ2italic-Пө\|\theta\|_{2}\leq\epsilon, is often employed when deriving generalization bounds.

In deep learning, the use of L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} regularization is prevalent and often leads to improved performance in practical settings [Hinton, 1986], although the theoretical motivation for its use is less clear.Indeed, it well known that overparameterized models overfit far less than one may expect [Zhang etВ al., 2016], and so the classical bias-variance tradeoff picture does not apply [Neyshabur etВ al., 2017, Belkin etВ al., 2018, Geiger etВ al., 2020].There is growing understanding that this is caused, at least in part, by the (implicit) regularization properties of stochastic gradient descent (SGD) [Soudry etВ al., 2017].The goal of this paper is to improve our understanding of the role of L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} regularization in deep learning.

We study the role of L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} regularization when training over-parameterized deep networks, taken here to mean networks that can achieve training accuracy 1 when trained with SGD.Specifically, we consider the early stopping performance of a model, namely the maximum test accuracy a model achieves during training, as a function of the L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} parameter О»рқңҶ\lambda.We make the following observations based on the experimental results presented in the paper.1.The number of SGD steps until a model achieves maximum performance is tвҲ—вүҲcО»subscriptрқ‘Ўрқ‘җрқңҶt_{*}\approx\frac{c}{\lambda}, where cрқ‘җc is a coefficient that depends on the data, the architecture, and all other hyperparameters.We find that this relationship holds across a wide range of О»рқңҶ\lambda values.2.If we train with a fixed number of steps, model performance peaks at a certain value of the L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} parameter.However, if we train for a number of steps proportional to О»вҲ’1superscriptрқңҶ1\lambda^{-1} then performance improves with decreasing О»рқңҶ\lambda.In such a setup, performance becomes independent of О»рқңҶ\lambda for sufficiently small О»рқңҶ\lambda.Furthermore, performance with a small, non-zero О»рқңҶ\lambda is often better than performance without any L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} regularization.

Figure 1a shows the performance of an overparameterized network as a function of the L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} parameter О»рқңҶ\lambda.When the model is trained with a fixed steps budget, performance is maximized at one value of О»рқңҶ\lambda.However, when the training time is proportional to О»вҲ’1superscriptрқңҶ1\lambda^{-1}, performance improves and approaches a constant value as we decrease О»рқңҶ\lambda.

As we demonstrate in the experimental section, these observations hold for a variety of training setups which include different architectures, data sets, and optimization algorithms.In particular, when training with vanilla SGD (without momentum), we observe that the number of steps until maximum performance depends on the learning rate О·рқңӮ\eta and on О»рқңҶ\lambda as tвҲ—вүҲcвҖІО·вӢ…О»subscriptрқ‘Ўsuperscriptрқ‘җвҖІвӢ…рқңӮрқңҶt_{*}\approx\frac{c^{\prime}}{\eta\cdot\lambda}.The performance achieved after this many steps depends only weakly on the choice of learning rate.

We present two practical applications of these observations.First, we propose a simple way to predict the optimal value of the L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} parameter, based on a cheap measurement of the coefficient cрқ‘җc.Figure 1b compares the performance of models trained with our predicted L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} parameter with that of models trained with a tuned parameter.In this realistic setting, we find that our predicted parameter leads to performance that is within 0.4% of the tuned performance on CIFAR-10, at a cost that is marginally higher than a single training run.As shown below, we also find that the predicted parameter is consistently within an order of magnitude of the optimal, tuned value.

As a second application we propose AutoL2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2}, a dynamical schedule for the L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} parameter.The idea is that large L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} values achieve worse performance but also lead to faster training.Therefore, in order to speed up training one can start with a large L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} value and decay it during training (this is similar to the intuition behind learning rate schedules).In Figure 1c we compare the performance of a model trained with AutoL2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2}В against that of a tuned but constant L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} parameter, and find that AutoL2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2}В outperforms the tuned model both in speed and in performance.

Our empirical observations apply in the presence of learning rate schedules.In particular, Figure 1a shows that the test accuracy remains approximately the same if we scale the training time as 1/О»1рқңҶ1/\lambda.As to our applications, in section 3 we propose an algorithm for predicting the optimal L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} value in the presence of learning rate schedules, and the predicted value gives comparable performance to the tuned result.As to the AutoL2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2}В algorithm, we find that in the presence of learning rate schedules it does not perform as well as a tuned but constant L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} parameter. Finally, we turn to a theoretical investigation of the empirical observations made above.As a first attempt at explaining these effects, consider the following argument based on the loss landscape.For overparameterized networks, the Hessian spectrum evolves rapidly during training [Sagun etВ al., 2017, Gur-Ari etВ al., 2018, Ghorbani etВ al., 2019].After a small number of training steps with no L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} regularization, the minimum eigenvalue is found to be close to zero.In the presence of a small L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} term, we therefore expect that the minimal eigenvalue will be approximately О»рқңҶ\lambda.In quadratic optimization, the convergence time is inversely proportional to the smallest eigenvalue of the Hessian111In linear regression with L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} regularization, optimization is controlled by a linear kernel K=XTвҖӢX+О»вҖӢIрқҗҫsuperscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ‘Үрқ‘ӢрқңҶрқҗјK=X^{T}X+\lambda I, where Xрқ‘ӢX is the sample matrix and IрқҗјI is the identity matrix in parameter space.Optimization in each kernel eigendirection evolves as eвҲ’ОівҖӢtsuperscriptрқ‘’рқӣҫрқ‘Ўe^{-\gamma t} where Оірқӣҫ\gamma is the corresponding eigenvalue.When О»>0рқңҶ0\lambda>0 and the model is overparameterized, the lowest eigenvalue of the kernel will be typically close to О»рқңҶ\lambda, and therefore the time to convergence will be proportional to О»вҲ’1superscriptрқңҶ1\lambda^{-1}., see Ali etВ al. [2019] for a recent discussion.Based on this intuition, we may then expect that convergence time will be proportional to О»вҲ’1superscriptрқңҶ1\lambda^{-1}.The fact that performance is roughly constant for sufficiently small О»рқңҶ\lambda can then be explained if overfitting can be mostly attributed to optimization in the very low curvature directions[Rahaman etВ al., 2018, Wadia etВ al., 2020].Now, our empirical finding is that the time it takes the network to reach maximum accuracy is proportional to О»вҲ’1superscriptрқңҶ1\lambda^{-1}.In some cases this is the same as the convergence time, but in other cases (see for example Figure 4a) we find that performance decays after peaking and so convergence happens later.Therefore, the loss landscape-based explanation above is not sufficient to fully explain the effect.

To gain a better theoretical understanding, we consider the setup of an infinitely wide neural network trained using gradient flow.We focus on networks with positive-homogeneous activations, which include deep networks with ReLU activations, fully-connected or convolutional layers, and other common components.By analyzing the gradient flow update equations of such networks, we are able to show that the performance peaks at a time of order О»вҲ’1superscriptрқңҶ1\lambda^{-1} and deteriorates thereafter.This is in contrast to the performance of linear models with L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} regularization, where no such peak is evident.These results are consistent with our empirical observations, and may help shed light on the underlying causes of these effects.

According to known infinite width theory, in the absence of explicit regularization, the kernel that controls network training is constant [Jacot etВ al., 2018].Our analysis extends the known results on infinitely wide network optimization, and indicates that the kernel decays in a predictable way in the presence of L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} regularization.We hope that this analysis will shed further light on the observed performance gap between infinitely wide networks which are under good theoretical control, and the networks trained in practical settings [Arora etВ al., 2019, Novak etВ al., 2019, Wei etВ al., 2018, Lewkowycz etВ al., 2020].

L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} regularization in the presence of batch-normalization [Ioffe and Szegedy, 2015] has been studied in [van Laarhoven, 2017, Hoffer etВ al., 2018, Zhang etВ al., 2018].These papers discussed how the effect of L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} on scale invariant models is merely of having an effective learning rate (and no L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2}). In this work we consider the effect of L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} regularization on overparameterized networks.We make two empirical observations: (1) The time it takes the network to reach peak performance is proportional to О»рқңҶ\lambda, the L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} regularization parameter, and (2) the performance reached in this way is independent of О»рқңҶ\lambda when О»рқңҶ\lambda is not too large.We find that these observations hold for a variety of overparameterized training setups; see the SM for some examples where they do not hold. We expect the peak performance to depend on О»рқңҶ\lambda and О·рқңӮ\eta, but not on other quantities such as the initialization scale.We verify this empirically in SM F.

Motivated by these observations, we suggest two practical applications.The first is a simple method for predicting the optimal L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} parameter at a given training budget.The performance obtained using this prediction is close to that of a tuned L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} parameter, at a fraction of the training cost.The second is AutoL2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2}, an automatic L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} parameter schedule.In our experiments, this method leads to better performance and faster training when compared against training with a tuned L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} parameter.We find that these proposals work well when training with a constant learning rate; we leave an extension of these methods to networks trained with learning rate schedules to future work.

We attempt to understand the empirical observations by analyzing the training trajectory of infinitely wide networks trained with L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} regularization.We derive the differential equations governing this trajectory, and solve them explicitly for MSE loss.The solution reproduces the observation that the time to peak performance is of order О»вҲ’1superscriptрқңҶ1\lambda^{-1}.This is due to an effect that is specific to deep networks, and is not present in linear models: during training, the kernel (which is constant for linear models) decays exponentially due to the L2subscriptрқҗҝ2L_{2} term.
