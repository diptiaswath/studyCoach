HypergraphsВ [8] generalize graphs by allowing a hyperedge to consist of multiple nodes that capture higher-order relationships in complex systems and datasetsВ [36].В Hypergraphs have been used for music recommendation on Last.fm dataВ [10], news recommendationВ [29], sets of product reviews on AmazonВ [37], and sets of co-purchased products at WalmartВ [1].В Beyond the internet, hypergraphs are used for analyzing higher-order structure in neuronal, air-traffic and food networksВ [6, 30].

In order to explore and understand higher-order relationships in hypergraphs, recent work has made use of cut-cost functions that are defined by associating each hyperedge with a specific set function. These functions assign specific penalties of separating the nodes within individual hyperedges. They generalize the notion of hypergraph cuts and are crucial for determining small-scale community structureВ [30, 33]. The most popular cut-cost functions with increasing capability to model complex multiway relationships are the unit cut-costВ [28, 27, 23], cardinality-based cut-costВ [43, 44] and general submodular cut-costВ [31, 47].В An illustration of a hyperedge and the associated cut-cost function is given in Figure 1.В In the simplest setting, all cut-cost functions take value either 0 or 1 (e.g., the case when Оі1=Оі2=1subscriptрқӣҫ1subscriptрқӣҫ21\gamma_{1}=\gamma_{2}=1 in Figure 1(b)), we obtain a unit cut-cost hypergraph.В In a slightly more general setting, the cut-costs are determined solely by the number of nodes in either side of the hyperedge cut (e.g., the case when Оі1=1/2subscriptрқӣҫ112\gamma_{1}=1/2 and Оі2=1subscriptрқӣҫ21\gamma_{2}=1 in Figure 1(b)), we obtain a cardinality-based hypergraph.В We refer to hypergraphs associated with arbitrary submodular cut-cost functions (e.g., the case when Оі1=1/2subscriptрқӣҫ112\gamma_{1}=1/2 and 0вүӨОі2вүӨ10subscriptрқӣҫ210\leq\gamma_{2}\leq 1 in Figure 1(b)) as general submodular hypergraphs.

Hypergraphs that arise from data science applications consist of interesting small-scale local structure such as local communitiesВ [33, 42].В Exploiting this structure is central to the above mentioned applications on hypergraphs and related applications in machine learning and applied mathematicsВ [7].В We consider local hypergraph clustering as the task of finding a community-like cluster around a given set of seed nodes, where nodes in the cluster are densely connected to each other while relatively isolated to the rest of the graph.В One of the most powerful primitives for the local hypergraph clustering task is the graph diffusion.В Diffusion on a graph is the process of spreading a given initial mass from some seed node(s) to neighbor nodes using the edges of the graph. Graph diffusions have been successfully employed in the industry, for example, both Pinterest and Twitter use diffusion methods for their recommendation systemsВ [16, 17, 22]. Google uses diffusion methods to perform clustering query refinementsВ [40]. Let us not forget PageRankВ [9, 39], GoogleвҖҷs model for their search engine.

Empirical and theoretical performance of local diffusion methods is often measured on the problem of local hypergraph clusteringВ [45, 20, 33].В Existing local diffusion methods only directly apply to hypergraphs with the unit cut-costВ [26, 42]. For the slightly more general cardinality-based cut-cost, they rely on graph reduction techniques which result in a rather pessimistic edge-size-dependent approximation errorВ [46, 26, 33, 43].В Moreover, none of the existing methods is capable of processing general submodular cut-costs.В In this work, we are interested in designing a diffusion framework that (i) achieves stronger theoretical guarantees for the problem of local hypergraph clustering, (ii) is flexible enough to work with general submodular hypergraphs, and (iii) permits computationally efficient algorithms.В We propose the first local diffusion method that simultaneously accomplishes these goals.

In what follows we describe our main contributions and previous work.В In SectionВ 2 we provide preliminaries and notations.В In SectionВ 3 we introduce our diffusion model from a combinatorial flow perspective.В In SectionВ 4 we discuss the local hypergraph clustering problem and Cheeger-type quadratic approximation error.В In SectionВ 6 we perform experiments using both synthetic and real datasets.

In this work we propose a generic local diffusion model that applies to hypergraphs characterized by a rich class of cut-cost functions that covers many previously studied special cases, e.g., unit, cardinality-based and submodular cut-costs.В We provide the first edge-size-independent Cheeger-type approximation error for the problem of local hypergraph clustering using any of these cut-costs.В In particular, assume that there exists a cluster Cрқҗ¶C with conductance ОҰвҖӢ(C)ОҰрқҗ¶\Phi(C), and assume that we are given a set of seed nodes that reasonably overlaps with Cрқҗ¶C, then the proposed diffusion model can be used to find a cluster C^^рқҗ¶\hat{C} with conductance at most OвҖӢ(ОҰвҖӢ(C))рқ‘ӮОҰрқҗ¶O(\sqrt{\Phi(C)}) (in the appendix we show that an в„“psubscriptв„“рқ‘қ\ell_{p}-norm version of the proposed model can achieve OвҖӢ(ОҰвҖӢ(C))рқ‘ӮОҰрқҗ¶O(\Phi(C)) asymptotically).В Our hypergraph diffusion model is formulated as a convex optimization problem.В It has a natural combinatorial flow interpretation that generalizes the notion of network flows over hyperedges.В We show that the optimization problem can be solved efficiently by an alternating minimization method.В In addition, we prove that the number of nonzero nodes in the optimal solution is independent of the size of the hypergraph, and it only depends on the size of the initial mass.В This key property ensures that our algorithm scales well in practice for large datasets.В We evaluate our method using both synthetic and real-world data. We show that our method improves accuracy significantly for hypergraphs with unit, cardinarlity-based and general submodular cut-costs for local clustering.

Recently, clustering methods on hypergraphs received renewed interest.В Different methods require different assumptions about the hyperedge cut-cost, which can be roughly categorized into unit cut-cost, cardinality-based (and submodular) cut-cost and general submodular cut-cost.В Moreover, existing methods can be either global, where the output is not localized around a given set of seed nodes, or local, where the output is a tiny cluster around a set of seed nodes.В Local algorithms are the only scalable ones for large hypergraphs, which is our main focus.В Many works propose global methods and thus they are not scalable to large hypergraphs [49, 3, 25, 34, 6, 48, 11, 30, 31, 12, 47, 32, 13, 24, 42].В Local diffusion-based methods are more relevant to our workВ [26, 33, 43].В In particular, iterative hypergraph min-cut methods for the local hypergraph clustering problem can be adoptedВ [43].В However, these methods require in theory and in practice a large seed set, i.e., they are not expansive and thus cannot work with one seed node.В The expansive combinatorial diffusionВ [45] is generalized for hypergraphsВ [26], which can detect a target cluster using only one seed node.В However, combinatorial methods have a large bias towards low conductance clusters as opposed to finding the target clusterВ [18].В The most relevant paper to our work isВ [33].В However, the proposed methods in [33] depend on a reduction from hypergraphs to directed graphs.В This results in an approximation error for clustering that is proportional to the size of hyperedges and induces performance degeneration when the hyperedges are large.В In fact, none of the above approaches (including global and local ones) has an edge-size-independent approximation error bound for even simple cardinality-based hypergraphs.В Moreover, existing local approaches do not work for general submodular hypergraphs.

In this section we evaluate the performance of HFD for local clustering.В First, we carry out experiments on synthetic hypergraphs with varying target cluster conductances and varying hyperedge sizes.В For the unit cut-cost setting, we show that HFD is more robust and has better performance when the target cluster is noisy; for a cardinality-based cut-cost setting, we show that the edge-size-independent approximation guarantee is important for obtaining good recovery results.В Second, we carry out experiments using real-world data.В We show that HFD significantly outperforms existing state-of-the-art diffusion methods for both unit and cardinality-based cut-costs.В Moreover, we provide a compelling example where specialized submodular cut-cost is necessary for obtaining good results.В Code that reproduces all results is available at https://github.com/s-h-yang/HFD.
