Clustering problems are widely studied in Combinatorial Optimization literature due to their vast applications in Operational Research,Machine Learning, Data Science and Engineering[49, 39, 10, 6, 9, 31, 36, 51, 38, 11, 37, 3]. Typically a fixed number of centers must be placed in a metric space such that a set of clients is served the best possible way. The quality of a clustering solution is captured through the p𝑝p-norm of the vector consisting of the distance of each client to its closest center, for some p≥1𝑝1p\geq 1 or p=∞𝑝p=\infty. For example k𝑘k-median and k𝑘k-means assume p=1𝑝1p=1 and 222 respectively, while k𝑘k-center assumes p=∞𝑝p=\infty [39, 37, 3].

Today’s access on vast data (that may be frequently updated over time) has motivatedthe study ofclustering problems in case of time-evolving clients, which dynamically change positions over time [14, 19, 18, 4]. In time-evolving clustering problems, centers may also change position over time so as to better capture the clients’ trajectories. For example, a city may want to reallocate the units performing rapid tests for Covid-19 so as to better serve neighborhoods with more cases, the distribution of which may substantially change from day to day. Other interesting applicationsof dynamic clustering include viral marketing, epidemiology, facility location (e.g. schools, hospitals), conference planning etc. [43, 18, 40, 41, 48].

Our work is motivated by the fact that in most settings of interest, clients can move in fairly complicated and unpredictable ways, and thus, an a-priori knowledge on such trajectories is heavily under question (most of the previous work assumes perfect knowledge on clients’ positions over time [18, 4, 14, 19]). To capture this lack of information we cast clustering problems under the perspective of online learning [23]. We study an online learning problem called Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering in which a learner selects at each round t𝑡t, the positions of k𝑘k centers trying to minimize the connection cost of some clients, the positions of which are unknown to the learner prior to the selection of the centers.

Based on the past positions of the clients R1,R2,…,Rt−1subscript𝑅1subscript𝑅2…subscript𝑅𝑡1R_{1},R_{2},\ldots,R_{t-1} an online learning algorithm must select at each round t𝑡t, a set of k𝑘k centers Ft⊆Vsubscript𝐹𝑡𝑉F_{t}\subseteq V such that theconnection cost of the clients over time is close to the connection cost of the optimal (static) solution F∗superscript𝐹∗F^{\ast}. If the cost of the online learning algorithm is at most α𝛼\alpha times the cost of F∗superscript𝐹∗F^{\ast}, the algorithm is called α𝛼\alpha-regret, whereas in case α=1𝛼1\alpha=1, the algorithm is called no-regret [23]. Intuitively, a low-regret online learning algorithm converges to the optimal positions of the centers (with respect to the overall trajectories of the clients) by just observing the clients’ dynamics.

Efficient Online Learning for Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering.The existence of no-regret online learning algorithms for Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering immediately follows by standard results in online learning literature [23]. Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering is a special case of Learning from Expert Advice problem for which the famous Multiplicative Weights Update Algorithm achieves no-regret [23]. Unfortunately using the MWUMWU\mathrm{MWU} for Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering is not really an option due to the huge time and space complexity that MWUMWU\mathrm{MWU} requires. In particular MWUMWU\mathrm{MWU} keeps a different weight (probability) for each of the possible (|V|k)binomial𝑉𝑘{|V|}\choose{k} possible placements of the k𝑘k centers, rendering it inapplicable even for small values of |V|𝑉|V| and k𝑘k.

Our Contribution and Techniques.We first show that constant regret cannot be achieved in polynomial time for Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering. In particular we prove that any O​(1)𝑂1O(1)-regret polynomial-time online learning algorithm for Dynamic k𝑘k-Clusteringimplies the existence of an O​(1)𝑂1O(1)-approximation algorithm for the Minimum-p𝑝p-Union problem [12]. Recent works on the theory of computational complexity establish that unless well-established cryptographic conjectures fail, there is no O​(1)𝑂1O(1)-approximation algorithm for MinMin\mathrm{Min}-p𝑝p-UnionUnion\mathrm{Union} [12, 5, 13]. This result narrows the plausible regret bounds achievable in polynomial time, and reveals an interesting gap between Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering and its offline counterparts, which admit polynomial-time O​(1)𝑂1O(1)-approximation algorithms.

Our main technical contribution consists of polynomial-time online learning algorithms for Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering with non trivial regret bounds. We present a Θ​(k)Θ𝑘\Theta(k)-regret polynomial-time deterministic online learning algorithm and a Θ​(r)Θ𝑟\Theta(r)-regret polynomial-time randomized online learning algorithm,where r𝑟r is the maximum number of clientsappearing in a single round (r=max1≤t≤T⁡|Rt|𝑟subscript1𝑡𝑇subscript𝑅𝑡r=\max_{1\leq t\leq T}|R_{t}|). Combining these algorithms, one can achieve Θ​(min⁡(k,r))Θ𝑘𝑟\Theta\left(\min(k,r)\right)-regret for Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering, which (to the best of our knowledge) is the first guarantee on the regret achievable in polynomial time. The regret bounds above are independent of the selected p𝑝p-norm, and hold for any p≥1𝑝1p\geq 1 and for p=∞𝑝p=\infty.

At a technical level, our approach consists of two major steps. In the first step, we consider an online learning problem, that can be regarded as the fractional relaxation of the Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering (see Section 3), where the fractional connection cost is given by the optimal value of an appropriate convex program andthe action space of the learner is the |V|𝑉|V|-dimensional simplex. For this intermediate problem, we design a no-regret polynomial-time online learning algorithm through the use of the subgradients of the fractional connection cost. We show that such subgradient vectors can be computed in polynomial time via the solution ofthe dual program of the fractional connection cost. In the second step of our approach (see Section 4 and Section 5), we provide computationally efficient online (deterministic and randomized) rounding schemes converting a vector lying in the|V|𝑉|V|-dimensional simplex (the action space of Fractional Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering) into k𝑘k locations for the centers on the metric space V𝑉V (the action space of Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering).

In Section 4, we present a deterministic rounding scheme that, combined with the no-regret algorithm for Fractional Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering, leads to a Θ​(k)Θ𝑘\Theta(k)-regret polynomial-time deterministic online learning algorithm for the original Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering.Interestingly, this regret bound is approximately optimal for all deterministicalgorithms. In Section 5, we show thatcombining the no-regret algorithm for Fractional Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering with arandomized rounding scheme proposed in [11]111This randomized rounding scheme was part of a 444-approximation algorithm for k𝑘k-median [11] leads to a Θ​(r)Θ𝑟\Theta(r)-regret randomized algorithm running in polynomial time. Combining these two online learning algorithms, we obtain a Θ​(min⁡(k,r))Θ𝑘𝑟\Theta(\min(k,r))-regret polynomial-time online learning algorithm for Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering, which is the main technical contribution of this work. Finally, in Section 6, we present the results of an experimental evaluation, indicating that for client locations generated in a variety of natural and practically relevant ways, the realized regret of the proposed algorithms is way smaller than Θ​(min⁡(k,r))Θ𝑘𝑟\Theta\left(\min(k,r)\right).

Related Work.Our work relates with the research line of Combinatorial Online Learning. There exists a long line of research studying low-regret online learning algorithms for various combinatorial domains such thatonline routing [28, 7], selection of permutations [46, 50, 20, 2, 29], selection of binary search trees [47], submodular optimization [25, 32, 44], matrix completion [26],contextual bandits [1, 17] and many more. Finally, in combinatorial games agents need to learn to play optimally against each other over complex domains [30, 15].As in the case of Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering in all the above online learning problems, MWU is not an option, due to the exponential number of possible actions.

Another research direction of Combinatorial Online Learning studies black-box reductions converting polynomial time offline algorithm (full information on the data) into polynomial time online learning algorithms. [34] showed that any (offline) algorithm solving optimally and in polynomial time the objective function, that the Follow the Leader framework suggests, can be converted into a no-regret online learning algorithm. [33] extended the previous result for specific class of online learning problems called linear optimization problems for which they showed that any α𝛼\alpha-approximation (offline) can be converted into an α𝛼\alpha-regret online learning algorithm. They also provide a surprising counterexample showing that such black-box reductions do not hold for general combinatorial online learning problems. Both thetime efficiency and the regret bounds of the reductions of [34] and [33] were subsequently improved by [42, 45, 35, 8, 16, 27, 21, 22, 24]. We remark thatthe above results do not apply in our setting sinceDynamic k𝑘k-Clustering can neither be optimally solved in polynomial-time nor is a linear optimization problem.

Our works also relates with the more recent line of research studying clustering problems with time-evolving clients. [18] and [4] respectively provide Θ​(log⁡(n​T))Θ𝑛𝑇\Theta\left(\log(nT)\right) and O​(1)𝑂1O(1)-approximation algorithm for a generalization of the facility location problem in which clients change their positions over time. The first difference of Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering with this setting is thatin the former case there is no constraint on the number of centers that can open and furthermore, cruciallyperfect knowledge of the positions of the clients is presumed. More closely related to our work are [14, 19], where the special case of Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering on a line is studied (the clients move on a line over time). Despite the fact that both works study online algorithms, which do not require knowledge on the clients’ future positions, they only provided positive results for k=1𝑘1k=1 and 222.

This work studies polynomial-time low-regret online learning algorithms for Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering, an online learning problem capturing clustering settings with time-evolving clients for which no information on their locations over time is available. We show that, under some well-established conjectures, O​(1)𝑂1O(1)-regret cannot be achieved in polynomial time and we provide a Θ​(min⁡(k,r))Θ𝑘𝑟\Theta(\min(k,r))-regret polynomial time algorithm with r𝑟r being the maximum number of clients appearing in a single round. At a technical level, we present a two-step approach where in the first step we provide a no-regret algorithm for the Fractional Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering while in the second step we provide online rounding scheme converting the sequence of fractional solutions, produced by the no-regret algorithm, into solutions of Dynamic k𝑘k-Clustering. Applying the same approach to other combinatorial online learning problems is an interesting research direction.
