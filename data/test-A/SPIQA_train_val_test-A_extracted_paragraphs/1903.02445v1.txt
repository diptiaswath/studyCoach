A basic single-agent movement problem can be modeled by an agent (representing a robot or a person) that has an initial state (also called configuration), a description of valid transitions between states, and a task to accomplish. Common tasks are to reach some desired position or geographical location while avoiding unwelcome (mobile or static) obstacles, collecting or distributing a set of items, or rearranging the environment to be of a specific form. The agent itself might have various features or restrictions, which are reflected in the definition of states and transitions. Arguably, given that we handle physical objects, a basic requirement is that the agent must never intersect itself as well as other objects. When several agents are present (in a multi-agent movement problem), the coordination between them also plays a major roleВ (see [12] and the references within).Problems based on motion planning are ubiquitous in various aspects of modern life. In recent years, the study of such problems has gained increasing interest from both practical and theoretical points of viewsВ [32, 19, 34, 25, 35]. Unfortunately, the perspective of parameterized complexityвҖ”a central paradigm to design algorithms for computationally hard problemвҖ”has been largely overlooked in this context. In this paper, we present a comprehensive picture of the parameterized complexity of a single-agent movement problem called the Snake Game problem, whose formulation is inspired by a classic video game of the same name.

In the past decade, the study of the theory behind the computational complexity of puzzles (such as video games) has become very popularВ [21, 22, 33]. Such puzzles are often based on motion planning problems that can model tasks to be performed by agents in real-life scenarios. Moreover, their formulations are frequently simple enough to provide a clean abstraction of basic issues in this regard, therefore making them attractive for laying foundations for general analysis. For example, a very long line of works analyzed the complexity of various push-block puzzles (see [13] and the references within), where a box-shaped agent with the ability to push/pull other boxes should utilize its ability in order to reach one position from another. We remark that more often than not, studies of the theory behind the computational complexity of puzzles only assert the NP-hardness or PSPACE-completeness of the puzzle at hand.

The classic game Snake is among the most well-known video games that involve the motion of a single agent. The game dates back to 1978, and has enjoyed implementation across a wide range of platforms since then. Unlike most other video games, the popularity of Snake has hardly decreased despite its ageвҖ”indeed, new versions of Snake still appear to this day. We study the parameterized complexity of a variant of Snake that was introduced by Biasi and OpheldersВ [3], which models real-world problems of motion planning for agents of a вҖңsnake-likeвҖқ shape. Given a snake-like robot with an initial position and a final position in an environment (modeled by a graph), our objective is to determine whether the robot can reach the final position from the initial position without intersecting itself. Roughly speaking, the position of the robot is modeled by a simple ordered path in the graph, and one position Pрқ‘ғP is reachable (in one step) from another position PвҖІsuperscriptрқ‘ғвҖІP^{\prime} if the path Pрқ‘ғP is obtained from PвҖІsuperscriptрқ‘ғвҖІP^{\prime} by adding one vertex to the beginning of PвҖІsuperscriptрқ‘ғвҖІP^{\prime} and removing one vertex from its end. The (immobile) obstacles in the environment are implicitly encoded in the input graphвҖ”specifically, вҖңobstacle-freeвҖқ physical locations are represented by vertices, and edges indicate which locations are adjacent. Note that the graph might not be planar as in real-life scenarios the environment is often not merely a plane.

Nowadays, robots of a вҖңsnake-likeвҖқ shape are of substantial interestвҖ“in particular, they are built and used in practice for medical operationsВ [11, 20, 2] as well as various inspection and rescue missions on both land and waterВ [28, 36, 23]. A snake-like shape and serpentine locomotion offer immediate advantages for such purposes; the restricted area of mobility also makes the requirement of the robot to avoid intersecting itself and other obstacles a highly non-trivial issue that is mandatory to take into account.Moreover, the Snake Game problem is a natural abstraction to model a wide-variety of other scenarios, which range from the transportation of linked wagons towed by a locomotor at an airport or a supermarket to the movement of a group of agents that travel in an вҖңant-likeвҖқ fashion and the construction of trains in amusement parks.

Biasi and OpheldersВ [3] proved that the Snake Game problem is PSPACE-complete even on grid graphs (see SectionВ 2). Additionally, they considered the version aligned with the video game, where вҖңfoodвҖқ items are located on vertices. Here, the task is not to reach a pre-specified position, but to collect all food items by visiting their verticesвҖ”when a food item is collected, the size of the snake increases by a fixed integer gвүҘ1рқ‘”1g\geq 1. They showed that this version is NP-hard even on rectangular grid graphs without вҖңholesвҖқ, and PSPACE-complete even when there are only two food items, or the initial size of the snake is 111.

We present a comprehensive picture of the parameterized complexity of the Snake Game problem parameterized by the size of the snake, kрқ‘ҳk.111Definitions of basic notions in Parameterized Complexity can be found in Section 2. Arguably, the choice of this parameter is the most natural and sensible one because in real-life scenarios as those mentioned above, the size of the snake-like robot is likely to be substantially smaller than the size of the environment. To some extent, our paper can be considered as pioneering work in the study of the parameterized complexity of motion planning problems where intermediate configurations are of importance (which has so far been largely neglected, see the next subsection), and may lay the foundations for further research of this topic.

FPT Algorithm. Our contribution is threefold. Our main result is the proof that the Snake Game problem is fixed-parameter tractable (FPT) with respect to kрқ‘ҳk. Specifically, we develop an algorithm that solves Snake Game in time kрқ’ӘвҖӢ(k)вӢ…nрқ’ӘвҖӢ(1)вӢ…superscriptрқ‘ҳрқ’Әрқ‘ҳsuperscriptрқ‘ӣрқ’Ә1k^{\mathcal{O}(k)}\cdot n^{\mathcal{O}(1)} where nрқ‘ӣn is the number of vertices in the input graph. We remark that our algorithm can also output the length of the shortest вҖңrouteвҖқ from the initial position to the final position (if any such route exists) within the same time complexity. The design of our algorithm involves a novel application of the method of color-coding to sparsify the ((kвҲ’1)рқ‘ҳ1(k-1)-th power of the) configuration graph of the problem. Roughly speaking, the configuration graph is the directed graph whose vertices represent the positions of the snake in the environment, and where there is an arc from one vertex uрқ‘ўu to another vertex vрқ‘Јv if the position represented by vрқ‘Јv is reachable in one step from the position represented by uрқ‘ўu. Observe that the number of vertices of the configuration graph equals the number of (simple) ordered paths on kрқ‘ҳk vertices in the input graph, which can potentially be hugeвҖ”for example, if the input graph is a clique, then there are (nk)вҖӢk!binomialрқ‘ӣрқ‘ҳрқ‘ҳ{n\choose k}k! configurations.

We first present a handy characterization of the reachability of one configuration from another in tвүҘ1рқ‘Ў1t\geq 1 steps, based on which we elucidate the structure of certain triplets of configurations. Then, we perform several iterations where we color the vertices of the input graph based on the method of color-codingВ [1], but where order between some of the colors is of importance. Within each coloring iteration, we test for every pair of vertices in the input graph whether there exists a particular path on kрқ‘ҳk vertices between themвҖ”in which case we pick one such path. The collection of paths found throughout these iterations form the vertex set of our new configuration graph (in addition to the initial and final positions), while our characterization of reachability determines the arcs. In particular, this new configuration graph, unlike the original configuration graph, has only kрқ’ӘвҖӢ(k)вӢ…nрқ’ӘвҖӢ(1)вӢ…superscriptрқ‘ҳрқ’Әрқ‘ҳsuperscriptрқ‘ӣрқ’Ә1k^{\mathcal{O}(k)}\cdot n^{\mathcal{O}(1)} vertices. We prove that this new configuration graph is a valid вҖңsparsificationвҖқ of the (kвҲ’1)рқ‘ҳ1(k-1)-th power of the original configuration graphвҖ”specifically, for any tвүҘ1рқ‘Ў1t\geq 1, the initial position can reach the final position in the original configuration graph within (kвҲ’1)вҖӢtрқ‘ҳ1рқ‘Ў(k-1)t steps if and only if the initial position can reach the final position in the new configuration graph within tрқ‘Ўt steps. Clearly, this is insufficient because the initial position may reach the final position in the original configuration graph within a number of steps that is not a multiple of (kвҲ’1)рқ‘ҳ1(k-1)вҖ”however, we find that this technicality can be overcome by adding, to the new configuration graph, a small number of new vertices as well as arcs ingoing from these new vertices to the vertex representing the final position.

Kernelization. Our second result is the proof that the Snake Game problem is unlikely to admit a polynomial kernel even on grid graphs. For this purpose, we present a non-trivial cross-composition (defined in Section 2) from Hamiltonian Cycle on grid graphs to the Snake Game problem. Our construction is inspired by the proof of NP-hardness of the version of Snake Game on grid graphs without holes in the presence of food, given by Biasi and OpheldersВ [3]. Roughly speaking, given tрқ‘Ўt instances of Hamiltonian Cycle on grid graphs, our construction of an instance of Snake Game is as follows. We position the tрқ‘Ўt input grid graphs so that they are aligned to appear one after the other, and connect them as вҖңpendantsвҖқ from a long path LрқҗҝL placed just above them. The initial position of the snake is at the beginning of LрқҗҝL, and its final position is at a short path that вҖңprotrudesвҖқ from the beginning of LрқҗҝL. Further, the size of the snake is set to be nрқ‘ӣn, the number of vertices in each of the input grid graphs. Intuitively, we show that the snake can reach the final position from the initial position if and only if the snake can enter and exit one of the input grid graphs. In particular, to reach the final position, the snake has to find a place where it can вҖңturn aroundвҖқ, which can only be (potentially) done inside one of the input grid graphs. Specifically, entering and exiting one of the input grid graphs, say, Gisubscriptрқҗәрқ‘–G_{i}, would imply that at some point of time, the snake must be fully inside Gisubscriptрқҗәрқ‘–G_{i} and hence exhibit a Hamiltonian cycle within it.

Treewidth-Reduction. Our last result is a treewidth-reduction procedure for the Snake Game problem. More precisely, we develop a polynomial-time algorithm that given an instance of Snake Game, outputs an equivalent instance of Snake Game where the treewidth of the graph is bounded by a polynomial in kрқ‘ҳk. Our procedure is based on the irrelevant vertex techniqueВ [30]. First, we exploit the relatively recent breakthrough result by Chekuri and ChuzhoyВ [7] that states that, for any positive integer tвҲҲв„•рқ‘Ўв„•t\in\mathbb{N}, any graph whose treewidth is at least dвӢ…tcвӢ…рқ‘‘superscriptрқ‘Ўрқ‘җd\cdot t^{c} (for some fixed constants cрқ‘җc and dрқ‘‘d)222Currently, the best known bound on cрқ‘җc is 101010, given by Chuzhoy in [8]. has a tГ—tрқ‘Ўрқ‘Ўt\times t-grid as a minor, and hence also a so called tрқ‘Ўt-wall as a subgraph (see Section 5). We utilize this result to argue that if the treewidth of our input graph is too large, then it has a cвҖӢkрқ‘җрқ‘ҳck-wall as a subgraph (for some fixed constant cрқ‘җc) such that no vertex of this cвҖӢkрқ‘җрқ‘ҳck-wall belongs to the initial or final positions of the snake. Thus, as long as we do not yet have a graph of small treewidth at hand, we can efficiently find an edge to contract, and eventually obtain a graph of small treewidth.

To the best of our knowledge, close to nothing is known on the parameterized complexity of problems of planning motion of agents where the motion plays an actual role. By this comment, we mean that the problem does not only have an initial state and a final state, and intermediate states (with transitions between them) are not defined/immaterial. When only an initial state and a final state are present, the problem is actually a static problem and the term movement refers to distances rather than motion. For example, a static version in this spirit of the Snake Game problem (that neglects intermediate states and hence does not enforce the requirement that the snake should never intersect itself) would just ask whether the vertex on which lies the tail of the snake in the final position is reachable from the vertex on which lies the head of the snake in the initial position, which can be directly solved in linear time using BFS. We also remark that almost all problems studied from the viewpoint of Parameterized Complexity are NP-complete rather than PSPACE-hard (with several notable exceptions such asВ [26, 10]).

While there is a huge line of works that explore the classical complexity and approximability of problems of planning motion of agents (see the beginning of the introduction), the only work in Parameterized Complexity that we are aware of is by Cesati and Wereham [6]. Roughly speaking, the input of the problem in [6] consists of a robot and obstacles that are each represented by a set of polyhedrons. Specifically, the input includes a set Oрқ‘ӮO of polyhedrons (the obstacles), as well as a set Pрқ‘ғP of polyhedrons (the robot) that are freely linked together at a set of linkage vertices Vрқ‘үV such that Pрқ‘ғP has kрқ‘ҳk degrees of freedom of movement. The objective is to decide whether a given final position of the robot is reachable from a given initial position of the robot where the robot is allowed to intersect neither itself nor the obstacles. ReifВ [29] proved that this problem (in three-dimensional Euclidean space) is PSPACE-hard, and Cesati and Wereham [6] adapted ReifвҖҷs proof and showed that the problem is W[SAT]-hard when parameterized by kрқ‘ҳk and hence unlikely to be FPT.

Lastly, when intermediate states are immaterial, it is less clear which problem is considered to be centered around motion rather than euclidean/graph distances and static constraintsвҖ”for example, in such scenarios finding a shortest path or a path with specific restrictions in a given graph may be highly relevant to motion planning (see e.g.В [17]), but is not centered around the motion itself in the sense above. In this context, it is noteworthy to mention the comprehensive work in Parameterized Complexity by Demaine, Hajiaghayi and Marx [14]. Roughly speaking, the authors addressed multi-agent problems on graphs where the agents have types, there is an initial position (being a single vertex) for each agent, and a desired configuration that all agents should form (e.g., every agent of type вҖңclientвҖқ should have an agent of type вҖңfacilityвҖқ nearby). Under some restrictions (e.g., some agents may move only a short distance), the objective is to make the minimum amount of movement so that afterwards the agents will form the desired configuration. Clearly, movement is a central component of this problem, but in a static senseвҖ”the intermediate configurations of the agents are immaterial (while moving to form the desired configuration, the agents can be in any configuration that they wish to, such as being placed all together on the same vertex, or being as far apart as possible). Demaine, Hajiaghayi and Marx [14] presented, among other results, a dichotomy that concerns the fixed-parameter tractability of problems of this form parameterized by the number of agents (more precisely, of agents of a certain type) and another parameter related to the desired configuration.

In this section, we prove that given an instance of Snake Game, we can get an equivalent instance of Snake Game where the treewidth of the graph is bounded by a polynomial in the size of the snake. Our proof is based on a theorem that, for a given undirected graph GрқҗәG and kвҲҲв„•рқ‘ҳв„•k\in\mathbb{N}, asserts the following statement. We can, in time рқ’ӘвҖӢ(|VвҖӢ(G)|+|EвҖӢ(G)|)рқ’Әрқ‘үрқҗәрқҗёрқҗә\mathcal{O}(|V(G)|+|E(G)|), either find a specific вҖңpatternвҖқ in the graph called a wall, or determine that GрқҗәG has treewidth kрқ’ӘвҖӢ(1)superscriptрқ‘ҳрқ’Ә1k^{\mathcal{O}(1)}. Given an instance вҹЁG,k,рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ,рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үвҹ©рқҗәрқ‘ҳрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Ү\langle G,k,\mathsf{init},\mathsf{fin}\rangle of Snake Game, we show how we use that pattern in GрқҗәG in order to decrease the number of vertices in GрқҗәG, yet retain an equivalent instance of Snake Game. Specifically, we utilize rerouting arguments to find a so called irrelevant edge (with respect to contraction), inspired by the classic work of Robertson and Seymour [31]. Because either way we obtain an exploitable structure (a wall or small treewidth), this method is known as вҖңwin/win approachвҖқ [9]. By applying this method repeatedly, we derive in polynomial time an equivalent instance of Snake Game, вҹЁGвҖІ,k,рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ,рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үвҹ©superscriptрқҗәвҖІрқ‘ҳрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Ү\langle G^{\prime},k,\mathsf{init},\mathsf{fin}\rangle, such that GвҖІsuperscriptрқҗәвҖІG^{\prime} has treewidth kрқ’ӘвҖӢ(1)superscriptрқ‘ҳрқ’Ә1k^{\mathcal{O}(1)}.

First, we present the formal definition of the specific pattern we are looking for (see Figure 5).

We would like to exploit the notion of an rрқ‘ҹr-wall to present our reduction. To this end, we say that a graph GрқҗәG contains an rрқ‘ҹr-wall if there exists a subgraph Hрқҗ»H of GрқҗәG such that Hрқҗ»H is an rрқ‘ҹr-wall.Now, we aim to show that, given an instance вҹЁG,k,рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ,рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үвҹ©рқҗәрқ‘ҳрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Ү\langle G,k,\mathsf{init},\mathsf{fin}\rangle of Snake Game where GрқҗәG contains a 3вҖӢk3рқ‘ҳ3\sqrt{k}-wall, we can efficiently decrease the number of vertices in GрқҗәG and still retain an equivalent instance of Snake Game. In particular, we consider a 3вҖӢk3рқ‘ҳ3\sqrt{k}-wall because of its following special property. For any two vertices sрқ‘ s and tрқ‘Ўt in a 3вҖӢk3рқ‘ҳ3\sqrt{k}-wall Hрқҗ»H, there exists a simple path (or simple cycle if s=tрқ‘ рқ‘Ўs=t) in Hрқҗ»H that starts with sрқ‘ s and ends with tрқ‘Ўt whose size is at least kрқ‘ҳk. Moreover, as it follows from the next observation, this property is preserved even if we contract any edge in Hрқҗ»H.

Next, we consider the case where we have two configurations рқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1subscriptрқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1\mathsf{conf}_{1} and рқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ2subscriptрқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ2\mathsf{conf}_{2}, and a (simple) path of size at least kрқ‘ҳk between the head of рқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1subscriptрқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1\mathsf{conf}_{1} and the tail of рқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ2subscriptрқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ2\mathsf{conf}_{2}. In addition, we suppose that the path does not вҖңintersectвҖқ рқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1subscriptрқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1\mathsf{conf}_{1} and рқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ2subscriptрқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ2\mathsf{conf}_{2} besides at these two vertices. We prove (in Lemma 5.1) that, in this case, рқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ2subscriptрқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ2\mathsf{conf}_{2} must be reachable from рқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1subscriptрқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1\mathsf{conf}_{1}. Intuitively, the snake can move from рқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1subscriptрқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1\mathsf{conf}_{1} through the path and thereby it can reach рқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ2subscriptрқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ2\mathsf{conf}_{2}.To this end, we start with a simple observation.In this observation, we consider the case where we have a configuration рқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1subscriptрқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1\mathsf{conf}_{1} and a (simple) path Pрқ‘ғP of size at least kрқ‘ҳk that starts at the head of рқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1subscriptрқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1\mathsf{conf}_{1}, ends at a vertex tрқ‘Ўt, and the remainder of Pрқ‘ғP and рқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1subscriptрқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1\mathsf{conf}_{1} do not intersect. Informally, our observation states that in this case, by making the snake traverse Pрқ‘ғP, we get that рқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1subscriptрқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1\mathsf{conf}_{1} can reach some configuration рқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ2subscriptрқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ2\mathsf{conf}_{2} where tрқ‘Ўt is the head, and the rest of the vertices of the snake is located on Pрқ‘ғP.Moreover, if Pрқ‘ғP is of size exactly kрқ‘ҳk, then it corresponds to a configuration that is reachable from рқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1subscriptрқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1\mathsf{conf}_{1}. Notice that in this case, the path is reachable from рқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1subscriptрқ–јрқ—Ҳрқ—Үрқ–ҝ1\mathsf{conf}_{1} in a вҖңreversedвҖқ order of vertices, as the first vertex of the path вҖңbecomesвҖқ the tail of the snake when the snake traverses the path.

Then, by the definition of рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣ\mathsf{TrRedAlgIter}, the algorithm from Proposition 5.1 reported that GрқҗәG has treewidth kрқ’ӘвҖӢ(1)superscriptрқ‘ҳрқ’Ә1k^{\mathcal{O}(1)}. Therefore, by the correctness of Proposition 5.1, it follows that GрқҗәG indeed has treewidth kрқ’ӘвҖӢ(1)superscriptрқ‘ҳрқ’Ә1k^{\mathcal{O}(1)}.

Now, observe that if рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣ\mathsf{TrRedAlgIter} does not report that GрқҗәG has treewidth kрқ’ӘвҖӢ(1)superscriptрқ‘ҳрқ’Ә1k^{\mathcal{O}(1)}, then it returns the instance вҹЁG/e,k,рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ,рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үвҹ©рқҗәрқ‘’рқ‘ҳрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Ү\langle G/e,k,\mathsf{init},\mathsf{fin}\rangle. Then, by the definition of рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣ\mathsf{TrRedAlgIter} and the correctness of Proposition 5.1 and Observation 5.3, HвҖІsuperscriptрқҗ»вҖІH^{\prime} is indeed a 3вҖӢk3рқ‘ҳ3\sqrt{k}-wall in GрқҗәG having vertices from neither рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ\mathsf{init} nor рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Ү\mathsf{fin}. Therefore, by Lemma 5.4, вҹЁG,k,рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ,рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үвҹ©рқҗәрқ‘ҳрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Ү\langle G,k,\mathsf{init},\mathsf{fin}\rangle is a Yes-instance if and only if вҹЁG/e,k,рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ,рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үвҹ©рқҗәрқ‘’рқ‘ҳрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Ү\langle G/e,k,\mathsf{init},\mathsf{fin}\rangle is a Yes-instance. Thus, вҹЁG/e,k,рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ,рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үвҹ©)\langle G/e,k,\mathsf{init},\mathsf{fin}\rangle) is equivalent to вҹЁG,k,рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ,рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үвҹ©рқҗәрқ‘ҳрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Ү\langle G,k,\mathsf{init},\mathsf{fin}\rangle.вҲҺ

Now, we argue that рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣ\mathsf{TrRedAlgIter} run in time polynomial in |VвҖӢ(G)|рқ‘үрқҗә|V(G)|.

The algorithm from Proposition 5.1 runs in time рқ’ӘвҖӢ(|VвҖӢ(G)|+|EвҖӢ(G)|)рқ’Әрқ‘үрқҗәрқҗёрқҗә\mathcal{O}(|V(G)|+|E(G)|). Given a 7вҖӢk7рқ‘ҳ7k-wall, by Observation 5.3, a 3вҖӢk3рқ‘ҳ3\sqrt{k}-wall HвҖІsuperscriptрқҗ»вҖІH^{\prime} having vertices from neither рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ\mathsf{init} nor рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Ү\mathsf{fin} can be found in time polynomial in kвүӨ|VвҖӢ(G)|рқ‘ҳрқ‘үрқҗәk\leq|V(G)|. The other steps of рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣ\mathsf{TrRedAlgIter} can be implemented in рқ’ӘвҖӢ(1)рқ’Ә1\mathcal{O}(1) time. As |EвҖӢ(G)|=рқ’ӘвҖӢ(|VвҖӢ(G)|2)рқҗёрқҗәрқ’Әsuperscriptрқ‘үрқҗә2|E(G)|=\mathcal{O}(|V(G)|^{2}), the observation follows.вҲҺ

Now, we present our reduction algorithm рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җ\mathsf{TrRedAlg} (see Algorithm 4).We denote the outputs of the algorithms рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣ\mathsf{TrRedAlgIter} and рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җ\mathsf{TrRedAlg} for an instance IрқҗјI by рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—ӢвҖӢ(I)рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣрқҗј\mathsf{TrRedAlgIter}(I) and рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—ҖвҖӢ(I)рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқҗј\mathsf{TrRedAlg}(I) respectively.

Now, we show that рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җ\mathsf{TrRedAlg} runs in time polynomial in |VвҖӢ(G)|рқ‘үрқҗә|V(G)|.

Consider an input instance вҹЁG,k,рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ,рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үвҹ©рқҗәрқ‘ҳрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Ү\langle G,k,\mathsf{init},\mathsf{fin}\rangle. At each iteration tвүҘ2рқ‘Ў2t\geq 2, рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җ\mathsf{TrRedAlg} calls рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣ\mathsf{TrRedAlgIter} with an instance вҹЁGt,k,рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ,рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үвҹ©superscriptрқҗәрқ‘Ўрқ‘ҳрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Ү\langle G^{t},k,\mathsf{init},\mathsf{fin}\rangle such that |VвҖӢ(Gt)|=|VвҖӢ(GtвҲ’1)|вҲ’1рқ‘үsuperscriptрқҗәрқ‘Ўрқ‘үsuperscriptрқҗәрқ‘Ў11|V(G^{t})|=|V(G^{t-1})|-1. By Lemma 5.4, рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣ\mathsf{TrRedAlgIter} works in time polynomial in |VвҖӢ(Gt)|рқ‘үsuperscriptрқҗәрқ‘Ў|V(G^{t})|. Therefore, as G1=Gsuperscriptрқҗә1рқҗәG^{1}=G, there are at most |VвҖӢ(G)|рқ‘үрқҗә|V(G)| iterations. Thus, we conclude that рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җ\mathsf{TrRedAlg} runs in time рқ’ӘвҖӢ(|VвҖӢ(G)|3)рқ’Әsuperscriptрқ‘үрқҗә3\mathcal{O}(|V(G)|^{3}).вҲҺ

Now, we show that for a given instance of Snake Game вҹЁG,k,рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ,рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үвҹ©рқҗәрқ‘ҳрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Ү\langle G,k,\mathsf{init},\mathsf{fin}\rangle, рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җ\mathsf{TrRedAlg} (вҹЁG,k,рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ,рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үвҹ©)=вҹЁGвҖІ,k,рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ,рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үвҹ©рқҗәрқ‘ҳрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—ҮsuperscriptрқҗәвҖІрқ‘ҳрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Ү(\langle G,k,\mathsf{init},\mathsf{fin}\rangle)=\langle G^{\prime},k,\mathsf{init},\mathsf{fin}\rangle is an equivalent to вҹЁG,k,рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ,рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үвҹ©рқҗәрқ‘ҳрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Ү\langle G,k,\mathsf{init},\mathsf{fin}\rangle, and also GвҖІsuperscriptрқҗәвҖІG^{\prime} has treewidth kрқ’ӘвҖӢ(1)superscriptрқ‘ҳрқ’Ә1k^{\mathcal{O}(1)}.

By Lemma 5.5, at each iteration of рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җ\mathsf{TrRedAlg}, рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–Ёрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ӣ\mathsf{TrRedAlgIter} returns an instance of Snake Game, that is equivalent to the previous one. Therefore, as the first instance is вҹЁG,k,рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ,рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үвҹ©рқҗәрқ‘ҳрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Ү\langle G,k,\mathsf{init},\mathsf{fin}\rangle, the output of рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җ\mathsf{TrRedAlg} denoted by вҹЁGвҖІ,k,рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ,рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үвҹ©superscriptрқҗәвҖІрқ‘ҳрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Ү\langle G^{\prime},k,\mathsf{init},\mathsf{fin}\rangle, is equivalent to вҹЁG,k,рқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қ,рқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Үвҹ©рқҗәрқ‘ҳрқ—Ӯрқ—Үрқ—Ӯрқ—Қрқ–ҝрқ—Ӯрқ—Ү\langle G,k,\mathsf{init},\mathsf{fin}\rangle. By the definition of рқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җрқ–ірқ—Ӣрқ–ұрқ–ҫрқ–Ҫрқ– рқ—…рқ—Җ\mathsf{TrRedAlg} and Lemma 5.5, it directly follows that GвҖІsuperscriptрқҗәвҖІG^{\prime} has treewidth kрқ’ӘвҖӢ(1)superscriptрқ‘ҳрқ’Ә1k^{\mathcal{O}(1)}.вҲҺ

Finally, we combine the last two lemmas to conclude the correctness of our algorithm of the reduction.

Observe that, a graph GрқҗәG on nрқ‘ӣn vertices and with treewidth рқҡқрқҡ рқҡқрқҡ \mathtt{tw} has рқ’ӘвҖӢ(рқҡқрқҡ 2вҖӢn)рқ’Әsuperscriptрқҡқрқҡ 2рқ‘ӣ\mathcal{O}(\mathtt{tw}^{2}n) edges as the tree decomposition of GрқҗәG can have at most nрқ‘ӣn bags and every bag contributes at most рқҡқрқҡ 2superscriptрқҡқрқҡ 2\mathtt{tw}^{2} edges. Thus, the following corollary follows directly from the TheoremВ 5.1.

In this paper, we presented a coherent picture of the parameterized complexity of the Snake Game problem. As our main contribution, we proved that Snake Game is FPT. This result was complemented by the proofs that Snake Game is unlikely to admit a polynomial kernel even on grid graphs, but admits a treewidth-reduction procedure. For our main contribution, we presented a novel application of the method of color-coding by utilizing it to sparsify the configuration graph of the problem. To some extent, our paper can be considered as pioneering work in the study of the parameterized complexity of motion planning problems where intermediate configurations are of importance (which has so far been largely neglected), and may lay the foundations for further research of this topic. In this regard, the choice of Snake Game was a natural starting point given that it involves only one mobile object as well as captures only the most basic property of a physical objectвҖ”it cannot intersect itself and other obstacles.

We conclude the paper with a few directions for further research. As a first step to broaden the scope of this kind of research, we suggest to consider the parameterized complexity of motion planning problems that involve several mobile agents. Possibly, not all mobile agents can be controlledвҖ”that is, the patterns of motion of some of them may be predefined. The controllable mobile agents themselves can have either a joint objective or independent tasks. Arguably, the most basic requirement of the mobile agents is to avoid collision with one another while they perform these tasks. We remark that a natural parameter in this regard is the number of mobile agents. Second, apart (or in addition to) the study of multiple agents, we also find it interesting to consider other types of motion as well as other shapes of robots. Moreover, one can study objectives that are more complicated than merely reaching one position from another, particularly objectives where the mobile agent can effect the environment rather than only move within it. Lastly, we also suggest to consider the study of the parameterized complexity of motion planning problems in geometric settings in 3DвҖ”while graphs provide a useful abstraction, some problems may require a more accurate representation of the robots and physical environment.
