Many important decisions today are made by machine learning algorithms. These range from showing advertisements to customersВ [59, 31], to awarding home loansВ [47, 56], to predicting recidivismВ [10, 32, 27]. It is important to ensure that such algorithms are fair and are not biased towards or against some specific groups in the population. A considerable amount of workВ [46, 68, 25, 45, 20, 67, 66] addressing this issue has emerged in the recent years.

Our paper considers fair algorithms for clustering. Clustering is a fundamental unsupervised learning problem where one wants to partition a given data-set.In machine learning, clustering is often used for feature generation and enhancement as well. It is thus important to consider the bias and unfairness issues when inspecting the quality of clusters.The question of fairness in clustering was first asked in the beautiful paper of ChierichettiВ et al.В [25] with subsequent generalizations by RГ¶sner and SchmidtВ [60].

In this paper, we give a much more generalized and tunable notion of fairness in clustering than that inВ [25, 60].Our main result is that any solution for a wide suite of vanilla clustering objectives can be transformed into fair solutions in our notion with only a slight loss in quality by a simple algorithm. 

Many works in fairnessВ [20, 25, 60, 19] work within the disparate impact (DI) doctrineВ [36]. Broadly speaking, the doctrine posits that any вҖңprotected classвҖқ must have approximately equal representation in the decisions taken (by an algorithm).Although the DI doctrine is a lawВ [1, 35] in the United States, violating the DI doctrine is by itself not illegalВ [5]; it is illegal only if the violation cannot be justified by the decision maker.In the clustering setting, this translates to the following algorithmic question : what is the loss in quality of the clustering when all protected classes are required to have approximately equal representation in the clusters returned?

Motivated thus, ChierichettiВ et al.В [25], and later RГ¶sner and SchmidtВ [60], model the set of points as partitioned into в„“в„“\ell colors, and the color proportion of each returned cluster should be similar to that in the original data. There are three shortcomings of these papers: (a) the fairness constraint was too stringent and brittle, (b) good algorithms were given only for the kрқ‘ҳk-center objective, and (c) the color classes werenвҖҷt allowed to overlap. We remark that the last restriction is limiting since an individual can lie in multiple protected classes (consider an African-American senior woman). In our work we address all these concerns: we allow the user to specify the fairness constraints, we give simple algorithms with provable theoretical guarantees for a large suite of objective functions, and we allow overlapping protected classes.

Our fairness notion.We propose a model which extends the model ofВ [25] to have в„“вүҘ2в„“2\ell\geq 2 groups of people which are allowed to overlap.For each group iрқ‘–i, we have two parameters ОІi,ОұiвҲҲ[0,1]subscriptрқӣҪрқ‘–subscriptрқӣјрқ‘–01\beta_{i},\alpha_{i}\in[0,1].Motivated by the DI doctrine, we deem a clustering solution fair if each cluster satisfies two properties: (a) restricted dominance (RD), which asserts that the fraction of people from group iрқ‘–i in any cluster is at most Оұisubscriptрқӣјрқ‘–\alpha_{i}, and (b) minority protection (MP), which asserts that the fraction of people from group iрқ‘–i in any cluster is at least ОІisubscriptрқӣҪрқ‘–\beta_{i}.Note that we allow ОІi,ОұisubscriptрқӣҪрқ‘–subscriptрқӣјрқ‘–\beta_{i},\alpha_{i}вҖҷs to be arbitrary parameters, and furthermore, they can differ across different groups.This allows our model to provide a lot of flexibility to users. For instance, our model easily capturesthe notions defined byВ [25] andВ [60].

We allow our protected groups to overlap. Nevertheless, the quality of our solutions depend on the amount of overlap. We define О”О”\Delta(similar toВ [20]) to be the maximum number of groups a single individual can be a part of. This parameter, as we argued above, is usually not 111, but can be assumed to be a small constant depending on the application.

Our results.Despite the generality of our model, we show that in a black-box fashion, we can get fair algorithms for any в„“psubscriptв„“рқ‘қ\ell_{p}-norm objective (this includes, kрқ‘ҳk-center, kрқ‘ҳk-median, and the widely used kрқ‘ҳk-means objective) if we allow for very small additive violations to the fairness constraint.We show that given any ПҒрқңҢ\rho-approximation algorithm рқ’ңрқ’ң{\cal A} for a given objective which could be returning widely unfair clusters, we can return a solution which is a (ПҒ+2)рқңҢ2(\rho+2)-approximation to the best clustering which satisfies the fairness constraints (TheoremВ 1). Our solution, however, can violate both the RD and MP property additively by 4вҖӢО”+34О”34\Delta+3. This is negligible if the clusters are large, and our empirical results show this almost never exceeds 333. Further in our experiments, our cost is at most 15% more than optimum, which is a much better factor compared to (ПҒ+2)рқңҢ2(\rho+2).

The black-box feature of our result is useful also in comparing the performance of any particular algorithm рқ’ңрқ’ң{\cal A}. This helps if one wishes to justify the property of an algorithm one might be already using. Our results can be interpreted to give a way to convert any clustering algorithm to its fair version. Indeed, our method is very simple вҖ“ we use the solution returned by рқ’ңрқ’ң{\cal A} to define a fair assignment problem and show that this problem has a good optimal solution. The fair assignment problem is then solved via iterative rounding which leads to the small additive violations. In the case of О”=1О”1\Delta=1 (disjoint groups), we can get a simpler, one-iteration rounding algorithm.

To be more precise, for any dataset we set О»рқңҶ\lambda according to the maximum violation of our algorithm reported in table 2 for Оҙ=0.2рқӣҝ0.2\delta=0.2 (e.g. О»рқңҶ\lambda is 1.541.541.54 for bankВ , 1.081.081.08 for censusВ , and 2.292.292.29 for creditcardВ ). Then, we solve the almost fair LP for that О»рқңҶ\lambda and compare its cost with our algorithmвҖҷs cost over that dataset.

Since solving the almost fair LP on the whole data is infeasible (in terms of running time), we sub-sample bankВ , censusВ , and creditcardВ to 1000, 600 and 600 points respectively, and report the average costs over 10 trials. Also, we only consider one sensitive attribute, namely marital for bankВ , sex for censusВ and education for creditcardВ to further simplify the LP and decrease the running time. fig.В 3 shows that the cost of our algorithm is very close to the almost fair LP cost (at most 15% more). Note that, since the cost of almost fair LP is a lower bound on the cost of Fair (k,p)рқ‘ҳрқ‘қ(k,p)-Clustering problem, we conclude that our cost is at most 15% more than optimum in practice, which is much better than the proved (ПҒ+2)рқңҢ2(\rho+2) factor in theoremВ 1.

In Figure 5, we havetwo plots for each dataset.In each plot, we compare three clustering: (1) Our algorithmwith О”=2О”2\Delta=2 (labelled вҖңbothвҖқ); (2) and (3) Our algorithm withО”=1О”1\Delta=1 with protected groups defined by the attribute on xрқ‘Ҙx-axis label. In each plot we only measure the balance for theattribute written in bold in the top right corner.

In datasets, such as bankВ , we see that fairness with respect to only the marital attribute leads to a large amount of unfairness in the default attribute. The fairest solution along both attributes is when they are both considered by our algorithm (О”=2О”2\Delta=2). Interestingly, there are datasets where fairness by one attribute is all that is needed. On the censusВ dataset, fairness by race leads to a fair solution on sex, but fairnessby sex leads to large amount of unfairness in race.

Finally, our results strongly suggest that finding a fair solution for two attributes is often only slightly more expensive (in terms of the clustering objective) than finding a fair solution for only one attribute.
