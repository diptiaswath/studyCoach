Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) are one of the most popular generative models [6]. A neural network, the generator, learns a map that takes random input noise to samples from a given distribution. The training involves using a second neural network, the critic, to discriminate between real samples and the generator output.

In particular, [2, 7] introduces a critic built around the Wasserstein distance between the distribution of true images and generated images.The Wasserstein distance is inherently based on a notion of distance between images which in all implementations of Wasserstein GANs (WGAN) so far has been the в„“2superscriptв„“2\ell^{2} distance. On the other hand, the imaging literature contains a wide range of metrics used to compare images [4] that each emphasize different features of interest, such as edges or to more accurately approximate human observer perception of the generated image.

There is hence an untapped potential in selecting a norm beyond simply the classical в„“2superscriptв„“2\ell^{2} norm. We could for example select an appropriate Sobolev space to either emphasize edges, or large scale behavior. In this work we extend the classical WGAN theory to work on these and more general Banach spaces.

Our contributions are as follows:вҖўWe introduce Banach Wasserstein GAN (BWGAN), extending WGAN implemented via a gradient penalty (GP) term to any separable complete normed space.вҖўWe describe how BWGAN can be efficiently implemented. The only practical difference from classical WGAN with gradient penalty is that the в„“2superscriptв„“2\ell^{2} norm is replaced with a dual norm. We also give theoretically grounded heuristics for the choice of regularization parameters.вҖўWe compare BWGAN with different norms on the CIFAR-10 and CelebA datasets. Using the Space L10superscriptрқҗҝ10L^{10}, which puts strong emphasize on outliers, we achieve an unsupervised inception score of 8.318.318.31 on CIFAR-10, state of the art for non-progressive growing GANs.

In terms of computational costs, the difference between general Banach Wasserstein GANs and the ones based on the в„“2superscriptв„“2\ell^{2} metric lies in the computation of the gradient of the dual norm. By the chain rule, any computational step outside the calculation of this gradient is the same for any choice of underlying notion of distance. This in particular includes any forward pass or backpropagation step through the layers of the network used as discriminator. If there is an efficient framework available to compute the gradient of the dual norm, as in the case of the Fourier transform used for Sobolev spaces, the computational expenses hence stay essentially the same independent of the choice of norm.

We analyzed the dependence of Wasserstein GANs (WGANs) on the notion of distance between images and showed how choosing distances other than the в„“2superscriptв„“2\ell^{2} metric can be used to make WGANs focus on particular image features of interest. We introduced a generalization of WGANs with gradient norm penalization to Banach spaces, allowing to easily implement WGANs for a wide range of underlying norms on images. This opens up a new degree of freedom to design the algorithm to account for the image features relevant in a specific application.

On the CIFAR-10 and CelebA dataset, we demonstrated the impact a change in norm has on model performance. In particular, we computed FID scores for Banach Wasserstein GANs using different Sobolev spaces Ws,psuperscriptрқ‘Ҡрқ‘ рқ‘қW^{s,p} and found a correlation between the values of both sрқ‘ s and pрқ‘қp with model performance.

While this work was motivated by images, the theory is general and can be applied to data in any normed space. In the future, we hope that practitioners take a step back and ask themselves if the в„“2superscriptв„“2\ell^{2} metric is really the best measure of fit, or if some other metric better emphasize what they want to achieve with their generative model.
