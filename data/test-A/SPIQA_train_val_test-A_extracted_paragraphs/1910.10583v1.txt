Bayesian statistics is a versatile mathematical framework for estimation and inference, which applications in bioinformatics [2], computational biology [47, 48], neuroscience [57], natural language processing [30, 40], computer vision [26, 31], robotics [15], machine learning [34, 53], etc. A Bayesian inference model is composed of an unknown parameter θ𝜃\theta from a known parameter space ΘΘ\Theta, an observed sample point x𝑥x from a sample space 𝒳⊆ℝm𝒳superscriptℝ𝑚\mathcal{X}\subseteq\mathbb{R}^{m}, a likelihood measure (or conditional density) p(⋅|θ)p(\cdot|\theta) over 𝒳𝒳\mathcal{X} and a prior distribution π​(⋅)𝜋⋅\pi(\cdot) over ΘΘ\Theta. The key objective of Bayesian statistics is the computation of the posterior distribution p(⋅|x)p(\cdot|x) over ΘΘ\Theta upon observing x𝑥x.

Unfortunately, computing the posterior is a challenging task in practice. Bayes’ theorem, which relates the posterior to the prior [49, Theorem 1.31], requires the evaluation of both the likelihood function p(⋅|θ)p(\cdot|\theta) and the evidence p​(x)𝑝𝑥p(x). Evaluating the likelihood p(⋅|θ)p(\cdot|\theta) at an observation x∈𝒳𝑥𝒳x\in\mathcal{X} is an intractable problem in many situations. For example, the statistical model may contain hidden variables ζ𝜁\zeta, and the likelihood p​(x|θ)𝑝conditional𝑥𝜃p(x|\theta) can only be computed by marginalizing out the hidden variables p​(x|θ)=∫p​(x,ζ|θ)​dζ𝑝conditional𝑥𝜃𝑝𝑥conditional𝜁𝜃differential-d𝜁p(x|\theta)=\int p(x,\zeta|\theta)\mathrm{d}\zeta [38, pp. 322]. In the g-and-k model, the density function does not exist in closed form and can only be expressed in terms of the derivatives of quantile functions, which implies that p​(x|θ)𝑝conditional𝑥𝜃p(x|\theta) needs to be computed numerically for each individual observation x𝑥x [23]. Likewise, evaluating the evidence p​(x)𝑝𝑥p(x) is intractable whenever the evaluation of the likelihood p​(x|θ)𝑝conditional𝑥𝜃p(x|\theta) is.
