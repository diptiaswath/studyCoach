Introduced by Inose and Yasude [InoseY1963IEEE], Sigma-Delta (Σ​ΔΣΔ\Sigma\Delta) modulators are nowadays widely used Analog-to-Digital converters. Such 1-bit ADCs operate at many times the Nyquist rate, and can achieve the same resolution as Nyquist ADCs with suitable signal processing [SchreT2005book]. For kth-order discrete-time Σ​ΔΣΔ\Sigma\Delta modulators, works by Daubechies, Güntürk and al. [DaubeD2003AoM, Guntu2003CPAM, GuntuT2004IEEE] provide estimates for the error between the filtered output and the input. Typically, they obtain a mean-squared error estimate in 𝒪⁡(1/Nk)𝒪1superscript𝑁𝑘\operatorname{\mathcal{O}}(1/N^{k}) for a time-varying input, where N𝑁N is the oversampling ratio, using for example a sinck+1superscriptsinc𝑘1\operatorname{sinc}^{k+1} filter [Thao2001ISCAS]. But for continuous-time Σ​ΔΣΔ\Sigma\Delta (CT-Σ​ΔΣΔ\Sigma\Delta) modulators, such general results remain partial. These CT-Σ​ΔΣΔ\Sigma\Delta modulators deliver more power-efficient operations than their discrete-time equivalent, as well as higher sampling rates [OrtmaG2006book, SchreT2005book, StraaP2008JSSC].

Privileged in high performance motor control [Soren2015PCIM], the Σ​ΔΣΔ\Sigma\Delta ADC is used to retrieve the phase currents which carry information on the rotor position if correctly filtered [SurroCMR2020arxivb]. Indeed, the Pulse-Width Modulation (PWM) of the input voltage creates ripples in the current measurements [SurroCMR2020ACC] that we can extract through a demodulation procedure using linear combination of iterated moving averages [SurroCMR2019IECON]. Therefore, knowing the error estimate of the Σ​ΔΣΔ\Sigma\Delta modulator is of utmost importance for this type of application.

We present a general technique to study higher-order CT-Σ​ΔΣΔ\Sigma\Delta modulators. Under regularity assumptions on the input and the filtering kernel, we prove for a second-order CT-Σ​ΔΣΔ\Sigma\Delta that the error estimate may be in o​(1/N2)𝑜1superscript𝑁2o(1/N^{2}).

This paper is organized as follows: we first detail the required definitions and technical lemmas; then we prove the error estimate on a specific second-order Σ​ΔΣΔ\Sigma\Delta modulator. The theory is finally validated on numerical examples.
