Meanwhile, the algorithms with relatively tight approximation factors do not necessarily scale well in practice (Ahmadian et al., 2019).

To overcome these computational barriers, Ergun et al. (2022) proposed a learning-augmented setting where we have access to some auxiliary information about the input data set. This is motivated by the fact that in practice we expect the dataset of interest to have exploitable structures relevant to the optimal clustering. For instance, a classifier’s predictions of points in a dataset can help group similar instances together. This notion was formalized in Ergun et al. Observe that even when the predicted clusters Pisubscript𝑃𝑖P_{i} are close to a set of true clusters Pi∗superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑖P_{i}^{*} in the sense that the label error rate α𝛼\alpha is very small, computing the means or medians of Pisubscript𝑃𝑖P_{i} can lead to arbitrarily bad solutions. This work belongs to a growing literature on learning-augmented algorithms. Machine learning has been used to improve algorithms for a number of classical problems, including data structures (Kraska et al., 2018; Mitzenmacher, 2018; Lin et al., 2022), online algorithms (Purohit et al., 2018), graph algorithms (Khalil et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2022a, b), computing frequency estimation (Du et al., 2021) , caching (Rohatgi, 2020; Wei, 2020), and support estimation (Eden et al., 2021). We refer the reader to Mitzenmacher andVassilvitskii (2020) for an overview and applications of the framework.

Another relevant line of work is clustering with side information. The works Balcan and Blum (2008); Awasthi et al. (2014); Vikram and Dasgupta (2016) studied an interactive clustering setting where an oracle interactively provides advice about whether or not to merge two clusters. Basu et al. (2004) proposed an active learning framework for clustering, where the algorithm has access to a predictor that determines if two points should or should not belong to the same cluster. Ashtiani et al. (2016) introduced a semi-supervised active clustering framework where the algorithm has access to a predictor that answers queries whether two particular points belong in an optimal clustering. The goal is to produce a (1+α)1𝛼(1+\alpha)-approximate clustering while minimizing the query complexity to the oracle.

Approximation stability, proposed in Balcan et al. (2013), is another assumption proposed to circumvent the NP-hardness of approximation for k𝑘k-means clustering. More formally, the concept of (c,α)𝑐𝛼(c,\alpha)-stability requires that every c𝑐c-approximate clustering is α𝛼\alpha-close to the optimal solution in terms of the fraction of incorrectly clustered points. This is different from our setting, where at most an α𝛼\alpha fraction of the points are incorrectly clustered and can worsen the clustering cost arbitrarily.

Gamlath et al. (2022) studies the problem of k𝑘k-means clustering in the presence of noisy labels, where the cluster label of each point created by either an adversarial or a randomperturbation of the optimal solution. In this work, we have different assumptions on the predicted cluster cluster Pisubscript𝑃𝑖P_{i} and the optimal cluster Pi∗superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑖P_{i}^{*}. Algorithm 2 operates sequentially on each cluster estimate; for the cluster estimate Pisubscript𝑃𝑖P_{i}, it samples a point x∈Pi𝑥subscript𝑃𝑖x\in P_{i} uniformly at random, and removes from Pisubscript𝑃𝑖P_{i} the ⌈α​mi⌉𝛼subscript𝑚𝑖\lceil\alpha m_{i}\rceil-many points that lie furthest from x𝑥x. It turns out that this subroutine already gives us a good median for the cluster Pi∗superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑖P_{i}^{*} with constant probability (lemma 14); to boost the success probability we repeat this subroutine some R𝑅R-many times (the exact expression is given in the pseudocode and justified in lemma 15), and pick the median with the lowest cost, denoted c^isubscript^𝑐𝑖\widehat{c}_{i}. Collecting the c^isubscript^𝑐𝑖\widehat{c}_{i} across i∈[k]𝑖delimited-[]𝑘i\in[k], we get our final solution {c^1,…,c^k}subscript^𝑐1…subscript^𝑐𝑘\{\hat{c}_{1},\dots,\hat{c}_{k}\}.

Although our algorithm itself is relatively straightforward, the analysis turns out to be more involved. We trace the proof at a high level in this section and mention the main steps, and defer all proofs to the appendix.

We see that it would suffice to allocate a center that works well for the true cluster Pi∗superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑖P_{i}^{*}, but we only have access to the set Pisubscript𝑃𝑖P_{i} with the promise that they have a significant overlap (as characterized by α𝛼\alpha). Fixing an arbitrary true median ci∗superscriptsubscript𝑐𝑖c_{i}^{*}, one key insight is that the “false" points, i.e. points in Pi\Pi∗\subscript𝑃𝑖superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑖P_{i}\backslash P_{i}^{*} will only significantly distort the median if they happen to lie far from ci∗superscriptsubscript𝑐𝑖c_{i}^{*}. If there were a way to identify and remove these false points which lie far from ci∗superscriptsubscript𝑐𝑖c_{i}^{*}, then simply computing the geometric median of the clipped data set should work well.

By a direct application of Markov’s inequality it is possible to show that a point x𝑥x picked uniformly at random will in fact lie at a distance on the order of the average clustering cost O​P​Ti/mi𝑂𝑃subscript𝑇𝑖subscript𝑚𝑖OPT_{i}/m_{i} with constant probability, as formalized in lemma 12.

As we will condition on this good event holding, it will be convenient to introduce the notation ℰℰ\mathcal{E}.

Having identified a good point x𝑥x to serve as a proxy for where the true median ci∗superscriptsubscript𝑐𝑖c_{i}^{*} lies, we need to figure out a good way to clip the data set so as to avoid false points which lie very far from ci∗superscriptsubscript𝑐𝑖c_{i}^{*}. We observe that since there are guaranteed to be at most ⌈α​n⌉𝛼𝑛\lceil\alpha n\rceil-many false points, if we were to remove the ⌈α​n⌉𝛼𝑛\lceil\alpha n\rceil-many points that lie farthest from x𝑥x (denoted Pi′superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑖′P_{i}^{\prime}), then we either remove false points that lie very far from ci∗superscriptsubscript𝑐𝑖c_{i}^{*}, or true points (Pi∗∩Pi′superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑖superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑖′P_{i}^{*}\cap P_{i}^{\prime}) which are at the same distance from ci∗superscriptsubscript𝑐𝑖c_{i}^{*} as the remaining false points (the points in Pi\(Pi′∪Pi∗)\subscript𝑃𝑖superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑖′superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑖P_{i}\backslash(P_{i}^{\prime}\cup P_{i}^{*}). In particular, this implies that the impact of the remaining false points is roughly dominated by the clustering cost of an equal number of true points, and we are able to exploit this to show that the clustering cost of Pi\Pi′\subscript𝑃𝑖subscriptsuperscript𝑃′𝑖P_{i}\backslash P^{\prime}_{i} with respect to its own median estimate c^isubscript^𝑐𝑖\widehat{c}_{i} is already close to that of the true center Pi∗superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑖P_{i}^{*}.

Since the event ℰℰ\mathcal{E} that the randomly sampled point x𝑥x is close to a true median ci∗superscriptsubscript𝑐𝑖c_{i}^{*} is true only with constant probability, we boost the success probability by running this subroutine some R𝑅R times and letting c^isubscript^𝑐𝑖\widehat{c}_{i} be the median estimate with respect to which the respective clipped data set had the lowest clustering cost.

We see from lemma 21 that the set Pi\Pi′\subscript𝑃𝑖subscriptsuperscript𝑃′𝑖P_{i}\backslash P^{\prime}_{i} differs from the true positives Pi∩Pi∗subscript𝑃𝑖superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑖P_{i}\cap P_{i}^{*} by sets of size at most ⌈α​n⌉𝛼𝑛\lceil\alpha n\rceil. It follows that as long as the distance between c^isubscript^𝑐𝑖\widehat{c}_{i} and ci∗superscriptsubscript𝑐𝑖c_{i}^{*} is on the order of OPTi/nsubscriptOPT𝑖𝑛\mathrm{OPT}_{i}/n, they will not influence the clustering cost by more than an O​(α​OPTi)𝑂𝛼subscriptOPT𝑖O(\alpha\mathrm{OPT}_{i}) additive term, which we will be able to absorb into the (1+O​(α))1𝑂𝛼(1+O(\alpha)) multiplicative approximation factor. We formalize this in lemma 16.

We finally put everything together to show that the clustering cost of the set of true points Pi∩Pi∗subscript𝑃𝑖superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑖P_{i}\cap P_{i}^{*} with respect to the estimate c^isubscript^𝑐𝑖\widehat{c}_{i} is only at most an additive O​(α​OPTi)𝑂𝛼subscriptOPT𝑖O(\alpha\mathrm{OPT}_{i}) more than the cost with respect to the true median ci∗superscriptsubscript𝑐𝑖c_{i}^{*}. The key technical point in the analysis is that we can only appeal to the fact that the cost of Pi\Pi′\subscript𝑃𝑖subscriptsuperscript𝑃′𝑖P_{i}\backslash P^{\prime}_{i} is close to O​P​Ti𝑂𝑃subscript𝑇𝑖OPT_{i}, and we cannot directly reason about c^isubscript^𝑐𝑖\widehat{c}_{i} apart from appealing to lemma 16.

We can now derive our main cost bound stated in lemma 18. Doing so only requires that we account for the mislabelled points Pi∗\Pi\superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑖subscript𝑃𝑖P_{i}^{*}\backslash P_{i} which were not accounted for during our clustering. Again, from lemma 16 it suffices to appeal to the fact that the estimate c^isubscript^𝑐𝑖\widehat{c}_{i} lies within an O​(α​OPTi/n)𝑂𝛼subscriptOPT𝑖𝑛O(\alpha\mathrm{OPT}_{i}/n) distance of the true median ci∗superscriptsubscript𝑐𝑖c_{i}^{*}.

We now formalize our main cost bound, success probability and run-time guarantees in theorem 19.
