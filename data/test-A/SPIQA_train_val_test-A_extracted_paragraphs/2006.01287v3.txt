Web services provide interoperability among disparate software applications and play a key role in service-oriented computing (Bouguettayaet al., 2017). Over the past few years, numerous Web services have been published as indicated by the Web service repository–ProgrammableWeb111https://www.programmableweb.com/. The proliferation of Web services brings great benefits in building versatile service-oriented applications and systems.

It is apparent that the quality of service-oriented applications and systems relies heavily on the quality of their component Web services. Thus, investigating the quality of Web services is an important task to ensure the reliability of the ultimate applications and the entire systems. The quality of Web services can be characterized by their functional and non-functional attributes. Quality-of-Service (QoS) represents the non-functional aspect of Web services, such as response time, throughput rate and failure probability (Papazoglou et al., 2007; Wuet al., 2015). Since there are many functionally equivalent or similar services offered on the Web, investigating non-functional QoS properties becomes the major concern for service selection (El Haddadet al., 2010; Zhanget al., 2011). However, the QoS value observed by users depends heavily on the Web service invocation context. Hence, the quality of the same Web service experienced by different users may be relatively different (Shaoet al., 2007). For this reason, it is important to acquire personalized QoS values for different users. Considering that users cannot invoke all Web services to obtain personalized QoS values on their own due to high time cost and huge resource overhead (Wuet al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2019), predicting missing QoS values based on existing observations plays an essential role in obtaining approximate personalized QoS values.

Matrix factorization (MF) is arguably the most popular technique adopted for QoS prediction (Zhang et al., 2019; Zhenget al., 2020; Ghafouriet al., 2020). However, most existing MF-based QoS prediction methods directly utilize L2subscript𝐿2L_{2}-norm to measure the difference between the observed QoS values and the predicted ones (Zheng and Lyu, 2013; Lo et al., 2015; Xu et al., 2016; Xieet al., 2016; Suet al., 2016; Wuet al., 2018a, 2019a). It is well-known that L2subscript𝐿2L_{2}-norm is sensitive to outliers (Zhaoet al., 2015; Caoet al., 2015; Xuet al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2017b). That is, the objective function value may be dominated by outliers during the L2subscript𝐿2L_{2}-norm minimization process, which will lead to severe approximation deviation between the observed normal values and the predicted ones. As a result, without taking outliers into consideration, existing MF-based methods may not achieve satisfactory performance. In recent years, there are some explorations on enhancing the robustness of MF-based QoS prediction methods by replacing L2subscript𝐿2L_{2}-norm with L1subscript𝐿1L_{1}-norm (Zhuet al., 2018). Although L1subscript𝐿1L_{1}-norm is more robust to outliers (Eriksson and VanDen Hengel, 2010; Zhenget al., 2012; Menget al., 2013), L1subscript𝐿1L_{1}-norm-based objective function is much harder to optimize and the solution is also unstable (Xuet al., 2012; Meng andDe La Torre, 2013). Moreover, L1subscript𝐿1L_{1}-norm is still sensitive to outliers, especially when outliers take significantly different values from the normal ones (Ding and Jiang, 2017; Xu et al., 2015). There are also some methods seeking to identify outliers explicitly by means of clustering algorithms (Zhuet al., 2010; Wuet al., 2019b; Hasnyet al., 2016), which usually treat all the elements in the smallest cluster as outliers (Wuet al., 2015; Heet al., 2014). However, it is difficult to choose the proper number of clusters. Consequently, these methods usually suffer from the misclassification issue. That is, either some outliers may not be eliminated successfully or some normal values may be selected as outliers falsely.

In this paper, we propose a robust QoS prediction method under the matrix factorization framework to deal with the aforementioned issues. Our method chooses to measure the discrepancy between the observed QoS values and the predicted ones by Cauchy loss (Barron, 2019; Liet al., 2018) instead of the L1subscript𝐿1L_{1}-norm loss or L2subscript𝐿2L_{2}-norm loss. It has been shown that Cauchy loss is much more robust to outliers than the L1subscript𝐿1L_{1}-norm loss and L2subscript𝐿2L_{2}-norm loss (Liet al., 2018; Xu et al., 2015). Theoretically, Cauchy loss allows nearly half of the observations to be out of the normal range before it gives incorrect results (Mizera andMüller, 2002). For a given QoS dataset, it is unlikely that nearly half of the observations are outliers. Thus, Cauchy loss is sufficient for outlier modeling and has the potential to provide better prediction results. Note also that our method does not explicitly identify outliers, which reduces the risk of misclassification and makes our method more general and more robust. In other words, our method is resilient to outliers. Considering that the QoS value of a Web service observed by a particular user may change over time, it is essential to provide time-aware personalized QoS prediction results. To achieve this goal, we further extend our method under the tensor factorization framework by taking the temporal information into consideration.

In summary, the main contributions of this paper include:•First, we propose a robust Web service QoS prediction method with outlier resilience. Our method measures the discrepancy between the observed QoS values and the predicted ones by Cauchy loss, which is robust to outliers.•Second, we extend our method to provide time-aware QoS prediction results under the tensor factorization framework by taking the temporal information into consideration.•Third, we conduct extensive experiments on both static and dynamic datasets to evaluate the performance of our method. The results demonstrate that our method can achieve better performance than state-of-the-art baseline methods.

Most existing QoS prediction methods assume that the QoS observations are reliable and rational. However, this assumption may not hold in the real world. This is because the observed QoS data can be affected by many factors. For example, there may be some malicious users submitting wrong QoS values deliberately. The service providers may also pretend to be service users and thus exaggerate the performance of their own Web services and depreciate the performance of their competitors’ Web services. In addition, the QoS values observed by users are largely dependent on the invocation environment such as network latency and server overload, which may lead some of the QoS values to deviate far from the normal range. In consideration of these complicated factors, we argue that it is highly possible that some of the QoS observations are outliers.

However, we are in lack of an oracle showing in advance which QoS observations are outliers. Here, we treat the rare extreme values which significantly differ from the remaining ones as outliers by following the definition in (Kimet al., 2016).To be more intuitive, in Figure 1 (a) and (b), we show the distribution of both response time and throughput of 100 Web services invoked by 3 randomly selected users from a publicly available dataset–WS-DREAM_dataset1222https://github.com/wsdream/wsdream-dataset/tree/master/dataset1. As can be seen, although a user tends to have different usage experiences on different Web services, most QoS values of these Web services observed by the three users fall into a normal range. For example, the response time mainly falls in the interval of [0,2]02[0,2]. However, there are also some observations deviating far from the normal range. As shown in Figure 1 (a), the response time experienced by user 2 even reaches up to 14 seconds, which is far beyond the normal range. Needless to say, such kind of observations should be treated as outliers. We further demonstrate the distribution of response time and throughput of 3 Web services experienced by 100 different users in Figure 1 (c) and (d). It can be observed that although the usage experiences of a Web service can vary widely among different users, the QoS values of the same Web service observed by the majority of users tend to fall into a normal range. Whereas, there are also some observations taking values far beyond the normal range. These phenomena verify the rationality of treating extreme values as outliers and also reveal the unavoidability of outliers in QoS observations.

The objective function based on L2subscript𝐿2L_{2}-norm as in Eq. (3) is smooth and can be optimized by the gradient descent method (Gemullaet al., 2011). However, the L2subscript𝐿2L_{2}-norm is sensitive to outliers (i.e., rare extreme values) (Zhaoet al., 2015). When the given observations contain outliers, the residuals between these outliers’ corresponding entries in 𝑿𝑿{\bm{X}} and their approximation entries in 𝑼​𝑺T𝑼superscript𝑺𝑇{\bm{U}}{\bm{S}}^{T} become huge due to the square operation. Therefore, when minimizing the objective function in Eq. (3), more priorities are given to these outliers, which unfortunately causes severe approximation deviation of the normal QoS values. As a result, the QoS prediction performance may degrade dramatically.

To make the model more robust to outliers, a common stategy is to replace L2subscript𝐿2L_{2}-norm with L1subscript𝐿1L_{1}-norm (Zhuet al., 2018; Wuet al., 2018b; Ke and Kanade, 2005; Eriksson and VanDen Hengel, 2010). Although L1subscript𝐿1L_{1}-norm is to some extent more robust to outliers than L2subscript𝐿2L_{2}-norm, the objective function based on L1subscript𝐿1L_{1}-norm as in Eq. (4) is a non-smooth function and it is much harder to optimize. What’s more, although the large residuals due to outliers are not squared in L1subscript𝐿1L_{1}-norm, they may still be quite large relative to the normal ones and thus one would expect that they would influence the objective function as well (Ding and Jiang, 2017).

As stated in the previous section, both L1subscript𝐿1L_{1}-norm and L2subscript𝐿2L_{2}-norm are sensitive to outliers. In order to make the MF-based methods more robust to outliers, we propose a novel QoS prediction method that utilizes Cauchy loss (Barron, 2019) as the measurement of the discrepancy between the observed QoS values and the predicted ones. It has been shown that Cauchy loss is resistant to outliers (Xu et al., 2015; Guanet al., 2017; Mizera andMüller, 2002). Thus our method is expected to be robust to outliers.

As pointed out in (Zhanget al., 2011), the QoS performance of Web services is highly related to the invocation time because the service status (e.g., number of users) and the network environment (e.g., network speed) may change over time. Thus, it is essential to provide time-aware personalized QoS information to help users make service selection at runtime (Zhuet al., 2017a). In this part, we aim to extend our method to make it suitable for time-aware personalized QoS prediction.

To achieve the goal, we choose to extend our method under the tensor factorization framework. A tensor is a multidimensional or N𝑁N-way array (Kolda and Bader, 2009). An N𝑁N-way tensor is denoted as 𝓧∈ℝI1×I2×⋯×IN𝓧superscriptℝsubscript𝐼1subscript𝐼2⋯subscript𝐼𝑁\bm{\mathcal{X}}\in\mathbb{R}^{I_{1}\times I_{2}\times\cdots\times I_{N}}, which has N𝑁N indices (i1,i2,⋯,iNsubscript𝑖1subscript𝑖2⋯subscript𝑖𝑁i_{1},i_{2},\cdots,i_{N})and its entries are denoted by 𝓧i1​i2​⋯​iNsubscript𝓧subscript𝑖1subscript𝑖2⋯subscript𝑖𝑁\bm{\mathcal{X}}_{i_{1}i_{2}\cdots i_{N}}. In this sense, a tensor can be treated as a generalized matrix and a matrix can also be treated as a two-way tensor.

For time-aware QoS prediction, we need to take the temporal information of QoS values into consideration. According to the definition of tensors, it is clear that we can model QoS observations with temporal information as a three-way tensor 𝓧∈ℝm×n×t𝓧superscriptℝ𝑚𝑛𝑡\bm{\mathcal{X}}\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times n\times t} whose each entry 𝓧i​j​ksubscript𝓧𝑖𝑗𝑘\bm{\mathcal{X}}_{ijk} represents the QoS value of Web service j𝑗j observed by user i𝑖i at time k𝑘k. Here, t𝑡t denotes the total number of time intervals. Accordingly, we can use an indicator tensor 𝓘∈ℝm×n×t𝓘superscriptℝ𝑚𝑛𝑡\bm{\mathcal{I}}\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times n\times t} to show whether the QoS values have been observed or not. If user i𝑖i has the record of Web service j𝑗j at time k𝑘k, 𝓘i​j​ksubscript𝓘𝑖𝑗𝑘\bm{\mathcal{I}}_{ijk} is set to 1; otherwise, its value is set to 0. The most commonly used evaluation metrics for QoS prediction include mean absolute error (MAE) (Zhenget al., 2011) and root mean square error (RMSE) (Zhenget al., 2011). Let ΠΠ\Pi denote the set of QoS values to be predicted (i.e., ΠΠ\Pi is the testing set) and let N=|Π|𝑁ΠN=|\Pi|, then MAE is calculated as:(20)M​A​E=∑q∈Π|q−q^|N,𝑀𝐴𝐸subscript𝑞Π𝑞^𝑞𝑁MAE=\frac{\sum_{q\in\Pi}|q-\hat{q}|}{N},and RMSE is calculated as:(21)R​M​S​E=∑q∈Π(q−q^)2N,𝑅𝑀𝑆𝐸subscript𝑞Πsuperscript𝑞^𝑞2𝑁RMSE=\sqrt{\frac{\sum_{q\in\Pi}(q-\hat{q})^{2}}{N}},where q^^𝑞\hat{q} denotes the predicted value for the observation q𝑞q. For both MAE and RMSE, smaller values indicate better performance.

However, according to the definition of MAE and RMSE, we can see that both MAE and RMSE are sensitive to outliers, which means that if ΠΠ\Pi contains outliers, then MAE and RMSE cannot truly reflect the QoS prediction performance. For example, suppose that q∗∈Πsubscript𝑞Πq_{*}\in\Pi is an outlier, then in order to get a small MAE value and RMSE value, the predicted q^∗subscript^𝑞\hat{q}_{*} should be close to q∗subscript𝑞q_{*} rather than the normal QoS value. As thus, a smaller MAE or RMSE value may not really indicate better performance. To overcome this limitation, we eliminate outliers from ΠΠ\Pi when calculating MAE and RMSE. Note that we do not have groundtruth labels for outliers. Therefore, we need to detect outliers from scratch. To achieve this goal, we employ the i𝑖iForest (short for isolation forest) method (Liuet al., 2008, 2012) for outlier detection. i𝑖iForest detects outliers purely based on the concept of isolation without employing any distance or density measure, making i𝑖iForest quite efficient and robust. i𝑖iForest will calculate an outlier score for each datum. The score takes value in the range of [0,1]01[0,1] and a larger value indicates more possibility to be outliers. Based on the outlier score, we can set the number of outliers flexibly. To intuitively show the effectiveness of i𝑖iForest, we report the outlier detection results of a randomly selected Web service from the static dataset in Figure 3, where the outlier ratio is set to 0.05. As can be seen, i𝑖iForest demonstrates good performance in outlier detection. It can detect both overinflated and underinflated outliers.

For ease of presentation, we name our method for static QoS prediction as CMF and our method for time-aware QoS prediction as CTF hereafter. For static QoS prediction, we compare CMF with the following five methods:•MF2: MF2 denotes the basic MF-based QoS prediction method (Zheng and Lyu, 2013) and it measures the discrepancy between the observed QoS values and the predicted ones by L2subscript𝐿2L_{2}-norm.•MF1: MF1 is also an MF-based QoS prediction method (Zhuet al., 2018). For time-aware QoS prediction, we compare our CTF with the following five methods:•NNCP: NNCP is a tensor-based time-aware QoS prediction method (Zhanget al., 2014). It is based on CP decomposition and imposes nonnegative constraints on all the factor matrices.•BNLFT: BNLFT is a biased nonnegative tensor factorization model (Luoet al., 2019). It incorporates linear biases into the model for describing QoS fluctuations, and it adds nonnegative constraints to the factor matrices as well.•WLRTF: WLRTF is an MLE (maximum likelihood estimation) based tensor factorization method (Chenet al., 2016). It models the noise of each datum as a mixture of Gaussian (MoG).•PLMF: PLMF is an LSTM (long short-term memory) (Hochreiter andSchmidhuber, 1997) based QoS prediction method (Xionget al., 2018). PLMF can capture the dynamic latent representations of usersand Web services.•TASR: TASR is a time-aware QoS prediction method (Dinget al., 2018). It integrates similarity-enhanced collaborative filtering model and the ARIMA model (a time series analysis model) (Box and Pierce, 1970).

Although MF1, CAP, TAP and DALF are able to deal with outliers to some extent for static QoS prediction, to our best knowledge, our method CTF is the first to take outliers into consideration for time-aware QoS prediction. Most existing QoS prediction methods fall into collaborative filtering methods (Wuet al., 2015), which can be further divided into two categories: memory-based methods (Zhenget al., 2011; Caoet al., 2013; Jianget al., 2011; Breeseet al., 1998; Sarwar et al., 2001) and model-based methods (Wuet al., 2018b; Rangarajan, 2018; Yanget al., 2018; Xieet al., 2016; Suet al., 2016). Memory-based methods predict unknown QoS values by employing the neighbourhood information of similar users and similar Web services (Zheng and Lyu, 2010), which further leads to user-based methods (Breeseet al., 1998), service-based methods (Sarwar et al., 2001) and hybrid methods (Zhenget al., 2011; Caoet al., 2013; Jianget al., 2011) that systematically combine the user-based methods and service-based methods. Memory-based methods usually suffer from the data sparsity problem (Zhang et al., 2019; Zheng and Lyu, 2013), due to the limited number of Web services a single user will invoke. Model-based methods can deal with the problem of data sparsity, thus they have gained the most popularity (Zhang et al., 2019). Model-based methods usually train a predefined prediction model based on existing QoS observations and then predict missing QoS values. For example, Wu et al. (Wuet al., 2017) propose to train a factorization machine model for QoS prediction. Luo et al. (Luoet al., 2015) introduce fuzzy neural networks and adaptive dynamic programming to predict QoS values. Matrix factorization is also a model-based technique and it has obtained the most attention (Zheng and Lyu, 2013; Lo et al., 2015; Xieet al., 2016; Suet al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2019). MF-based methods factorize the user-service matrix into two low-rank factor matrices with one factor matrix capturing the latent representations of users and another revealing the latent representations of Web services. Therefore, MF-based methods are able to automatically model the contributions to a specific QoS value from the user side and service side simultaneously, which usually results in better prediction performance. In addition, MF-based methods possess high flexibility of incorporating side information such as location (Heet al., 2014), contexts (Wuet al., 2018a, b) and privacy (Liu et al., 2018). MF-based methods can also be easily generalized for time-aware QoS prediction under the tensor factorization framework (Zhanget al., 2011, 2014; Luoet al., 2019; Wanget al., 2019). There are also a few other kinds of time-aware QoS prediction methods like time series model-based methods (Dinget al., 2018; Aminet al., 2012) and neural networks-based methods (Xionget al., 2018).

Although there are various QoS prediction methods, few of them have taken outliers into consideration. However, as analyzed in Section 2, some QoS observations indeed should be treated as outliers. Thus, the performance of existing methods may not be reliable. For example, most existing MF-based QoS prediction methods directly utilize L2subscript𝐿2L_{2}-norm to measure the discrepancy between the observed QoS values and the predicted ones (Zheng and Lyu, 2013; Lo et al., 2015; Xu et al., 2016; Xieet al., 2016; Suet al., 2016; Wuet al., 2018a). It is widely accepted that L2subscript𝐿2L_{2}-norm is not robust to outliers (Caoet al., 2015; Xuet al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2017b). As a consequence, the performance of MF-based methods may be severely influenced when QoS observations contain outliers.

In order to obtain reliable QoS prediction results, it is necessary to take outliers into consideration. One popular method to reduce the effects of outliers is replacing L2subscript𝐿2L_{2}-norm with L1subscript𝐿1L_{1}-norm because L1subscript𝐿1L_{1}-norm is more robust to outliers (Ke and Kanade, 2005; Eriksson and VanDen Hengel, 2010; Zhenget al., 2012; Menget al., 2013). For example, an L1subscript𝐿1L_{1}-norm low-rank MF-based QoS prediction method is introduced in (Zhuet al., 2018). However, L1subscript𝐿1L_{1}-norm-based objective function is non-smooth and thus much harder to optimize (Xuet al., 2012; Meng andDe La Torre, 2013). Besides, L1subscript𝐿1L_{1}-norm is still sensitive to outliers, especially when outliers are far beyond the normal range of QoS values (Ding and Jiang, 2017; Xu et al., 2015).

Another line of reliable QoS prediction is detecting outliers explicitly based on clustering algorithms. In (Wuet al., 2015), Wu et al. propose a credibility-aware QoS prediction method, which employs a two-phase k𝑘k-means clustering algorithm to identify untrustworthy users (i.e., outliers). Su et al. (Suet al., 2017) propose a trust-aware QoS prediction method, which provides reliable QoS prediction results via calculating the reputation of users by a beta reputation system and identifies outliers based on k𝑘k-means clustering as well. In (Wuet al., 2019b), a data-aware latent factor model is introduced, which utilizes the density peaks-based clustering algorithm (Rodriguez andLaio, 2014) to detect unreliable QoS values. However, it is difficult to choose a proper number of clusters, thus either some outliers may not be eliminated successfully or some normal values may be selected as outliers falsely. Our method does not detect outliers explicitly. Therefore it will not suffer from the misclassification issue.

In this paper, we have proposed a novel robust QoS prediction method, which utilizes Cauchy loss to measure the discrepancy between the observed QoS values and the predicted ones. Owing to the robustness of Cauchy loss, our method is resilient to outliers. That is, there is no need to detect outliers explicitly. Therefore, our method will not suffer from the problem of misclassification. Considering that the QoS performance may change over time, we have further extended our method to make it suitable for time-aware QoS prediction. To evaluate the efficiency and effectiveness of our method, we have conducted extensive experiments on both static and dynamic datasets. Experimental results have demonstrated that our method can achieve better performance than existing methods.
