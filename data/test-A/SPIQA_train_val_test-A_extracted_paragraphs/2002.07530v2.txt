In this work, we studied the scaling of optimistic logistic bandit algorithms for a particular GLM: the logistic model. We explicitly showed that previous algorithms suffered from prohibitive scaling introduced by the quantity κ𝜅\kappa, because of their sub-optimal treatment of the non-linearities of the reward signal. Thanks to a novel non-linear approach, we proved that they can be improved by deriving tighter prediction bounds. By doing so, we gave a rigorous justification for an algorithm that resembles the heuristic algorithm empirically evaluated in Filippi et al., (2010). This algorithm exhibits a regret bound that only suffers from a κ𝜅\sqrt{\kappa} dependency, compared to κ𝜅\kappa for previous guarantees. Further, we showed that a more careful algorithmic design leads to yet better guarantees, where the leading term of the regret is independent of κ𝜅\kappa. This result bridges the gap between logistic bandits and linear bandits, up to polynomial terms in constants of interest (e.g S𝑆S).

The theoretical value of the regret upper-bound of Logistic-UCB-2 can be highlighted by comparing it to the Bayesian regret lower bound provided by Dong et al., (2019). Namely, they show that for any logistic bandit algorithm, and for any polynomial form p𝑝p and ϵ>0italic-ϵ0\epsilon>0, there exist a problem instance such that the regret is at least Ω​(p​(d)​T1−ϵ)Ω𝑝𝑑superscript𝑇1italic-ϵ\Omega(p(d)T^{1-\epsilon}). This does not contradict our bound, as for hard problem instance one can have κ=T𝜅𝑇\kappa=T in which case the second term of Logistic-UCB-2 will scale as d2​Tsuperscript𝑑2𝑇d^{2}T.Note that other corner-cases instances further highlight the theoretical value of our regret bounds. Namely, note that κ=T𝜅𝑇\kappa=\sqrt{T} turns GLM-UCB’s regret guarantee vacuous as it will scale linearly with T𝑇T. On the other hand for this case the regret of Logistic-UCB-1 scales as T3/4superscript𝑇34T^{3/4}, and the regret of Logistic-UCB-2 continues to scale as T𝑇\sqrt{T}.

An important avenue for future work consists in extending our results to other generalized linear models. This can be done naturally by extending our work. Indeed, the properties of the sigmoid that we leverage are rather weak, and might easily transfer to other inverse link functions. We first used the fact that μ˙˙𝜇\dot{\mu} represents the variance of the reward in order to use Theorem 1. This is not a specificity of the logistic model, but is a common relationship observed for all exponential models and their related mean function (Filippi et al.,, 2010, §2). We also used the generalized self-concordance property of the logistic loss, which is a consequence of the fact that |μ¨|≤μ˙¨𝜇˙𝜇|\ddot{\mu}|\leq\dot{\mu}. This control is quite mild, and other mean functions might display similar properties (with other constants). This is namely the case of another generalized linear model: the Poisson regression.

The lessons learned here for optimistic algorithms might be transferred to randomized algorithms (such as Thompson Sampling) that are often preferred in practical applications thanks to their superior empirical performances. Extending our approach to such algorithms would therefore be of significant practical importance.
