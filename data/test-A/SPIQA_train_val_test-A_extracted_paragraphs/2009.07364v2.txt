Recently, neural networks have shown substantial progress in NLP tasks (Devlin etВ al., 2019; Radford etВ al., 2019). To understand and explain their behavior, a natural question emerge: how much linguistic knowledge is encoded in these neural network systems?

An efficient approach to reveal information encoded in internal representations uses diagnostic classifiers (Alain and Bengio, 2017). Referred to as вЂњprobesвЂќ, diagnostic classifiers are trained on pre-computed intermediate representations of neural NLP systems. The performance on tasks they are trained to predict are used to evaluate the richness of the linguistic representation in encoding the probed tasks. Such tasks include probing syntax (Hewitt and Manning, 2019; Lin etВ al., 2019; Tenney etВ al., 2019a), semantics (Yaghoobzadeh etВ al., 2019), discourse features (Chen etВ al., 2019; Liu etВ al., 2019; Tenney etВ al., 2019b), and commonsense knowledge (Petroni etВ al., 2019; Poerner etВ al., 2019).

However, appropriate criteria for selecting a good probe is under debate. The traditional view that high-accuracy probes are better is challenged by Hewitt and Liang (2019), who proposed that the high accuracy could be attributed to either (1) that the representation contains rich linguistic knowledge, or (2) that the probe learns the task. To circumvent this ambiguity, they proposed to use the improvement of probing task performance against a control task (predicting random labels from the same representations), i.e., the вЂњselectivityвЂќ criterion.Recently, Pimentel etВ al. (2020), challenged this dichotomy from an information theoretic viewpoint. They proposed to use an вЂњinformation gainвЂќ criterion, which empirically is the reduction in cross entropy from a вЂњcontrol function taskвЂќ probing from randomized representation.

In this paper, we show the вЂњnon-exclusive-orвЂќ dichotomy raised by Hewitt and Liang (2019) is valid information-theoretically. There is a difference between the original NLP model learning the task and the probe learning the task.

In addition, we show that the вЂњselectivityвЂќ criterion and the вЂњcontrol functionвЂќ criterion are comparably accurate. Pimentel etВ al. (2020) formulated their errors with the difference in a pair of KL divergences. We show that the error of the вЂњselectivityвЂќ criterion (Hewitt and Liang, 2019), if measured from cross entropy loss, can be formulated in the difference in a pair of KL divergences as well. When randomizations are perfect, these two criteria differ by only constant terms.

Empirically, on a POS tag probing task on English, French and Spanish translations, we show that the вЂњselectivityвЂќ and the вЂњcontrol functionвЂќ criteria highly agree with each other. We rank experiments with over 10,000 different hyperparameter settings using these criteria. The Spearman correlation of the Hewitt and Liang (2019) vs. Pimentel etВ al. (2020) criteria are on par with the correlations of вЂњaccuracy vs. cross entropy lossвЂќ вЂ“ two very strong baselines.

Overall, we recommend using control mechanisms to select probes, instead of relying on merely the probing task performance. When randomization is done well, controlling the target or representations are equivalent.

Diagnostic probes were originally intended to explain information encoded in intermediate representations (Adi etВ al., 2017; Alain and Bengio, 2017; Belinkov etВ al., 2017).Recently, various probing tasks have queried the representations of, e.g., contextualized word embeddings (Tenney etВ al., 2019a, b) and sentence embeddings (Linzen etВ al., 2016; Chen etВ al., 2019; Alt etВ al., 2020; Kassner and SchГјtze, 2020; Maudslay etВ al., 2020; Chi etВ al., 2020).

The task of probing is usually formulated as a classifier problem, with the representations as input, and the features indicating information as output. A straightforward method to train a classifier is by minimizing cross entropy, which is the approach we follow. Note that Voita and Titov (2020) derived training objectives from minimum description lengths, resulting in cross entropy loss and some variants.
