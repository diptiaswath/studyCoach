Diverse real-world systems can be abstracted as complex networks, each of which can be represented by a graph 𝒢=(𝒱,ℰ)𝒢𝒱ℰ\mathcal{G}=(\mathcal{V},\mathcal{E}) with the node set 𝒱𝒱\mathcal{V} and the edge set ℰℰ\mathcal{E}. For example, a node vi∈𝒱subscript𝑣𝑖𝒱v_{i}\in\mathcal{V} in a computer network can be a router; while an edge ei​jsubscript𝑒𝑖𝑗e_{ij} symbolizes a fiber link connecting two routers visubscript𝑣𝑖v_{i} and vjsubscript𝑣𝑗v_{j}. As functional components, failures of nodes and/or edges could severely deteriorate the system normal operations. A typical example was the Italian nation-wide blackout on September 28 2003 due to cascading failures of power stations starting from a single powerline (Crucitti et al., 2004). Another recent example was the outage of the Internet service of the American operator Century Link (Jazmin, 2020) on August 30, 2020 arising from a single router malfunction.

In the domain of network science (Boccaletti et al., 2006; Strogatz, 2001; Albert et al., 2000), percolation theory (Almeira et al., 2020; Radicchi, 2015) has shown that the failure of a fraction of nodes would not only cause the damage of their local structures, but also introduce some cascading failure, even leading to the collapse of a whole network. An interesting observation is a kind of phase transition phenomenon, that is, the network connectivity would experience an abrupt change when the number of simultaneously failed nodes reaching some threshold. These findings not only have been reported by physics scientists, but also have motivated researchers in other domains about the following network dismantling problem, that is, how to particularly attack a set of nodes to dismantle a network into many disconnected tiny subnetworks.

Following the literature (Liu and Wang, 2022; Wandelt et al., 2018; Braunstein et al., 2016), the network dismantling (ND) problem can be formally defined as to find a target attack node set (TAS), denoted by 𝒱tsubscript𝒱𝑡\mathcal{V}_{t} (𝒱t⊆𝒱subscript𝒱𝑡𝒱\mathcal{V}_{t}\subseteq\mathcal{V}), for its removal leading to the disintegration of a network into many disconnected components, among which the giant connected component (GCC), denoted as 𝒢tsubscript𝒢𝑡\mathcal{G}_{t}, is smaller than a predefined threshold ΘΘ\Theta. Formally, the objective of network dismantling is to find such a TAS 𝒱∗⊆𝒱superscript𝒱𝒱\mathcal{V}^{*}\subseteq\mathcal{V} with the minimum size, that is,(1)𝒱∗≡min⁡{𝒱t⊆𝒱:|𝒢t|/|𝒢|≤Θ}.superscript𝒱:subscript𝒱𝑡𝒱subscript𝒢𝑡𝒢Θ\mathcal{V}^{*}\equiv\min\big{\{}\mathcal{V}_{t}\subseteq\mathcal{V}:|\mathcal{G}_{t}|/|\mathcal{G}|\leq\Theta\big{\}}.The ND problem has been proven as non-deterministic polynomial hard (NP-hard) (Braunstein et al., 2016).

For its NP-hardness, many heuristic solutions have been proposed for dismantling large networks (Mugisha and Zhou, 2016; Fan et al., 2020; Grassia et al., 2021). Most of them employ some technique to measure and rank the importance of a node to network topological stability and select the top-ranked nodes into a TAS until reaching the GCC threshold. For example, many network centrality measures have been used to rank nodes, like the degree (Albert et al., 2000), betweeness centrality (Freeman, 1977), closeness centrality (Bavelas, 1950), harmonic centrality (Boldi and Vigna, 2014), eigenvector centrality (Bonacich, 1987) and etc. In the last two years, a few studies have employed deep neural networks to learn node representations for node ranking and network dismantling (Liu and Wang, 2022; Zhang and Wang, 2022; Fan et al., 2020). These ND-oriented neural networks have reported to achieve better performance over traditional centrality-based approaches.

Inspired by the recent successes, we would like to further investigate the potentials of neural networks for the ND problem. We note that both the ND-oriented neural models of NEES (Liu and Wang, 2022) and NIRM (Zhang and Wang, 2022) mainly focus on encoding nodes’ topological characteristics yet with few attention on encoding their functional capabilities. Instead, we argue that both should be encoded for ranking nodes to more efficiently dismantle a network. In this paper, we argue that network robustness can be inspected from two aspects: functional stability and topological integrity. We need to encode both functional capability of a node and its topological characteristics for node ranking and network dismantling. We further elaborate our motivations as follows.

We regard the functional capability of a node as its diffusion competence, e.g., its competence in relaying information flows in computer networks (or vehicle flows in road networks, etc.). It is well-known that information transmission often follows the rule of shortest-path from a source to a destination for the sake of efficiency. The network-wide functional efficiency can be well maintained, if the shortest-path between any source-destination pair is small enough. Indeed, the betweenness centrality (BC) is based on such philosophy,111The Betweenness Centrality (BC) measures the importance of a node by the ratio of number of shortest-paths passing through a node to network-wide all shortest-paths. The BC computation is often with prohibitive cost especially in large networks due to the network-wide shortest-path computation.  and a node with a high BC value suggests its pivotal importance for network-wide information diffusion. Inspired by the BC, we argue that the diffusion competence of a node can be measured from its importance for relaying information. Unlike the BC, we propose a graph diffusion network (GDN) to encode node diffusion competence.

We regard the topological characteristics of a node as its role significance in the whole network, which is not only determined by its egonet structural pattern but also the significance of such an egonet pattern compared with others. We refer the egonet of a node as its one-hop neighbors together with their edges connecting to the node. It is common that many egonets share with similar or even the same structure, such as the star and triangle structure. On the one hand, the egonet pattern often indicates the importance of the node to its regional structure stability, like the removal of a star center resulting disconnected neighbors in the egonet. Considering many possible egonet patterns, we can group them into only a few roles. On the other hand, two nodes of the same role, i.e., with similar egonet patterns, may be farther away to each other in a network, which makes it difficult to further distinguish their significance to the topological integrity of the whole network. In this paper, we propose to construct a kind of role graph on which to encode role significance for each node. Fig. 1 illustrates the roles discovered by the RoIX algorithm (Henderson et al., 2012), where the aforementioned phenomena can be observed.

In light of the above, we propose a network representation learning framework, called DCRS, to encode each node Diffusion Competence and Role Significance for scoring and ranking node importance to network robustness, by which a TAS is constructed for network dismantling. In particular, we design a GDN neural module to encode each node a diffusion competence representation that will be converted to its diffusion score via a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) network. We construct a role graph based on nodes’ role similarities, on which we adopt a Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) to encode each node a role significance representation and another MLP for converting to its role score. Finally, we use a gate unit to fuse the two scores into a dismantling score for each node. Experiments on both real-world networks and synthetic networks validate the superiority of our DCRS over the state-of-the-art competitors in terms of smaller TAS size in most cases.

The remainder is organized as follows: Section 2 reviews the related work. Our DCRS scheme is presented in Section 3 and experimented in Section 4. The paper is concluded in Section 5 with some discussions.

Most existing solutions of estimating node importance is to first compute some centrality metric for each node, and then rank nodes and select the top-K𝐾K important nodes to form a TAS. Degree centrality (DC) (Albert et al., 2000) counts the number of neighbors, and Collective Influence (CI) (Morone and Makse, 2015) is proposed as an improved version of DC, considering its multi-hop neighbors’ degrees. Some path-based centralities focus on global topological properties. Betweeness centrality (BC) (Freeman, 1977) evaluates the importance of relaying information flows, and Closeness centrality (CC) (Bavelas, 1950) describes the average distance of the node from other nodes. Harmonic Centrality (HC) (Boldi and Vigna, 2014) is the sum of the reciprocal of the shortest path distances from all other nodes to the given node. In addition, Eigenvector centrality (EC) (Bonacich, 1987) and PageRank (PR) (Page et al., 1999) belong to the iterative refinement centralities: the former is determined by the number and importance of neighbors; While the latter applies the random walk with restart to obtain convergence probabilities as node importance measures.

Recently, many deep neural networks have been proposed to learn node embedding for various downstream tasks, like the node classification and link prediction (Henderson et al., 2012; Ribeiro et al., 2017; Rossi et al., 2018; Zhang and Kou, 2022). Although they are not designed for the ND task, we can convert nodes’ embeddings into dismantling scores for ranking and selecting nodes by some appropriate objective function. We will experiment a few classic network embedding schemes with our objective function.

In the last two years, a few ND-oriented neural models have been designed, including the FINDER (Fan et al., 2020), NEES (Liu and Wang, 2022) and NIRM (Zhang and Wang, 2022). The FINDER adopts a reinforcement learning framework and enables a kind of reinsertion operation, that is, heuristically reinserting already selected attack nodes into the original network. This paper focuses on the one-pass network dismantling task without reinsertion, that is, all nodes in a TAS are considered to be attacked at the same time. Both the NEES and NIRM are designed for one-pass network dismantling: The NEES (Liu and Wang, 2022) hierarchically merges some compact substructures to convert a network into a coarser one with fewer nodes and edges, during which nodes’ importance to network robustness are also learned. The NIRM (Zhang and Wang, 2022) designs a neural network to encode nodes’ topological characteristics, which is trained by tiny synthetic network instances with their novel label propagation algorithm. We will compare their performance with ours in the experiment section.

Node role significance is used to evaluate the topological characteristics to network robustness. Starting from the egonet of a node, we apply the RoIX (Henderson et al., 2012) to classify nodes into a few roles. Based on the node role, we propose to construct a role graph on which to encode role representation for each node.

We compare our DCRS against 14 competent algorithms that all rank node importance for selecting the top-K𝐾K ones as attack nodes. These algorithms can be generally classified into two categories:

Node centrality-based approaches:Many classic centralities can be used for network dismantling.•DC (Degree Centrality) (Albert et al., 2000) remove nodes in the order of node degree.•BC (Betweeness Centrality) (Freeman, 1977) calculates the frequency of all-pairs shortest-paths through a node.•CC (Closeness Centrality) (Bavelas, 1950) is the reciprocal of the average shortest path distance between the node and other nodes.•EC (Eigenvector Centrality) (Bonacich, 1987) is based on the eigenvectors of the adjacency matrix.•HC (Harmonic Centrality) (Boldi and Vigna, 2014) is a variant of the CC algorithm that can be applied to disconnected original network.•CI (Collective Influence) (Morone and Makse, 2015) measures a node importance by considering the high-order local structure centered at the node.•PR (PageRank) (Page et al., 1999) is based on the random walk with restart.

Node encoding-based approaches:We explore the capabilities of neural networks for node encoding on the ND task.•DW (DeepWalk) (Perozzi et al., 2014) is built upon the skip-gram model to learn nodes’ embeddings.•NV (Node2Vec) (Grover and Leskovec, 2016) extends DeepWalk with biased second order random walks to explore the high-oder neighbors.•RV (Role2Vec) (Ahmed et al., 2022) adopts attributed random walks to learn structural role-based embeddings.•GCN (Kipf and Welling, 2017) is based on message passing and aggregation to learn nodes’ embeddings.•GAT (Veličković et al., 2018) extends the GCN by leveraging the masked self-attention mechanism for nodes’ embedding.•NIRM (Zhang and Wang, 2022) is a state-of-the-art neural model particularly designed for the ND task, which encodes both local structural and high-order topological characteristics for scoring and ranking nodes.•NEES (Liu and Wang, 2022) is another state-of-the-art neural model for the ND task, which hierarchically merges compact substructures and extract essential structures to learn and fuse nodes’ importance at different scales.Note that except NIRM and NEES, others are for network representation learning. To suit the ND task, for the GCN and GAT we add a final linear layer that takes the nodes’ embedding as input and outputs scores. For DW, NW and RV, we train a MLP for each of them to convert nodes’ embeddings to scores. They all adopt the same objective function as our model.

This paper has designed a DCRS neural model for ranking and selecting attack nodes to dismantle a network. We have designed a graph diffusion neural network to encode node diffusion competence for measuring node importance to network functional stability, and have proposed to construct a role graph on which to encode node role significance for measuring node importance to network topological integrity. A new objective function has been designed for training the proposed neural model. Experiments on real-world and synthetic networks have validated the superiority of our model over the state-of-the-art competitors in terms of its mostly requiring much fewer attack nodes for network dismantling.

Given its NP-hardness, we acknowledge that our solution to the network dismantling problem is still far from perfection. For example, how to encode regional structural patterns with high-order egonets and how to fast construct the role graph for a very large network still remain further investigations.
