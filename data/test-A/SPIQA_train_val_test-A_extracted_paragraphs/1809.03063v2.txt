Learning how to classify instances based on labeled examples is a fundamental task in machine learning. The goal is to find, with high probability, the correct label c​(x)𝑐𝑥c(x) of a given test instance x𝑥x coming from a distribution 𝛍𝛍{\bm{\upmu}}. Thus, we would like to find a good-on-average “hypothesis” hℎh (also called the trained model) that minimizes the error probability Prx←𝛍⁡[h​(x)≠c​(x)]subscriptPr←𝑥𝛍ℎ𝑥𝑐𝑥\Pr_{x\leftarrow{\bm{\upmu}}}[h(x)\neq c(x)], which is referred to as the risk of hℎh with respect to the ground truth c𝑐c. Due to the explosive use of learning algorithms in real-world systems (e.g., using neural networks for image classification) a more modern approach to the classification problem aims at making the learning process, from training till testing, more robust. Namely, even if the instance x𝑥x is perturbed in a limited way into x′superscript𝑥′x^{\prime} by an adversary A𝐴A, we would like to have the hypothesis hℎh still predict the right label for x′superscript𝑥′x^{\prime}; hence, minimizing the “adversarial risk”Prx←𝛍⁡[h​(x′)≠c​(x′)​ for some ​x′​ “close” to ​x]subscriptPr←𝑥𝛍ℎsuperscript𝑥′𝑐superscript𝑥′ for some superscript𝑥′ “close” to 𝑥\Pr_{x\leftarrow{\bm{\upmu}}}[h(x^{\prime})\neq c(x^{\prime})\text{ for some }x^{\prime}\text{ ``close'' to }x]of the hypothesis hℎh under such perturbations, where “close” is defined by a metric. An attack to increase the risk is called an “evasion attack” (see e.g., [8, 14]) due to the fact that x′superscript𝑥′x^{\prime} “evades” the correct classification.One major motivation behind this problem comes from scenarios such as image classification, in which the adversarially perturbed instance x′superscript𝑥′x^{\prime} would still “look similar” to the original x𝑥x, at least in humans’ eyes, even though the classifier hℎh might now misclassify x′superscript𝑥′x^{\prime} [28]. In fact, starting with the work of Szegedy et al. [48] an active line of research (e.g., see [5, 8, 29, 42, 14, 53])investigated various attacks and possible defenses to resist such attacks. The race between attacks and defenses in this area motivates a study of whether or not such robust classifiers could ever be found, if they exist at all.

A closely related notion of robustness for a learning algorithm deals with the training phase. Here, we would like to know how much the risk of the produced hypothesis hℎh might increase, if an adversary A𝐴A tampers with the training data 𝒯𝒯{\mathcal{T}}with the goal of increasing the “error” (or any “bad” event in general) during the test phase.Such attacks are referred to as poisoning attacks [9, 52, 47, 36, 51], and the line of research on the power and limitations of poisoning attacks contains numerous attacks and many defenses designed (usually specifically) against them (e.g., see [1, 52, 47, 41, 44, 13, 19, 36, 20, 18, 43, 21, 16, 17] and references therein).

The state of affairs in attacks and defenses with regard to the robustness of learning systems in both the evasion and poisoning contexts leads us to our main question:What are the inherent limitations of defense mechanisms for evasion and poisoning attacks? Equivalently, what are the inherent power of such attacks?Understanding the answer to the above question is fundamental for finding the right bounds that robust learning systems can indeed achieve, and achieving such bounds would be the next natural goal.

In the context of evasion attacks, the most relevant to our main question above are the recent works of Gilmer et al. [27], Fawzi et al. [23], and Diochnos et al. [22]. In all of these works, isoperimetric inequalities for specific metric probability spaces (i.e., for uniform distributions over the n𝑛n-sphere by [27], for isotropic n𝑛n-Gaussian by [23], and for uniform distribution over the Boolean hypercube by [22]) were used to prove that problems on such input spaces are always vulnerable to adversarial instances.111More formally, Gilmer et al. [27] designed specific problems over (two) n𝑛n-spheres, and proved them to be hard to learn robustly, but their proof extend to any problem defined over the uniform distribution over the n𝑛n-sphere. Also, Fawzi et al. [23] used a different notion of adversarial risk that only considers the hypothesis hℎh and is independent of the ground truth c𝑐c, however their proofs also extend to the same setting as ours. The work of Schmidt et al. [45] shows that, at least in some cases, being robust to adversarial instances requires more data. However, the work of Bubeck et al. [12] proved that assuming the existence of classifiers that are robust to evasion attacks, they could be found by “few” training examples in an information theoretic way.

In the context of poisoning attacks, some classical results about malicious noise [50, 31, 7] could be interpreted as limitations of learning under poisoning attacks. On the positive (algorithmic) side, the works of Diakonikolas et al. [15] and Lia et al. [35] showed the surprising power of algorithmic robust inference over poisoned data with error that does not depend on the dimension of the distribution. These works led to an active line of work (e.g., see [13, 19, 20, 18, 43, 21] and references therein) exploring the possibility of robust statistics over poisoned data with algorithmic guarantees. The works of [13, 20] showed how to do list-docodable learning, and [18, 43] studied supervised learning.

Demonstrating the power of poisoning attacks, Mahmoody and Mahloujifar [37] showed that, assuming an initial Ω​(1)Ω1\Omega(1) error, a variant of poisoning attacks that tamper with ≈pabsent𝑝\approx p fraction of the training data without using wrong labels (called p𝑝p-tampering) could always increase the error of deterministic classifiers by Ω​(p)Ω𝑝\Omega(p) in the targeted poisoning model [10] where the adversary knows the final test instance. Then Mahloujifar et al. [36] improved the quantitative bounds of [37] and also applied those attacks to degrade the confidence parameter of any PAC learners under poisoning attacks. Both attacks of [37, 36] were online, in the sense that the adversary does not know the future examples, and as we will see their attack model is very relevant to this work. Koh and Liang [32] studied finding training examples with most influence over the final decision over a test instance x𝑥x–enabling poisoning attacks. Here, we prove the existence of O​(m)𝑂𝑚O(\sqrt{m}) examples in the training set that can almost fully degrade the final decision on x𝑥x, assuming Ω​(1)Ω1\Omega(1) initial error on x𝑥x.

The work of Bousquet and Elisseeff [6] studied how specific forms of stability of the hypothesis (which can be seen as robustness under weak forms of “attacks” that change one training example) imply standard generalization (under no attack). Our work, on the other hand, studies generalization under attack while the adversary can perturb a lot more (but still sublinear) part of instances.

The works of Madry et al. [38] and Schmidt et al. [45] employ an alternative definition of adversarial risk inspired by robust optimization. This definition is reminiscent of the definition of “corrupted inputs” used by Feige et al. [24] (and related works of [39, 25, 2]) as in all of these works, a “successful” adversarial example x′superscript𝑥′x^{\prime} shall have a prediction h​(x′)ℎsuperscript𝑥′h(x^{\prime}) that is different from the true label of the original (uncorrupted) instance x𝑥x. However, such definitions based on corrupted instances do not always guarantee that the adversarial examples are misclassified. In fact, even going back to the original definitions of adversarial risk and robustness from [48], many papers (e.g., the related work of [23]) only compare the prediction of the hypothesis over the adversarial example with its own prediction on the honest example, and indeed ignore the ground truth defined by the concept c𝑐c.)In various “natural” settings (such as image classification) the above two definition and ours coincide. We refer the reader to the work of Diochnos et al. [22] where these definitions are compared and a taxonomy is given, which we will use here as well. See Appendix A for more details.

In this work, we draw a connection between the general phenomenon of “concentration of measure” in metric measured spaces and both evasion and poisoning attacks. Indeed, one natural way of proving concentration results is to use isoperimetric inequalities that characterize the shape of sets with minimal boundaries (and thus minimal measure after expansion). However, we emphasize that bounds on concentration of measure could be proved even if no such isoperimetric inequalities are known, and e.g., approximate versions of such inequalities would also be sufficient. Indeed, in addition to proofs by isoperimetric inequalities, concentration of measure results are proved using tools from various fields such as differential geometry, bounds on eigenvalues of the Laplacian, martingale methods, etc, [40]. Thus, by proving Theorem 1.1, we pave the way for a wide range of results against robust classification for learning problems over any concentrated space. To compare, the results of [27, 23, 22] have better constants due to their use of isoperimetric inequalities, while we achieve similar asymptotic bounds with worse constants but in broader contexts.

We also prove variants of Theorem 1.1 that deal with the average amount of perturbation done by the adversary with the goal of changing the test instance x𝑥x into a misclassified x′superscript𝑥′x^{\prime}. Since a big motivation for studying the hardness of classifiers against adversarial perturbations comes from the challenges that have emerged in the area of image classifications, here we comment on possible ideas from our work that might be useful for such studies. Indeed, a natural possible approach is to study whether or not the metric measure space of the images is concentrated or not. We leave such studies for interesting future work. Furthermore, the work of [23] observed that vulnerability to adversarial instances over “nice” distributions (e.g., n𝑛n-Gaussian in their work, and any concentrated distribution in our work) can potentially imply attacks on real data assuming that the data is generated with a smooth generative model using the mentioned nice distributions. So, as long as one such mapping could be found for a concentrated space, our impossibility results can potentially be used for deriving similar results about the generated data (in this case image classification) as well.

One natural family of metric probability spaces for which Theorem 1.1 entails new impossibility results are product measure spaces under Hamming distance.
