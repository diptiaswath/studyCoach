“No man is an island, entire of itself …”, the beginning of a well-known poem by the 17th century English poet John Donne, might be able to explain why social networking websites are so popular.These social media not only make communications convenient and enrich our lives but also bring us data, of an unimaginable amount, that is intrinsically networked.Social network data nowadays is widely used in research on social science, network dynamics, and as an inevitable fate, data mining and machine learning (?).Similar examples of networked data such as traffic networks (?), chemical interaction networks (?), citation networks (?) also abound throughout the machine learning world.

Admittedly, many efforts have been made to design practical algorithms for learning from networked data, e.g., (?; ?; ?; ?).However, not many theoretical guarantees of these methods have been established, which is the main concern of this paper. More specifically, this paper deals with risk bounds of classifiers trained with networked data (Clanet) whose goal is to train a classifier with examples in a data graph G𝐺G.Every vertex of G𝐺G is an object and described by a feature vector X∈𝒳𝑋𝒳X\in\mathcal{X}{} that is drawn independently and identically (i.i.d.) from an unknown distribution, while every edge corresponds to a training example whose input is a pair of feature vectors (X,X′)𝑋superscript𝑋′(X,X^{\prime}) of the two ends of this edge and whose target value Y𝑌Y is in {0,1}01\{0,1\}.

A widely used principle to select a proper model from a hypothesis set is Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM). ? (?) establish risk bounds for ERM on complete data graphs, and the bounds are independent of the distribution of the data.These bounds are of the order O​(log⁡(n)/n)𝑂𝑛𝑛O(\log(n)/n), where n𝑛n is the number of vertices in the complete graph.However, in practice it is very likely that one cannot collect examples for all pairs of vertices and then G𝐺G is usually incomplete, thus techniques based on complete U𝑈U-processes in (?) cannot be applied and the risk bounds of the order O​(log⁡(n)/n)𝑂𝑛𝑛O(\log(n)/n) are no longer valid in this setting.By generalizing the moment inequality for U𝑈U-processes to the case of incomplete graphs, we prove novel risk bounds for the incomplete graph.

We show that the “low-noise” condition, also called the Mammen-Tsybakov noise condition (?), which is commonly assumed in many typical learning problems with networked data, e.g., ranking (?) and graph reconstruction (?), can be used to reasonably bound the dependencies of two examples that share a common vertex and then leads to tighter risk bounds.

Compared to Janson’s decomposition which is based on line graphs, our method utilizes the additional dependency information in the data graph G𝐺G.For example, the complete line graph with three vertices (i.e., triangle) corresponds to two different data graphs, as illuminated in Figure 1.Hence, line graph based methods ignore some important information in the data graph.This negligence makes it unable to improve bounds, no matter whether considering weighted ERM or not (see Section A.1 in the online appendix).In Section 6.2, we show that our bounds are tighter than that of line graph based methods.

In this paper, we consider weighted ERM of the symmetric Clanet problem and establish new universal risk bounds under the “low-noise” condition.These new bounds are tighter in the case of incomplete graphs and can be degenerate to the known tightest bound when graphs are complete.Based on this result, one can train a classifier with a better risk bound by putting proper weights on training examples.We propose an efficient algorithm to obtain the approximate optimal weighting vector and prove that the algorithm is a FPTAS for the weighting vector optimization problem.Finally, we discuss two cases to show the merits of our new risk bounds.
