Much of the research in convex optimization has focused on the oracle model, where an algorithm optimizing some objective function f:ℝd→ℝ:𝑓→superscriptℝ𝑑ℝf:\mathbb{R}^{d}\rightarrow\mathbb{R} does so by sequential interaction with, e.g., a gradient oracle(given a query x∈ℝd𝑥superscriptℝ𝑑x\in\mathbb{R}^{d}, the oracle returns ∇f​(x)∇𝑓𝑥\nabla f(x)), (Nemirovski and Yudin, 1983; Nesterov, 2004; Bubeck, 2015).111Throughout we assume that f𝑓f is differentiable, though our results carry over to the case where f𝑓f is non-differentiable and given by a sub-gradient oracle. This generalization is immediate as our analysis and algorithms are stable under finite-precision arithmetic and convex functions are almost everywhere differentiable. In the early 1990s,Arkadi Nemirovski introduced the parallel version of this problem (Nemirovski, 1994): instead of submitting queries one by one sequentially, the algorithm can submit in parallel up to Q≥1𝑄1Q\geq 1 queries. We refer to the depth of such a parallel algorithm as the number of rounds of interaction with the oracle, and the work as the total number of queries (in particular work ≤Q×\leq Q\times depth). In this paper we study the optimal depth achievable for highly parallel algorithms, namely we consider the regime Q=poly​(d)𝑄poly𝑑Q=\mathrm{poly}(d). We focus on non-smooth convex optimization, that is we want to optimize a Lipschitz, convex function f𝑓f on the unit Euclidean ball.

Our key result is a new form a quadratic acceleration: while for purely sequential methods the critical depth at which one can improve upon local search is O~​(d)~𝑂𝑑\widetilde{O}(d), we show that in the highly parallel regime the critical depth is O~​(d)~𝑂𝑑\widetilde{O}(\sqrt{d}).

Classically, when Q=1𝑄1Q=1, it is known that gradient descent’s query complexity is order optimal for any target accuracy ε𝜀\varepsilon in the range [d−1/2,1]superscript𝑑121\left[d^{-1/2},1\right].More precisely, it is known that the query complexity of gradient descent is O​(1/ε2)𝑂1superscript𝜀2O(1/\varepsilon^{2}) and that for any ε𝜀\varepsilon in the range [d−1/2,1]superscript𝑑121\left[d^{-1/2},1\right], and for any algorithm, there exists a Lipschitz and convex function f𝑓f on which the number of oracle queries the algorithm makes to achieve additive ε𝜀\varepsilon accuracy is Ω​(1/ε2)Ω1superscript𝜀2\Omega(1/\varepsilon^{2}).Furthermore, whenever ε𝜀\varepsilon is smaller than d−1/2superscript𝑑12d^{-1/2} there exists a better algorithm (i.e., with smaller depth), namely the center of gravity whose depth is O​(d​log⁡(1/ε))𝑂𝑑1𝜀O(d\log(1/\varepsilon)). Consequently, an alternative formulation of these results is that, for Q=1𝑄1Q=1, gradient descent is order optimal if and only if the depth is smaller than O~​(d)~𝑂𝑑\widetilde{O}(d). (See previously cited references for the exact statements.)

The main result of this paperis to show that in the highly parallel regime (Q=poly​(d)𝑄poly𝑑Q=\mathrm{poly}(d)), gradient descent is order optimal if and only if the depth is smaller than O~​(d)~𝑂𝑑\widetilde{O}(\sqrt{d}). Thus one has a “quadratic" improvement over the purely sequential setting in terms of the critical depth at which naive local search becomes suboptimal.

The only if part of the above statement follows from Duchi et al. (2012), where randomized smoothing with accelerated gradient descent was proposed (henceforth referred to as distributed randomized smoothing (Scaman et al., 2018)), and shown to achieve depth d1/4/εsuperscript𝑑14𝜀d^{1/4}/\varepsilon, which is order better than 1/ε21superscript𝜀21/\varepsilon^{2} exactly when the latter is equal to d𝑑\sqrt{d}.A first key contribution of our workis a matching lower bound showing that, when the depth is smaller than O~​(d)~𝑂𝑑\widetilde{O}(\sqrt{d}), no significant improvement over gradient descent is possible, i.e., Q=1𝑄1Q=1 and Q=poly​(d)𝑄poly𝑑Q=\mathrm{poly}(d) have essentially the same power. Importantly we note that our lower bound applies to randomized algorithms. The previous state of the art lower bound was that gradient descent is optimal up to depth O~​(d1/3)~𝑂superscript𝑑13\widetilde{O}(d^{1/3}) (Balkanski and Singer, 2018). In fact the construction in the latter paper is exactly the same as the original construction of Nemirovski in (Nemirovski, 1994) (however the final statements are different, as Nemirovski was concerned with an ℓ∞subscriptℓ\ell_{\infty} setting instead of ℓ2subscriptℓ2\ell_{2}, see also Diakonikolas and Guzmán (2018) for more results about non-Euclidean setups).

A second key contribution of this work is to improve the state of the art complexity of parallel algorithms with depth between d𝑑\sqrt{d} and d𝑑d. Improving the depth d1/4/εsuperscript𝑑14𝜀d^{1/4}/\varepsilon of Duchi et al. (2012) was explicitly mentioned as an open problem by Scaman et al. (2018). Leveraging the recent higher order acceleration schemes of Gasnikov et al. (2018); Jiang et al. (2018); Bubeck et al. (2018), we propose a new method with depth d1/3/ε2/3superscript𝑑13superscript𝜀23d^{1/3}/\varepsilon^{2/3}. This means that for any value of ε𝜀\varepsilon in the range [d−1,d−1/2]superscript𝑑1superscript𝑑12\left[d^{-1},d^{-1/2}\right] there is an algorithm that is order better than both gradient descent and center of gravity. We leave this question, as well as the question of the optimal work among optimal depth algorithms, for future works.

Though Nemirovski’s prescient work stood alone for decades, more recentlythe subfield of parallel/distributed optimization is booming,propelled by problems in machine learning, see e.g., (Boyd et al., 2011). Chief among those problems is how to leverage mini-batches in stochastic gradient descent as efficiently as possible (Dekel et al., 2012). The literature on this topic is sprawling, see for example (Duchi et al., 2018) which studies the total work achievable in parallel stochastic convex optimization, or (Zhang and Xiao, 2018) where the stochastic assumptions are leveraged to take advantage of second order information. More directly related to our work is (Nemirovski, 1994; Diakonikolas and Guzmán, 2018; Balkanski and Singer, 2018) from the lower bound side (we directly improve upon the result in the latter paper), and (Duchi et al., 2012; Scaman et al., 2018) from the upper bound side (we directly improve upon the depth provided by the algorithms in those works).

Throughout this section we let f:ℝd→ℝ:𝑓→superscriptℝ𝑑ℝf:\mathbb{R}^{d}\rightarrow\mathbb{R} denote a differentiable L𝐿L-Lipschitz function that obtains its minimum value at x∗∈ℝdsuperscript𝑥superscriptℝ𝑑x^{*}\in\mathbb{R}^{d} with ‖x∗‖2≤Rsubscriptnormsuperscript𝑥2𝑅\|x^{*}\|_{2}\leq R. The main result of this section is the following theorem, which provides an O~​(d1/3/ε2/3)~𝑂superscript𝑑13superscript𝜀23\widetilde{O}(d^{1/3}/\varepsilon^{2/3})-depth highly-parallel algorithm that computes an ε𝜀\varepsilon-optimal point with high probability.

Our starting point for obtaining this result are the O​(d1/4/ε)𝑂superscript𝑑14𝜀O(d^{1/4}/\varepsilon) depth highly parallel algorithms of (Duchi et al., 2012). This paper considers the convolution of f𝑓f with simple functions, e.g. Gaussians and uniform distributions, and shows this preserves the convexity and continuity of f𝑓f while improving the smoothness and thereby enables methods like accelerated gradient descent (AGD) to run efficiently. Since the convolved function can be accessed efficiently in parallel by random sampling, working with the convolved function is comparable to working with the original function in terms of query depth (up to the sampling error). Consequently, the paper achieves its depth bound by trading off the error induced by convolution with the depth improvements gained from stochastic variants of AGD.

To improve upon this bound, we apply a similar approach of working with the convolution of f𝑓f with a Gaussian. However, instead of applying standard stochastic AGD we consider accelerated methods which build a more sophisticated model of the convolved function in parallel. Instead of using random sampling to approximate only the gradient of the convolved function, we obtain our improvements by using random sampling to glean more local information with each highly-parallel query and then use this to minimize the convolved function at an accelerated rate.

To enable the use of these more sophisticated models we develop a general acceleration framework that allows us to leverage any subroutine for approximate minimization a local model/approximate gradient computations into an accelerated minimization scheme. We believe this framework is of independent interest, as we show that we can analyze the performance of this method just in terms of simple quantities regarding the local model. This framework is discussed in Section 3.1 and in Appendix C where we show how it generalizes multiple previous results on near-optimal acceleration.

Using this framework, proving Theorem 4 reduces to showing that we can minimize high quality local models of the convolved function. Interestingly, it is possible to nearly obtain this result by simply random sampling to estimate all derivatives up to some order k𝑘k and then use this to minimize a regularized k𝑘k-th order Taylor approximation to the function. Near-optimal convergence for such methods under Lipschitz bounds on the k𝑘k-th derivatives were recently given by (Gasnikov et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2018; Bubeck et al., 2018) (and follow from our framework). This approach can be shown to give a highly-parallel algorithm of depth O~​(d1/3+c/ε2/3)~𝑂superscript𝑑13𝑐superscript𝜀23\widetilde{O}(d^{1/3+c}/\varepsilon^{2/3}) for any c>0𝑐0c>0 (with an appropriately large k𝑘k). Unfortunately, the work of these methods is O​(dpoly​(1/c))𝑂superscript𝑑poly1𝑐O(d^{\mathrm{poly}(1/c)}) and expensive for small c𝑐c.

To overcome this limitation, we leverage the full power of our acceleration framework and instead show that we can randomly sample to build a model of the convolved function accurate within a ball of sufficiently large radius. In Section 3.2 we bound this quality of approximation and show that this local model can be be optimized to sufficient accuracy efficiently. By combining this result with our framework we prove Theorem 4. We believe this demonstrates the utility of our general acceleration scheme and we plan to further explore its implications in future work.
