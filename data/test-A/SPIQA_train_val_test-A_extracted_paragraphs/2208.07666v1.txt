\section{Introduction}
Assigning indivisible items to agents with preferences is one of the most fundamental problems in computer science and economics~\cite{AGT,rothe2015}.
Examples of such problems include university housing assignment, student course placement, employee shift assignments, and professional sports drafts.
In this kind of problems, we are given a set of agents and a set of indivisible items, and each agent has a strict ordinal preference over the items. 
The goal of the problem is to find an assignment that satisfies efficiency and fairness with regard to the cardinal preferences behind the ordinal ones.
In practice, elicitation of the precise cardinal preferences may be difficult.
This motivates us to make the common assumption that the only ordinal information on preferences is available, which is often the case.


Randomization is frequently used to achieve both efficiency and fairness when assigning indivisible items.
The standard method for defining efficiency and fairness in random assignments with ordinal preferences is to use stochastic dominance (SD) relation.
An agent prefers one random assignment over another with respect to the SD relation if she obtains at least as much utility from the former assignment as the latter for all possible cardinal utilities consistent with the ordinal preference.

We consider \emph{sd-efficiency} as an efficiency concept, which states that no agent can be made better off without making at least one other agent worse off with respect to the SD relation. The sd-efficiency means efficiency in the ex ante sense and also leads to efficiency in the ex post sense.
Additionally, as a concept of fairness, we consider \emph{sd-envy-freeness}, which states that every agent prefers her assignment to that of every other agent with respect to the SD relation. 
Note that the sd-envy-freeness guarantees fairness in the ex ante sense, but not in the ex post sense. The ex post unfairness is inevitable in assignment of indivisible items.
For other efficiency and fairness criteria, see, e.g., \cite{AGMW2015}.


In a random assignment problem in which each agent receives one object, \citet{BM2001} proposed the \emph{probabilistic serial} mechanism.
The mechanism outputs a random assignment that is both sd-efficient and sd-envy-free.
\citet{kojima2009} generalized the result to the case where each agent can receive more than one item and the agents' preferences are additively separable over the items.
Note that these studies focused on the unconstrained case.

In this study, we consider a general setting in which each agent can consume a set of items only if it satisfies her feasibility constraints. 
We suppose that constraints satisfy the \emph{hereditary property}, that is, any subset of a feasible set is also feasible.
A matroid is a typical structure that possesses this property.
Matroids can represent various constraints that naturally occur in many assignment problems.
For example, in the context of weekly employee shift assignments, partition matroid can be used to represent a feasibility constraint if each employee can work at most one time slot in a day.
Even if each employee is additionally constrained to work at most five days a week, the feasibility constraint is still a matroid (for the formal definition, see Section~\ref{sec:model}).


We identify the condition under which an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment exists. Our contributions are detailed below.
\subsection{Our contributions}
We investigate the existence of sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignments in 16 settings according whether: 
(i) the number of agents is $2$ or arbitrary $n$,
(ii) the constraints are matroids or general hereditary constraints,
(iii) the constraints of the agents are identical or heterogeneous, and 
(iv) the ordinal preferences of the agents are identical or heterogeneous.

We demonstrate the impossibility of an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment even when there are either
\begin{itemize}
    \item $2$ agents with identical preferences
    (Theorem~\ref{thm:2-gen-het-id}) or 
    \item $3$ agents with identical partition matroid constraints
    (Theorem~\ref{thm:3-mat-id-het}).
\end{itemize}
As tractability results, we demonstrate that an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment always exists when there are
\begin{itemize}
    \item $2$ agents with matroid constraints
    (Theorem~\ref{thm:2-mat-het-het}),
    \item agents with identical preferences and heterogeneous matroid constraints
    (Theorem~\ref{thm:n-mat-het-id}), or 
    \item identical agents 
    (Theorem~\ref{thm:n-gen-id-id}).
\end{itemize}
Moreover, we provide polynomial-time algorithms that find desired assignments in the settings of Theorems~\ref{thm:2-mat-het-het} and \ref{thm:n-mat-het-id}.
By considering the inclusion relation, we obtain the results shown in Figure~\ref{fig:our_results}.

The existence of an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment is open when there are $2$ agents with identical constraints.
Meanwhile, we demonstrate that finding such an assignment is NP-hard (Theorem~\ref{thm:NPhard}).

\emph{Anonymity} is another fairness concept in which permuting agents' names does not affect the resulting outcome.
We show an anonymous mechanism that always produces an sd-efficient assignment (Theorem~\ref{thm:anonymous}).

This study does not address the strategic issue because no mechanism simultaneously satisfies sd-efficiency, sd-envy-freeness, and sd-weak-strategy-proofness~\cite{kojima2009}, even for $2$ agents with no constraints.
\subsection{Related work}
Random assignment problems under partition matroids constraints are studied as multi-type resource allocation problems~\cite{MT2015,MX2016,WSG+2020,GSW+2021}.
Compared to our study, these studies assume that ordinal preferences of agents over bundles are available.

Although we deal with constraints on the agent side, constraints on the item side have also been studied~\cite{FSZ2018,BCKM2013}.
\citet{FSZ2018} provided an extension of the probabilistic serial mechanism that outputs an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment if the set of feasible integral vectors of items forms an integral polymatroid.
Their proof heavily depends on the (generalized) Birkhoff--von Neumann theorem: every fractional assignment can be expressed as a probability distribution over deterministic assignments.
Note that the theorem also holds for our problem if the constraints are matroids.
This yields an expectation that the probabilistic serial mechanism produces an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment for the constraints are matroids, but it is not the case, as we will show in Theorem~\ref{thm:3-mat-id-het}.


Assignment (or matching) problems under constraints have recently been the subject of active research.
Examples of real-world applications in which constraints must be considered include refugee resettlement \citep{delacretaz2016refugee}, college admissions with budget constraints \citep{abizada2016stability}, school choice with bullying \citep{kasuya2016anti}, and day-care allocation \citep{okumura2019school}. 
\citet{ASS2020} provided a generalization of the probabilistic serial that satisfies desirable properties while satisfying the constraint with a small error.
\section{Model}
\label{sec:model}
For each nonnegative integer $k$, we define $[k]\coloneqq\{1,2,\dots,k\}$.
An instance of our problem is a tuple $(N,E,(\succ_i, \cF_i)_{i\in N})$, where
$N=[n]$ represents the set of agents and $E=\{e_1,e_2,\dots,e_m\}$ represents the set of indivisible items.
Each agent $i\in N$ has a strict preference $\succ_i$ over $E$ and can consume a set of items in $\cF_i\subseteq 2^E$, which is the feasible set family of $i$.
We assume that $\cF_i$ is given by a membership oracle for each $i\in N$.
The preferences over sets of items are additively separable across items, meaning that each agent $i$ has a cardinal utility function $u_i\colon E\to\mathbb{R}_{++}$, and when she receives $E'\in\cF_i$ her utility is $\sum_{e\in E'}u_i(e)$. Here, $\mathbb{R}_{++}$ is the set of positive real numbers.
We assume that the preference of each agent $i$ has no ties (i.e., $u_i(e)\neq u_i(e')$ for any distinct $e,e'\in E$) and that the central authority knows only the ordinal preferences $\succ_i$ that is consistent with $u_i$. That is, $\succ_i$ is a strict order on $E$ such that $e\succ_i e'$ if and only if $u_i(e)>u_i(e')$.

For each agent $i\in N$, we assume that the pair $(E, \cF_i)$ forms an \emph{independence system}: the feasible set family $\cF_i\subseteq 2^E$ is nonempty and satisfies the hereditary property, that is, $X\subseteq Y\in\cF_i$ implies $X\in\cF_i$. 
We denote the convex hull of the characteristic vectors of the members of $\cF_i$ by $\conv(\cF_i)$.
We note that $\conv(\cF_i) \subseteq[0,1]^E$. 
We will also consider a special case where each $(E,\cF_i)$ is a \emph{matroid}, which is an independence system satisfying a property called the \emph{augmentation axiom}: if $X,Y\in\cF_i$ and $|X|<|Y|$ then there exists $e\in X\setminus Y$ such that $X\cup\{e\}\in\cF_i$.
A simple example of a matroid is a {\em partition matroid}, which represents a constraint in which items are categorized and the number of items we can take from each category is constrained. More precisely, a partition matroid $(E, \cF)$ is determined by a partition $E_1, E_2,\dots, E_k$ of $E$ and capacities $q_1, q_2, \dots, q_k\in \mathbb{Z}_+$, and $\cF$ is of the form $\{X\subseteq E\mid |X\cap E_i|\leq q_i~(i \in [k])\}$.


A deterministic {\em assignment} is a list $\bX=(X_1,\dots,X_n)$ of subsets of $E$ such that 
(i) $X_i\in \cF_i$ for all $i\in N$ and
(ii) $X_i\cap X_j=\emptyset$ for all distinct $i,j\in N$.
Let $\cX$ be the set of all deterministic assignments.
A \emph{lottery assignment} is a probability distribution over $\cX$.
We will simply refer to a lottery assignment as an assignment when it is clear from the context.
We denote the set of all assignments by $\Delta(\cX)$.

A \emph{fractional assignment} is a matrix $\pi\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times E}$, where $\pi_{ie}$ is the probability that agent $i\in N$ receives item $e\in E$. 
For each $i\in N$, we denote the row in $\pi$ corresponding to agent $i$ by $\pi_i$, that is, $\pi_i = (\pi_{ie})_{e\in E} \in [0,1]^E$.
A lottery assignment $p\in \Delta(\cX)$ induces a fractional assignment $\pi^p\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times E}$ such that $\pi^p_{ie}=\Pr_{\bX\sim p}[e\in X_i]=\sum_{\bX\in\cX:\, e\in X_i}p_{\bX}$ for all $i\in N$ and $e\in E$.

Let $P\coloneqq \conv(\cX)\subseteq \mathbb{R}^{N\times E}$ be the convex hull of the characteristic vectors of the members of $\cX$.
By definition, a fractional assignment belongs to $P$ if and only if it is induced by an assignment.
A fractional assignment is said to be feasible if it belongs to $P$.
According to Carath\'eodory's theorem, there exists a corresponding lottery assignment with a support size of not more than $|N|\cdot |E|+1$ for any feasible fractional assignment~(\citealt[p.94]{Schrijver1998}).
\subsection{Desirable properties}
For a preference $\succ_i$, let $U(\succ_i,e)\coloneqq \{e'\in E\mid e'\succeq_i e\}$ be the set of items that are not worse than $e$ with respect to $\succ_i$.
We say that $x\in\mathbb{R}_+^E$ \emph{stochastically dominates} $y\in\mathbb{R}_+^E$, denoted by $x\succ_i^\sd y$, if $\sum_{e'\in U(\succ_i,e)} x_{e'}\ge \sum_{e'\in U(\succ_i,e)} y_{e'}$ for all $e\in E$ and the strict inequality holds for some element.
If $x\succ_i^\sd y$ or $x= y$, we say $x$ \emph{weakly stochastically dominates} $y$ and denote $x\succeq_i^\sd y$.
Note that $x$ stochastically dominates $y$ if and only if the expected utility of $x$ is greater than that of $y$ for any possible cardinal utility consistent with $\succ_i$.

A natural notion of efficiency is defined as Pareto-efficiency with respect to the SD relation.
\begin{definition}[sd-efficiency]
An assignment $p\in \Delta(\cX)$ is called \emph{sd-efficient} if
there is no other assignment $q\in\Delta(\mathcal{X})$ such that $\pi_i^q \succeq_i^\sd \pi_i^p$ for all $i\in N$.
\end{definition}
In other words, an assignment is sd-efficient if and only if it is Pareto-efficient for some possible cardinal utility consistent with $(\succ_i)_{i\in N}$.

A weaker notion of efficiency can be defined as an ex post sense.
An assignment $p\in \Delta(\cX)$ is called \emph{ex post efficient} if any $\bX\in \cX$ with $p_{\bX}>0$ is sd-efficient.
By definition, sd-efficiency implies ex post efficiency. On the other hand, ex post efficiency does not imply sd-efficiency as shown in Example~\ref{ex:simple} below.


The envy-freeness with respect to the SD relation is defined as follows.
\begin{definition}[sd-envy-freeness]\label{def:sd-envy-free}
An assignment $p\in\Delta(\cX)$ is called \emph{sd-envy-free} if
\[\sum_{e'\in U(\succ_i, e)}\pi^p_{ie'}\ge \sum_{\bX\in\cX}p_{\bX}\max_{Y\subseteq X_j:\,Y\in\cF_i}|Y\cap U(\succ_i, e)|\] 
for any $i,j\in N$ and $e\in E$.
Particularly, if the constraints are identical (i.e., $\cF_1=\dots=\cF_n$), an assignment $p\in\Delta(\cX)$ is sd-envy-free if
$\pi^p_i\succeq_i^\sd \pi^p_j$ for any $i,j\in N$.
\end{definition}
Definition~\ref{def:sd-envy-free} means that an assignment is sd-envy-free if and only if it is envy-free for every possible cardinal utility consistent with $(\succ_i)_{i\in N}$. 




First, we observe that ex post efficiency does not imply sd-efficiency even with two agents.
\begin{example}[\mbox{ex post efficiency does not imply sd-efficiency}]\label{ex:simple}
Consider an instance $(N,E,(\succ_i, \cF_i)_{i\in N})$ where
$N=\{1,2\}$, $E=\{a,b,c,d\}$, and $\cF_i=\{X\subseteq E \mid |X\cap\{c,d\}|\le 1 \text{ and }|X|\le 2\}$ and $a \succ_i b \succ_i c \succ_i d$ for both $i=1,2$.
Note that $(E,\cF_1)~(=(E,\cF_2))$ is a matroid.
Define three feasible fractional assignments as follows:
\begin{align}
\pi^{(1)}&=
\begin{pNiceMatrix}[first-row,first-col]
& a & b & c & d \\
1  & 1/2 & 1/2 & 1/2 & 1/2\\
2  & 1/2 & 1/2 & 1/2 & 1/2
\end{pNiceMatrix},\quad
\pi^{(2)}=
\begin{pNiceMatrix}[first-row,first-col]
& a & b & c & d \\
1  & 1 & 1 & 0 & 0\\
2  & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0
\end{pNiceMatrix},\quad
\pi^{(3)}=
\begin{pNiceMatrix}[first-row,first-col]
& a & b & c & d \\
1  & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0\\
2  & 1 & 1 & 0 & 0
\end{pNiceMatrix}.
\end{align}
Then, the three assignments are sd-efficient (and hence ex post efficient), and $\pi^{(1)}$ is sd-envy-free while $\pi^{(2)}$ and $\pi^{(3)}$ are not.
Note that the feasible fractional assignment $(\pi^{(2)}+\pi^{(3)})/2$ is ex-post efficient but not SD-efficient because it is dominated by $\pi^{(1)}$.
\end{example}


To achieve sd-efficiency and sd-envy-freeness in the current constrained setting, it may seem natural to consider the generalized version of the probabilistic serial mechanism in which each agent consumes the best remaining item while preserving feasibility. 
This na\"ive approach, however, does not always work as shown in the following example.
\begin{example}[probabilistic serial does not work]\label{ex:PSisBad}
Consider an instance $(N,E,(\succ_i, \cF_i)_{i\in N})$ where
$N=\{1,2\}$, $E=\{e_1,e_2,e_3,e_4,e_5,e_6,e_7\}$, $e_1\succ_i e_2\succ_i\dots\succ_i e_7~(i=1,2)$, 
\begin{align}
    \cF_1&=\{X\subseteq E\mid |X\cap\{e_1,e_2,e_3,e_5\}|\le 2\}, \text{and}\\
    \cF_2&=\{X\subseteq E\mid |X\cap\{e_1,e_2,e_3\}|\le 1\}.
\end{align}
Note that $(E,\cF_1)$ and $(E,\cF_2)$ are matroids.
For this instance, a generalized probabilistic serial mechanism outputs 
\[
\pi = 
\begin{pNiceMatrix}[first-col,first-row]
   & e_1 & e_2 & e_3 & e_4 & e_5 \\
1  & 1/2 & 1/2 & 1 & 0 & 0\\
2  & 1/2 & 1/2 & 0 & 1 & 1\\
\end{pNiceMatrix}
\]
However, this is not sd-envy-free because the total amount of items assigned to agent $2$ is larger than that of $1$, which causes $1$ to envy $2$.
\end{example}
\section{Related Properties of Matroids}
We introduce several matroid properties, which will be used in our analyses.

Let $(E,\cF)$ be a matroid and $\conv(\cF)\subseteq \mathbb{R}_+^E$ be the convex hull of the characteristic vectors of the members of $\cF$.
The {\em rank function} $r:2^E\to \mathbb{R}$ of matroid $(E,\cF)$ is defined by $r(X)=\max\{|Y|\mid Y\subseteq X,~Y\in \cF\}$. 
This function satisfies the following properties.
\begin{itemize}
\item $r(X)\in \mathbb{Z}$ and $0\leq r(X)\leq |X|$ for any $X\subseteq E$, 
\item $X\subseteq Y\subseteq E$ implies $r(X)\leq r(Y)$ (monotonicity), and
\item $r(X)+r(Y)\geq r(X\cup Y)+r(X\cap Y)$ for any $X, Y\subseteq E$ (submodularity).
\end{itemize}
A matroid rank function is a special case of a {\em polymatroid rank function}, which requires monotonicity, submodularity, and $r(\emptyset)=0$ (that is, only the second and third conditions above are required).
Using the rank function of matroid $(E, \cF)$, the polytope $\conv(\cF)$ is expressed as follows:
\[\conv(\cF)=\set{y\in \mathbb{R}_+^E| y(X)\leq r(X) \ (\forall X\subseteq E)},\]
where $y(X)=\sum_{e\in X}y_e$.

For any vector $x\in \mathbb{R}_+^E$, define a polytope $\conv(\cF)^x \coloneqq\set{y\in \conv(\cF) \mid y\leq x}\subseteq \mathbb{R}_+^E$, and a function $r^x\colon 2^E\to \mathbb{Z}$ by 
$r^x(X)=\min\set{r(Y)+x(X\setminus Y) | Y\subseteq X}$.
The following statement is a specialization of the well-known fact on polymatroid.
\begin{proposition}[{\citealt[p.47]{Fujibook}}]\label{prop:polymatroid-reduction}
For any matroid $(E, \cF)$ whose rank function is $r$ and any vector $x\in \mathbb{R}_+^E$, the function $r^x$ is a polymatroid rank function and 
\[\conv(\cF)^x=\set{y\in \mathbb{R}_+^E| y(X)\leq r^x(X)~(\forall X\subseteq E)}.\]
\end{proposition}
As in the model section, for any total order $\succ$ on $E$, we denote $x\succ^\sd y$ if  $x\in \mathbb{R}_+^E$ stochastically dominates $y\in \mathbb{R}_+^E$ with respect to $\succ$.
The vector that is lexicographically maximum with respect to $\succ$ stochastically dominates all other vectors in the polytope $\conv(\cF)^x$, according to the following lemma.
Note that this claim does not hold in general if $(E, \cF)$ is an arbitrary independence system%
\footnote{Consider the convex hull of the non-matroid family 
$\cF=\big\{\emptyset, \{e_1\}, \{e_2\}, \{e_3\}, \{e_2,e_3\}\big\}$ and the total order $\succ$ such that $e_1\succ e_2\succ e_3$, and let $x=(x_{e_1},x_{e_2},x_{e_3})=(1,1,1)$. The lexicographically maximal solution is $y^*=(1,0,0)\in \conv(\cF)^x$, but $y^*$ does not stochastically dominate $(0,1,1)\in \conv(\cF)^x$.}%
.
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:lexopt=sdopt}
For any matroid $(E,\cF)$, any vector $x\in \mathbb{R}_+^E$, and any total order $\succ$ on $E$, let $y^*$ be the lexicographically maximum vector in $\conv(\cF)^x$ with respect to $\succ$. Then, $y^*\succeq^\sd y$ holds for every vector $y\in \conv(\cF)^x$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Assume that the elements of $E=\{e_1, e_2, \dots, e_m\}$ satisfy $e_1\succ e_2\succ \cdots \succ e_m$ without losing generality.
For each $k \in [m]$, define $E_k\coloneqq\{e_1,e_2,\dots,e_k\}$, that is, $E_k=\set{e\in E|e\succeq e_k}$ and $E_0=\emptyset$.
Define a vector $y^*\in \mathbb{R}_+^E$ by $y^*(e_i)=r^x(E_i)-r^x(E_{i-1})$ for each $i \in [k]$.
We now show that $y^*$ belongs to $\conv(\cF)^x$. 
From Proposition~\ref{prop:polymatroid-reduction}, it suffices to show that $y^*(X)\leq r^x(X)$ holds for any $X\subseteq E$. We use an induction on $j\coloneqq\max\set{i|e_i\in X}$. The claim is trivial for $j=1$. For $j\geq 2$, we have $y^*(X)=y^*(X-e_j)+y^*(e_j)\leq r^x(X-e_j)+y^*(e_j)=r^x(X-e_j)+r^x(E_j)-r^x(E_{j-1})\leq r^x(X)$, where the first inequality follows from the induction hypothesis and the last one from submodularity.
Thus, $y^*\in \conv(\cF)^x$ is shown.

Note that $y^*(E_i)=r^x(E_i)$ holds for $i \in [k]$.
This together with the fact that any $y\in \conv(\cF)^x$ satisfies $y(E_i)\leq r^x(E_i)$ for $i \in [k]$ implies that $y^*$ is lexicographically maximum with respect to $\succ$ in $\conv(\cF)^x$ and stochastically dominates all other vectors in $\conv(\cF)^x$. 
\end{proof}

For a matroid $(E, \cF)$ and a total order $\succ$ on $E$, let $F\colon \mathbb{R}_+^E\to\mathbb{R}_+^E$ be a function that returns the lexicographically maximum vector with respect to $\succ$ in $\conv(\cF)^x$ for any given vector $x\in \mathbb{R}_+^E$.
We refer to this function $F$ as the {\em choice function} induced from $(E, \cF)$ and $\succ$.
For example, if $E=\{e_1,e_2,e_3,e_4\}$, $\cF=\{X\subseteq E\mid |X\cup\{e_1,e_3\}|\le 1~\text{and}~|X|\le 2\}$, $e_1\succ e_2\succ e_3\succ e_4$, and $x=(x_{e_1},x_{e_2},x_{e_3},x_{e_4})=(1/2,1,1,1)$, the induced choice $F[x]$ is $(1/2,1,1/2,0)$.
The following fact is an immediate consequence of Lemma~\ref{lem:lexopt=sdopt}.
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:choice-sd}
For any matroid $(E, \cF)$ and any total order $\succ$ on $E$, let $F\colon \mathbb{R}_+^E\to\mathbb{R}_+^E$ be the choice function induced from $(E, \cF)$ and $\succ$. For any $x, y\in \mathbb{R}_+^E$, the condition $x\geq y$ implies $F[x]\succeq^\sd F[y]$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:lexopt=sdopt}, $F[x]\succeq^\sd z$ for every $z\in \conv(\cF)^x$. 
Because $F[y]\in \conv(\cF)^y$ and $x\geq y$ imply $F[y]\in \conv(\cF)^x$, we obtain $F[x]\succeq^\sd F[y]$.
\end{proof}



Recall that $P\coloneqq \conv(\cX)\subseteq \mathbb{R}^{N\times E}$ denotes the polytope corresponding to the set of feasible fractional assignments.
When $(E, \cF_i)$ is a matroid for every $i\in N$, the following properties are known to hold for $P$: 
(i) $P$ is represented as
\begin{align}
    P=\left\{\pi\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times E} \mathrel{}:\mathrel{} 
    \begin{array}{l}
    \pi_i\in \conv(\cF_i) \quad(\forall i\in N),\\[1pt]
    \sum_{i\in N}\pi_{ie}\le 1 \quad(\forall e\in E)
    \end{array}
    \right\} \label{eq:ra-polytope}
\end{align}
\cite{Schrijver2003};
(ii) For a given feasible fractional assignment $\pi\in P$, we can compute in polynomial-time an assignment that induces $\pi$ (see \cite{GLS2012}).
Note that property (i) can be viewed as a generalization of the Birkhoff--von Neumann theorem.


Finally, we sate a sufficient condition for an assignment to be sd-envy-free on matroid constraints.
Let $F_i$ be the choice function induced from $(E,\cF_i)$ and $\succ_i$.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:ef}
Suppose that the constraints are matroid. For any assignment $p\in\Delta(\cX)$, if $\pi_i^p\succeq^\sd_i F_i[\pi_j^p]$ for any $i,j\in N$ then $p$ is sd-envy-free.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Apply Lemma~\ref{lem:lexopt=sdopt} with $x\coloneqq \pi^p_j$. Then, 
$F_i[\pi^p_j]\succeq_i^\sd y$ holds for every $y\in \conv(\cF_i)^x$.
For each $e\in E$, define $i$'s fractional assignment
$y^{(e)}=\sum_{\bX\in\cX}p_{\bX}\cdot g(\bX,i,j,e)$, where $g(\bX,i,j,e)$ is the characteristic vector of the set $\argmax\{\,|Y\cap U({\succ_i}, e)|\mid  Y\subseteq X_j,\ Y\in \cF_i\,\}$.
Since $y^{(e)}\in \conv(\cF_i)^x$, we have
 $F_i[\pi^p_j]\succeq_i^\sd y^{(e)}$, and hence
\begin{align*}
    F_i[\pi^p_j](U(\succ_i,e))\geq y^{(e)}(U(\succ_i,e)) 
    &=\sum_{\bX\in\cX}p_{\bX}\max_{Y\subseteq X_j:\,Y\in\cF_i}|Y\cap U(\succ_i, e)|.
\end{align*}
Thus, we can conclude that the statement holds. 
\end{proof}
\section{Tractability Results}
Now, we provide our tractability results.
\subsection{Two agents with matroid constraints}
First, we provide a polynomial-time algorithm to find an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment for the case where there are only two agents (i.e., $N=\{1,2\}$) and the constraints $\cF_1$ and $\cF_2$ are matroids. Note that the natural generalization of the probabilistic serial mechanism does not work for this setting as illustrated by Example~\ref{ex:PSisBad}.

Let $(w_{ie})_{i\in N, e\in E}$ be positive weights such that $w_{ia}>w_{ib}$ if and only if $a\succ_i b$.
A typical example of such weights is $w_{ie}=|\{e'\in E\mid e\succeq_i e'\}|$.
A feasible fractional assignment $x\in P$ that maximizes $\sum_{i\in N}\sum_{e\in E}w_{ie}x_{ie}$ is sd-efficient. 
In fact, for any different feasible fractional assignment $x'\in P$ such that $x_i'\succeq_i^\sd x_i$ for each $i\in\{1,2\}$, 
we have $\sum_{i\in N}\sum_{e\in E}w_{ie}x'_{ie}>\sum_{i\in N}\sum_{e\in E}w_{ie}x_{ie}$.


One may expect that a feasible fractional assignment that satisfies sd-efficiency and sd-envy-freeness can be found by computing a maximum weight feasible fractional assignment satisfying an sd-envy-free constraint. If the constraints are identical, such an optimization problem is formulated as the following linear programming:
\begin{align}
    \begin{array}{rl}
         \max       &  \sum_{i\in\{1,2\}}\sum_{e\in E}w_{ie}x_{ie}\\
         \text{s.t.}& x_1\succeq_1^\sd x_2,\\ 
                    & x_2\succeq_2^\sd x_1,\\ 
                    & x\in P.
    \end{array}
\end{align}
However, this is not the case in general.
To observe this, let us consider an instance where 
$E=\{e_1,e_2,e_3\}$,
$\cF_1=\cF_2=\{X\subseteq E\mid |X\cap \{e_1,e_2\}|\le 1\}$, 
$e_3\succ_1 e_2\succ_1 e_1$, and $e_2\succ_2 e_1\succ_2 e_3$.
Then, the optimal solution of the linear programming is $(x_1,x_2)=((0,0,1),(0,1,0))$, but this is sd-dominated by $((1,0,1),(0,1,0))$.

In our approach, the following notion will be useful.
\begin{definition}[sd-proportional]
An assignment $p\in \Delta(\cX)$ is called \emph{sd-proportional} if 
$\pi^p_i\succeq_i^\sd x$ for any $i\in N$ and $x\in \conv(\cF_i)$ with $x\leq \frac{1}{2}\cdot\bm{1}$, where $\bm{1}$ is the all-ones vector.
\end{definition}
Note that sd-proportional assignments must exist.
Indeed, any assignment that induces $(F_1[\frac{1}{2}\cdot\bm{1}],F_2[\frac{1}{2}\cdot\bm{1}])\in P$ satisfies sd-proportionality.

We observe that sd-proportionality is a stronger condition than sd-envy-freeness.
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:prop2ef}
If the number of agents is $2$, sd-proportionality implies sd-envy-freeness.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Let $x$ be a feasible fractional assignment that satisfies sd-proportionality, that is, $x_i\succeq^\sd_i F_i[\frac{1}{2}\cdot\bm{1}]$ for each $i=1,2$.
It is sufficient to prove that $x_i\succeq^\sd_i F_i[\bm{1}-x_i]$. 
In fact, this implies $x_i\succeq^\sd_i F_i[x_j]$ because 
$F_i[\bm{1}-x_i]\succeq^\sd_i F_i[x_j]$ by $\bm{1}-x_i\ge x_j$ and Lemma~\ref{lem:choice-sd} for $\{i,j\}=\{1,2\}$.

Suppose to the contrary that $x_{i}(U(\succ_i,e))<F_i[\bm{1}-x_i](U(\succ_i,e))$ for some $i\in\{1,2\}$ and $e\in E$.
Let $y_i=\frac{1}{2}(x_i+F_i[\bm{1}-x_i])$. Note that $F_i[y_i]=y_i$. 
Because $2y_i=x_i+F_i[\bm{1}-x_i]\le\bm{1}$, we have $F_i[\frac{1}{2}\cdot\bm{1}]\succeq_i^\sd y_i$. Particularly, we have 
$F_i[\frac{1}{2}\cdot\bm{1}](U(\succ_i,e))\ge y_{i}(U(\succ_i,e))$.
Meanwhile, we have 
\begin{align}
    y_{i}(U(\succ_i,e))
    &=\sum_{e':\,e'\succeq_i e}\frac{1}{2}(x_{ie'}+F_i[\bm{1}-x_i]_{e'})
    >x_{i}(U(\succ_i,e))
    \ge F_i[\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot \bm{1}](U(\succ_i,e)),
\end{align}
a contradiction.
\end{proof}




\begin{theorem}\label{thm:2-mat-het-het}
A feasible fractional assignment that satisfies sd-efficiency and sd-envy-freeness always exists and can be computed in polynomial time if the number of agents is $2$ and the constraints are matroids. 
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Consider the following linear programming to compute a maximum weight feasible fractional assignment satisfying sd-proportionality (instead of sd-envy-freeness):
\begin{align} 
    \begin{array}{rl}
         \max       &  \sum_{i\in\{1,2\}}\sum_{e\in E} w_{ie}x_{ie}\\\label{eq:propLP}
         \text{s.t.} & x_i\succeq_i^\sd F_i[\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot\bm{1}] \qquad (i\in\{1,2\}),\\ 
                    & x\in P,
    \end{array}
\end{align}
where $w_{ie}=|\{e'\in E\mid e\succeq_i e'\}|$ for each $i\in \{1,2\}$ and $e\in E$.
The ellipsoid method can solve the problem in polynomial time.
Let $x^*$ be an optimal solution to the problem.
By Lemma~\ref{lem:prop2ef}, $x^*$ is an sd-envy-free feasible fractional assignment.

Now, it suffices to prove that $x^*$ is sd-efficient.
To obtain a contradiction, suppose that $x^*$ is not sd-efficient, that is, there exists a feasible fractional assignment $y~(\ne x^*)$ such that $y_i\succeq_i^\sd x^*_i$ for $i=1,2$.
Then, $y$ is a feasible solution of \eqref{eq:propLP}. Moreover, the objective value $\sum_{i\in\{1,2\}}\sum_{e\in E} w_{ie} y_{ie}$ is larger than $\sum_{i\in\{1,2\}}\sum_{e\in E}w_{ie}x^*_{ie}$. This contradicts the optimality of $x^*$.
\end{proof}
\subsection{Matroid constraints and identical preferences}
Next, we provide a polynomial-time algorithm to find an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment for the case where the preferences are identical and the constraints are (heterogeneous) matroids.
Suppose that $E=\{e_1,e_2,\dots,e_m\}$ and 
the preference of each agent $i$ satisfies $e_1\succ_i e_2\succ_i \cdots \succ_i e_m$ without losing generality.
We use $\succ$ to represent $\succ_i$ for simplicity.

Recall that the natural generalization of the probabilistic serial mechanism does not work for this setting as shown in Example~\ref{ex:PSisBad}.
We generalize the mechanism in a different way. 
In our algorithm, we regard each item as a divisible item of probability shares and process the items one at a time. 
During the first round, each agent ``eats'' $e_1$ at the same speed while $e_1$ is not eaten up and is available for her.
Similarly, at the $k$th round, each agent eats $e_k$ at the same speed while it remains and is available for her.
Our algorithm is formally described in Algorithm~\ref{alg:psip}, where $\chi_{e_k}$ is a characteristic vector in $\{0,1\}^E$.
Note that Algorithm~\ref{alg:psip} can be implemented to run in polynomial time because $\epsilon_i=\max\{\epsilon\mid x_i+\epsilon\cdot\chi_{e_k}\in \conv(\cF_i)\}$ at line~\ref{line:psip-sat} is equal to 
\begin{align}
\max\{\epsilon\mid x_i+\epsilon\cdot\chi_{e_k}\in \conv(\cF_i)\}
&=\max\{\epsilon\mid x_i(X)+\epsilon\le r_i(X)~(e_k\in \forall X\subseteq E)\}\\
&=\min\{r_i(X)-x_i(X) \mid e_k\in X\subseteq E\}\\
&=\min\bigl\{r_i(Y\cup\{e_k\})-x_i(Y\cup\{e_k\}) \mid Y\subseteq E\setminus\{e_k\}\bigr\},
\end{align}
and the final minimization problem can be solved in polynomial-time~\cite{cunningham1984}.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Heterogeneous matroid constraints and identical preferences}\label{alg:psip}
Let $\bm{x}\ot\bm{0}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times E}$\;
\For{$k\ot 1,2,\dots,m$}{
\While{True}{
    Let $\epsilon_i\ot\max\{\epsilon\mid x_i+\epsilon\cdot\chi_{e_k}\in \conv(\cF_i)\}$ $(\forall i\in N$)\;\label{line:psip-sat}
    Let $N^+\ot\{i\in N\mid \epsilon_i>0\}$ and $s\ot \sum_{i\in N}x_{ie_k}$\;
    \lIf{$N^+=\emptyset$ or $s=1$}{\textbf{Break}}
    Let $\epsilon^*\ot \min\{\min_{i\in N^+}\epsilon_i,\, (1-s)/|N^+|\}$\;
    Update $x_i\ot x_i+\epsilon^*\cdot\chi_{e_k}$ for each $i\in N^+$\;
  }
}
\Return an assignment $p\in\Delta(\cX)$ s.t.\ $\pi^p=\bm{x}$\;
\end{algorithm}

For the instance in Example~\ref{ex:PSisBad}, Algorithm~\ref{alg:psip} outputs an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment $p$ such that
\begin{align}
\pi^p = 
\begin{pNiceMatrix}[first-row,first-col]
& e_1 & e_2 & e_3 & e_4 & e_5 \\
1  & 1/2 & 1/2 & 1 & 1/2 & 0\\
2  & 1/2 & 1/2 & 0 & 1/2 & 1
\end{pNiceMatrix}.
\end{align}


\begin{theorem}\label{thm:n-mat-het-id}
A feasible fractional assignment that satisfies sd-efficiency and sd-envy-freeness always exists and can be computed in polynomial time if the constraints are matroids, and the preferences are identical. 
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
It suffices to prove that the outcome of Algorithm~\ref{alg:psip} satisfies sd-efficiency and sd-envy-freeness.
Let $\bm{x}$ be the fractional assignment obtained at the end of Algorithm~\ref{alg:psip}.
We prove sd-efficiency and sd-envy-freeness of $\bm{x}$ by contradiction.

\paragraph{sd-efficiency}
Suppose to the contrary that there exists a feasible fractional assignment $\bm{y}~(\ne \bm{x})$ such that $y_i\succeq_i^\sd x_i$ for all $i\in N$.
Let $\ell\in[m]$ be the smallest index that satisfies $y_{ie_\ell}>x_{ie_\ell}$ for some $i\in N$.
Let $i^*\in N$ be such an agent $i$ with smallest $x_{ie_\ell}$.
Then, we have $y_{ie_t}=x_{ie_t}$ for all $t\in [\ell-1]$ and $y_{je_\ell}\ge x_{je_\ell}$ for all $j\in N$.
Hence, $i^*$ must have eaten more of $e_\ell$, a contradiction.


\paragraph{sd-envy-freeness}
We will show $x_i \succeq^\sd_i F_i[x_j]$ for any $i,j \in N$. This and Proposition~\ref{prop:ef} imply sd-envy-freeness of $p$.
Suppose that there exists $i,j \in N$ and $e\in E$ such that $x_i(U(\succ,e))<F_i[x_j](U(\succ,e))$.
Let $z=F_i\big[(\max\{x_{ie},\,F_i[x_j]_e\})_{e\in E}\big]\in\conv(\cF_i)$. Note that $x_i(U(\succ,e))<F_i[x_j](U(\succ,e))\le z(U(\succ,e))$ by Lemma~\ref{lem:choice-sd}.
Let $\ell\in[m]$ be the smallest index that satisfies $x_i(U(\succ,e_\ell))<z(U(\succ,e_\ell))$.
We have $x_{ie_t}=z_{e_t}$ for all $t\in[\ell-1]$ because, for each $t\in[\ell-1]$,
$x_i(U(\succ,e_t))\ge z(U(\succ,e_t))$ by the choice of $\ell$ and 
$x_i(U(\succ,e_t))\le z(U(\succ,e_t))$ by Lemma~\ref{lem:choice-sd}.
Hence, $x_{ie_\ell}<z_{e_{\ell}}\le \max\{x_{ie_\ell},F_i[x_j]_{e_\ell}\} = F_i[x_j]_{e_\ell}\le x_{je_\ell}$.
Thus, when $i$ has eaten $x_{ie_\ell}$ units of $e_\ell$ in the algorithm, 
$e_\ell$ has not been eaten up, particularly $j$ is still eating $e_\ell$ because $x_{je_\ell}>x_{ie_\ell}$.  
Additionally, $i$ can eat $e_\ell$ more while preserving her feasibility because $x_{ie_t}=z_{e_t}$ for all $t\in[\ell-1]$, $x_{ie_\ell}<z_{e_\ell}$, and $z\in\conv(\cF_i)$.
This contradicts the behavior of the algorithm.
\end{proof}
\subsection{Identical constraints and preferences}
Finally, we provide the existence result when the constraints and preferences are identical.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:n-gen-id-id}
For any instance with identical constraints and preferences, an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment must exist. 
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Let $\bX^*\in\cX$ be a deterministic assignment such that the set of assigned items $\bigcup_{i\in N}X^*_i$ is lexicographically maximum with respect to the common preference. Let $\bX^{(1)}\coloneqq \bX^*$, then, for each $i$ with $1<i\leq n$, let $\bX^{(i)}$ be obtained by shifting the bundles of $\bX^*$ by $i-1$, that is, $\bX^{(i)}\coloneqq (X^*_i,\ldots,X^*_n,X^*_1,\ldots,X^*_{i-1})$.
Define the assignment $p\in\Delta(\cX)$ by setting $p_{\bX^{(i)}}=1/n$ for each $i\in [n]$. Then, $p$ is sd-envy-free because $\pi^p_1=\dots=\pi^p_n$. 
To observe that $p$ is sd-efficient, suppose to the contrary that there exists an assignment $q\in\Delta(\cX)$ with $q\neq p$, such that $\pi_i^q \succeq^\sd \pi_i^p$ for all $i\in N$ with respect to the common preference. 
We define vectors $v^p,v^q\in \mathbb{R}^E$ by $v^p_e=\sum_{i\in N}\pi_{ie}^p$ and $v^q_e=\sum_{i\in N}\pi_{ie}^q$ for each $e\in E$. That is, $v^p_e$, $v^q_e$ are the probabilities that item $e$ is allocated to someone under $p$ and $q$, respectively.
Since $\pi_i^q \succeq^\sd \pi_i^p$ for all $i\in N$, we obtain $v^q \succeq^\sd v^p$. 
Because $v^p$ coincides with the characteristic vector of $\bigcup_{i\in N}X^*_i$, 
this implies that the support of $q$ contains an assignment $\bm{Y}=(Y_1, Y_2,\dots,Y_n)\in \cX$ such that $\bigcup_{i\in N}Y_i$ is lexicographically larger than $\bigcup_{i\in N}X^*_i$. This contradicts the choice of $\bX^*$.
\end{proof}

We note that an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment can be computed in polynomial time when the agents have identical matroid constraints and identical preferences.
Furthermore, even if $n=2$, computing such an assignment is NP-hard for general identical constraints (see Theorem~\ref{thm:NPhard}).
\section{Impossibility Results}
In this section, we present impossibility results.
\subsection{Identical preferences}
We first consider the case where the preferences are identical.
We demonstrate that an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment must exist if the constraints are matroids (Theorem~\ref{thm:n-mat-het-id}), but such an assignment may not exist in general.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:2-gen-het-id}
An sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment may not exist even with two agents, and the preferences are identical.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Let $(N,E,(\succ_i, \cF_i)_{i\in N})$ be an instance where
$N=\{1,2\}$, $E=\{e_1,e_2,e_3,e_4\}$, $\cF_1=2^{\{e_1\}}\cup 2^{\{e_2\}}\cup 2^{\{e_3,e_4\}}$, $\cF_2=2^E$, and $e_1\succ_i e_2 \succ_i e_3 \succ_i e_4\ (i=1,2)$.
Then, no assignment in this instance satisfies sd-efficiency and sd-envy-freeness simultaneously.

Suppose to the contrary that $p\in\Delta(\cX)$ is an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment.
Because $\cF_2$ is $2^E$ and $p$ is sd-efficient, we have $p_{\bX}>0$ only if $\bX=(X_1,X_2)$ satisfies $X_1\cup X_2=E$.
Thus, $\pi^p_{1e}+\pi^p_{2e}=1$ for all $e\in E$.
By the sd-envy-freeness of $p$, we must have $\pi^p_{1e_1}=\pi^p_{2e_1}=1/2$, and hence $p_{(\{e_1\},\{e_2,e_3,e_4\})}=1/2$.
Additionally, $\pi^p_{1e_2}=\pi^p_{2e_2}=1/2$ by the sd-envy-freeness, and hence $p_{(\{e_2\},\{e_1,e_3,e_4\})}=1/2$.
However, we have
\begin{align}
\sum_{e\in U(\succ_i, e_4)}\pi^p_{1e}
<\sum_{\bX\in\cX}p_{\bX}\max_{X\subseteq X_2:\,X\in\cF_1}|X\cap U(\succ_1, e_4)|
\end{align}
because the left-hand side is $1$ and the right-hand side is $2$.
This contradicts sd-envy-freeness of $p$.
\end{proof}
\subsection{Identical matroid constraints}
Next, we observe the case where the constraints are an identical matroid. 
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:3-mat-id-het}
An assignment that satisfies sd-efficiency and sd-envy-freeness may not exist even with three agents and identical matroid constraints.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Let $(N,E,(\succ_i, \cF_i)_{i\in N})$ be an instance where
$N=\{1,2,3\}$, $E=\{a,b,c,d,e\}$, $\cF_i=\{X\subseteq E\mid |X\cap\{a,b,c\}|\le 1\}$ for all $i\in N$, and 
\begin{align}
    &d\succ_1 a \succ_1 b \succ_1 c \succ_1 e,\\
    &d\succ_2 b \succ_2 e \succ_2 a \succ_2 c,\\
    &a\succ_3 d \succ_3 e \succ_3 b \succ_3 c.
\end{align}
Then, no assignment in this instance satisfies sd-efficiency and sd-envy-freeness simultaneously.

To obtain a contradiction, suppose that there exists a feasible fractional assignment $\pi\in P$ that satisfies sd-efficiency and sd-envy-freeness.
Then, by sd-efficiency, each agent receives a unit amount of item $\{a,b,c\}$ and $2/3$ amount of $\{d,e\}$.
Let $\pi_{1d}=\alpha$, $\pi_{3a}=\beta$, and $\pi_{2c}=\gamma$.
By sd-envy-freeness, the assignment $\pi$ can be written as follows:
\[
\setlength{\arraycolsep}{4pt}
\begin{pNiceMatrix}[first-row,first-col]
& a & b & c & d & e\\
1  & 1{-}3\alpha{+}\beta & {-}1{+}3\alpha{-}\beta{+}2\gamma & 1{-}2\gamma & \alpha    &  \frac{2}{3}-\alpha\\[2pt]
2  & 3\alpha{-}2\beta  &  1{-}3\alpha{+}2\beta{-}\gamma & \gamma    & \alpha    &  \frac{2}{3}{-}\alpha\\[2pt]
3  & \beta           &  1{-}\beta{-}\gamma           & \gamma    & 1{-}2\alpha & {-}\frac{1}{3}{+}2\alpha\\
\end{pNiceMatrix}.
\]
As $\pi$ is a feasible fractional assignment, we have $\pi_{3d}=1-2\alpha\ge 0$ and $\pi_{2a}=3\alpha-2\beta\ge 0$.
Thus, we obtain
\begin{align}
\alpha\le 1/2 \quad\text{and}\quad \beta\le 3\alpha/2\le 3/4. \label{eq:alpha_beta_upper}
\end{align}
Moreover, we have $\alpha+2\gamma=\pi_{1d}+\pi_{1a}+\pi_{1b}\ge \pi_{2d}+\pi_{2a}+\pi_{2b}=1+\alpha-\gamma$ by sd-envy-freeness; therefore,
\begin{align}
\gamma\ge 1/3. \label{eq:gamma_lower}
\end{align}
For a sufficiently small positive $\epsilon$, let 
\begin{align}
\pi' &\coloneqq ~\pi +
\begin{pNiceMatrix}[first-row,first-col]
& a & b & c & d & e\\
1  & \epsilon  & -\epsilon & 0 & 0 & 0\\
2  & -\epsilon &  \epsilon & 0 & 0 & 0\\
3  & 0         & 0         & 0 & 0 & 0\\
\end{pNiceMatrix}
\quad\text{and}\quad
\pi'' \coloneqq ~\pi + 
\begin{pNiceMatrix}[first-row,first-col]
& a & b & c & d & e\\
1  & -\epsilon &  0 & \epsilon  & \epsilon  &  -\epsilon\\
2  & 0         &  0 & 0         & 0         &  0\\
3  & \epsilon  &  0 & -\epsilon & -\epsilon & \epsilon\\
\end{pNiceMatrix}.
\end{align}
Because $\pi'$ and $\pi''$ improve $\pi$, they must be infeasible.
By the infeasibility of $\pi'$, we have (i) $\pi_{1b}=-1+3\alpha-\beta+2\gamma=0$ or (ii) $\pi_{2a}=3\alpha-2\beta=0$ (because $\pi_{1b}>0$ implies $\pi_{2b}<1$ and $\pi_{2a}>0$ implies $\pi_{1a}<1$).
Additionally, by the infeasibility of $\pi''$, we have (iii) $\pi_{1a}=1-3\alpha+\beta=0$ or (iv) $\pi_{3d}=1-2\alpha=0$ 
(because $\pi_{1c}=1-2\gamma\le 1/3<1$, $\pi_{1d}=\alpha\le 1/2<1$, $\pi_{1e}=2/3-\alpha\ge 1/6>0$, $\pi_{3a}=\beta\le 3/4<1$, $\pi_{3c}=\gamma\ge 1/3>0$, and $\pi_{3e}=-1/3+2\alpha\le 2/3<1$ from \eqref{eq:alpha_beta_upper} and \eqref{eq:gamma_lower}).
We consider four possible cases separately.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Case 1} (i) $-1+3\alpha-\beta+2\gamma=0$ and (iii) $1-3\alpha+\beta=0$.
Then, $\gamma=0$, which contradicts $\gamma\ge 1/3$ from \eqref{eq:gamma_lower}.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Case 2} (i) $-1+3\alpha-\beta+2\gamma=0$ and (iv) $1-2\alpha=0$.
Then, $\gamma=(1-3\alpha+\beta)/2=(-1/2+\beta)/2\ge 1/3$ from \eqref{eq:gamma_lower}.
This implies $\beta\ge 7/6$, which contradicts $\beta=\pi_{3a}\le 1$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Case 3} (ii) $3\alpha-2\beta=0$ and (iii) $1-3\alpha+\beta=0$.
Then, $\alpha=2/3$, which contradicts $\alpha\le 1/2$ from \eqref{eq:alpha_beta_upper}.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Case 4} (ii) $3\alpha-2\beta=0$ and (iv) $1-2\alpha=0$.
Then, $\alpha=1/2$ and $\beta=3/4$.
Hence, $\pi_{3b}=1/4-\gamma\ge 0$, which contradicts $\gamma\ge 1/3$ from \eqref{eq:gamma_lower}.
\smallskip

Thus, no assignment in the instance satisfies sd-efficiency and sd-envy-freeness simultaneously.
\end{proof}



We can also demonstrate that, for any $n\ge 3$, there exists an instance that has no assignment that simultaneously satisfies sd-efficency and sd-envy-freeness.
Consider an instance $(N,E,(\succ_i,\cF_i)_{i\in N})$ where $N=\{1,2,\dots,n\}$, $E=\{a,b,c,d,e,o_6,\dots,o_{2n}\}$, $\cF_i=\{X\subseteq E\mid |X\cap\{a,b,c\}|\le 1\}$ for all $i\in N$, and 
\begin{align}
    &d\succ_1 a \succ_1 b \succ_1 c \succ_1 e \succ_1 o_6\succ_1\cdots,\\
    &d\succ_2 b \succ_2 e \succ_2 a \succ_2 c \succ_2 o_6\succ_2\cdots,\\
    &a\succ_3 d \succ_3 e \succ_3 b \succ_3 c \succ_3 o_6\succ_3\cdots,\\
    &o_{2i-1} \succ_i o_{2i} \succ_i \cdots \quad(i=4,5,\dots,n).
\end{align}
Then, analysis similar to that in the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:3-mat-id-het} demonstrates that this instance does not have an assignment that satisfies both sd-efficency and sd-envy-freeness.
\section{Computational hardness}
In this section, we demonstrate that, 
finding an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment is computationally hard even for the simplest setting: two agents with identical preferences and constraints.
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:NPhard}
Even when there are two agents with identical preferences and constraints, finding an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment is NP-hard.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
We reduce from the \emph{PARTITION} problem, which is known to be NP-hard~\cite{GJ1979}. 
An instance of the problem consists of $k$ integers $a_1,a_2,\dots,a_k$, and it is a Yes-instance if and only if there exists $I\subseteq[k]$ such that $\sum_{i\in I}a_i=\sum_{i\in [k]\setminus I}a_i$.
Given an instance of the problem $(a_1,a_2,\dots,a_n)$, we construct an instance of $(N,E,(\succ_i,\cF_i)_{i\in N})$ as follows:
$N=\{1,2\}$, $E=\{e_1,e_2,\dots,e_k\}$, $e_1\succ_ie_2\succ_ie_k~(i\in \{1,2\})$, and $\cF_1=\cF_2=\{E'\subseteq E\mid \sum_{e_i\in E'}a_i\le \sum_{i\in[k]}a_i/2\}$.
If the instance of the PARTITION problem is a Yes-instance, an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment $p\in\Delta(\chi)$ must satisfy $\pi^p_1=\pi^p_2=\frac{1}{2}\cdot\bm{1}$.
On the other hand, if the instance of the PARTITION problem is a No-instance, any feasible assignment $p\in\Delta(\chi)$ cannot satisfy $\pi^p_1=\pi^p_2=\frac{1}{2}\cdot\bm{1}$.
Hence, finding an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment is NP-hard.
\end{proof}
\section{Anonymous mechanism}
In this section, we consider \emph{anonymity} as a fairness concept.
A mechanism is called \emph{anonymous} if permuting agents' names does not affect the resulting outcome.
Note that anonymity implies \emph{equal treatment of equals}, namely, two agents with identical preferences and constraints get the same fractional assignment.

In general, the set of feasible fractional assignments of $(N,E,(\succ_i)_{i\in N})$ can be written as follows:
\begin{align}
    P = \left\{\pi^p\in \mathbb{R}^{N\times E} \mid p\in\Delta(\cX)\right\}.
\end{align}
Note that $P$ is a nonempty polytope.
Let us consider a mechanism that outputs the optimal solution of the following convex programming:
\begin{align}
    \begin{array}{rl}
         \min       &  \sum_{i\in N}\sum_{e\in E}\left(\sum_{e'\in E:\,e'\succeq_i e}(1-x_{ie'})\right)^2\\
         \text{s.t.}&  x\in P.
    \end{array}
\end{align}
This convex programming has a unique optimal solution because the objective function is strictly convex and the feasibility region is nonempty, closed, bounded, and convex~\cite{RockWets98}.
Hence, the mechanism is anonymous because the convex programming does not depend on the ordering of agents.

Let $x^*$ be the optimal solution to the problem.
We show that $x^*$ is sd-efficient by contradiction.
Suppose that $x^*$ is not sd-efficient, that is, there exists a feasible fractional assignment $y\in P\setminus\{x^*\}$ such that $y_i\succeq_i^\sd x^*_i$ for all $i\in N$. 
Then the objective value of $x^*$ (i.e., $\sum_{i\in N}\sum_{e\in E}(\sum_{e'\in E:\,e'\succeq_i e}(1-x^*_{ie'}))^2$) is larger than the objective value of $y$ (i.e., $\sum_{i\in N}\sum_{e\in E}(\sum_{e'\in E:\,e'\succeq_i e}(1-y_{ie'}))^2$).
This contradicts the optimality of $x^*$.



\begin{theorem}\label{thm:anonymous}
There exists an anonymous mechanism that always produces an sd-efficient assignment.
\end{theorem}
\section{Concluding remarks}
In this study, we have examined the existence of an sd-efficient and sd-envy-free assignment in random assignment problems under constraints.
The existence of such an assignment is open for the case of two agents with identical constraints.
In addition, we have provided a mechanism that satisfies sd-efficiency and anonymity.
A future work is to develop mechanisms that satisfy other fairness and efficiency concepts with existence guarantees.
