The problem of matrix decomposition has been a popular and fundamental topic under extensive investigations across several disciplines, including signal processing, machine learning, natural language processing [9, 10, 31, 48, 32, 7], just to name a few. From the decomposition, one can construct useful representations of the original data matrix.However, for any matrix 𝒀∈ℝp×n𝒀superscriptℝ𝑝𝑛\bm{Y}\in\mathbb{R}^{p\times n} that can be factorized as a product of two matrices 𝑨∈ℝp×r𝑨superscriptℝ𝑝𝑟\bm{A}\in\mathbb{R}^{p\times r} and 𝑿∈ℝr×n𝑿superscriptℝ𝑟𝑛\bm{X}\in\mathbb{R}^{r\times n}, there exist infinitely many decompositions, simply because one can use any r×r𝑟𝑟r\times r invertible matrix 𝑸𝑸\bm{Q} to construct 𝑨′=𝑨​𝑸superscript𝑨′𝑨𝑸\bm{A}^{\prime}=\bm{A}\bm{Q} and 𝑿′=𝑸−1​𝑿superscript𝑿′superscript𝑸1𝑿\bm{X}^{\prime}=\bm{Q}^{-1}\bm{X} such that 𝒀=𝑨​𝑿=𝑨′​𝑿′𝒀𝑨𝑿superscript𝑨′superscript𝑿′\bm{Y}=\bm{A}\bm{X}=\bm{A}^{\prime}\bm{X}^{\prime}, while 𝑨′≠𝑨superscript𝑨′𝑨\bm{A}^{\prime}\neq\bm{A} and 𝑿′≠𝑿superscript𝑿′𝑿\bm{X}^{\prime}\neq\bm{X}. Thus,in various applications, additional structures and priors are imposed for a unique representation [21, 14]. For example, principal component analysis (PCA) aims to find orthogonal representations which retain as many variations in 𝒀𝒀\bm{Y} as possible [16, 22], whereas independent component analysis (ICA) targets the representations of statistically independent non-Gaussian signals [25].

In this paper, we are interested in finding a unique, sparse, low-dimensional representation of 𝒀𝒀\bm{Y}. To this end, we study the decomposition of a low-rank matrix 𝒀∈ℝp×n𝒀superscriptℝ𝑝𝑛\bm{Y}\in\mathbb{R}^{p\times n} that satisfies𝒀=𝑨​𝑿,𝒀𝑨𝑿\displaystyle\bm{Y}=\bm{A}\bm{X},(I.1)where 𝑨∈ℝp×r𝑨superscriptℝ𝑝𝑟\bm{A}\in\mathbb{R}^{p\times r} is an unknown deterministic matrix, with r<min⁡{n,p}𝑟𝑛𝑝r<\min\{n,p\}, and 𝑿∈ℝr×n𝑿superscriptℝ𝑟𝑛\bm{X}\in\mathbb{R}^{r\times n} is an unknown sparse matrix.

Finding a unique sparse decomposition of (I.1) turns out to be important in many applications. For example, if the matrix 𝑿𝑿\bm{X} is viewed as a low-dimensional sparse representation of 𝒀𝒀\bm{Y}, finding such representation is ubiquitous in signal recovery, image processing and compressed sensing, just to name a few. If columns of 𝒀𝒀\bm{Y} are viewed as linear combinations of columns of 𝑨𝑨\bm{A} with 𝑿𝑿\bm{X} being the sparse coefficient, then (I.1) can be used to form overlapping clusters of the n𝑛n columns of 𝒀𝒀\bm{Y} via the support of 𝑿𝑿\bm{X} with columns of 𝑨𝑨\bm{A} being viewed as r𝑟r cluster centers [11, 6].Furthermore, we could form a p×r𝑝𝑟p\times r low-dimensional representation of 𝒀𝒀\bm{Y} via linear combinations with sparse coefficients. Such sparse coefficients greatly enhance the interpretability of the resulting representations [27, 20, 3], in the same spirit as the sparse PCA, but generalizes to the factorization of non-orthogonal matrices.

To motivate our approach, consider the simple case that 𝑨𝑨\bm{A} has orthonormal columns, namely, 𝑨T​𝑨=𝑰rsuperscript𝑨𝑇𝑨subscript𝑰𝑟\bm{A}^{T}\bm{A}=\bm{I}_{r}222𝑰rsubscript𝑰𝑟\bm{I}_{r} is the identity matrix of size r×r𝑟𝑟r\times r.. Then it is easy to see that the sparse coefficient matrix 𝑿𝑿\bm{X} is recovered by multiplying 𝒀𝒀\bm{Y} on the left by 𝑨Tsuperscript𝑨𝑇\bm{A}^{T},𝑨T​𝒀=𝑨T​𝑨​𝑿=𝑿.superscript𝑨𝑇𝒀superscript𝑨𝑇𝑨𝑿𝑿\displaystyle\bm{A}^{T}\bm{Y}=\bm{A}^{T}\bm{A}\bm{X}=\bm{X}.(I.2)The problem of finding such orthonormal matrix 𝑨𝑨\bm{A} boils down to successively finding a unit-norm direction 𝒒𝒒\bm{q} that renders 𝒒T​𝒀superscript𝒒𝑇𝒀\bm{q}^{T}\bm{Y} as sparse as possible [34, 40, 36],min𝒒‖𝒒T​𝒀‖sparsitys.t.‖𝒒‖2=1.\displaystyle\min_{\bm{q}}\quad\left\|\bm{q}^{T}\bm{Y}\right\|_{\rm sparsity}\qquad\operatorname{s.t.}\quad\left\|\bm{q}\right\|_{2}=1.(I.3)However, the natural choice of the sparsity penalty, such as the ℓpsubscriptℓ𝑝\ell_{p} norm for p∈[0,1]𝑝01p\in\left[0,1\right],leads to trivial and meaningless solutions, as there always exists 𝒒𝒒\bm{q} in the null space of 𝑨Tsuperscript𝑨𝑇\bm{A}^{T} such that 𝒒T​𝒀=𝟎superscript𝒒𝑇𝒀0\bm{q}^{T}\bm{Y}=\bm{0}.

To avoid the null space of 𝑨Tsuperscript𝑨𝑇\bm{A}^{T},we instead choose to find the unit direction 𝒒𝒒\bm{q} that maximizes the ℓ4subscriptℓ4\ell_{4} norm of 𝒒T​𝒀superscript𝒒𝑇𝒀\bm{q}^{T}\bm{Y} asmax𝒒‖𝒒T​𝒀‖4s.t.‖𝒒‖2=1.\max_{\bm{q}}\quad\left\|\bm{q}^{T}\bm{Y}\right\|_{4}\qquad\operatorname{s.t.}\quad\left\|\bm{q}\right\|_{2}=1.(I.4)The above formulation isbased on the key observation that the objective value is maximized when 𝒒𝒒\bm{q} coincides with one column of 𝑨𝑨\bm{A} (see, Section II, for details) whilethe objective value is zero when 𝒒𝒒\bm{q} lies in the null space of 𝑨Tsuperscript𝑨𝑇\bm{A}^{T}. The ℓ4subscriptℓ4\ell_{4} norm objective function and its variants have been adopted as a sparsity regularizer in a line of recent works [30, 46, 45, 36, 44], arguably because solving (I.4) requires a milder restriction onsparsity level of 𝑿𝑿\bm{X} to recover 𝑨𝑨\bm{A} comparing to solving (I.3).However, all previous works study the setting where 𝑨𝑨\bm{A} has full row rank and, to the best of our knowledge, the setting where 𝑨𝑨\bm{A} has full column rank has not been studied elsewhere. As we will elaborate below, when 𝑨𝑨\bm{A} has full column rank, analysis of the optimization landscape of (I.4) becomes more difficult since the null space of 𝑨Tsuperscript𝑨𝑇\bm{A}^{T} persists as a challenge for solving the optimization problem: they form a flat region of locally optimal solutions.

This paper characterizes the nonconvex optimization landscape of (I.4) and proposes a guaranteed procedure that avoids the flat null region and provably recovers the global solution to (I.4), which corresponds to one column of 𝑨𝑨\bm{A}. More specifically, we demonstrate that, despite the non-convexity, (I.4) still possesses benign geometric property in the sense that any strict local solution with large objective value is globally optimal and recovers one column of 𝑨𝑨\bm{A}, up to its sign. See, Theorem III.1 in Section III-A for the population level result and Theorem III.4 for the finite sample result.

We further extend these results to the general case when 𝑨𝑨\bm{A} only has full column rank in Theorem III.6 of Section III-B. To recover a general 𝑨𝑨\bm{A} with full column rank, our procedure first resorts to a preconditioning procedure of 𝒀𝒀\bm{Y} proposed in Section II-C and then solves an optimization problem similar to (I.4).From our analysis of the optimization landscape, the intriguing problem boils down to developing algorithms to recover nontrivial local solutions by avoiding regions with small objective values. We thus propose a simple initialization scheme in Section IV-A and prove in Theorem IV.3 that such initialization, proceeded with any second-order descent algorithm [19, 26], suffices to find the global solution, up to some statistical error. Our theoretical analysis provides explicit rates of convergence of the statistical error and characterizes the dependence on various dimensions, such as p𝑝p, r𝑟r and n𝑛n, as well as the sparsity of 𝑿𝑿\bm{X}.

Numerical simulation results are provided in Section V. Finally, in Section VI, we conclude our results and discuss several future directions of our work. All the proofs and supplementary simulations are deferred to the Appendix.

In this paper, we study the unique decomposition of a low-rank matrix 𝒀𝒀\bm{Y} that admits a sparse representation. Under model 𝒀=𝑨​𝑿𝒀𝑨𝑿\bm{Y}=\bm{A}\bm{X} where 𝑿𝑿\bm{X} has i.i.d. Bernoulli-Gaussian entries and 𝑨𝑨\bm{A} has full column rank, we propose a nonconvex procedure that provably recovers 𝑨𝑨\bm{A}, a quantity that can be further used to recover 𝑿𝑿\bm{X}. We provide a complete analysis for recovering one column of 𝑨𝑨\bm{A}, up to the sign, by showing that any second-order descent algorithm with a simple and data-driven initialization provably attains the global solution, despite the nonconvex nature of this problem.

There are several directions worth further pursuing. For instance, it is of great interest to develop a complete analysis of the deflation procedure for recovering the full matrix 𝑨𝑨\bm{A}. It is worth studying this decomposition problem in presence of some additive errors, that is, 𝒀=𝑨​𝑿+𝑬𝒀𝑨𝑿𝑬\bm{Y}=\bm{A}\bm{X}+\bm{E}. Our current procedure only tolerates 𝑬𝑬\bm{E} that has small entries. We leave the problem of modifying our procedure to accommodate a moderate / large 𝑬𝑬\bm{E} to future research.
