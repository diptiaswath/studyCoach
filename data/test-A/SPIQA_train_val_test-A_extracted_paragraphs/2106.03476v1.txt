Metrics that capture the similarity between vertices in a graph have played a pivotal role in the quest for understanding the structure of large-scale networks. Typical examples include personalized PageRank (PPR) (Pageet al., 1999), Katz similarity (Katz, 1953) and SimRank (Jeh and Widom, 2002), each of which can be thought of as a random walk-based measure on graphs. These metrics have found applications in recommender systems (Kim and El Saddik, 2011), link prediction (Lü and Zhou, 2011; Songet al., 2009), etc.

A remarkably important random walk-based metric for measuring vertex similarity is the effective resistance. Given a graph G𝐺G treated as a resistor network, the effective resistance RG​(s,t)subscript𝑅𝐺𝑠𝑡R_{G}(s,t) between two vertices s,t𝑠𝑡s,t in G𝐺G is the energy dissipation in the network when routing one unit of current from s𝑠s to t𝑡t. It is well known that the effective resistance is inherently related to the behaviour of random walks on graphs111We only consider simple random walks in the paper: suppose we are at vertex v𝑣v, we jump to a neighbor of v𝑣v with probability 1/deg⁡(v)1degree𝑣1/\deg(v), where deg⁡(v)degree𝑣\deg(v) is the degree of vertex v𝑣v.. Concretely, the effective resistance between s𝑠s and t𝑡t is proportional to the commute time κ​(s,t)𝜅𝑠𝑡\kappa(s,t), defined as the expected number of steps a random walk starting at s𝑠s visits vertex t𝑡t and then goes back to s𝑠s (Chandra et al., 1996). Using this interpretation, we can deduce that the smaller RG​(s,t)subscript𝑅𝐺𝑠𝑡R_{G}(s,t) is, the more similar two vertices s,t𝑠𝑡s,t are.

Indeed, effective resistance has proven ubiquitous in numerous applications including graph clustering (Alevet al., 2018; Fortunato, 2010), recommender systems (Kunegis andSchmidt, 2007), measuring robustness of networks (Ellens et al., 2011), spectral sparsification (Spielman andSrivastava, 2011), graph convolutional networks (Ahmadet al., 2021), location-based advertising (Yu et al., 2019), among others. Moreover, in the theoretical computer science community, the use of effective resistance has led to a breakthrough line of work for provably speeding up the running time of many flow-based problems in combinatorial optimization (Anari and Gharan, 2015; Christiano et al., 2011; Madryet al., 2014).

Despite of the importance of effective resistance, we still lack efficient methods to compute or approximate them on massive graphs. These results, though theoretically competitive, require access to the entire input graph. Given the rapid growth of modern networks, such polynomial time algorithms (even those running in near linear time in the number of vertices and edges) are prohibitively costly. This motivates the following question:Can we obtain a competitive estimation to RG​(s,t)subscript𝑅𝐺𝑠𝑡R_{G}(s,t) while exploring only a small portion of the graph?

We address this question by exploiting the paradigm of local or sub-linear algorithms. This computational model is particularly desirable in applications where one requires the effective resistances amongst only a few number of vertex pairs. Despite that the effective resistance is a key tool in large-scale graph analytics, designing local algorithms for estimating it is a largely unexplored topic.

In this paper, we provide several local algorithms for estimating pairwise effective resistances with provable performance guarantees. For any specified vertex pair s,t𝑠𝑡s,t, our algorithms output an estimate of RG​(s,t)subscript𝑅𝐺𝑠𝑡R_{G}(s,t) with an arbitrarily small constant additive error, while exploring a small portion of the graph.To formally state our results, we utilize the well-known adjacency list model (Ron, 2019), which assumes query access to the input graph G𝐺G and supports the following types of queries in constant time:•degree query: for any specified vertex v𝑣v, the algorithm can get the degree deg⁡(v)degree𝑣\deg(v) of v𝑣v;•neighbor query: for any specified vertex v𝑣v and index i≤deg⁡(v)𝑖degree𝑣i\leq\deg(v), the algorithm can get the i𝑖i-th neighbor of v𝑣v;•uniform sampling: the algorithm can sample a vertex v𝑣v of G𝐺G uniformly at random.

A Markov chain is said to be aperiodic if for any state i𝑖i there are no restrictions on when it is possible for the process to enter state i𝑖i.

Informally, the mixing time of the graph G𝐺G refers to the number of steps needed before a random walk on G𝐺G converges to its stationary distribution. In this paper, we developed a number of local algorithms for estimating the pairwise effective resistances, a fundamental metric for measuring the similarity of vertices in a graph. Our algorithms explore only a small portion of the graph while provides a good approximation to RG​(s,t)subscript𝑅𝐺𝑠𝑡R_{G}(s,t) for any specified s,t𝑠𝑡s,t. Our algorithms are desirable in applications where the effective resistances of a small number of vertex pairs are needed. Our experiments on benchmark datasets validate the performance of these local algorithms.
