Discussion:Our theoretical results suggest it is always beneficial for statisticalpower to build tests on ℓ1subscriptℓ1\ell_{1} norms rather than ℓ2subscriptℓ2\ell_{2} norm ofdifferences between kernel distribution representatives (Propositions3.1, 3.4). In practice, however,optimizing test locations with ℓ1subscriptℓ1\ell_{1}-norm tests leads tonon-smooth objective functions that are harder to optimize. Ourexperiments confirm the theoretical benefit of the ℓ1subscriptℓ1\ell_{1}-basedframework. The benefit is particularly pronounced for a large number J𝐽Jof test locations –as the difference between ℓ1subscriptℓ1\ell_{1} and ℓ2subscriptℓ2\ell_{2} normsincreases with dimension (see in supp. mat. Lemmas 8, 12)– as well as for large dimension of the nativespace (Figure 2). The benefit of ℓ1subscriptℓ1\ell_{1} distances fortwo-sample testing in high dimension has also been reported by[34], though their framework does notlink to kernel embeddings or to the convergence of probability measures. Further work should consider extending these results to goodness-of-fit testing, where the L1superscript𝐿1L^{1} geometry was shown empirically to provide excellent performance [16].

In this paper, we show that statistics derived from the Lpsuperscript𝐿𝑝L^{p}distances between well-chosen distribution representatives are well suited for the two-sample problem as these distances metrize the weak convergence (Theorem 2.1). We then compare the power of tests introduced in [5] and their ℓ1subscriptℓ1\ell_{1} counterparts and we show that ℓ1subscriptℓ1\ell_{1}-based statistics have better power with high probability (Propositions 3.1, 3.4). As with state-of-the-artEuclidean approaches, the framework leads totractable computations and learns interpretable locations of where thedistributions differ.Empirically, on all 4 synthetic and 3 real problems investigated,the ℓ1subscriptℓ1\ell_{1} geometry gives clear benefits compared to the Euclideangeometry. The L1superscript𝐿1L^{1} distance is known to be well suited for densities, to control differences or estimation [7]. Itis also beneficial for kernel embeddings of distributions.
