Vector Quantisation (VQ)В [12] is a basic building block of many machine learning techniques.It is often used to help learning unsupervised representations for vision and language tasks, including data compressionВ [1, 39, 36], recognitionВ [26, 3, 44, 24, 23], and generationВ [37, 31, 11, 32, 47, 34, 33].VQ quantises continuous feature vectors into a discrete space by embedding them to the closest vectors in a codebook of representatives or codevectors.Quantisation has been shown to simplify optimization problems by reducing a continuous search space to a discrete one.

Despite its success, VQ has some drawbacks when applied to deep networksВ [37].One of them is that quantisation stops gradients from back-propagating to the codevectors.This has been linked to codebook collapseВ [36], which means that only a small subset of active codevectors are optimized alongside the learnable features, while the majority of them are not used at all (see the green вҖңdeadвҖқ points in Fig.В 1(a)).As a result, many recent methodsВ [11, 10, 44, 32, 6, 47] fail to utilise the full expressive power of a codebook due to the low codevector utilisation, especially when the codebook size is large.This significantly limits VQвҖҷs effectiveness.

To tackle this issue, we propose a new alternative quantiser called Clustering VQ-VAE (CVQ-VAE).We observe that classical clustering algorithms, such as refined initialization kрқ‘ҳk-meansВ [4] and kрқ‘ҳk-means++В [2], use a dynamic cluster initialization approach.For example, kрқ‘ҳk-means++ randomly selects a data point as the first cluster centre, and then chooses the next new centre based on a weighted probability calculated from the distance to the previous centres.Analogously, CVQ-VAE dynamically initializes unoptimized codebooks by resampling them from the learned features (Fig.В 2).This simple approach can avoid codebook collapse and significantly enhance the usage of larger codebooks by enabling optimization of all codevectors (achieving 100%percent100100\% codebook utilisation in Fig.В 1(c)).

While CVQ-VAE is inspired by previous dynamic cluster initialization techniquesВ [4, 2], its implementation in deep networks requires careful consideration.Unlike traditional clustering algorithmsВ [25, 4, 14, 2] where source data points are fixed, in deep networks features and their corresponding codevectors are mutually and incrementally optimized.Thus, simply sampling codevectors from a single snapshot of features would not work well because any mini-batch used for learning cannot capture the true data distribution, as demonstrated in our offline version in Tab.В 3.To fix this issue, we propose to compute running averages of the encoded features across different training mini-batches and use these to improve the dynamic reinitialization of the collapsed codevectors.This operation is similar to an online feature clustering method that calculates average features across different training iterations (Fig.В 2).While this may seem a minor change, it leads to a very significant improvement in terms of performance (Fig.В 1(e)).

As a result of these changes, CVQ-VAE significantly outperforms the previous models VQ-VAEВ [37] and SQ-VAEВ [36] on various datasets under the same setting, and with no other changes except for swapping in the new quantiser.Moreover, we conduct thorough ablation experiments on variants of the method to demonstrate the effectiveness of our design and analyse the importance of various design factors.Finally, we incorporate CVQ-VAE into large models (e.g. VQ-GANВ [11] and LDMВ [32]) to further demonstrate its generality and potential in various applications.

VQ-VAEВ [37] learns to quantise the continuous features into a discrete space using a restricted number of codebook vectors.By clustering features in the latent space, VQ-VAE can automatically learn a crucially compact representation and store the domain information in the decoder that does not require supervision.This discrete representation has been applied to various downstream tasks, including image generationВ [31, 44, 6, 22, 17], image-to-image translationВ [11, 30, 10, 32], text-to-image synthesisВ [30, 9, 29, 18], conditional video generationВ [28, 40, 42], image completionВ [11, 10, 46], recognitionВ [26, 3, 44, 24, 23] and 3D reconstructionВ [27, 34, 33].

Among them, VQ-GANВ [11], ViT-VQGANВ [44], RQ-VAEВ [22], and MoVQВ [46] aim to train a better discrete representation through deeper network architectures, additional loss functions, multichannel or higher resolution representations. However, none of them tackle the codebook collapse issue for the unoptimized вҖңdeadвҖқ point.

To address this issue, additional training heuristics are proposed in recent works.SQ-VAEВ [36] improves VQ-VAE with stochastic quantisation and a trainable posterior categorical distribution.VQ-WAEВ [38] builds upon SQ-VAE by directly encouraging the discrete representation to be a uniform distribution via a Wasserstein distance.The most related works are HVQ-VAEВ [39] and JukeboxВ [8] that use codebook reset to randomly reinitialize unused or low-used codebook entries.However, they only assign a single sampled anchor to each unoptimized codevector.In contrast, our CVQ-VAE considers the changing of features in deep networks and designs an online clustering algorithm by running average updates across the training mini-batch.Additionally, our work bridges codebook reset in Jukebox for music generation to the more general class of running average updates that are applicable to image compression and generation problems in computer vision.

VQ is in the context of unsupervised representation learning.Our main goal is to learn a discrete codebook that efficiently utilizes all codebook entries within it.To achieve this, our quantisation method, as illustrated in Fig.В 2, is conceptually similar to VQ-VAEВ [37], except that our codevectors are dynamically initialized rather than being sampled from a fixed uniform or Gaussian distribution.In the following sections, we provide a general overview of VQ (Sec.В 3.1), followed by our proposed CVQ-VAE (Sec.В 3.2).

The choice of initial points is a crucial aspect of unsupervised codebook learning.Classical clustering methods like refined kрқ‘ҳk-meansВ [4] and kрқ‘ҳk-means++В [2] are dynamically-initialized, which means that each new clustering centre is initialized based on previously calculated distance or points.This leads to a more robust and effective clustering result, as reported in comparative studiesВ [5].

Analogously, we build a dynamically-initialized vector quantized codebook in deep networks. However, unlike traditional clustering settings, the data points, i.e. the encoded features z^^рқ‘§\hat{z} in the deep network, are also updated during training instead of being fixed.Therefore, a dynamical initialization strategy should take into account the changing feature representations during training.

To mitigate the codebook collapse issue, several methods have been proposed, likenormalized codevectors in ViT-VQGANВ [44].However, these methods only optimize the active entries, rather than the entire codebook.Recently, SQ-VAEВ [36], SeQ-GANВ [13], and VQ-WAEВ [38] assume that the codebook follows a fixed distribution.Although these methods achieve high perplexity, the reconstruction quality is not always improved (Tab.В 4).The most relevant work to ours is codebook reset, which randomly reinitializes the unused or low-used codevectors to high-usage onesВ [39] or encoder outputsВ [8].However, these methods rely only on a temporary single value for initialization and miss the opportunity of exploiting online clustering across different training steps.

VQ embeds the continuous features into a discrete space with a finite size, i.e. KрқҗҫK codebook entries.The codebook size has significant effects on traditional clustering. In Tab.В 4a, we showed the performances of various quantisers with different codebook sizes.Our CVQ-VAE benefits greatly from a larger number of codebook entries, while SQ-VAEВ [36] shows smaller improvements.It is worth noting that not all quantizers automatically benefit from a larger codebook size, such as VQ-VAEвҖҷs performance on the CIFAR10 dataset shown in Tab.В 4a (bottom).

Recent concurrent studiesВ [36, 13, 38] have explicitly promoted a large perplexity by optimizing a perplexity-related loss.However, as illustrated in Tab.В 4a, a larger perplexity does not always guarantee a lower rFID.This suggests that a uniform distribution of perplexity, represented by the highest score, may not be the optimal solution for the codebook.

Table 4b presents the results on various codebook dimensionalities.Interestingly, the performance of the quantizers does not exhibit a straightforward positive correlation with the number of codebook dimensionality.In fact, some smaller codebook dimensionalities yield better results than larger ones, indicating that the choice of codebook dimensionality should be carefully considered depending on the specific application and dataset.Based on this observation, a low-dimensional codebook can be employed to represent images and used in downstream tasks, as demonstrated in the latent diffusion model (LDM)В [32].The relevant downstream applications on generation can be found in Sec.В 5.

An evaluation of various anchor sampling methods is reported in Tab.В 4c.The results indicate that the offline version with only one reinitialization is highly sensitive to the anchor sampling methods.Interestingly, the random, unique, closest, and probabilistic random versions perform similarly for online version, up to some random variations (rFID from 2.23 to 2.59 on MNIST, and from 25.99 to 26.62 on CIFAR10).As discussed in Sec.В 3.2, different anchor sampling methods have significant effects on traditional clusteringВ [4, 14].However, our experiments demonstrate that the codebook reinitialization needs to consider the fact that the encoded features change along with the deep network is trained.The results highlight the effectiveness of our online version with the running average updates, which is insensitive to the different instantiations.

Some latest worksВ [39, 8] also consider updating the unoptimized codevectors, called codebook reset.In Tab.В 4d, we compare these methods with VQ-VAEвҖҷs architectureВ [37] under the same experimental setting, except for the different quantisers.As discussed in Sec.В 3.2, HVQ-VAEВ [39] resets the low usage codevectors using the high usage ones, which learns a narrow space codebook, resulting in limited improvement.The hard encoded features presented inВ [8] achieve better results (3.17вҶ’вҶ’\to2.25, 41.08вҶ’вҶ’\to29.16, and 4.31вҶ’вҶ’\to3.91) than the HVQ-VAEВ [39] by adding noise signal to ensure the independent anchors for each codebook entry.In contrast, our CVQ-VAE calculates the running average updates, resulting in a significant improvement.This further suggests that the online clustering centre along with the different training mini-batches is crucial for proper codebook reinitialization.

Except for data compression, our CVQ-VAE can also be easily applied to downstream tasks, such as generation and completion.Following existing worksВ [11, 32, 47], we conduct a simple experiment to verify the effectiveness of the proposed quantisers.Although this simple yet effective quantiser can be applied to more applications, it is beyond the main scope of this paper.

We made minor modifications to the baseline LDMВ [32] system when adapting it with our quantiser for the downstream tasks.We first replace the original quantiser from VQGANВ [11] with our proposed CVQ-VAEвҖҷs quantiser.Then, we trained the models on LSUNВ [43] and ImageNetВ [7] for generation (8Г—\times downsampling).Following the setting in LDMВ [32], we set the sampling step as 200 during the inference.

TablesВ 5 andВ 6 compares our proposed CVQ-VAE to the state-of-the-art methods on LSUN and ImageNet datasets for unconditional and class-conditional image generation.The results show that our model consistently improves the generated image quality under the sample compression ratio, as in the reconstruction.This confirms the advantages of using a better codebook for downstream tasks.Our CVQ-VAE also outperforms the LDM-8вҲ— that is trained with KвҖӢLрқҗҫрқҗҝKL-regularized latent space, indicating that exploring a better discrete codebook is worth pursuing for unsupervised representation learning.Our CVQ-VAE also achieves comparable results to LDM-4 (3.02 vs. 2.95), whereas the LDM-4 uses a 4Г—\times higher resolution representation, requiring more computational costs.

Example results are presented in Fig.В 4.As we can see, even with 8Г—\times downsampling, the proposed CVQ-VAE is still able to generate reasonable structures for these complicated scenes with various instances.Although there are artifacts on windows in the two scenarios, the other high-frequency details are realistic, such as the sheet on the bed.

We have introduced CVQ-VAE, a novel codebook reinitialization method that tackles the codebook collapse issue by assigning the online clustered anchors to unoptimized code entries.Our proposed quantiser is a simple yet effective solution that can be integrated into many existing architectures for representation learning.Experimental results show that our CVQ-VAE significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art VQ models on image modeling, yet without increasing computational cost and latent size.We hope this new plug-and-play quantiser will become an important component of future vector methods that use VQ in their learned architecture.
