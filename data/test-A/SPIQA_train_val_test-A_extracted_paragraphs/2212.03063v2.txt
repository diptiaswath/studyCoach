A common learning strategy in image classification is to maximisethe likelihood of the conditional distribution P​(Y|X)𝑃conditional𝑌𝑋P(Y|X) of a classlabel Y𝑌Y given an input image X𝑋X over a training set. This objectiveis performed by exploiting the statistical dependence between inputimages and class labels in the training set to yield good predictionson a test set. Although this learning strategy works well for the“in-distribution” setting in which the test and training setsare from the same domain, recent works (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Lv et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022)have pointed out its limitations in dealing with out-of-distribution(OOD) samples. This deficiency acts as a significant barrier to real-worldapplications, where the test data may come from various domains andmay follow different distributions.

The OOD generalisation task (Shen et al., 2021; Hendrycks et al., 2021)aims to address the issue by requiring deep learning models to infergeneralisable knowledge, which can be discovered by seeking the truecausation of labels or the causal dependence between inputs and labels (Schölkopf et al., 2021; Schölkopf and von Kügelgen, 2022; Arjovsky et al., 2019).However, owing to causal inference, statistical correlations in trainingdata do not always imply the true causation of features to labels (Yao et al., 2021; Glymour et al., 2016; Schölkopf and von Kügelgen, 2022).On the other hand, domain-specific nuisance features that arevariant across domains can be learnt via statistical correlations (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Deng and Zhang, 2022; Lv et al., 2022).These features are captured incidentally from confounding factorssuch as background, or viewpoint, which change across domains andconfound the causal dependence between images and labels. For example,when most training images of the elephant class are found with grass(Fig. 1), by statistical learning,a deep learning model is prone to taking into account features fromthe grass (e.g., the green colour) as a signature for the elephantclass. This bias would mislead the model to wrong predictions on testdata from a different domain (e.g., when an elephant appears on astage or in a sketch painting). Here, by observing the statisticaldependence between the grass and the elephant class, one cannot concludeeither one of them is the cause of the other. Technically, a model,which learns by the statistical correlation objective P​(Y|X)𝑃conditional𝑌𝑋P\left(Y|X\right)on a specific domain, might capture the spurious dependence inducedby confounders from that domain, leading to inferior generalisationability.

Intuitively, we should model only the true causal dependence betweenX𝑋X and Y𝑌Y. In causal inference literature, such dependence isrepresented as P​(Y|do​(X))𝑃conditional𝑌do𝑋P(Y|\mathrm{do}(X)). Here, the do​(X)do𝑋\mathrm{do}(X) operator meansan intervention on X𝑋X, which allows us to remove all spurious associationsbetween X𝑋X and Y𝑌Y (Glymour et al., 2016; Schölkopf and von Kügelgen, 2022).P​(Y|do​(X))𝑃conditional𝑌do𝑋P(Y|\mathrm{do}(X)) can be estimated via back-door adjustment (Glymour et al., 2016).However, a well-known drawback of this approach is the need for observableconfounders. This requirement is not realistic and confounders arealso hard to be explicitly modelled (e.g., decomposing domain backgroundvariables) (Von Kügelgen et al., 2021; Glymour et al., 2016). An alternativeapproach to the issue is front-door adjustment (FA) (Schölkopf and von Kügelgen, 2022; Glymour et al., 2016),which supports causal inference with hidden confounders, making theproblem setting more practical. Implementation of FA requires thechoice of a mediator, which we regard as the semantic informationof images that helps access the causal dependence, in other words,features extracted from images that are always predictive oflabels. Further, the front-door formula appeals to a feasible wayof modelling the combination of the class-predictive semantics ofan input image with the style and/or context of other images. Suchcapability is naturally supported by style transfer models as illustratedin Fig. 1. These methods have demonstratedtheir ability to not only transfer artistic styles but also effectivelycapture the semantic content of images (Huang and Belongie, 2017; Zhang et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2021),which we propose that they hold promise for fulfilling the front-doorrequirements. In this work, we propose a novel front-door adjustment-basedmethod to learn the causal distribution P​(Y|do​(X))𝑃conditional𝑌do𝑋P(Y|\mathrm{do}(X)) in OOD generalisation.We realise our method using two different style transfer models: AdaINNeural Style Transfer (NST) (Huang and Belongie, 2017) and Fourier-basedStyle Transfer (FST) (Xu et al., 2021). Our use of style transferto estimate FA is novel and sensible for OOD generalisation, whichwe show via the superior generalisation capacity of our model on widelyused benchmark datasets. In summary, our main contributions include:•A formulation of OOD generalisation under the perspective of causalinference;•A causal inference solution to OOD generalisation based on FA, whichwe successfully implemented by two different style transfer strategies.Our source code is available at: https://github.com/nktoan/Causal-Inference-via-Style-Transfer-for-OOD-Generalisation.•Extensive experiments on various benchmark datasets, showing the superiorityof our proposed algorithm over state-of-the-art OOD generalisationmethods.

Causal Inference has been a fundamental research topic. Itaims to reconstruct causal relationships between variables and estimatecausal effects on variables of interest (Yao et al., 2021; Glymour et al., 2016).Causal inference has been applied to many deep learning research problems,especially in domain adaptation (DA) (Magliacane et al., 2018; Rojas-Carulla et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2013; Yue et al., 2021)and domain generalisation (DG) (Wang et al., 2022; Mahajan et al., 2021; Mitrovic et al., 2021).However, various difficulties curtail its development in deep learning.The most common application of causal inference is removing spuriouscorrelations of domain-specific confounders that affect the generalisationability of deep models (Yang et al., 2021b; Li et al., 2021a). Existingmethods often adopt back-door adjustment to derive the causal dependence.Examples include visual grounding (Huang et al., 2022),semantic segmentation (Zhang et al., 2020), and object detection (Wang et al., 2020).However, in practice, confounders may not be always observed. In addition,they are hard to be derived (Glymour et al., 2016; Von Kügelgen et al., 2021).In this case, front-door adjustment (FA) (Pearl, 1995; Glymour et al., 2016)is often preferred. FA makes use of a mediator variable to capturethe intermediate causal effect. The effectiveness of the FA approachhas also been verified in many tasks such as vision-language tasks (Yang et al., 2021b),image captioning (Yang et al., 2021a), and DG (Li et al., 2021a).The main difference between our front-door-based algorithm and othersis the interpretation of the front-door formula in causal learning.To the best of our knowledge, the incarnation of style transfer algorithms (Huang and Belongie, 2017; Jing et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021b)in FA is novel. It is a sensible and effective way for DG, which isproven by our experimental results. Asides, to deal with hidden confounders,instrumental (Baiocchi et al., 2014; Tang et al., 2021)or proxy (Yue et al., 2021; Miao et al., 2018) variablescan also be used. However, in the DG problem, identifying the appropriatemediator variable for FA presents fewer challenges and offers greaterpracticality compared to selecting instrumental or proxy variables.Causal inference methods are not straightforward to be technicallyimplemented for practical computer vision tasks. Our work can be seenas the first attempt to effectively apply FA via style transfer algorithmsfor DG.

Domain Generalisation (DG) aims to build image classificationmodels that can generalise well to unseen data. Typical DG methodscan be divided into three approaches including domain-invariant featureslearning (Muandet et al., 2013; Peng and Saenko, 2018), meta-learning (Zhao et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020, 2021a; Dou et al., 2019),and data/feature augmentation (Zhou et al., 2021b; Huang et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2020b).Domain-invariant features can be learnt from different source distributionsvia adversarial learning (Li et al., 2018a, b; Motiian et al., 2017),domain alignment (Muandet et al., 2013; Zhou et al., 2022), or variationalBayes (Xiao et al., 2021). Meta-learning addresses the DG problemby simulating domain shift, e.g., by splitting data from source domainsinto pseudo-training and pseudo-test data (Li et al., 2018c).Data/feature augmentation (Zhou et al., 2020b; Wang et al., 2022) canbe used to improve OOD generalisation by enhancing the diversity oflearnt features. Instead of seeking domain-invariant features, ourmethod aims to learn the causal mechanism between images and targets,which is robust and invariant against domain shift (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021a; Lv et al., 2022).Among data augmentation methods, our method is related to MixStyle (Zhou et al., 2021b)and EFDMix (Zhang et al., 2022) due to the use of style transfer(i.e., the AdaIN algorithm (Huang and Belongie, 2017)) to createnovel data samples. However, our method considers preserving the contentof images as it is crucial in causal learning. Meanwhile, MixStyleonly interleaves some style transfer layers into image classificationmodels. In addition, both MixStyle and EFDMix rely on statisticalpredictions, which are sensitive to domain shift (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Mao et al., 2022).More, we regard the AdaIN algorithm as a tool to implement our proposedfront-door formula for estimating the causal dependence, resultingin generalisable predictions. Similarly, the key advantage of ourmethod over FACT (Xu et al., 2021) is that we use the Fourier-basedtransformation as an implementation of style transfer to infer thecausal dependence.

Causal Inference for DG. The connection between causalityand DG has recently drawn considerable attention from the researchcommunity. Much of the current line of work relies on the assumptionthat there exists causal mechanisms or dependences between inputsor input features and labels, which will be invariant across domains (Teshima et al., 2020; Arjovsky et al., 2019; Mao et al., 2022; Lv et al., 2022; Duong and Nguyen, 2022).Many causality-inspired approaches (Lv et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Mahajan et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2023)learn the domain-invariant representation or content feature thatcan be seen as a direct cause of the label. Owing to the principleof invariant or independent causal mechanisms (Schölkopf and von Kügelgen, 2022; Peters et al., 2017; Mitrovic et al., 2021),the causal mechanism between the content feature and the label remainsunchanged regardless of variations in other variables, such as domainstyles. Some methods (Wang et al., 2022; Mitrovic et al., 2021; Lv et al., 2022)enforce regularisation with different data transformations on thelearnt feature to achieve the content feature. For example, CIRL (Lv et al., 2022)performs causal intervention via Fourier-based data augmentation (Xu et al., 2021).On the other hand, MatchDG (Mahajan et al., 2021) assumes a causalgraph where the content feature is independent of the domain giventhe hidden object and proposes a method that enforces this constraintto the classifier by alternately performing contrastive learning andfinding good positive matches. One drawback of MatchDG is its relianceon the availability of domain labels. Causal mechanism transfer (Teshima et al., 2020)aims to capture causal mechanisms for DA via nonlinear independentcomponent analysis (Hyvarinen et al., 2019). Asides, TCM (Yue et al., 2021)leverages the proxy technique (Yue et al., 2021; Miao et al., 2018)to perform causal inference for DA (where the target domain is provided)rather than DG.

In contrast, our method postulates that the causal dependence betweeninputs and labels in DG can be identified by removing spurious correlationsinduced by hidden confounders (Glymour et al., 2016). Onthe other hand, some methods (Huang et al., 2023; Lv et al., 2022)assume that there is no confounding variable in their proposed causalgraph, which might be unlikely to be satisfied as proposed in manyprevious methods (Sun et al., 2021; Jiang and Veitch, 2022). Anadvantage of our approach over the above methods is that we do notrely on imposing regularisation objectives, which may have gradientconflicts with other learning objectives (Chen et al., 2023).Our method proposes to use the FA theorem (Pearl, 1995; Glymour et al., 2016)and estimate the front-door formula via style transfer algorithms (Huang and Belongie, 2017; Jing et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021b).Note that CICF (Li et al., 2021a) also removes spurious correlationsusing the FA theorem. However, while CICF deploys a meta-learningalgorithm to compute the front-door formula, we interpret the formulavia style transfer, which generates counterfactual images for causallearning.

Let X𝑋X and Y𝑌Y be two random variables representing an input imageand its label, respectively. They often perform well in the “in-distribution”(ID) setting where both training and test samples are from the samedomain. However, they may not generalise well in “out-of-distribution”(OOD) setting where test samples are from an unseen domain. As wewill show below, a possible reason for this phenomenon is that P​(Y|X)𝑃conditional𝑌𝑋P\left(Y|X\right)is domain-dependent while a proper model for OOD generalisationshould be domain-invariant.

In order to use front-door adjustment, we assume that there is a randomvariable Z𝑍Z acting as a mediator between X𝑋X and Y𝑌Y as illustratedin Fig. 2(b). Z𝑍Z must satisfy three conditionsas follows (Glymour et al., 2016): (i) All directed paths fromX𝑋X to Y𝑌Y flow through Z𝑍Z. (ii) X𝑋X blocks all back-door pathsfrom Z𝑍Z to Y𝑌Y. (iii) There are no unblocked back-door paths fromX𝑋X to Z𝑍Z.

The first two conditions suggest that Z𝑍Z should capture all andonly semantic information in X𝑋X that is always predictive of Y𝑌Y,while the last condition means that we should be able to estimatethe causal relationship X→Z→𝑋𝑍X\rightarrow Z by using just the observedtraining images. Such variable Z𝑍Z can be captured by several learningmethods, such as style transfer models (Huang and Belongie, 2017; Jing et al., 2019)or contrastive learning (Von Kügelgen et al., 2021; Mahajan et al., 2021). Evenif the assumptions do not hold perfectly in practice and the mediatorcaptures some spurious features in X𝑋X, our method simply revertsto the standard empirical risk minimisation (ERM) model (Vapnik, 1998),which is widely applicable and can be used on any dataset or problem.Importantly, the more semantically meaningful information that themediator Z𝑍Z captures from X𝑋X, the better our method can approximateP​(Y|do​(x))𝑃conditional𝑌do𝑥P\left(Y|\mathrm{do}(x)\right). The only remaining problem is modelling P​(Y|z,x′)𝑃conditional𝑌𝑧superscript𝑥′P(Y|z,x^{\prime}).In the next section, we propose a novel way to model P​(Y|z,x′)𝑃conditional𝑌𝑧superscript𝑥′P(Y|z,x^{\prime})in front-door adjustment by leveraging neural style transfer.

The main difficulty of modelling P​(Y|z,x′)𝑃conditional𝑌𝑧superscript𝑥′P(Y|z,x^{\prime}) is designing a networkthat can combine the semantic features z𝑧z of an image x𝑥x withanother image x′superscript𝑥′x^{\prime} without changing the class-predictive semanticinformation in z𝑧z. Here, we want a model that can fuse the contentof x𝑥x with the style of x′superscript𝑥′x^{\prime}. A family of models that has suchcapability is neural style transfer (NST). Within the scope of thispaper, we consider AdaIN NST (Huang and Belongie, 2017) as a representativeNST method and use it to model P​(Y|z,x′)𝑃conditional𝑌𝑧superscript𝑥′P(Y|z,x^{\prime}). We note that it is straightforwardto use other NST methods, e.g., (Zhang et al., 2022) in place ofAdaIN NST. We refer this version to as FAST (Front-doorAdjustment via StyleTransfer). Below, we describe AdaIN NST andthe training objective of our method.

We argue three main reasons behind the success of our methods in OODgeneralisation. Owing to the AdaIn NST model, our methods could mapany novel domain back to the learnt ones without hurting the contentof images, which is beneficial for computing P​(Y|d​o​(X))𝑃conditional𝑌𝑑𝑜𝑋P(Y|do(X)). We visuallyjustify this point in Fig. 3.

On the other hand, our great performance would be largely contributedby modelling the causal dependence via front-door adjustment, whichwe show to be invariant across domains in Sections 3, 4.In Table 2, we showthe effectiveness of using P​(Y|d​o​(X))𝑃conditional𝑌𝑑𝑜𝑋P(Y|do(X)) over P​(Y|X)𝑃conditional𝑌𝑋P(Y|X) in both thetraining and inference phases, where the method with causality inboth phases clearly outperforms the others. Many recent state-of-the-artcausality-based methods in OOD generalisation enforce regularisationwith different data transformations on the learnt feature to achievethe domain-invariant feature (Mahajan et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Lv et al., 2022).Although achieving promising results, the regularisation objectivesmay have gradient conflicts with other learning objectives of themodel, leading to inferior performance in some cases (Chen et al., 2023).

Asides, our front-door adjustment solution is orthogonal and moreflexible, since various stylised images can be taken into accountto eliminate spurious correlations of confounders, favouring the causaldependence P​(Y|d​o​(X)).𝑃conditional𝑌𝑑𝑜𝑋P(Y|do(X)). This is validated by the superior performanceof FAGT across all the experimented datasets. Further,our estimation of the causal front-door formula via style transferalgorithms in Eq. 9 is reasonable andgeneral, enabling various stylised images in both FAST and FAFTto be taken into account for mitigating the negative correlationsof confounders.

This paper studies the problem of Out-of-distribution (OOD) generalisationfrom the causality perspective. We show that statistical learningmethods are ineffective for OOD generalisation due to spurious correlationsfrom domain-specific confounders into model training, resulting ininstability to domain shifting. The most commonly used causal inferencemethod, back-door adjustment, is unfeasible since the method requiresthe observation of confounders, which is impractical in the setting.We address the issue via front-door adjustment where features essentialto label prediction are considered the mediator variable. It is nottrivial to interpret the front-door formula, and we propose novelmethods that estimate it based on many style transfer models. We evaluatedour methods on several benchmark datasets. Experimental results showthe superiority of our method over existing baselines in OOD generalisation.
