There has been a growing interest in interpretable machine learning, which seeks to help people understand their models. While interpretable machine learning encompasses a wide range of problems, it is a fairly uncontroversial hypothesis that there exists a trade-off between a modelвҖҷs complexity and general notions of interpretability. This hypothesis suggests a seemingly natural connection to the field of learning theory, which has thoroughly explored relationships between a function classвҖҷs complexity and generalization. Interpretability meets learning theory:Semenova etВ al. (2019) study the performance generalization of models learned from complex classes when they can be globally well-approximated by simpler (e.g. interpretable) classes. In such cases, their theory argues that if the complex class has many models that perform about as optimally on training data, generalization from the complex class can be more closely bounded in terms of the simpler classвҖҷs complexity. In our corresponding results, we similarly aim to avoid involving the larger classвҖҷs complexity. However, we directly study generalization via a functionвҖҷs local explainability, rather than instantiate abstract вҖқcomplexвҖқ and вҖңsimpleвҖқ classes for global approximations. The two are fundamentally different technical problems; standard learning theory results cannot be directly applied as they are for single-function global approximations.

Statistical localized regression:(Fan, 1993; Fan & Gijbels, 1996) are canonical results which bound the squared error of a nonparametric function defined using locally fit models. These local models are both simple (e.g. linear) and similarly trained by weighting real examples with a kernel (i.e. neighborhood). However, in these works, each local model is only used to make a prediction at its source point and the theory requires shrinking the kernel width towards 0 as the sample size grows.We instead fit local models as explanations for a trained model (i.e. which is considered the вҖңtrue regression functionвҖқ) and more importantly, care about the performance of each local model over whole (non-zero) neighborhoods. Unlike localized regression, this allows us to use uniform convergence to bound test error with empirical and generalization terms. While the previous results do not have empirical terms, the learning rates are exponential in the number of samples.

Learning Theory: One line of related work also studies how to explain generalization of overparameterized classes. As standard uniform convergence on these classes often leads to vacuous bounds, a general approach that has followed from (Nagarajan & Kolter, 2019; Zhang etВ al., 2017; Neyshabur etВ al., 2014) has been to study implications of different biases placed on the learned models. We study what would happen if an overparameterized model had an unexplored type of bias, one that is inspired by local explainability. Additionally, our workвҖҷs technical approach also parallels another line of existing results which likewise try to apply uniform convergence on a separate surrogate class. This includes PAC-Bayesian bounds, a large family of techniques that come from looking at a stochastic version of in parameter space (McAllester, 1998; 2003; Langford & Caruana, 2002; Langford & Shawe-Taylor, 2003). In a different vein, some results in deep learning look at compressed/sparsified/explicitly regularized surrogates of neural networks (Arora etВ al., 2018; Dziugaite & Roy, 2017). In our case, the surrogate class is a collection of local explanations.

The generalization error of the function fрқ‘“f is typically bounded by some notion of the representational capacity/complexity of fрқ‘“f. While standard results bound complexity in terms of parameter counts, there is theoretical value in deriving bounds involving other novel terms. By doing so, we can understand how regularizing for those terms can affect the representation capacity, and in turn, the generalization error of fрқ‘“f. Especially when fрқ‘“fвҖҷs complexity may be intractable to bound on its own, introducing these terms provides a potentially useful new way to understand fрқ‘“fвҖҷs generalization.

Here specifically, we are interested in establishing a general connection between the representation complexity and the local intrepretability of any fрқ‘“f.This naturally requires coming up with a notion that appropriately quantifies the complexity of рқ’ўрқ’ў\mathcal{G}, which we discuss in the first part of this section. In the second part, we then relate this quantity to the generalization of fрқ‘“f to derive our first main result.

Main result. With the above key lemma in hand, we are now ready to prove our main result, which bounds the generalization error of fрқ‘“f in terms of the complexity of рқ’ўрқ’ў\mathcal{G}, thereby establishing a connection between model generalization and local interpretability.

This result decomposes the test error of fрқ‘“f into four quantities. The first quantity corresponds to the training error of fрқ‘“f on the training set Sрқ‘ҶS. The second and the third correspond to the mirrored neighborhood fidelity of fрқ‘“f with respect to gрқ‘”g (computed on test and training data respectively). The fourth and final quantity corresponds to a term that bounds the complexity of рқ’ўрқ’ў\mathcal{G} in terms of the вҖңdisjointedness factorвҖқ and the complexity of the simpler function class рқ’ўlocalsubscriptрқ’ўlocal\mathcal{G}_{\text{local}}.

Takeaway. A key aspect of this bound is the trade-off that it captures with varying neighborhood widths. Consider shrinking the neighborhood widths to smaller and smaller values, in turn creating less and less overlap between the neighborhoods of the training data. Then, on the one hand, weвҖҷd observe that the complexity term (the fourth term on the R.H.S) increases. Specifically, since в„ӣ^SвҖӢ(рқ’ўlocal)subscript^в„ӣрқ‘Ҷsubscriptрқ’ўlocal\hat{\mathcal{R}}_{S}(\mathcal{G}_{\text{local}}) typically scales as OвҖӢ(1/m)рқ‘Ӯ1рқ‘ҡO(1/\sqrt{m}), as we go from the one extreme of full overlap to the other extreme of complete disjointedness, the complexity term would increase from OвҖӢ(1/m)рқ‘Ӯ1рқ‘ҡO(1/\sqrt{m}) to OвҖӢ(1)рқ‘Ӯ1O(1) (eventually rendering the bound trivial). This tradeoff is intuitive. A function fрқ‘“f that is hardly amenable to being fit by local explanations would requireextremely tiny neighborhoods for рқ’ўlocalsubscriptрқ’ўlocal\mathcal{G}_{\text{local}} to locally approximate it (i.e. make the MNF terms small). On the other hand, when fрқ‘“f is easy to interpret, then weвҖҷd expect it to be well-approximated by рқ’ўlocalsubscriptрқ’ўlocal\mathcal{G}_{\text{local}} even with wider neighborhoods. This allows one to afford smaller values for both the complexity and MNF terms. In other words, an easy-to-interpret fрқ‘“f enjoys better generalization guarantees.

Caveats. Our bound has two limitations worth noting. First, for high-dimensional datasets (like image datasets), practical choices of Nxsubscriptрқ‘Ғрқ‘ҘN_{x} can lead to almost no overlap between neighborhoods, thus rendering the bound trivial in practice. A second caveat is that the second quantity, MNFвҖӢ(f,g)MNFрқ‘“рқ‘”\textsf{MNF}(f,g), requires unlabeled test data to be computed, which may be limiting if one is interested in numerically computing this bound in practice. We now turn our attention to a more subtle kind of generalization that is both unstudied yet important. Typically, the way gxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘”superscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІg_{x^{\prime}} is learned at any source point xвҖІsuperscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІx^{\prime} is by fitting a finite set of points sampled near xвҖІsuperscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІx^{\prime}, with the hope that this fit generalizes to unseen, neighboring target points. Naturally, we would want to ask: how well do the explanations gxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘”superscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІg_{x^{\prime}} themselves generalize in this sense?

The subtlety in this question is that it is not always a worthwhile question to ask. In particular, assume that we learn gxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘”superscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІg_{x^{\prime}} by sampling a set SxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘Ҷsuperscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІS_{x^{\prime}} of nearby points from a Gaussian centered at xвҖІsuperscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІx^{\prime}, and that we care about the fit of gxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘”superscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІg_{x^{\prime}} generalizing to the same Gaussian. Here, we have access to unlimited amounts of data from the known Gaussian distribution (and free labels using fвҖӢ(вӢ…)рқ‘“вӢ…f(\cdot)), so we can be sure that with sufficiently large SxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘Ҷsuperscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІS_{x^{\prime}}, gxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘”superscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІg_{x^{\prime}} will fit to arbitrarily small error on local neighborhoods. Hence, the above generalization question is neither conceptually nor practically interesting here.

However, consider finite sample-based local explanation settings like MAPLE (Plumb etВ al., 2018) and RL-LIM (Yoon etВ al., 2019) where the training procedure is vastly different from this: in these procedures, the goal is to learn local explanations gxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘”superscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІg_{x^{\prime}} in a way that is sensitive to the local structure of the (unknown) underlying data distribution Dрқҗ·D. So, instead of fitting the gxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘”superscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІg_{x^{\prime}} to samples drawn from an arbitrarily defined Gaussian distribution, here one first draws a finite sample Sрқ‘ҶS from the underlying distribution Dрқҗ·D (and then labels it using fрқ‘“f).Then, across all xвҖІвҲҲрқ’іsuperscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІрқ’іx^{\prime}\in\mathcal{X}, one reuses a reweighted version of the same dataset Sрқ‘ҶS (typically, points xрқ‘Ҙx in Sрқ‘ҶS that are near xвҖІsuperscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІx^{\prime} are weighted more) and then learns a gxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘”superscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІg_{x^{\prime}} that fits this reweighted dataset. Contrast this with the former setting, where for each xвҖІsuperscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІx^{\prime}, one has access to a fresh dataset (namely, SxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘Ҷsuperscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІS_{x^{\prime}}) to learn gxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘”superscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІg_{x^{\prime}}. This distinction then makes it interesting to wonder when the reuse of a common dataset Sрқ‘ҶS could cause the explanations to generalize poorly.

Motivated by this question, we present TheoremВ 2. By using LemmaВ 4.1, we provide a bound on the вҖңtest MNFвҖқ (which corresponds to the fit of gxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘”superscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІg_{x^{\prime}} on the unseen data averaged across Dрқҗ·D) in terms of the вҖңtrain MNFвҖқ (which corresponds to the fit of gxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘”superscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІg_{x^{\prime}} on Sрқ‘ҶS, averaged across xвҖІsuperscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІx^{\prime}) and the complexity term from LemmaВ 4.1. We must however caution the reader that this theorem does not answer the exact question posed in the above paragraph; it only addresses it indirectly as we discuss shortly.

Understanding the overall bound. We first elaborate on how this bound provides an (indirect) answer to our question about how well explanations generalize.Consider a procedure like MAPLE that learns gрқ‘”g using the finite dataset Sрқ‘ҶS. For each xвҖІвҲҲрқ’іsuperscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІрқ’іx^{\prime}\in\mathcal{X}, we would expect this procedure to have learned a gxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘”superscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІg_{x^{\prime}} that fits well on at least those target points xрқ‘Ҙx in Sрқ‘ҶS that are near xвҖІsuperscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІx^{\prime}.In doing so, itвҖҷs reasonable to expect the training procedure to have implicitly controlled the вҖңtrain MNFвҖқ term The reasoning for this is that the train MNF computes the error in the fit of gxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘”superscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІg_{x^{\prime}} on Sрқ‘ҶS for different values of xвҖІsuperscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІx^{\prime}, and sums these up in a way that errors corresponding to nearby values of (x,xвҖІ)рқ‘Ҙsuperscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІ(x,x^{\prime}) are weighted more (where the weight is given by pNxmirвҖӢ(xвҖІ)subscriptрқ‘қsubscriptsuperscriptрқ‘Ғmirрқ‘Ҙsuperscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІp_{N^{\textsf{mir}}_{x}}(x^{\prime})).Now, our bound suggests that when this train MNF is minimized, this carries over to test MNF too (provided the complexity term is not large). The indirectness of our result. Existing finite sample-based explainers do not explicitly minimize the train MNF term (e.g., MAPLE minimizes an error based upon NF). However, as argued above, they have implicit control over train MNF. Hence, our bound essentially treats MNF as a surrogate for reasoning about the generalization of the explanations learned by an arbitrary procedure. As such, our bound does not comment on how well the exact kind of fidelity metric used during training generalizes to test data. Nevertheless, we hope that this result offers a concrete first step towards quantifying the generalization of explanations. Furthermore, we also note that one could also imagine a novel explanation-learning procedure that does explicitly minimize the train MNF term to learn gрқ‘”g; in such a case our bound would provide a direct answer to how well its explanations generalize. Indeed, we derive such a theoretically-principled algorithm in Appendix A.

Takeaway.While the above bound captures a similar trade-off with neighborhood width as the Theorem 1, it is worth pausing to appreciate the distinct manner in which this tradeoff arises here. Caveats. We remark that this particular bound applies only when the dataset Sрқ‘ҶS is used to learn only gрқ‘”g i.e., fрқ‘“f and the neighborhoods must be learned beforehand with separate data. Now, if one is interested in a bound where Sрқ‘ҶS is also used to simultaneously learn fрқ‘“f, the only change to the bound is an added factor corresponding to the complexity of в„ұв„ұ\mathcal{F}. Another caveat is that our bound only tells us how well the explanations gxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘”superscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІg_{x^{\prime}} generalize on average over different values of xвҖІsuperscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІx^{\prime}. This does not tell us anything about the quality of the generalization of gxвҖІsubscriptрқ‘”superscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІg_{x^{\prime}} for an arbitrary value of xвҖІsuperscriptрқ‘ҘвҖІx^{\prime}. That being said, just as average accuracy remains a central metric for performance (despite ignoring discrepancies across inputs), average MNF can still be a useful quantity for evaluating an explainerвҖҷs overall performance.

In this work, we have studied two novel connections between learning theory and local explanations. We believe these results may be of use in guiding the following directions of future work: (1) developing new local explanation algorithms inspired by our theory and the metric of MNF; (2) resolving caveats or otherwise strengthening the theory presented in this paper; and (3) exploring applications of our techniques beyond interpretability, such as the general problem of deep learning generalization or others that require reasoning about the complexity of randomized functions.
