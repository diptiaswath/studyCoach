Given an image IрқҗјI, a dialog history of past Q&A pairs: H={(Q1,A1),вҖҰ,(QtвҲ’1,AtвҲ’1)}рқҗ»subscriptрқ‘„1subscriptрқҗҙ1вҖҰsubscriptрқ‘„рқ‘Ў1subscriptрқҗҙрқ‘Ў1H=\{(Q_{1},A_{1}),...,(Q_{t-1},A_{t-1})\}, and the current tрқ‘Ўt-th round question Qрқ‘„Q, a Visual Dialog (VisDial) agentВ [9] is expected to provide a good answer AрқҗҙA. Our community has always considered VQAВ [5] and VisDial as sister tasks due to their similar settings: Q&A grounded by IрқҗјI (VQA) and Q&A grounded by (I,H)рқҗјрқҗ»(I,H) (VisDial). Indeed, from a technical point view вҖ” just like the VQA models вҖ” a typical VisDial model first uses encoder to represent IрқҗјI, Hрқҗ»H, and Qрқ‘„Q as vectors, and then feed them into decoder for answer AрқҗҙA. Thanks to the recent advances in encoder-decoder frameworks in VQAВ [22, 38] and natural language processingВ [39], the performance (NDCGВ [1]) of VisDial in literature is significantly improved from the baseline 51.63%В [2] to the state-of-the-art 64.47%В [11].

However, in this paper, we want to highlight an important fact: VisDial is essentially NOT VQA with history! And this fact is so profound that all the common heuristics in the vision-language community вҖ” such as the multimodal fusionВ [38, 46] and attention variantsВ [22, 25, 26] вҖ” cannot appreciate the difference. Instead, we introduce the use of causal inferenceВ [27, 28]: a graphical framework that stands in the cause-effect interpretation of the data, but not merely the statistical association of them. Before we delve into the details, we would like to present the main contributions: two causal principles, rooted from the analysis of the difference between VisDial and VQA, which lead to a performance leap вҖ” a farewell to the 60%-s and an embrace for the 70%-s вҖ” for all the baseline VisDial models111Only those with codes&reproducible results due to resource limit. in literatureВ [9, 21, 41, 26], promoting them to the state-of-the-art in Visual Dialog Challenge 2019В [2].

Figure 1 compares the causal graphs of existing VisDial models and the one applied with the proposed two principles. Although a formal introduction of them is given in SectionВ 3.2, now you can simply understand the nodes as data types and the directed links as modal transformations. For example, VвҶ’AвҶ’рқ‘үрқҗҙV\rightarrow A and QвҶ’AвҶ’рқ‘„рқҗҙQ\rightarrow A indicate that answer AрқҗҙA is the effect caused by visual knowledge Vрқ‘үV and question Qрқ‘„Q, through a transformation, e.g., a multi-modal encoder.

P1 suggests that we should remove the direct input of dialog history to the answer model. This principle contradicts most of the prevailing VisDial modelsВ [9, 15, 41, 26, 43, 16, 11, 32], which are based on the widely accepted intuition: the more features you input, the more effective the model is. It is mostly correct, but only with our discretion of the data generation process. In fact, the VisDialВ [9] annotators were not allowed to copy from the previous Q&A, i.e., HвҶӣAвҶӣрқҗ»рқҗҙH\nrightarrow A, but were encouraged to ask consecutive questions including co-referenced pronouns like вҖңitвҖқ and вҖңthoseвҖқ, i.e., HвҶ’QвҶ’рқҗ»рқ‘„H\rightarrow Q, and thus the answer AрқҗҙA is expected to be only based on question Qрқ‘„Q and reasoned visual knowledge Vрқ‘үV. Therefore, a good model should reason over the context (I,H)рқҗјрқҗ»(I,H) with Qрқ‘„Q but not to memorize the bias. However, the direct path HвҶ’AвҶ’рқҗ»рқҗҙH\rightarrow A will contaminate the expected causality. Figure 2(a) shows a very ridiculous bias observed in all baselines without P1: the top answers are those with length closer to the average length in the history answers. We will offer more justifications for P1 in SectionВ 4.1.

P2 implies that the model training only based on the association between (I,H,Q)рқҗјрқҗ»рқ‘„(I,H,Q) and AрқҗҙA is spurious. By вҖңspuriousвҖқ, we mean that the effect on AрқҗҙA caused by (I,H,Q)рқҗјрқҗ»рқ‘„(I,H,Q) вҖ” the goal of VisDial вҖ” is confounded by an unobserved variable Uрқ‘ҲU, because it appears in every undesired causal path (a.k.a., backdoorВ [28]), which is an indirect causal link from input (I,H,Q)рқҗјрқҗ»рқ‘„(I,H,Q) to output AрқҗҙA: QвҶҗUвҶ’AвҶҗрқ‘„рқ‘ҲвҶ’рқҗҙQ\leftarrow U\rightarrow A and QвҶҗHвҶ’UвҶ’AвҶҗрқ‘„рқҗ»вҶ’рқ‘ҲвҶ’рқҗҙQ\leftarrow H\rightarrow U\rightarrow A. We believe that such unobserved Uрқ‘ҲU should be users as the VisDial dataset essentially brings humans in the loop. Figure 2(b) illustrates how the userвҖҷs hidden preference confounds them. Therefore, during training, if we focus only on the conventional likelihood PвҖӢ(A|I,H,Q)рқ‘ғconditionalрқҗҙрқҗјрқҗ»рқ‘„P(A|I,H,Q), the model will inevitably be biased towards the spurious causality, e.g., it may score answer вҖңYes, he isвҖқ higher than вҖңYesвҖқ, merely because the users prefer to see a вҖңheвҖқ appeared in the answer, given the history context of вҖңheвҖқ. It is worth noting that the confounder Uрқ‘ҲU is more impactful in VisDial than in VQA, because the former encourages the user to rank similar answers subjectively while the latter is more objective. A plausible explanation might be: VisDial is interactive in nature and a not quite correct answer is tolerable in one iteration (i.e., dense prediction); while VQA has only one chance, which demands accuracy (i.e., one-hot prediction).

By applying P1 and P2 to the baseline causal graph, we have the proposed one (the right one in Figure 1), which serves as a model-agnostic roadmap for the causal inference of VisDial. To remove the spurious effect caused by Uрқ‘ҲU, we use the do-calculusВ [28] PвҖӢ(A|dвҖӢoвҖӢ(I,H,Q))рқ‘ғconditionalрқҗҙрқ‘‘рқ‘ңрқҗјрқҗ»рқ‘„P\left(A|do(I,H,Q)\right), which is fundamentally different from the conventional likelihood PвҖӢ(A|I,H,Q)рқ‘ғconditionalрқҗҙрқҗјрқҗ»рқ‘„P(A|I,H,Q): the former is an active intervention, which cuts off UвҶ’QвҶ’рқ‘Ҳрқ‘„U\rightarrow Q and HвҶ’QвҶ’рқҗ»рқ‘„H\rightarrow Q, and sample (where the name вҖңcalculusвҖқ is from) every possible U|Hconditionalрқ‘Ҳрқҗ»U|H, seeking the true effect on AрқҗҙA only caused by (I,H,Q)рқҗјрқҗ»рқ‘„(I,H,Q); while the latter likelihood is a passive observation that is affected by the existence of Uрқ‘ҲU. The formal introduction and details will be given in SectionВ 4.3. In particular, given the fact that once the dataset is ready, Uрқ‘ҲU is no longer observed, we propose a series of effective approximations in SectionВ 5.

We validate the effectiveness of P1 and P2 on the most recent VisDial v1.0 dataset. We show significant boosts (absolute NDCG) by applying them in 4 representative baseline models: LFВ [9] (вҶ‘вҶ‘\uparrow16.42%), HCIAEВ [21] (вҶ‘вҶ‘\uparrow15.01%), CoAttВ [41] (вҶ‘вҶ‘\uparrow15.41%), and RvAВ [26] (вҶ‘вҶ‘\uparrow16.14%). Impressively, on the official test-std server, we use an ensemble model of the most simple baseline LFВ [9] to beat our 2019 winning performance by 0.2%, a more complex ensemble to beat it by 0.9%, and lead all the single-model baselines to the state-of-the-art performances.

Visual Dialog. Visual DialogВ [9, 10] is more interactive and challenging than most of the vision-language tasks, e.g., image captioningВ [45, 44, 4] and VQAВ [5, 38, 37, 36]. Specifically, DasВ et al.В [9] collected a large-scale free-form visual dialog dataset VisDialВ [7]. They applied a novel protocol: during the live chat, the questioner cannot see the picture and asks open-ended questions, while the answerer gives free-form answers. Another dataset GuessWhat?! proposed byВ [10] is a goal-driven visual dialog: questioner should locate an unknown object in a rich image scene by asking a sequence of closed-ended вҖңyes/noвҖқ questions. We apply the first setting in this paper. Thus, the key difference is that the users played an important role in the data collection process.

All of the existing approaches in the VisDial task are based on the typical encoder-decoder frameworkВ [15, 12, 33, 11, 32, 47]. They can be categorized by the usage of history. 1) Holistic: they treat history as a whole to feed into models like HACANВ [43], DANВ [16] and CorefNMNВ [18]. 2) Hierarchical: they use a hierarchical structure to deal with history like HREВ [9]. 3) Recursive: RvAВ [26] uses a recursive method to process history. However, they all overlook the fact that the history information should not be directly fed to the answer model (i.e., our proposed Principle 1). The baselines we used in this paper are LFВ [9]: the earliest model, HCIAEВ [21]: the first model to use history hierarchical attention, CoAttВ [41]: the first one to a co-attention mechanism, and RvAВ [26]: the first one for a tree-structured attention mechanism.

Causal Inference. Recently, some worksВ [24, 6, 23, 34, 40] introduced causal inference into machine learning, trying to endow models the abilities of pursuing the cause-effect. In particular, we use the PearlвҖҷs structural causal model (SCM) proposed byВ [28] to hypothesize the data generation process, which is a model-agnostic framework that reflects the nature of the data.

In this section, we formally introduce the visual dialog task and describe how the popular encoder-decoder framework follows the baseline causal graph shown in Figure 1. More details of causal graph can be found inВ [28, 29].

Causal Graph. Causal graphВ [28], as shown in Figure 1, describes how variables interact with each other, expressed by a directed acyclic graph рқ’ў={рқ’©,в„°}рқ’ўрқ’©в„°\mathcal{G}=\{\mathcal{N},\mathcal{E}\} consisting of nodes рқ’©рқ’©\mathcal{N} and directed edges в„°в„°\mathcal{E} (i.e., arrows). рқ’©рқ’©\mathcal{N} denote variables, and в„°в„°\mathcal{E} (arrows) denote the causal relationships between two nodes,i.e., AвҶ’BвҶ’рқҗҙрқҗөA\rightarrow B denotes that AрқҗҙA is the cause and BрқҗөB is the effect, meaning the outcome of BрқҗөB is caused by AрқҗҙA. Causal graph is a highly general roadmap specifying the causal dependencies among variables.

As we will discuss in the following part, all of the existing methods can be revisited in the view of the baseline graph shown in Figure 1.

Feature Representation and Attention in Encoder. Visual feature is denoted as node IрқҗјI in the baseline graph, which is usually a fixed feature extracted by Faster-RCNNВ [31] based on ResNet backboneВ [13] pre-trained on Visual GenomeВ [19]. For language feature, the encoder firstly embeds sentence into word vectors, followed by passing the RNNВ [14, 8] to generate features of question and history, which are denoted as {Q,H}рқ‘„рқҗ»\{Q,H\}.

Most of existing methods apply attention mechanismВ [42] in encoder-decoder to explore the latent weights for a set of features. A basic attention operation can be represented as рқ’ҷ~=AвҖӢtвҖӢtвҖӢ(рқ’і,рқ’Ұ)bold-~рқ’ҷрқҗҙрқ‘Ўрқ‘Ўрқ’ірқ’Ұ\bm{\tilde{x}}=Att(\mathcal{X},\mathcal{K}) where рқ’ірқ’і\mathcal{X} is the set of features need to attend, рқ’Ұрқ’Ұ\mathcal{K} is the key (i.e., guidance) and рқ’ҷ~bold-~рқ’ҷ\bm{\tilde{x}} is the attended feature of рқ’ірқ’і\mathcal{X}. Details can be found in most visual dialog methodsВ [21, 41, 43]. In the baseline graph, the sub-graph {IвҶ’V,QвҶ’V,HвҶ’QвҶ’V}formulae-sequenceвҶ’рқҗјрқ‘үformulae-sequenceвҶ’рқ‘„рқ‘үвҶ’рқҗ»рқ‘„вҶ’рқ‘ү\{I\rightarrow V,Q\rightarrow V,H\rightarrow Q\rightarrow V\} denotes a series of attention operations for visual knowledge Vрқ‘үV. Note that the implementation of the arrows are not necessarily independent, such as co-attentionВ [41], and the process can be written as Input:{I,Q,H}вҮ’Output:{V}:InputвҮ’рқҗјрқ‘„рқҗ»Output:рқ‘ү{\textit{Input}}:\{I,Q,H\}\Rightarrow{\textit{Output}}:\{V\}, where possible intermediate variables can be added as mediator nodes into the original arrows. However, without loss of generality, these mediators do not affect the causalities in the graph.

After obtaining the features from the encoder, existing methods will fuse them and feed the fused ones into a decoder to generate an answer.In the baseline graph, node AрқҗҙA denotes the answer model that decodes the fused features from {HвҶ’A,QвҶ’A,VвҶ’A}formulae-sequenceвҶ’рқҗ»рқҗҙformulae-sequenceвҶ’рқ‘„рқҗҙвҶ’рқ‘үрқҗҙ\{H\!\rightarrow\!A,Q\!\rightarrow\!A,V\!\rightarrow\!A\} and then transforms them into an answer sentence.In particular, the decoder can be generative, i.e., to generate an answer sentence using RNN; or discriminative, i.e., to select an answer sentence by using candidate answer classifiers.

When should we draw an arrow from one node pointing to another? According to the definition in SectionВ 3.2, the criterion is that if the node is the cause and the other one is the effect. Intrigued, letвҖҷs understand P1 by discussing the rationale behind the вҖңdouble-blindвҖқ review policy. Given three variables: вҖңWell-known ResearcherвҖқ (Rрқ‘…R), вҖңHigh-quality PaperвҖқ (Pрқ‘ғP), and вҖңAcceptвҖқ (AрқҗҙA). From our community common sense, we know that RвҶ’PвҶ’рқ‘…рқ‘ғR\!\rightarrow\!P since top researchers usually lead high-quality research, and PвҶ’AвҶ’рқ‘ғрқҗҙP\rightarrow A is even more obvious. Therefore, for the good of the community, the double-blind prohibits the direct link RвҶ’AвҶ’рқ‘…рқҗҙR\rightarrow A by author anonymity, otherwise the bias such as personal emotions and politics from Rрқ‘…R may affect the outcome of AрқҗҙA.

The story is similar in VisDial. Without loss of generality, we only analyze the path HвҶ’QвҶ’AвҶ’рқҗ»рқ‘„вҶ’рқҗҙH\!\rightarrow\!Q\!\rightarrow A. If we inspect the role of Hрқҗ»H, we can find that it is to help Qрқ‘„Q resolve some co-references like вҖңitвҖқ and вҖңtheirвҖқ. As a result, Qрқ‘„Q listens to Hрқҗ»H. Then, we use Qрқ‘„Q to obtain AрқҗҙA. Here, Qрқ‘„Q becomes a mediator which cuts off the direct association between Hрқҗ»H and AрқҗҙA that makes PвҖӢ(A|Q,H)=PвҖӢ(A|Q)рқ‘ғconditionalрқҗҙрқ‘„рқҗ»рқ‘ғconditionalрқҗҙрқ‘„P(A|Q,H)\!=\!P(A|Q), like the вҖңHigh-quality PaperвҖқ that we mentioned in the previous story. However, if we set an arrow from Hрқҗ»H to AрқҗҙA: HвҶ’AвҶ’рқҗ»рқҗҙH\rightarrow A, the undesirable bias of Hрқҗ»H will be learned for the prediction of AрқҗҙA, that hampers the natural process of VisDial, such as the interesting bias illustrated in Figure 2(a). Another example is discussed in Figure 4 that AрқҗҙA prefers to match the words in Hрқҗ»H, even though they are literally nonsense about Qрқ‘„Q if we add the direct link HвҶ’AвҶ’рқҗ»рқҗҙH\rightarrow A. After we apply P1, these phenomena will be relieved, such as the blue line illustrated in Figure 2(a), which is closer to the NDCG ground truth average answer length , denoted as the green dashed line. Please refer to other qualitative studies in SectionВ 6.5.

Before discussing P2, we first introduce an important concept in causal inferenceВ [28]. In causal graph, the fork-like pattern in Figure 3(a) contains a confounder Uрқ‘ҲU, which is the common cause for Qрқ‘„Q and AрқҗҙA (i.e., QвҶҗUвҶ’AвҶҗрқ‘„рқ‘ҲвҶ’рқҗҙQ\leftarrow\!U\!\rightarrow\!A). The confounder Uрқ‘ҲU opens a backdoor path started from Qрқ‘„Q, making Qрқ‘„Q and AрқҗҙA spuriously correlated even if there is no direct causality between them.

In the data generation process of VisDial, we know that not only both of the questioner and answerer can see the dialog history, but also the answer annotators can look at the history when annotating the answer. Their preference after seeing the history can be understood as a part of the human nature or subtleties conditional on a dialog context, and thus it has a causal effect on both Qрқ‘„Q and AрқҗҙA. Moreover, due to the fact that the preference is nuanced and uncontrollable, we consider it as an unobserved confounder for Qрқ‘„Q and AрқҗҙA.

It is worth noting that the confounder hinders us to find the true causal effect. LetвҖҷs take the graph in Figure 3(b) as an example. The causal effect from Qрқ‘„Q to AрқҗҙA is 0; however, we can quickly see that PвҖӢ(A|Q)вҲ’PвҖӢ(A)рқ‘ғconditionalрқҗҙрқ‘„рқ‘ғрқҗҙP(A|Q)-P(A) is nonzero because Qрқ‘„Q and AрқҗҙA are both influenced by Uрқ‘ҲU and thus are correlated (thanks to ReichenbachвҖҷs common cause principleВ [28]). That is, if we are given Qрқ‘„Q, the any likelihood change for AрқҗҙA will be sensible compared to nothing is given. Therefore, if we consider PвҖӢ(A|Q)рқ‘ғconditionalрқҗҙрқ‘„P(A|Q) as our VisDial model, it will still predict nonsense answers even if Qрқ‘„Q has nothing to do with AрқҗҙA. As illustrated in Figure 2(b), model will prefer the candidates about вҖңheвҖқ even though Qрқ‘„Q is not given, that means it captures the confounder Uрқ‘ҲU but not the true rationale between Qрқ‘„Q and AрқҗҙA. Next, we will introduce a powerful technique that makes the Qрқ‘„Q and AрқҗҙA in Figure 3(b) вҖңindependentвҖқ, i.e., no causal relation.

The technique is do-calculus introduced inВ [28, 29]. Specifically, do(Q=q)рқ‘„рқ‘һ(Q=q) denotes that we deliberately assign a value qрқ‘һq to variable Qрқ‘„Q (i.e., intervention), rather than passively observe Q=qрқ‘„рқ‘һQ=q. As illustrated in Figure 3(c), do(Q=q)рқ‘„рқ‘һ(Q=q) can be understood as cutting all the original incoming arrows to Qрқ‘„Q, and then making Qрқ‘„Q and Uрқ‘ҲU independent. Therefore, we can have the well-known backdoor adjustmentВ [28]: PвҖӢ(A|dвҖӢoвҖӢ(Q=q))=вҲ‘uPвҖӢ(A|Q=q,u)вҖӢPвҖӢ(u)рқ‘ғconditionalрқҗҙрқ‘‘рқ‘ңрқ‘„рқ‘һsubscriptрқ‘ўрқ‘ғconditionalрқҗҙрқ‘„рқ‘һрқ‘ўрқ‘ғрқ‘ўP(A|do(Q=q))=\sum\nolimits_{u}P(A|Q=q,u)P(u). Note that this is different from Bayes rule PвҖӢ(A|Q=q)=вҲ‘uPвҖӢ(A|Q=q,u)вҖӢPвҖӢ(u|Q=q)рқ‘ғconditionalрқҗҙрқ‘„рқ‘һsubscriptрқ‘ўрқ‘ғconditionalрқҗҙрқ‘„рқ‘һрқ‘ўрқ‘ғconditionalрқ‘ўрқ‘„рқ‘һP(A|Q=q)=\sum\nolimits_{u}P(A|Q=q,u)P(u|Q=q) thanks to the independence PвҖӢ(u|Q=q)=PвҖӢ(u)рқ‘ғconditionalрқ‘ўрқ‘„рқ‘һрқ‘ғрқ‘ўP(u|Q=q)=P(u) introduced by do-calculus. LetвҖҷs revisit Figure 3(b) by using do-calculus. We can find that PвҖӢ(A|dвҖӢoвҖӢ(Q=q))вҲ’PвҖӢ(A)=0рқ‘ғconditionalрқҗҙрқ‘‘рқ‘ңрқ‘„рқ‘һрқ‘ғрқҗҙ0P(A|do(Q=q))-P(A)=0, that is to say, any intervention of Qрқ‘„Q will not influence the probability of AрқҗҙA, meaning the correct relation between Qрқ‘„Q and AрқҗҙA: no causal relation. Dataset. Our proposed principles are evaluated on the recently released real-world dataset VisDial v1.0. Specifically, the training set of VisDial v1.0 contains 123K images from COCO datasetВ [20] with a 10-round dialog for each image, resulting in totally 1.2M dialog rounds. The validation and test sets were collected from Flickr, with 2K and 8K COCO-like images respectively. The test set is further split into test-std and test-challenge splits, both with the number of 4K images that are hosted on the blind online evaluation server. Each dialog in the training and validation sets has 10 rounds, while the number in test set is uniformly distributed from 1 to 10. For each dialog, a list of 100 answer candidates is given for evaluation. In the following, the results are reported on the validation and test-std set.

Metrics. As mentioned in SectionВ 3.1, NDCG is recommended by the official and accepted by the community. There are some other retrieval-based metrics like MRR (Mean Reciprocal Rank), where the ground-truth answer is generated by the single user. Note that the only answer may easily influenced by the single userвҖҷs preference (i.e., length). We argue that this may be the reason why the models with history shortcut achieve higher MRR, (e.g., due to the bias illustrated in Figure 2) and lower NDCG. Therefore, retrieval-based metrics are not consistent with NDCG. According to the mentioned reasons and space limitation, we only show the results on NDCG in the main paper. For completeness, the further discussion between NDCG and other retrieval-based metrics and the performance on all metrics will be given in the supplementary materials.

We report the performance of the following base models, including LFВ [9], HCIAEВ [21], CoAttВ [41] and RvAВ [26]:

LFВ [9]. This naive base model has no attention module. We expand the model by adding some basic attention operations, including question-based history attention and question-history-based visual attention refinement.

HCIAEВ [21]. The model consists of question-based history attention and question-history-based visual attention.

CoAttВ [41]. The model consists of question-based visual attention, image-question-based history attention, image-history-based question attention, and the final question-history-based visual attention.

RvAВ [26]. The model consists of question-based visual attention and history-based visual attention refinement.

Pre-processing. For language pre-processing, we followed the process introduced byВ [9]. First, we lowercased all the letters in sentences, converted digits to words and removed contractions. After that, we used Python NLTK toolkit to tokenize sentences into word lists, followed by padding or truncating captions, questions, and answers to the length of 40, 20 and 20, respectively. Then, we built a vocabulary of the tokens with the size of 11,322, including 11,319 words that occur at least 5 times in train v1.0 and 3 instruction tokens. We loaded the pre-trained word embeddings from GloVeВ [30] to initialize all word embeddings, which were shared in encoder and decoder, and we applied 2-layers LSTMs to encode word embedding and set their hidden state dimension to 512. For the visual feature, we used bottom-up-attention featuresВ [4] given by the officialВ [1].

Implementation of Principles. For Principle 1 (P1), we eliminated the history feature in the final fused vector representation for all models, while kept other parts unchanged. For HCIAEВ [21] and CoAttВ [41], we also blocked the history guidance to the image. For Principle 2 (P2), we trained the models using the preference score, which can be counted from question type or given by the official (i.e., dense annotations in VisDial v1.0 training set). Specifically, for вҖңquestion typeвҖқ, we first defined 55 types and marked answers occurred over 5 times as preferred answers, then used the preference to train our model by (R2subscriptрқ‘…2R_{2}) loss proposed in SectionВ 5.2. вҖңAnswer score samplingвҖқ was directly used to fine-tune our pre-trained model by the proposed loss functions. For вҖңhidden dictionaryвҖқ, we set a matrix for Nрқ‘ҒN as 100 and dрқ‘‘d as 512 to realize Dusubscriptрқҗ·рқ‘ўD_{u}. The dictionary is initialized with the features of top-100 popular answers, then trained by dense annotations with R3subscriptрқ‘…3R_{3} loss. More details can be found in supplementary materials. Note that the implementations following P1 and P2 are flexible.

Training. We used softmax cross-entropy loss to train the model with P1, and used AdamВ [17] with the learning rate of 4Г—10вҲ’34superscript1034\times 10^{-3} which decayed at epoch 5, 7, 9 with the decay rate of 0.4. The model was trained for 15 epochs totally. In addition, DropoutВ [35] was applied with ratio of 0.4 for RNN and 0.25 for fully connected layers. Other settings were set by default.

Overall, all of the implementations can improve the performances of base models. Specifically, the implementations of P2 can further boost performance by at most 11.75% via hidden dictionary learning. The qualitative results illustrated in Figure 4 and Figure 5 show the following advantages of our principles.

History Bias Elimination. After applying P1, the harmful patterns learned from history are relieved, like the answer-length bias shown in Figure 2(a) as we mentioned. The example on the top of Figure 4 shows the word-match bias in baseline. From this example, we can observe that the word вҖңsinkвҖқ from history is literally unrelated to the current question, However, for the baseline model, some undesirable candidates (i.e., with low relevance score) containing the word вҖңsinkвҖқ can be found in the top ranked answers due to the wrong direct history shortcut. To further confirm the conjecture, we counted the word-match cases of objective words (e.g., вҖңsinkвҖқ and вҖңcounterвҖқ) on the validation set for the top-10 candidates of the ranked lists. The statistic indicates that P1 can decrease about 10% word matching from history (from вҲјsimilar-to\sim5200 times of baseline to вҲјsimilar-to\sim4800 times using P1). The bottom example shows that, when the answer вҖңyesвҖқ exists in history, baseline model will tend to rank вҖңyesвҖқ in a high place. More Reasonable Ranking. Figure 5 shows that the baseline model only focuses on ground truth answers like вҖңnoвҖқ or вҖңyesвҖқ and does not care about the rank of other answers with similar meaning like вҖңnopeвҖқ or вҖңyes, he isвҖқ. In this paper, we proposed two causal principles for improving the VisDial task. They are model-agnostic, and thus can be applied in almost all the existing methods and bring major improvement. The principles are drawn from our in-depth causal analysis of the VisDial nature, which is however unfortunately overlooked by our community. For technical contributions, we offered some implementations on how to apply the principles into baseline models. We conducted extensive experiments on the official VisDial dataset and the online evaluation servers. Promising results demonstrate the effectiveness of the two principles. As moving forward, we will stick to our causal thinking to discover other potential causalities hidden in embodied Q&A and conversational visual dialog tasks.
