We consider the following high-dimensional linear regression model. Consider n𝑛n samples of a vector β∗∈ℝpsuperscript𝛽superscriptℝ𝑝\beta^{*}\in\mathbb{R}^{p} in a vector form Y=X​β∗+W𝑌𝑋superscript𝛽𝑊Y=X\beta^{*}+W for some X∈ℝn×p𝑋superscriptℝ𝑛𝑝X\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times p} and W∈ℝn𝑊superscriptℝ𝑛W\in\mathbb{R}^{n}. Given the knowledge of Y𝑌Y and X𝑋X the goal is to infer β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} using an efficient algorithm and the minimum number n𝑛n of samples possible. Throughout the paper we call p𝑝p the number of features, X𝑋X the measurement matrix and W𝑊W the noise vector.

We focus on the high-dimensional case where n𝑛n may be much smaller than p𝑝p and p𝑝p grows to infinity, a setting that has been very popular in the literature during the last years [Chen et al., 2001], [Donoho, 2006], [Candes et al., 2006], [Foucart and Rauhut, 2013], [Wainwright, 2009]. In this case, and under no additional structural assumption, the inference task becomes impossible, even in the noiseless case W=0𝑊0W=0, as the underlying linear system becomes underdetermined. Most papers address this issue by imposing a sparsity assumption on β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*}, which refers to β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} having only a limited number of non-zero entries compared to its dimension [Donoho, 2006], [Candes et al., 2006], [Foucart and Rauhut, 2013]. During the past decades, the sparsity assumption led to a fascinating line of research in statistics and compressed sensing, which established, among other results, that several polynomial-time algorithms, such as Basis Pursuit Denoising Scheme and LASSO, can efficiently recover a sparse β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} with number of samples much smaller than the number of features [Candes et al., 2006], [Wainwright, 2009], [Foucart and Rauhut, 2013]. For example, it is established that if β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} is constrained to have at most k≤p𝑘𝑝k\leq p non-zero entries, X𝑋X has iid N​(0,1)𝑁01N(0,1) entries, W𝑊W has iid N​(0,σ2)𝑁0superscript𝜎2N(0,\sigma^{2}) entries and n𝑛n is of the order k​log⁡(pk)𝑘𝑝𝑘k\log\left(\frac{p}{k}\right), then both of the mentioned algorithms can recover β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*}, up to the level of the noise. Different structural assumptions than sparsity have also been considered in the literature. For example, a recent paper [Bora et al., 2017] makes the assumption that β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} lies near the range of an L𝐿L-Lipschitz generative model G:ℝk→ℝp:𝐺→superscriptℝ𝑘superscriptℝ𝑝G:\mathbb{R}^{k}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^{p} and it proposes an algorithm which succeeds with n=O​(k​log⁡L)𝑛𝑂𝑘𝐿n=O(k\log L) samples.

A downside of all of the above results is that they provide no guarantee in the case n𝑛n is much smaller than k​log⁡(pk)𝑘𝑝𝑘k\log\left(\frac{p}{k}\right). Consider for example the case where the components of a sparse β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} are binary-valued, and X,W𝑋𝑊X,W follow the Gaussian assumptions described above. Supposing that σ𝜎\sigma is sufficiently small, it is a straightforward argument that even when n=1𝑛1n=1, β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} is recoverable from Y=⟨X,β∗⟩+W𝑌𝑋superscript𝛽𝑊Y=\langle{X},{\beta^{*}}\rangle+W by a brute-force method with probability tending to one as p𝑝p goes to infinity (whp). On the other hand, for sparse and binary-valued β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*}, the Basis Pursuit method in the noiseless case [Donoho and Tanner, 2006] and the Basis Pursuit Denoising Scheme in the noisy case [Gamarnik and Zadik, 2017b] have been proven to fail to recover a binary β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} with n=o​(k​log⁡(pk))𝑛𝑜𝑘𝑝𝑘n=o(k\log\left(\frac{p}{k}\right)) samples. Furthermore, LASSO has been proven to fail to recover a vector with the same support of β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*}, with n=o​(k​log⁡p)𝑛𝑜𝑘𝑝n=o(k\log p) samples [Wainwright, 2009]. This failure to capture the complexity of the problem accurately enough for small sample sizes also lead to an algorithmic hardness conjecture for the regime n=o​(k​log⁡(pk))𝑛𝑜𝑘𝑝𝑘n=o(k\log\left(\frac{p}{k}\right)) [Gamarnik and Zadik, 2017a], [Gamarnik and Zadik, 2017b]. While this conjecture still stands in the general case, as we show in this paper, in the special case where β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} is rational-valued and the magnitude of the noise W𝑊W is sufficiently small, the statistical computational gap can be closed and β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} can be recovered even when n=1𝑛1n=1.

The structural assumption we impose on β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} is that its entries are rational numbers with denominator equal to some fixed positive integer value Q∈ℤ>0𝑄subscriptℤabsent0Q\in\mathbb{Z}_{>0}, something we refer to as the Q𝑄Q-rationality assumption. Note that for any Q𝑄Q, this assumption is trivially satisfied by the binary-valued β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} which was discussed above. The 1-rationality assumption corresponds to β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} having integer entries, which is well-motivated in practise. For example, this assumption appears frequently in the study of global navigation satellite systems (GPS) and communications [Hassibi and Boyd, 1998], [Hassibi and Vikalo, 2002], [Brunel and Boutros, 1999], [Borno, 2011].In the first reference the authors propose a mixed linear/integermodel of the form Y=A​x+B​z+W𝑌𝐴𝑥𝐵𝑧𝑊Y=Ax+Bz+W where z is an integer valued vector corresponding to integer multiples of certain wavelength.Several examples corresponding to regression models with integer valued regression coefficients and zero noise (though not always in the same model)are also discussed in the book [Foucart and Rauhut, 2013].In particular one application is the so-called Single-Pixel camera. In this model a vector β𝛽\beta corresponds to color intensities of an image for differentpixels and thus takes discrete values. The model assumes no noise, which is one of the assumptions we adopt in our model, though the corresponding regression matrix has i.i.d. +1/−1+1/-1 Bernoulli entries, as opposed to a continuous distribution we assume. Two other applications involving noiseless regression models foundin the same reference are MRI imaging and Radar detection.

A large body of literature on noiseless regression type models is a series of papers on phase retrieval. Here the coefficientsof the regression vector β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} and the entries of the regression matrix X𝑋X are complex valued, but the observation vector Y=X​β∗𝑌𝑋superscript𝛽Y=X\beta^{*} is onlyobserved through absolute values. This model has many applications, including crystallography, see [Candes et al., 2015].The aforementioned paper provides many references to phase retrieval model including the cases when the entries of β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} have a finite support. We believethat our method can also be extended so that to model the case where the entries of the regression vector have a finite support,even if irrationally valued, and the entries of Y𝑌Y are only observedthrough their magnitude. In other words, we expect that the method of the present paper applies to the phase retrieval problem at least in some of the cases and this isone of the current directions we are exploring.

Noiseless regression model with integer valued regression coefficients were also important in the theoretical development of compressivesensing methods. Specifically, Donoho [Donoho, 2006] andDonoho and Tanner [Donoho and Tanner, 2005],[Donoho and Tanner, 2006],[Donoho and Tanner, 2009]consider a noiseless regression model of the form A​B𝐴𝐵ABwhere A𝐴A is a random (say Gaussian) matrix and B𝐵B is the unit cube [0,1]psuperscript01𝑝[0,1]^{p}. One of the goals of these papers was to count number of extreme points of theprojected polytope A​B𝐴𝐵AB in order to explain the effectiveness of the linear programming based methods. The extreme points of thispolytope can only appear as projections of extreme points of B𝐵B which are all length-p binary vector, namely one deals with noiseless regressionmodel with binary coefficients – an important special case of the model we consider in our paper.

In the Bayesian setting, where the ground truth β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} is sampled according to a discrete distribution [Donoho et al., 2013] proposes a low-complexity algorithm which provably recovers β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} with n=o​(p)𝑛𝑜𝑝n=o(p) samples. This algorithm uses the technique of approximate message passing (AMP) and is motivated by ideas from statistical physics [Krzakala et al., 2012]. Even though the result from [Donoho et al., 2013] applies to the general discrete case for β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*}, it requires the matrix X𝑋X to be spatially coupled, a property that in particular does not hold for X𝑋X with iid standard Gaussian entries. Furthermore the required sample size for the algorithm to work is only guaranteed to be sublinear in p𝑝p, a sample size potentially much bigger than the information-theoretic limit for recovery under sufficiently small noise (n=1𝑛1n=1). In the present paper, where β∗superscript𝛽\beta^{*} satisfies the Q𝑄Q-rationality assumption, we propose a polynomial-time algorithm which applies for a large class of continuous distributions for the iid entries of X𝑋X, including the normal distribution, and provably works even when n=1𝑛1n=1.
