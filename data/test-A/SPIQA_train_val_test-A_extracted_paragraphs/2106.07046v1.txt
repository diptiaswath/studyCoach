In this paper we consider the fundamental problem of computing an approximately optimal policy in a Markov decision process (MDP) given by a generative model. We consider a standard MDP model with a known set of states and actions. If an agent chooses an action at a given state, a known reward is immediately given to the agent and the agent probabilistically transitions to a new state by an (unknown) fixed distribution (as a function of the state-action pair). Given access to a generative model [9], i.e. an oracle which when queried by a state-action pair returns an independent sample from the distribution over next states, our goal is to find a policy, i.e. a choice of action per state, that approximately maximizes a measure of (cumulative) reward over time, e.g. discounted reward, average reward, etc.

Solving MDPs with a generative model is a fundamental problem in learning theory and a classic model for decision making under uncertainty and reinforcement learning [15, 18]. It is a prominent theoretical test-bed for learning algorithms and has been studied extensively over past 20 years [9]. Multiple algorithms have been proposed for the problem [15] and there has been extensive research on improving the sample complexity for finding an approximately optimal policy [21, 16, 20, 1].

In certain settings, the sample complexity of the problem is settled. For example, consider discounted MDPs (DMDPs) where the goal is to minimize the γ𝛾\gamma-discounted reward, i.e. the sum of the rewards where the reward at step t≥0𝑡0t\geq 0 is discounted by γt∈[0,1)superscript𝛾𝑡01\gamma^{t}\in[0,1). Similarly, in the case of finite-horizon MDPs, again optimal sample and query complexities are known [16] for sufficiently small ϵitalic-ϵ\epsilon.

Another popular class of MDPs are infinite-horizon average-reward MDPs (AMDPs) [12]. AMDPs arise naturally in controlling computer systems and communication networks, where a controller makes frequent decisions, and for inventory systems with frequent restocking decisions [15]. In comparison to existing near-optimal DMDP methods for generative models [9], recent methods for provably solving AMDPs suffer from two additional limitations. First, they work with randomized policies, i.e. ones which choose a distribution over actions in every state. Correspondingly, these methods require stronger condition of mixing bound on all randomized policies.Second, they use dynamic samples from the model, as opposed to the case for DMDPs where the sampling process can be completely oblivious, i.e. a fixed number of samples can be generated per state-action pair.

They formally prove an upper bound of O~​(Atot​H3/ϵ2)~𝑂subscriptAtotsuperscript𝐻3superscriptitalic-ϵ2\widetilde{O}(\mathrm{A_{tot}}H^{3}/\epsilon^{2}) in sample complexity, for ϵ∈(0,1)italic-ϵ01\epsilon\in(0,1). Through reduction to DMDP lower bounds, they also obtain a lower bound of Ω~​(Atot​H3/ϵ2)~ΩsubscriptAtotsuperscript𝐻3superscriptitalic-ϵ2\widetilde{\Omega}(\mathrm{A_{tot}}H^{3}/\epsilon^{2}).

Average-reward MDPs with bounded mixing time are another fundamental class of MDPs [10, 13], though less studied in terms of sample complexity. The first sample complexity bounds in the setting of a generative model is Wang [22], which applies a primal-dual method for the minimax problem related to the linear programming formulation, and proves an upper bound of O​(τ2​Atot​tmix2/ϵ2)𝑂superscript𝜏2subscriptAtotsuperscriptsubscript𝑡mix2superscriptitalic-ϵ2O(\tau^{2}\mathrm{A_{tot}}t_{\mathrm{mix}}^{2}/\epsilon^{2}) where τ𝜏\tau denotes an upper bound on the ergodicity of all stationary distribution under arbitrary policies, i.e. there exists some distribution 𝐪∈Δ𝒮𝐪superscriptΔ𝒮\mathbf{q}\in\Delta^{\mathcal{S}} satisfying 1/τ⋅𝐪≤𝝂π≤τ⋅𝐪⋅1𝜏𝐪superscript𝝂𝜋⋅𝜏𝐪\sqrt{1/\tau}\cdot\mathbf{q}\leq{\boldsymbol{\nu}}^{\pi}\leq\sqrt{\tau}\cdot\mathbf{q} for all policies π𝜋\pi and its induced stationary distribution 𝝂πsuperscript𝝂𝜋{\boldsymbol{\nu}}^{\pi}. Recently,  Jin and Sidford [8] design a similar primal-dual stochastic mirror descent and improve the bound to O​(Atot​tmix2/ϵ2)𝑂subscriptAtotsuperscriptsubscript𝑡mix2superscriptitalic-ϵ2O(\mathrm{A_{tot}}t_{\mathrm{mix}}^{2}/\epsilon^{2}), removing the ergodicity assumption through an improved analysis of the optimality conditions of the minimax problem. In contrast to value iteration, Q-learning, and sample-based methods for DMDPs, both known efficient methods for AMDPs use a linear programming formulation, dynamic sampling, a stronger mixing condition, and only compute randomized stationary policies. On the hardness side, there is no known lower bound for AMDPs with bounded mixing time.

Finally, we note that our lower bound statement is applicable to any algorithms yielding deterministic policy and using deterministic oblivious sampling, which already matches our upper bound results in Section 2 nearly tightly. We present our lower bound under this setting in our main paper for clarity and simplicity. However, we believe one can extend the result to algorithms with randomized policies and using dynamic samples; we think these are interesting future directions to further strengthen the lower bound and discuss them in more detail in Appendix B.

In this paper, we have shown an Ω​(Atot​tmix​ϵ−2)ΩsubscriptAtotsubscript𝑡mixsuperscriptitalic-ϵ2\Omega(\mathrm{A_{tot}}t_{\mathrm{mix}}\epsilon^{-2}) sample complexity lower bound for AMDPs with mixing time bound tmixsubscript𝑡mixt_{\mathrm{mix}}, and a matching upper bound (up to logarithmic and poly​(1/ϵ)poly1italic-ϵ\mathrm{poly}(1/\epsilon) factors) obtained through reduction to DMDPs . Our work suggest a few open directions which we believe would help clarify the structure of AMDPs and its connection with DMDPs:

While the authors suspect that an O~​(Atot​tmix​ϵ−2)~𝑂subscriptAtotsubscript𝑡mixsuperscriptitalic-ϵ2\widetilde{O}(\mathrm{A_{tot}}t_{\mathrm{mix}}\epsilon^{-2}) upper bound on the required sample complexity may be attainable, it seems to require new ideas in leveraging the mixing structure of AMDP more directly, instead of reducing it to DMDPs. Further, it would be interesting to obtain algorithms with efficient running times as has been shown for DMDPs [16] in certain ϵitalic-ϵ\epsilon-regimes.

In certain cases, assuming global mixing time bound for all policies, even for all deterministic stationary policies (as we do in the paper), can be restrictive. We ask if it is possible to obtain sample complexity dependence in terms of the mixing time of the optimal policy, or in terms of some alternative parameters like diameter [7], or bias span [3, 6] that can be smaller than tmixsubscript𝑡mixt_{\mathrm{mix}} for certain types of AMDPs.
