Every day millions of deliveries of goods are made and very often it is desirable to minimize their delivery time. In the majority of the cases, these deliveries depend on distribution schedules of ships, trains, and trucks that form a dynamic network that changes over time. Usually, these distribution schedules are constrained due to physical limitations, laws, available infrastructure, and costs of extra resources. These constraints are usually “hard” since it is difficult, if not impossible, to bypass them. On the other hand, careful changes on the existing scheduling timetable can provide significant gains in the time a good needs to reach its destination.

As an example, consider the scenario where there are three cities A𝐴A, B𝐵B, and C𝐶C with bidirectional connections. Between cities A𝐴A and B𝐵B there exists a connection at 9am and different connection at 11am. Between cities B𝐵B and C𝐶C there exists a connection at 9am and another one at 8pm. Assume that every connection takes one hour. Observe then, that under the current timetable, a journey starting from A𝐴A can reach C𝐶C at 8pm, while a journey starting from C𝐶C can reach A𝐴A at 11am. However, if we delay the 9am connection between B𝐵B and C𝐶C until 10am, then A𝐴A can reach C𝐶C at 10am, and C𝐶C can still reach A𝐴A at 11am.Figure 1 depicts this example.

The inherent temporal nature of timetabling combined with the existence of the underlying distribution network allows us to concisely formulate the scheduling scenario described above as an optimization problem over a temporal graph Kempe et al. (2002). At a high level, a temporal graph consists of a fixed set of vertices and a timetable that shows the available connections, or temporal edges, at any point in time111A common assumption is that the time is discretized.. A temporal path in such a graph is a sequence of temporal edges which, additionally to the usual connectivity, respects the time constraints, i.e., temporal edges that are used later in the sequence, are available later in time than the ones that come before Whitbeck et al. (2012). As we have seen in the example above, there exist cases where delaying some temporal edges makes some temporal paths “faster”. In other words, the delaying of some edges can make the existing infrastructure more efficient with respect to reachability times.

The problem studied in this paper, which we term ReachFast, can be summarized as follows.Given a temporal graph and a set of sources, delay some temporal edges such that the maximum time any source needs to reach all the vertices is minimized. 

First, we study the most fundamental version of ReachFast where there is a single source in the graph. Here we distinguish between two cases: either some constraint on the delays is imposed, or no such constraints exist. We study two natural constrained versions that can appear in real life:•ReachFast​(k)ReachFast𝑘\textsc{ReachFast}(k), where k𝑘k bounds the number of edges we can delay;•ReachFastTotal​(k)ReachFastTotal𝑘\textsc{ReachFastTotal}(k), where k𝑘k bounds the overall delay over all edges.In Theorem 2 we prove that both constrained versions are 𝚆​[2]𝚆delimited-[]2\mathtt{W}[2]-hard, and thus 𝙽𝙿𝙽𝙿\mathtt{NP}-hard, when parameterized by k𝑘k. We complement these negative results with Algorithm 1 that solves (unconstrained) ReachFast in polynomial time.

Then, we depart from the single source case and study the problem when there are more sources. In Theorem 5, we prove that ReachFast becomes 𝙽𝙿𝙽𝙿\mathtt{NP}-hard even if there are just two sources and the temporal graph is rather restricted: the graph has lifetime 1 and has (static) diameter 6. This indicates that tractability requires some constraints in the input temporal graph. We derive, efficient algorithms for ReachFast for several graph classes of the underlying network. Firstly, we consider several parallel paths with common endpoints. For this class, Algorithm 5.2 solves ReachFast two sources in polynomial time, when the sources are the endpoints of the paths. For trees, Algorithm 5.1 solves ReachFast in polynomial time for any number of sources. Our last result considers graphs of bounded treewidth; this class of graphs has received a lot of attention in the past. We show that in this case ReachFast can be encoded in Monadic Second Order logic, where the size of the formula depends only on the deadline by which we wish to reach all the vertices from every source.Then, using Courcelle’s Theorem, we get a fixed parameter tractable algorithm for the problem, parameterized by the treewidth of the graph and the lifetime of the optimal solution that works for any number of sources.

The modification of temporal graphs such that an objective is optimized, has received significant attention recently, mainly motivated by virus-spread minimization Braunstein andIngrosso (2016); Enright and Kao (2018). A line of work studies minimization of reachability sets for a set of sources over a temporal graph using a variety of operations: delaying operations were first studied in Deligkas and Potapov (2020), alongside merging operations; edge-deletion and temporal edge-deletions operations were studied in Enright et al. (2021a); Enright and Meeks (2018); reordering of the temporal edges were studied in Enright et al. (2021b). Finally, Molter et al. (2021) studies the relationship between the delaying and the edge-deletion operations under the reachability-minimization objective.All of the above mentioned reachability problems heavily depend on the computation of temporal paths, a problem that has received a lot of attention Wu et al. (2014); Wang et al. (2015); Wu et al. (2016, 2012) and different definitions of temporal paths have been proposed Casteigts et al. (2021); Thejaswi andGionis (2020).Finally, in Li et al. (2018) a similar idea to ours was explored under a slightly different objective. There, every temporal edge had a traversal-time that depends on the time it is used and the goal was to find temporal paths that minimize the overall traversal-time. The authors observed that, for their objective, it might be beneficial to wait until they use an edge, since this might decrease the total traversal-time.

We view our paper as part of a greater agenda whose goal is to make existing infrastructures more efficient, by making minimal changes to the currently-adopted solutions.We introduced and we studied the complexity of ReachFast problem, where the goal was to minimize the maximum reaching-time of a set of sources towards every vertex of the graph using delaying operations.

Since we study optimization problems that are 𝙽𝙿𝙽𝙿\mathtt{NP}-hard, instead of focusing on restricted classes of instances, one can ask to relax the optimality condition and ask for some approximate solutions. Here we would like to point out that Theorem 5 implies the problem is 𝙰𝙿𝚇𝙰𝙿𝚇\mathtt{APX}-hard even for two sources, however existence of a constant approximation for some constant c>7/6𝑐76c>7/6 is an interesting open question. In both, ReachFast (k) and ReachFastTotal (k) there are two criteria one might wish to optimize: the maximum reaching-time, i.e., a deadline D𝐷D, and k𝑘k. Our reduction from Hitting Set is approximation-preserving for k𝑘k and hence a (1−o​(1))​log⁡(|V​(G)|)1𝑜1𝑉𝐺(1-o(1))\log(|V(G)|) approximation algorithm for value k𝑘k is unlikely, unless 𝙿=𝙽𝙿𝙿𝙽𝙿\mathtt{P}=\mathtt{NP}. Similarly, for fixed k𝑘k, the reduced instance we obtain either admits a solution with D=3𝐷3D=3 or does not admit a solution, and polynomial time algorithm that approximates D𝐷D for fixed k𝑘k is again highly unlikely.

Our work also creates several interesting directions for future research, all of which can be studied under the delaying operations. Instead of minimizing the maximum reaching-time of any source, we could try to minimize the average time a source needs to reach all the vertices of the graph. Another objective is to minimize the maximum, or average, time a vertex is reached by any of the sources; this would be desirable, for example, in the case where a company has several warehouses over a country. The dual version of the problem is intriguing as well. Here, instead of sources, we have a set of sinks, i.e., every vertex wants to reach them as fast as possible. This model can capture for example the scenario where the sinks correspond to hospitals. While some of our results, like the fast converge algorithm, work for special cases of the setting, we expect that novel algorithmic techniques are required in order to tackle them.
