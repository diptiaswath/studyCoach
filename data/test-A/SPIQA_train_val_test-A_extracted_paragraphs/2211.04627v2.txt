The degeneracy of an undirected graph G𝐺G, denoted δ𝛿\delta, is the smallest δ𝛿\delta such that every induced subgraph of G𝐺G has a node with degree at most δ𝛿\delta. The problem of finding the degeneracy of a graph is a subproblem of the k𝑘k-core decomposition problem, where a k𝑘k-core is a maximal connected subgraph in which all nodes have degree at least k𝑘k, and k𝑘k-core decomposition problem is to find all k𝑘k-cores of a given graph G𝐺G. The degeneracy of G𝐺G is the maximum k𝑘k for which G𝐺G contains a (non-empty) k𝑘k-core.

The k𝑘k-core decomposition problem, and consequently the degeneracy problem, can be solved using a well known linear time “peeling” algorithm (peeling, ). Starting with k=1𝑘1k=1, the algorithm repeatedly removes all nodes in G𝐺G with degree less than k𝑘k and their incident edges, until the only remaining nodes have degree at least k𝑘k and their connected components are k𝑘k-cores. Then k𝑘k is incremented, and the algorithm is repeated on the remaining subgraph. There has been much work done to improve the practical running time of k𝑘k-core decomposition fully in-memory (kcore1, ), with fully external-memory (kcore2, ), and with semi external-memory (kcore3, ). There are also algorithms for approximate computing of k𝑘k-core in dynamic, streaming, and sketching settings (dynamickcore1, ; streamingkcore1, ; kcoresketching, ).

While k𝑘k-core algorithms can be used to compute degeneracy, they may incur unnecessary costs and space for processing cores for small k𝑘k. There are algorithms which directly compute the degeneracy of the graph. The SemiDeg+ algorithm from (semideg, ) is the state-of-the-art in exact degeneracy computation. In a semi-streaming model, (colton1, ), (semistream1, ), and (semistream2, ) propose (2+ϵ)2italic-ϵ(2+\epsilon)-approximate solutions and (colton2, ) proposes a (1+ϵ)1italic-ϵ(1+\epsilon)-approximate solution. These require each edge to be scanned.

The degeneracy of a graph is used for many applications in graph analysis and graph theory including measuring the sparcity of a graph, approximating dominating sets (domset1, ; domset2, ), approximating the arboricity of a graph within a constant factor (arboricity1, ), and maximal clique enumeration (maxclique1, ). The degeneracy is at least half the maximum density of any subgraph (colton1, ). A sublinear approximate algorithm for degeneracy is the algorithm presented in (sublineardense, ) for computing maximum density of any subgraph. It runs in O~​(n)~𝑂𝑛\tilde{O}(n) time and implies a (4+ϵ)4italic-ϵ(4+\epsilon)-approximate algorithm for degeneracy.

In this work, we present a (1+ϵ)1italic-ϵ(1+\epsilon)-approximate O​(n​log⁡n)𝑂𝑛𝑛O(n\log n) time solution to the degeneracy problem where n𝑛n is the number of nodes in the graph. Hence, our algorithm is sublinear (in the number of edges for dense graphs). Our algorithm can be extended to a sublinear time algorithm for k𝑘k-core decomposition, but the time savings over other algorithms are particularly exhibited empirically for the degeneracy problem. We avoid the overhead of full k𝑘k-core decomposition and directly compute degeneracy of the given graph G𝐺G by sampling the neighbors of nodes starting with those with the highest degree above a certain threshold l𝑙l. We then determine if the subgraph induced by those nodes contains an l𝑙l-core by repeatedly eliminating nodes which did not sample a sufficient number of other high degree nodes, similar to the peeling algorithm. If it does, then we output l𝑙l as the (approximate) degeneracy of G𝐺G. We prove the theoretical guarantees of our algorithm and we also propose optimizations which improve its practical running time. We also compare our solution to the (1+ϵ)1italic-ϵ(1+\epsilon)-approximate algorithm of (colton2, ) and the SemiDeg+ algorithm from (semideg, ) on massive real-world webgraphs and show that our algorithm is substantially faster than both on almost all datasets. In 1983, Matula and Beck (peeling, ) presented the linear time peeling algorithm for k𝑘k-core decomposition. Although it is superseded in practice by heuristic methods in other work and presents unnecessary overhead for degeneracy computation (as do other algorithms which enumerate all k𝑘k-cores), it is an efficient method for core computation especially for small graphs and graphs with small degeneracy.

(kcoresketching, ) and (colton2, ) present (1+ϵ)1italic-ϵ(1+\epsilon)-approximate solutions for k𝑘k-core decomposition and degeneracy computation respectively. The goal of both algorithms is to minimize memory usage in a streaming / semi-streaming model, which make them inefficient in terms of running time in the incident-list model due to the requirement of making a random choice for every edge in the graph, especially with large graphs. However, their techniques are related to our work.

The work in (sublineardense, ) proposes a (2+ϵ)2italic-ϵ(2+\epsilon)-approximate solution to the densest subgraph problem in sublinear time, which implies a (4+ϵ)4italic-ϵ(4+\epsilon)-approximate sublinear algorithm for degeneracy, and (sublineardense, ) uses the same model as ours, the incident-list model, for their graph input representation. The SemiDeg+ algorithm from 2022 (semideg, ) is the state-of-the-art in practical exact degeneracy computation, so we use it as a comparison baseline for the experiments on the running times of our approximate algorithm.

We refer to our approach as NESD (Neighbor Sampling for Degeneracy). We have devised O​(n​log⁡n)𝑂𝑛𝑛O(n\log n) algorithms for degeneracy and for k𝑘k-core in the incident-list model. Both algorithms give provably close approximate solutions, with high probability. Our algorithm features various optimizations which may be of independent interest.
