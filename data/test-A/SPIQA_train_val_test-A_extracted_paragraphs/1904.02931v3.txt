Deep neural networks (DNNs)have been successfully applied to domains such as text, speech,and image processing. Recurrent neural networks(RNNs)В (?;В ?)is a class of DNNs equipped with the capability of processing sequential data ofvariable length. The great success of RNNs has been seen in,e.g., machine translationВ (?),speech recognitionВ (?), and anomalydetectionВ (?;В ?).

While it has been experimentally shown that RNNs are a powerful tool to process,predict, and model sequential data, there are known drawbacks in RNNs such as interpretability and costly inference.A research line that attacks this challenge is automata extractionВ (?;В ?). Focusing on RNNsвҖҷ use as acceptors (i.e., receiving an input sequence and producing a single Boolean output), these works extract a finite-state automaton from an RNN as a succinct and interpretable surrogate.Automata extraction exposes internal transition between the states of an RNN in the form of anautomaton, which is then amenable to algorithmic analyses such as reachability and model checkingВ (?).Automata extraction can also be seen as model compression: finite-state automata are usually more compact, and cheaper to run, than neural networks.

Most of the existing automata extraction techniques target Boolean-output RNNs, which however excludes many applications.In sentiment analysis, it is desired to knowthe quantitative strength of sentiment, besides its (Boolean) existenceВ (?).RNNs with real values as their output are also useful in classification tasks. Forexample, predicting class probabilities is a key in some approaches tosemi-supervised learningВ (?) and ensembleВ (?).

This motivates extraction of quantitative finite-state machines as abstraction of RNNs. We find the formalism of weighted finite automata (WFAs) suited for this purpose. A WFA is a finite-state machineвҖ”much like a deterministic finite automaton (DFA)вҖ”but its transitions as well as acceptance values are real numbers (instead of Booleans).

Our main contribution is a procedure that takes a (real-output) RNN Rрқ‘…R, andreturns a WFA ARsubscriptрқҗҙрқ‘…A_{R} that abstracts Rрқ‘…R. The procedure is based on the WFAlearning algorithm inВ (?), that is in turn based onthe famous LвҲ—superscriptL\mathrm{L}^{*}В algorithm for learningDFAsВ (?).These algorithms learn automata by a series of so-called membership queries and equivalence queries.In our procedure, a membership query is implemented by an inference of the given RNN Rрқ‘…R.We iterate membership queries and use their results to construct a WFA AрқҗҙA and togrow it.

The role of equivalence queries is to say when to stop this iteration:it asks if AрқҗҙA and Rрқ‘…R are вҖңequivalent,вҖқ that is, if the WFA AрқҗҙA obtained sofar is comprehensive enough to cover all the possible behaviors of Rрқ‘…R.This is not possible in generalвҖ”RNNs are more expressive than WFAsвҖ”therefore we inevitably resort to an approximate method.Ourtechnical novelty lies in the method for answering equivalence queries;notably it uses a regression methodвҖ”e.g., theGaussian process regression (GPR) and the kernel ridge regression (KRR)вҖ”forabstraction of the state space of Rрқ‘…R.

We conducted experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of our approach.In particular, we are concerned with the following questions:1) how similar the behavior of the extracted WFA ARsubscriptрқҗҙрқ‘…A_{R}, and that of the original RNN Rрқ‘…R, are;2) how applicable our method is to an RNN that is a more expressive model than WFAs; and3) how efficient the inference of the WFA ARsubscriptрқҗҙрқ‘…A_{R} is, when compared with the inference of Rрқ‘…R.The experiments we designed for the questionsВ 1) andВ 3) are withRNNs trained using randomly generated WFAs. The results show, onthe questionВ 1), that the WFAs extracted by our method approximate theoriginal RNNs accurately. This is especially so when compared with a baseline algorithm (a straightforward adaptation of ?В (?)). On the questionВ 3), the inference ofthe extracted WFAs are about 1300 times faster than that of the original RNNs.On the questionВ 2), we devised an RNN that models a weighted variant of a (non-regular) language of balanced parentheses.Although this weighted language isbeyond WFAsвҖҷ expressivity, we found that our method extractsWFAs that successfully approximate the RNN up-to a certain depth bound.

A major potential application of WFA extraction is to analyze an RNN Rрқ‘…R via its interpretable surrogate ARsubscriptрқҗҙрқ‘…A_{R}. The theory of WFAs offers a number of analysis methods, such as bisimulation metricВ (?)вҖ”a distance notion between WFAsвҖ”that will allow us to tell how apart two RNNs are.

Another major potential applicationis as вҖңa poormanвҖҷs RNN Rрқ‘…R.вҖқ While RNN inference is recognized to be rather expensive (especially for edge devices), simpler models by WFAs should be cheaper to run.Indeed, ourexperimental results show (В§4) that WFA inference isabout 1300times faster than inference of original RNNs.

Since WFAs are defined over a finite alphabet, we restrict to RNNs Rрқ‘…R that take sequences over a finite alphabet. This restriction should not severely limit the applicability of our method. Indeed, such RNNs (over a finite alphabet) have successfully applications in many domains, includingintrusion predictionВ (?),malware detectionВ (?), andDNA-protein binding predictionВ (?). The relationship between RNNs and automatahas been studied in bothnon-quantitativeВ (?;В ?;В ?;В ?) andquantitativeВ (?;В ?) settings.Some of these works feature automata extraction from RNNs; we shall now discuss recent ones among them.

The work by ?В (?) is a pioneer in automata extraction from RNNs. They extract DFAs from RNNs, using a variation of LвҲ—superscriptL\mathrm{L}^{*}algorithm, much like this work.We provide a systematic comparison inВ В§3.3, identifying some notable similarities and differences.

?В (?) extract a WFA from a black-box sequence acceptor whose example is an RNN.Their method does not use equivalence queries; in contrast,we exploit the internal state space of an RNN to approximately answer equivalence queries.

DeepStellarВ (?) extracts Markov chains from RNNs, and uses them for coverage-based testing and adversarial sample detection. Their extraction, differently from our LвҲ—superscriptL\mathrm{L}^{*}-like method, uses profiling from the training data and discrete abstraction of the state space.

?В (?) propose a new RNN architecture that makes it easier to extract a DFA from a trained model. To apply their method, one has to modify the structure of an RNN before training, while our method does not need any special structure to RNNs and can be applied to already trained RNNs.

?В (?) introduce a neural network architecture that can represent (restricted forms of) CNNs and RNNs. WFAs could also be expressed by their architecture, but extraction of automata is out of their interest.

A major approach to optimizing neural networks is by compression:model pruningВ (?), quantizationВ (?), anddistillationВ (?).Combination and comparison with these techniques is interesting future work.

As a result, the performance ismore influenced by the amount of counterexamplesвҖ”i.e., how long timeextraction takesвҖ”than on their вҖңqualities.вҖқ

The exception of this trend is рқҗ‘рқҗҶрқҗ‘вҖӢ(2)рқҗ‘рқҗҶрқҗ‘2\mathbf{RGR}(2), which took a longer time but performedworse than рқҗҒрқҗ…рқҗ’вҖӢ(3000)рқҗҒрқҗ…рқҗ’3000\mathbf{BFS}(3000), рқҗҒрқҗ…рқҗ’вҖӢ(5000)рқҗҒрқҗ…рқҗ’5000\mathbf{BFS}(5000), and рқҗ‘рқҗҶрқҗ‘вҖӢ(5)рқҗ‘рқҗҶрқҗ‘5\mathbf{RGR}(5). In particular, рқҗ‘рқҗҶрқҗ‘вҖӢ(2)рқҗ‘рқҗҶрқҗ‘2\mathbf{RGR}(2)performed well for smaller alphabets (|ОЈ|вҲҲ{4,6,10}ОЈ4610|\Sigma|\in\{4,6,10\}) but not sowhen |ОЈ|=15ОЈ15|\Sigma|=15. The role of the parameter Mрқ‘ҖM in рқҗ‘рқҗҶрқҗ‘вҖӢ(M)рқҗ‘рқҗҶрқҗ‘рқ‘Җ\mathbf{RGR}(M) (i.e., inAlgorithmВ 1) is a threshold to control how many wordsconfiguration regions of a WFA are investigated with. Thus, we conjecture thatthe use of too small Mрқ‘ҖM limits the input space to be investigated excessively,which is more critical as the input space is larger, eventually biasing thecounterexamples hв„Һh (in AlgorithmВ 1), though the RNNs aretrained on the uniform distribution, and making refinement of WFAs lesseffective.

In the bottom table in Table 1 (the вҖңrealisticвҖқ setting),рқҗ‘рқҗҶрқҗ‘вҖӢ(2)рқҗ‘рқҗҶрқҗ‘2\mathbf{RGR}(2) performs significantly better than the other (and the best among all the procedures) in terms of accuracy. This is the case even for a large alphabet(|ОЈ|=15ОЈ15|\Sigma|=15). This indicates that, in the cases that an RNN is trained with anonuniform dataset,making the investigated input space larger by big Mрқ‘ҖM could even degrade the accuracyperformance. A possible reason for this degradation is as follows.Some words (such as aвҖӢbвҖӢaрқ‘Һрқ‘Ҹрқ‘Һaba) are prohibited in the sample set Tрқ‘ҮT, andthe behaviors of the RNN Rрқ‘…R for those prohibited words areunexpected. Therefore, those prohibited words should not beuseful to refine a WFA. The use of small Mрқ‘ҖM could prevent such meaningless (or even harmful) counterexamples hв„Һh from being investigated.This discussion raises another question: how can we find an optimal Mрқ‘ҖM? We leave it as future work.

We conducted experiments about inference time, comparing the original RNNs Rрқ‘…R and the WFAs AрқҗҙA that we extracted from Rрқ‘…R.We used the same RNNs Rрқ‘…R and WFAs AрқҗҙA as inВ В§4.1, where the latter are extracted using рқҗ‘рқҗҶрқҗ‘вҖӢ(2вҖӢвҖ“вҖӢ5)рқҗ‘рқҗҶрқҗ‘2вҖ“5\mathbf{RGR}(2\text{--}5) and рқҗҒрқҗ…рқҗ’вҖӢ(500вҖӢвҖ“вҖӢ5000)рқҗҒрқҗ…рқҗ’500вҖ“5000\mathbf{BFS}(500\text{--}5000).We note that the inference of RNNs utilizes GPUs while that of WFAs is solely done by CPUs.

We observed thatthe inference time of the extracted WFAs AрқҗҙA was about 1,300 times faster than the target RNNs Rрқ‘…R, taking the average over different settings (AppendixВ B.3). This demonstrates the potential use of the extracted WFAs as a computationally cheaper surrogate for RNNs. We attribute the acceleration to the following: 1) WFAs use only linear computation while RNNs involve nonlinear ones; and 2) overall, extracted WFAs are smaller in size.Provided that the accuracy of extracted WFAs can be high (as we observed in В§4.1), we believethe replacement of RNNs by WFAs is a viable option in some application scenarios.

We proposed a method that extracts a WFA from an RNN, focusing on RNNs that take a wordwвҲҲОЈвҲ—рқ‘ӨsuperscriptОЈw\in\Sigma^{*} and return a real value.We used regression to investigate and abstract the internal states of RNNs.We experimentally evaluated our method, comparing its performance with a baseline whose equivalence queries are based on simple breadth-first search.

One future work is a detailed comparison with other methods for model compression.Another future work is to use machine learning methods to find a counterexample in the equivalence query, such as reinforcement learningВ (?) adversarial attacksВ (?), and acquisition functions of GPR.Finally, we need a means to optimize parameter Mрқ‘ҖM of our method for aspecific problem. It may also be helpful to extend our method so that theinvestigated words can be restricted to a fixed language LвҠӮОЈвҲ—рқҗҝsuperscriptОЈL\subset\Sigma^{*}; IfLрқҗҝL identifies the input space of the training dataset for RNNs, we could avoidinvestigating the input space on which the RNNs are not trained, and thereforewe could seek only вҖңmeaningfulвҖқ counterexamples even in using large Mрқ‘ҖM.
