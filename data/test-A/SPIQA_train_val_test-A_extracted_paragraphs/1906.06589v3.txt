The remarkable performance of machine learning (ML) in solving many classification tasks has facilitated its adoption in various domains ranging from recommendation systems to critical health-care management.Many ML-as-a-Service platforms (e.g., Google API, Amazon AWS) enable novice data owners to train ML models and release the models either as a blackbox prediction API or as model parameters that can be accessed in whitebox fashion.

ML models are often trained on data with sensitive user information such as clinical records and personal photos. Hence, ML models trained using sensitive data can leak private information about their data owners.This has been demonstrated through various inference attacksВ (Fredrikson, Jha, and Ristenpart 2015; Hitaj, Ateniese, and PГ©rez-Cruz 2017; Carlini etВ al. 2018), and most notably the membership inference attack (MIA)В (Shokri etВ al. 2017) which is the focus of our work.An MIA adversary with a blackbox or whitebox access to a target model aims to determine if a given target sample belonged to the private training data of the target model or not.MIAs are able to distinguish the members from non-members by learning the behavior of the target model on member versus non-member inputs.They use different features of the target model for this classification, e.g., model predictions (Shokri etВ al. 2017), model loss, and gradients of the model parameters for given inputВ (Nasr, Shokri, and Houmansadr 2019).MIAs are particularly more effective against deep neural networksВ (Shokri etВ al. 2017; Salem etВ al. 2019), because, with their large capacities, such models can better memorize their training data.

Recent work has investigated several defenses against membership inference attacks.In order to provide the worst case privacy guarantees, Differential Privacy (DP) based defenses add very large amounts of noise to the learning objective or model outputsВ (Papernot etВ al. 2017; Chaudhuri, Monteleoni, andSarwate 2011). This results in models with unacceptable tradeoffs between privacy and utilityВ (Jayaraman and Evans 2019), therefore questioning their use in practice.Sablayrolles et al.В (Sablayrolles etВ al. 2019) showed that membership privacy is a weaker notion of privacy than DP, which improves with generalization of ML models.Similarly, Nasr et al.В (Nasr, Shokri, and Houmansadr 2018) proposed adversarial regularization targeted to defeat MIAs by improving the target modelвҖҷs generalization.However, as we demonstrate, the adversarial regularization and other state-of-the-art regularizations, including label smoothingВ (Szegedy etВ al. 2016) and dropoutВ (Srivastava etВ al. 2014), fail to provide acceptable membership privacy-utility tradeoffs (simply called вҖҳtradeoffsвҖҷ here onward).MemguardВ (Jia etВ al. 2019), a blackbox defense, improves model utility, but it cannot protect the model from whitebox MIAs and even the simple threshold based MIAsВ (Yeom etВ al. 2018).In summary, existing defenses against MIAs offer poor tradeoffs between model utility and membership privacy.

To this end, our work proposes a defense against MIAs that significantly improves the tradeoffs compared to prior defenses.That is, for a given degree of membership privacy (i.e., MIA resistance), our defense produces models with significantly higher classification performances compared to prior defenses.Our defense, called Distillation for Membership Privacy (DMP), leverages knowledge distillationВ (Hinton, Vinyals, and Dean 2014), which transfers the knowledge of large models to smaller models, and is primarily used for model compression.Intuitively, DMP protects membership privacy by thwarting the access of the resulting models to the private training data.The first pre-distillation phase of DMP trains an unprotected model on the private training data without any privacy protection.Next, in distillation phase, DMP selects/generates reference data and transfers the knowledge of the unprotected model into predictions of the reference data.In the final post-distillation phase, DMP trains a protected model on the reference data labeled in the previous phase.Unlike conventional distillation, we use the same architectures for the unprotected and protected models.

Similar to adversarial regularization and PATE, DMP assumes access to a possibly sensitive and вҖңunlabeledвҖқ reference data drawn from the same distribution as the вҖңlabeledвҖқ private training data, and uses such reference data to train its final models; the reference data is not publicly available.This is a highly realistic assumption as typical model generating entities (e.g., banks) possess huge amounts of вҖңunlabeledвҖқ data (but limited labeled data due to the expensive labeling process).Furthermore, we show that this assumption can be relaxed by synthesizing reference data using generator networksВ (Micaelli and Storkey 2019).While some prior workВ (Papernot etВ al. 2017) combined distillation and DP to protect data privacy, our work is the first to study the promise of knowledge distillation as the sole technique to train membership privacy-preserving models.Our key contributions are summarized below:

-We propose a defense against MIAs, called Distillation for Membership Privacy (DMP).-Given an unprotected model trained on a private training data and a reference sample, we provide a novel result that the lower the entropy of prediction of the model on the reference sample, the lower the sensitive membership information in the prediction. We use this result to select/generate appropriate reference data so as to improve the membership privacy due to DMP.-We perform an extensive evaluation of DMP to show the state-of-the-art tradeoffs between membership privacy and model accuracy of DMP. For instance, at a fixed high degrees of membership privacy, DMP achieves 30% to 140% higher classification accuracies compared to state-of-the-art defenses across various classification tasks.

Membership inference attacks.(Shokri etВ al. 2017) introduced membership inference attacks (MIAs).Given a target model trained on a private training data and a target sample, MIA adversary aims to infer whether the target sample is a member of the private training data.(Shokri etВ al. 2017) proposed to train a neural network to distinguish the features of the target model on members and non-members. They assumed a partial access to the private trainin data.(Salem etВ al. 2019) relaxed this assumptions and showed the transferability of MIAs across datasets.These works relied on the blackbox features of target models, e.g., model predictions to mount MIAs.(Nasr, Shokri, and Houmansadr 2019) proposed to use whitebox features of target models, e.g., model gradients, along with the blackbox features, to further enhance the MIA accuracy.Above works used generalization gap (i.e., difference in train and test accuracy) of target models to mount strong MIAs.The more recent MIA literature focuses on deriving features that can better distinguish the behavior of target models on members and non-membersВ (Leino and Fredrikson 2019; Song and Mittal 2020).

Defenses against membership inference attacks.  MIAs exploit differences in behaviors of target models on members and non-members.Regularization techniques, including dropout and label smoothing, reduce the difference in terms of accuracies of the target model on members and non-members, and mitigate MIAs to some extentВ (Shokri etВ al. 2017).(Nasr, Shokri, and Houmansadr 2018) proposed adversarial regularization (AdvReg) tailored to defeat MIAs. AdvReg simultaneously trains the target and attack models in a game theoretic manner, and regularizes the target model using the accuracy of the attack model.The final target models that use above regularization defenses can be deployed in whitebox manner, i.e., similar to DMP, they are whitebox defenses. Hence, we thoroughly compare our DMP defense with all these regularization techniques.However, as shown inВ (Song and Mittal 2020) and seen from the original workВ (Nasr, Shokri, and Houmansadr 2018), AdvReg is not an effective defense, because it either fails to mitigate MIA or incurs large drops in model utility (classification accuracy).Jia et al.В (2019) proposed MemGuard, a blackbox defense that adds noise to the output of the target model such that the noisy output is both accurate and fools the given MIA attack model.However, MemGuard does not defend against the simplest of threshold based attacksВ (Yeom etВ al. 2018; Sablayrolles etВ al. 2019). We omit MemGuard and other blackbox defenses, e.g., top-k predictionsВ (Shokri etВ al. 2017), from evaluations.

Differential privacy based defenses such as DP-SGDВ (Abadi etВ al. 2016) and PATEВ (Papernot etВ al. 2017) are whitebox defenses and provide strong theoretical membership privacy guarantees.However, asВ (Jayaraman and Evans 2019) showвҖ”and we confirm in our workвҖ”target models trained using DP-SGD and PATE have prohibitively low classification accuracies rendering them unusable.

Knowledge distillation.(BuciluЗҺ, Caruana, and Niculescu-Mizil 2006) and (Ba and Caruana 2014) proposed knowledge distillation, which uses the outputs of a large teacher model to train a smaller student model, in order to compress large models to smaller models.The outputs used for distillation can vary, e.g.,(Hinton, Vinyals, and Dean 2014) use class probabilities generated by the teacher as the outputs, while (Romero etВ al. 2014) use the intermediate activations along with class probabilities of the teacher.It is well established that knowledge distillation produces students with accuracies similar to their teachersВ (Crowley, Gray, and Storkey 2018; Zagoruyko and Komodakis 2016). This also allows DMP to produce highly accurate target models.Note that, although we use term вҖңdistillationвҖқ, DMP uses teacher and student models of the same sizes, because DMP is not concerned with the size of the resulting model.

Membership inference attacks.  Below we give the threat model and MIA methodology that we consider in this work.

Threat model.  The primary goal of the adversary is to infer the membership of a target sample (x,y)xрқ‘Ұ(\textbf{x},y) in the private training data Dрқ—Қрқ—Ӣsubscriptрқҗ·рқ—Қрқ—ӢD_{\mathsf{tr}} of a target model Оёрқңғ\theta.Our DMP defense uses private, unlabeled reference data Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} for knowledge transfer, which itself could be privacy sensitive, hence, we consider a secondary goal to infer membership of a target sample in Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}}.Following the previous works, we assume a strong adversary with the knowledge of: target model parameters (the strongest whitebox case), half of the members of Dрқ—Қрқ—Ӣsubscriptрқҗ·рқ—Қрқ—ӢD_{\mathsf{tr}} and equal number of non-members. Similarly, to assess the MIA risk to Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}}, we assume that the adversary has half of the members of Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} and the equal number of non-members. Note that, the assumptions on the partial availability of private Dрқ—Қрқ—Ӣsubscriptрқҗ·рқ—Қрқ—ӢD_{\mathsf{tr}} and private Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} facilitates the assessment of defenses under a very strong adversary.The adversary can compute various whitebox and blackbox features of the target model and train an attack model. The adversary cannot poison Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} as it is not publicly available.

Now, we present our defense Distillation For Membership Privacy (DMP),which is motivated by the poor membership privacy-utility tradeoffs provided by existing MIA defenses (В§В 2).First, we give an intuition behind DMP and detail the DMP training. Finally, to achieve the desired tradeoffs, we give a criterion to tune the selection or generation (e.g., using GANs) of reference data used in DMP.

Notations. DMP is a strong meta-regularization technique built on this intuition. DMP aims to protect its target models against the membership inference attacks that exploit the gap between the target modelвҖҷs losses on the members and non-members, by reducing the gap.

DMP achieves this via knowledge transfer and restricts the direct access of Оёpsubscriptрқңғp\theta_{\textsf{p}} to the private Dрқ—Қрқ—Ӣsubscriptрқҗ·рқ—Қрқ—ӢD_{\mathsf{tr}}, which significantly reduces the membership information leakage to Оёpsubscriptрқңғp\theta_{\textsf{p}}.However, unlike existing knowledge transfer, DMP proposes an entropy-based criterion to select/generate Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}}. Simply put, soft labels of the unprotected model Оёрқ—Һрқ—үsubscriptрқңғрқ—Һрқ—ү\theta_{\mathsf{up}} on Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} should have low entropy and the Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} should be far from decision boundaries of Оёрқ—Һрқ—үsubscriptрқңғрқ—Һрқ—ү\theta_{\mathsf{up}}, i.e., far from Dрқ—Қрқ—Ӣsubscriptрқҗ·рқ—Қрқ—ӢD_{\mathsf{tr}}, in the input feature space.Intuitively, such samples are easy to classify and none of the members of Dtrsubscriptрқҗ·trD_{\textsf{tr}} significantly affects their predictions, and therefore, these predictions do not leak membership information of any particular member.

Details of the DMP technique. Fine-tuning the DMP defense. As mentioned before, the appropriate choice of reference data Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} is important to achieve the desired privacy-utility tradeoffs in DMP.In this section, we show that Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} with low entropy predictions of unprotected model Оёрқ—Һрқ—үsubscriptрқңғрқ—Һрқ—ү\theta_{\mathsf{up}} strengthens membership privacy and derive an entropy-based criterion to select/generateXрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}}.

Empirical verification of PropositionВ 1. We randomly pick Dрқ—Қрқ—Ӣsubscriptрқҗ·рқ—Қрқ—ӢD_{\mathsf{tr}} of size 10k from Purhcase100 data and train Оёрқ—Һрқ—үsubscriptрқңғрқ—Һрқ—ү\theta_{\mathsf{up}}. Then, we sort the rest of Purhcase100 data based on entropy of the predictions of Оёрқ—Һрқ—үsubscriptрқңғрқ—Һрқ—ү\theta_{\mathsf{up}} on the data. We form first Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} using the first 10k data with the lowest entropies, second Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} using the following 10k data, and so on. Finally we train multiple protected models, Оёрқ—үsubscriptрқңғрқ—ү\theta_{\mathsf{p}}вҖҷs, using each of the Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}}вҖҷs.Figure 2 (left) shows the increase in the MIA risk and Figure 2 (right) shows the increase in the classification performance of Оёpsubscriptрқңғp\theta_{\textsf{p}} with the increase in average entropy of the Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} used.This tradeoff is because, although the higher entropy predictions contain more useful information (Nayak etВ al. 2019; Hinton, Vinyals, and Dean 2014) and lead to high accuracy of Оёрқ—үsubscriptрқңғрқ—ү\theta_{\mathsf{p}}, they also contain higher membership information about Dрқ—Қрқ—Ӣsubscriptрқҗ·рқ—Қрқ—ӢD_{\mathsf{tr}} and lead to higher MIA risk.

We briefly review the four MIAs we use for evaluations. Following previous works, we use the accuracy of MIAs on target models as a measure of their membership privacy.

Bounded loss (BL) attack В (Yeom etВ al. 2018) decides membership using a threshold on the target modelвҖҷs loss on the target sample. When 0-1 loss is used, the attack accuracy is simply the difference in training and test accuracy of target model. We denote BL attack accuracy by Aрқ–»рқ—…subscriptрқҗҙрқ–»рқ—…A_{\mathsf{bl}}.

NN attack В (Salem etВ al. 2019) uses a shadow dataset dssubscriptрқ‘‘рқ‘ d_{s} drawn from the same distribution as Dрқ—Қрқ—Ӣsubscriptрқҗ·рқ—Қрқ—ӢD_{\mathsf{tr}}. The attacker splits dssubscriptрқ‘‘рқ‘ d_{s} in dsвҖІsubscriptsuperscriptрқ‘‘вҖІрқ‘ d^{\prime}_{s} and dsвҖІвҖІsubscriptsuperscriptрқ‘‘вҖІвҖІрқ‘ d^{\prime\prime}_{s}, trains a shadow model Оёssubscriptрқңғрқ‘ \theta_{s} on dsвҖІsubscriptsuperscriptрқ‘‘вҖІрқ‘ d^{\prime}_{s}, computes predictions of Оёssubscriptрқңғрқ‘ \theta_{s} on dsвҖІsubscriptsuperscriptрқ‘‘вҖІрқ‘ d^{\prime}_{s} and dsвҖІвҖІsubscriptsuperscriptрқ‘‘вҖІвҖІрқ‘ d^{\prime\prime}_{s}, labels the predictions of dsвҖІsubscriptsuperscriptрқ‘‘вҖІрқ‘ d^{\prime}_{s} as members and that of dsвҖІвҖІsubscriptsuperscriptрқ‘‘вҖІвҖІрқ‘ d^{\prime\prime}_{s} as non-members, and trains binary attack model on the predictions. We denote NN attack accuracy by AnnsubscriptрқҗҙnnA_{\textsf{nn}}. Due to their small sizes, DMP cannot be evaluated with CIFAR datasets, hence we omit NN attack evaluation for CIFAR datasets.

NSH attacks В (Nasr, Shokri, and Houmansadr 2019) are similar to NN attacks. They concatenate various whitebox (e.g., model gradients) and/or blackbox (e.g., model loss, predictions) features of target model, while NN attack uses only the target model predictions. PATEВ (Papernot etВ al. 2017), a semi-supervized learning technique, requires a compatible pair of generator and disciminator to achieve acceptable performances. Below, we provide further key insights in to DMP defense and defer their detailed discussion to Appendix.

Increasing the temperature of softmax layer of the unprotected model, Оёрқ—Һрқ—үsubscriptрқңғрқ—Һрқ—ү\theta_{\mathsf{up}}, used to transfer the knowledge of Оёрқ—Һрқ—үsubscriptрқңғрқ—Һрқ—ү\theta_{\mathsf{up}}, can further reduce the membership leakage of Dрқ—Қрқ—Ӣsubscriptрқҗ·рқ—Қрқ—ӢD_{\mathsf{tr}}. This is because, at higher softmax temperatures, predictions of Оёрқ—Һрқ—үsubscriptрқңғрқ—Һрқ—ү\theta_{\mathsf{up}} have uniform distribution over all classes and contain no useful information for MIAs.Similarly, reducing the size of Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} reduces MIA risk due to DMP, but comes at the cost of reduction in Aрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ңрқ—Қsubscriptрқҗҙрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ңрқ—ҚA_{\mathsf{test}}.

We evaluate the privacy risk to Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}}, as it can be of sensitive nature, e.g., in case of Texas medical records dataset.Our results in appendix show that given the final DMP model, Оёрқ—үsubscriptрқңғрқ—ү\theta_{\mathsf{p}}, and a target sample, MIA adversary (who mounts BL, NN, or NSH attacks) cannot decide if the sample belonged to Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} with sufficient confidence.This is expected, because DMP trains its Оёрқ—үsubscriptрқңғрқ—ү\theta_{\mathsf{p}} on noisy, soft-labels of Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}}, which do not contain any sensitive information about Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} and its ground-truth labels, which is necessary for MIAs to succeedВ (Yeom etВ al. 2018).We provide detailed results in Appendix.

Following previous worksВ (Papernot etВ al. 2018, 2017), including the state-of-the-art MIA defense AdvRegВ (Nasr, Shokri, and Houmansadr 2018), we assume availability of Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}}.However, in privacy sensitive domains such as patient medical records, Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} may not be available.Hence, we show that the assumption can be relaxed by using Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} synthesized from private Dрқ—Қрқ—Ӣsubscriptрқҗ·рқ—Қрқ—ӢD_{\mathsf{tr}} to train DMP models.For CIFAR10, we use DC-GAN to generate synthetic Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} of sizes 12.5k, 25k, and 37.5k from Dрқ—Қрқ—Ӣsubscriptрқҗ·рқ—Қрқ—ӢD_{\mathsf{tr}} of size 25k. We then train three DMP models and evaluate their MIA risk using whitebox NSH attack. We note that for 12.5k, 25k, and 37.5k synthetic Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} samples, (Eрқ—Җрқ–ҫрқ—Үsubscriptрқҗёрқ—Җрқ–ҫрқ—ҮE_{\mathsf{gen}}, Aрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ңрқ—Қsubscriptрқҗҙрқ—Қрқ–ҫрқ—Ңрқ—ҚA_{\mathsf{test}}, Aрқ—җрқ–»subscriptрқҗҙрқ—җрқ–»A_{\mathsf{wb}}) of DMP are (2.1, 53.0, 50.3), (3.5, 56.8, 51.3), and (5.0, 57.5, 52.1), respectively. Note that, DMP outperforms existing defenses even with synthetic Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} (TablesВ 3,В 4).

In DMP, the reference data, Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}}, is selected such that the predictions of DMPвҖҷs unprotected model Оёрқ—Һрқ—үsubscriptрқңғрқ—Һрқ—ү\theta_{\mathsf{up}} on Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} have low entropies. Due to memorization, predictions of Оёрқ—Һрқ—үsubscriptрқңғрқ—Һрқ—ү\theta_{\mathsf{up}} on Dрқ—Қрқ—Ӣsubscriptрқҗ·рқ—Қрқ—ӢD_{\mathsf{tr}} also have low entropies. Hence, an adaptive adversary may exploit this peculiar Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} selection in DMP. Based on this intuition, we investigate the possibility of an adaptive MIA, which labels a target sample as a member if the sample is close to some Xрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝsubscriptрқ‘Ӣрқ—Ӣрқ–ҫрқ–ҝX_{\mathsf{ref}} datum in feature space. However, such attack has accuracy close to random guess. This is because, we observe that the proximity of two samples in feature space has no correlation with the entropy of predictions of given Оёрқ—Һрқ—үsubscriptрқңғрқ—Һрқ—ү\theta_{\mathsf{up}} on those samples, which is the selection criterion of DMP. We leave further investigation of adaptive attacks on DMP to future work.

We proposed Distillation for Membership Privacy (DMP), a knowledge distillation based defense against membership inference attacks that significantly improves the membership privacy-model utility tradeoffs compared to state-of-the-art defenses. We provided a novel criterion to generate/select reference data in DMP and achieve the desired tradeoffs. Our extensive evaluation demonstrated the state-of-the-art privacy-utility tradeoffs of DMP.
