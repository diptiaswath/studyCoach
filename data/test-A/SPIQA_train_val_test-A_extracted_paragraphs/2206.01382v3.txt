NNS dates back to Minsky and Papert [21], who studied the tradeoff between query time and indexing space in high dimensions.Due to the “curse of dimensionality”, linear scan outperforms space/data partitioning solutions on solving exact NNS given a linear indexing space [28].Given a polynomial indexing space n𝒪​(1)​d𝒪​(1)superscript𝑛𝒪1superscript𝑑𝒪1n^{{\mathcal{O}}\left(1\right)}d^{{\mathcal{O}}\left(1\right)}, there is no known algorithm to solve exact NNS in truly sublinear time.If there exists such an algorithm, the Strong Exponential Time Hypothesis (SETH) [16], a fundamental conjecture in computational complexity theory, is wrong [3, 29].

Due to the hardness of exact NNS, researchers study efficient solutions for approximate NNS (ANNS).Given an approximation factor c′>1superscript𝑐′1c^{\prime}>1, ANNS aims at finding the point 𝐩′∈𝐗superscript𝐩′𝐗\mathbf{p}^{\prime}\in\mathbf{X} within distance c′⋅‖𝐩−𝐪‖⋅superscript𝑐′norm𝐩𝐪c^{\prime}\cdot\|\mathbf{p}-\mathbf{q}\|.Since we can reduce ANNS to its “decision version”, called (c,r)𝑐𝑟(c,r)-approximate near neighbor ((c,r)𝑐𝑟(c,r)-NN) for an approximation factor c>1𝑐1c>1 and a radius r𝑟r [14], solving ANNS boils down to (c,r)𝑐𝑟(c,r)-NN.The problem (c,r)𝑐𝑟(c,r)-NN that asks to return any point within distance c​r𝑐𝑟cr to 𝐪𝐪\mathbf{q} if there is a point within distance r𝑟r to 𝐪𝐪\mathbf{q} is the key algorithmic component of many ANNS solvers.Locality-sensitive hashing (LSH) [17] is the first provable sublinear query time solution using a subquadratic indexing space for (c,r)𝑐𝑟(c,r)-NN in high dimensions.

In principle, LSH hashes “close” points, i.e. within radius r𝑟r to 𝐪𝐪\mathbf{q}, into the same bucket with 𝐪𝐪\mathbf{q} with high probability, and hashes “far away” points, i.e. outside radius c​r𝑐𝑟cr to 𝐪𝐪\mathbf{q}, into the same bucket with low probability.Given a parameter 0<ρ<10𝜌10<\rho<1, LSH answers (c,r)𝑐𝑟(c,r)-NN in 𝒪​(d​nρ)𝒪𝑑superscript𝑛𝜌{\mathcal{O}}\left(dn^{\rho}\right) time and uses 𝒪​(n​d+n1+ρ)𝒪𝑛𝑑superscript𝑛1𝜌{\mathcal{O}}\left(nd+n^{1+\rho}\right) space.Since the exponent ρ𝜌\rho governs the complexity of LSH, it is desirable to have LSH families with the smallest ρ𝜌\rho.LSH families with optimal values ρ≈1/c2𝜌1superscript𝑐2\rho\approx 1/c^{2} on Euclidean distance are well studied in theory [1, 5].In practice, to our knowledge, only Falconn [2] with the cross-polytope LSH family asymptotically achieves ρ≈1/c2𝜌1superscript𝑐2\rho\approx 1/c^{2} on the Euclidean unit sphere.

Despite provable sublinear query time guarantees, the indexing space of LSH is a primary bottleneck.Since different queries require different values of r𝑟r, practical LSH-based solutions often use hundreds of hash tables to guarantee the querying performance.To overcome this bottleneck, Lv et al. [19] propose a multi-probing trick that sequentially checks nearby buckets of the query’s bucket.This is because the hash values of the nearest neighbors of 𝐪𝐪\mathbf{q} tend to be close to the hash value of 𝐪𝐪\mathbf{q} on the hashing space [23].Therefore, multi-probe LSH achieves a similar success probability with a smaller number of hash tables than the standard LSH.

We observe that LSH and its multi-probe schemes do not efficiently answer ANNS on high recall regimes.Since the distance gaps between the nearest neighbors are tiny in many real-world data sets, we need a very small approximation factor c𝑐c, e.g. c=1.01𝑐1.01c=1.01.This requirement degrades the performance of LSH-based solutions as ρ→1→𝜌1\rho\rightarrow 1.In this case, multi-probe LSH has to increase the number of probes and the number of candidates.That significantly reduces the querying performance since the probed buckets different from the query bucket tend to have more far away points.Empirically, multi-probe Falconn [2] with L=100𝐿100L=100 hash tables on the Glove300 data set needs 4,000 probes and computes more than 0.1⋅n⋅0.1𝑛0.1\cdot n number of distances to achieve 90% recall.This is the rationale that LSH-based solutions are less favorable for ANNS on high recall regimes than the recent graph-based solutions [7], e.g. HNSW [20].

To further facilitate LSH-based solutions for ANNS, we propose a filtering mechanism that can efficiently filter out the far away points in any bucket before querying.Our proposed mechanism is also locality-sensitive in the sense that far away points will be filtered out with high probability and close points will be pruned with low probability.Therefore, we can safely remove far away points while building the index to improve the query time and reduce the indexing space simultaneously.This feature significantly increases the performance of multi-probe LSH since it dramatically reduces the number of distance computations while preserving the search recall.

We implement the proposed filtering mechanism for angular distance based on the asymptotic property of the concomitant of extreme order statistics [12, 24].Similar to Falconn [2], our approach utilizes many random projection vectors and only considers specific vectors 𝐫isubscript𝐫𝑖\mathbf{r}_{i} that are closest or furthest to the query 𝐪𝐪\mathbf{q}.The key difference is that we use the random projection value 𝐱⊤​𝐫isuperscript𝐱topsubscript𝐫𝑖\mathbf{x}^{\top}\mathbf{r}_{i} to estimate 𝐱⊤​𝐪superscript𝐱top𝐪\mathbf{x}^{\top}\mathbf{q} by leveraging the asymptotic behavior of these projection values.This feature makes our filtering mechanism locality-sensitive.

Theoretically, our proposed locality-sensitive filtering (LSF) scheme, named Falconn++, asymptotically achieves smaller ρ𝜌\rho, a parameter governing the query time complexity, than Falconn.Empirically, Falconn++ with a new add-on that applies multi-probing on both querying and indexing phases achieves significantly higher recall-speed tradeoffs than Falconn on many real-world data sets.We further innovate Falconn++ with several heuristics that make it competitive with HNSW [20], a recent efficient graph-based solution for ANNS on high recall regimes.

Connection to recent LSF frameworks.Our proposed LSF mechanism combined with Falconn can be seen as a practical instance of the asymmetric LSF frameworks recently studied in theory [4, 11].These frameworks use different filtering conditions on the data and query points to govern the space-time tradeoff of ANNS.In particular, given two thresholds tu,tqsubscript𝑡𝑢subscript𝑡𝑞t_{u},t_{q}, over the random choice of 𝐫isubscript𝐫𝑖\mathbf{r}_{i}, the LSF frameworks study the probability where both 𝐱𝐱\mathbf{x} and 𝐪𝐪\mathbf{q} pass the filter corresponding to 𝐫isubscript𝐫𝑖\mathbf{r}_{i}, i.e. Pr​[𝐱⊤​𝐫i≥tu,𝐪⊤​𝐫i≥tq]Prdelimited-[]formulae-sequencesuperscript𝐱topsubscript𝐫𝑖subscript𝑡𝑢superscript𝐪topsubscript𝐫𝑖subscript𝑡𝑞\textrm{\bf Pr}\left[\mathbf{x}^{\top}\mathbf{r}_{i}\geq t_{u},\mathbf{q}^{\top}\mathbf{r}_{i}\geq t_{q}\right].Indeed, Falconn++ can be seen as a specific design of the asymmetric LSF framework where we utilize cross-polytope LSH to implement the filtering condition for queries and the asymptotic property of CEOs to implement the filtering condition for data points.We believe that our approach is much simpler in terms of theoretical analysis and practical implementation.Empirically, we observe that Falconn++ with practical add-ons provides higher search recall than the corresponding instantiation of the theoretical LSF frameworks.

Multi-probing for indexing: A new add-on.We note that multi-probing is motivated by the observation that randomly perturbing 𝐪𝐪\mathbf{q} generates many perturbed objects that tend to be close to 𝐪𝐪\mathbf{q} and to be hashed into the same bucket with 𝐪𝐪\mathbf{q}’s nearest neighbors [23].This observation is also true in the reverse case where we perturb 𝐪𝐪\mathbf{q}’s nearest neighbors to form new perturbed objects that tend to be hashed into the 𝐪𝐪\mathbf{q}’s bucket.Hence, multi-probing can be used for indexing where we hash one point into multiple buckets.However, the downside of this approach is an increase of the table size from n𝑛n to i​P​r​o​b​e​s⋅n⋅𝑖𝑃𝑟𝑜𝑏𝑒𝑠𝑛iProbes\cdot n points where i​P​r​o​b​e​s≥1𝑖𝑃𝑟𝑜𝑏𝑒𝑠1iProbes\geq 1 is the number of indexing probes for one data point.Therefore, multi-probing for indexing has not been used in practice.

We present Falconn++, a practical locality-sensitive filtering scheme for ANNS on angular distance.Theoretically, Falconn++ asymptotically has lower query time complexity than Falconn, an optimal LSH scheme.Though our implementation of Falconn++ lacks many optimized routines, Falconn++ with several heuristic add-ons gives higher recall-speed tradeoffs than Falconn.It is also competitive with HNSW, an efficient representative of graph-based solutions on high search recall regimes.
