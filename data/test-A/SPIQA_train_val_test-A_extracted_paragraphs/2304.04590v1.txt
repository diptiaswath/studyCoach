Biomedical literature search is an essential step for knowledge discovery and clinical decision support (Gopalakrishnan et¬†al., 2019; Ely et¬†al., 2005; Jin et¬†al., 2022).It has several distinct properties from other information retrieval (IR) tasks:(1) Most queries are short. The average length of a query is about 3.5 tokens in PubMed111https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/, a widely used biomedical literature search engine (Dogan et¬†al., 2009; Fiorini et¬†al., 2018a, b);(2) Large-scale relevant query-document pairs can be easily collected from the user click logs.TripClick (Rekabsaz et¬†al., 2021), a recently released benchmark for biomedical literature retrieval, contains 1.3 million query-document relevance signals collected from the Trip Database222https://www.tripdatabase.com/ logs;(3) Users mostly browse the documents on the first page (Dogan et¬†al., 2009), and the top 31% most clicked documents account for 80% clicks in the released Trip Database logs.These unique characteristics motivate the augmentation of biomedical literature search by directly retrieving from the click logs of similar queries, since queries with similar information needs tend to have similar document clicks (Baeza-Yates et¬†al., 2007; Yin et¬†al., 2009; Zhuang et¬†al., 2022).This approach is essentially similar to recent retrieval augmentation methods (Khandelwal et¬†al., 2020; Frisoni et¬†al., 2022; Yuan et¬†al., 2023).Fig 1 shows an example query and clicked documents from its similar queries in the training set of TripClick.The objective of this work is to augment biomedical literature search with such documents.

One model can simply return clicked documents from the logs of similar queries as the retrieval results, which is analogous to memory-based collaborative filtering in recommendation systems (Su and Khoshgoftaar, 2009).However, this might miss relevant documents that have not been clicked before.Therefore, clicked documents from logs are better used to augment an existing retriever, and they are theoretically retriever-agnostic.We choose to use dense retrievers, where queries and documents are encoded and matched in low-dimensional embedding space, because recent work has shown that dense retrievers based on pre-trained language models such as BERT (Devlin et¬†al., 2019) outperform traditional sparse retrievers on various tasks (Karpukhin et¬†al., 2020; Khattab and Zaharia, 2020; Xiong et¬†al., 2021).

In this paper, we introduce Log-Agumented DEnse Retrieval (LADER), which is a novel and simple plug-in module that augments dense retrievers by interpolating the document scores aggregated from logs of similar queries and the original scores from a dense retriever.We conduct experiments on the recently introduced TripClick benchmark (Rekabsaz et¬†al., 2021) and show that: (1) LADER outperforms previous state-of-the-art (SOTA) models on queries of all frequency groups, with close to 40% relative NDCG@10 improvement on the frequent (‚ÄúHEAD‚ÄĚ) queries; (2) LADER improves the backbone dense retriever by 24%-37% relative NDCG@10 while not requiring additional training, and it is expected to have further performance improvements with more logs.Our regression analysis shows that queries with higher frequency, lower entropy of similar query scores, and higher entropy of similar document scores, tend to benefit more from log augmentation.

We evaluate our LADER method on the TripClick benchmark (Rekabsaz et¬†al., 2021), which contains 692k unique queries and 1.5M documents (PubMed abstracts).Based on their frequencies, the queries are divided into HEAD (>>44), TORSO (6‚Äď44), and TAIL (<<6) subsets.Both the validation and test sets contain 1,175 queries for each HEAD, TORSO and TAIL subset.Following (Rekabsaz et¬†al., 2021; Hofst√§tter et¬†al., 2022), we use two sets of relevance scores:The ‚ÄúRAW‚ÄĚ relevance is used to judge TORSO and TAIL queries, where clicked documents have a score of 1 and other documents have a score of 0.The Graded Document Click-Through Rate (Chuklin et¬†al., 2015; Craswell et¬†al., 2008) (‚ÄúDCTR‚ÄĚ) relevance is used to judge the HEAD queries, where the document score is defined as the number of clicks divided by the number of exposure.To train the dense retriever (¬ß2.1), we use 10M triples released by (Hofst√§tter et¬†al., 2022), where each triple contains a TripClick training query, a clicked document, and a non-relevant document sampled from BM25 negatives.

We present LADER, a simple and novel plug-in module that uses search logs to augment dense retrievers.Our results show that LADER achieves new SOTA on TripClick, and can largely improve the performance of its backbone retriever without additional training.We also provide thorough analyses of its characteristics.

One limitation of LADER is that it increases the search latency by about Nq/NdsubscriptūĚĎĀūĚĎěsubscriptūĚĎĀūĚĎĎN_{q}/N_{d} (45% for TripClick) due to the additional query-to-query retrieval step.Another drawback of this study is that we only use data from one search engine.It remains interesting to test the generalizability of LADER to a different search engine, which will be beneficial for cold-starting new literature search initiatives.
