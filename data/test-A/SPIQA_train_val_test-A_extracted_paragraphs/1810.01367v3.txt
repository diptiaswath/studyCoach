Reversible generative models use cheaply invertible neural networks to transform samples from a fixed base distribution.Examples include NICEВ (Dinh etВ al., 2014), Real NVPВ (Dinh etВ al., 2017), and GlowВ (Kingma & Dhariwal, 2018).These models are easy to sample from, and can be trained by maximum likelihood using the change of variables formula.However, this requires placing awkward restrictions on their architectures, such as partitioning dimensions or using rank one weight matrices, in order to avoid an рқ’ӘвҖӢ(D3)рқ’Әsuperscriptрқҗ·3\mathcal{O}(D^{3}) cost determinant computation.

Recently, Chen etВ al. (2018) introduced a continuous-time analog of normalizing flows, defining the mapping from latent variables to data using ordinary differential equations (ODEs).In their model, the likelihood can be computed using relatively cheap trace operations.A more flexible, but still restricted, family of network architectures can be used to avoid this рқ’ӘвҖӢ(D2)рқ’Әsuperscriptрқҗ·2\mathcal{O}(D^{2}) time cost.

Extending this work, we introduce an unbiased stochastic estimator of the likelihood that has рқ’ӘвҖӢ(D)рқ’Әрқҗ·\mathcal{O}(D) time cost, allowing completely unrestricted architectures.Furthermore, we have implemented GPU-based adaptive ODE solvers to train and evaluate these models on modern hardware.We call our approach Free-form Jacobian of Reversible Dynamics (FFJORD).

These approaches broadly fall into three categories:Normalizing flows. The full computational cost of integrating the instantaneous change of variables (2) is рқ’ӘвҖӢ(DвҖӢHвҖӢL^)рқ’Әрқҗ·рқҗ»^рқҗҝ\mathcal{O}(DH\widehat{L}) where Dрқҗ·D is dimensionality of the data, Hрқҗ»H is the size of the hidden state, and L^^рқҗҝ\widehat{L} is the number of function evaluations (NFE) that the adaptive solver uses to integrate the ODE. In general, each evaluation of the model is рқ’ӘвҖӢ(DвҖӢH)рқ’Әрқҗ·рқҗ»\mathcal{O}(DH) and in practice, Hрқҗ»H is typically chosen to be close to Dрқҗ·D. Since the general form of the discrete change of variables equation (1) requires рқ’ӘвҖӢ(D3)рқ’Әsuperscriptрқҗ·3\mathcal{O}(D^{3})-cost, one may wonder whether the number of evaluations L^^рқҗҝ\widehat{L} depends on Dрқҗ·D.

We train VAEs using FFJORD flows with increasing latent dimension Dрқҗ·D. The NFE throughout training is shown in Figure 5.In all models, we find that the NFE increases throughout training, but converges to the same value, independent of Dрқҗ·D.This phenomenon can be verified with a simple thought experiment.Take an в„қDsuperscriptв„қрқҗ·\mathbb{R}^{D} isotropic Gaussian distribution as the data distribution and set the base distribution of our model to be an isotropic Gaussian.Then the optimal differential equation is zero for any Dрқҗ·D, and the number evaluations is zero.We can conclude that the number of evaluations is not dependent on the dimensionality of the data but the complexity of its distribution, or more specifically, how difficult it is to transform its density into the base distribution.

Crucial to the scalability of Real NVP and Glow is the multiscale architecture originally proposed in Dinh etВ al. (2017). We compare an single-scale encoder-decoder style FFJORD with a multiscale FFJORD on the MNIST dataset where both models have a comparable number of parameters and plot the total NFEвҖ“in both forward and backward passesвҖ“against the loss achieved in Figure 6. We find that while the single-scale model uses approximately one half as many function evaluations as the multiscale model, it is not able to achieve the same performance as the multiscale model.

The number of function evaluations required to integrate the dynamics is not fixed ahead of time and is a function of the data, model architecture, and model parameters.We find that this tends to grow as the models trains and can become prohibitively large, even when memory stays constant due to the adjoint method.Various forms of regularization such as weight decay and spectral normalizationВ (Miyato etВ al., 2018) can be used to reduce the this quantity but their use tends to hurt performance slightly.

In theory, we can model any differential equation (given mild assumptions based on existence and uniqueness of the solution), but in practice our reliance on general-purpose ODE solvers restricts us to non-stiff differential equations that can be efficiently solved.ODE solvers for stiff dynamics exist, but they evaluate fрқ‘“f many more times to achieve the same error.We find that using a small amount of weight decay sufficiently constrains the ODE to be non-stiff.

We have presented FFJORD, a reversible generative model for high dimensional data which can compute exact log-likelihoods and can be sampled from efficiently.Our model uses continuous-time dynamics to produce a generative model which is parameterized by an unrestricted neural network.All required quantities for training and sampling can be computed using automatic-differentiation, HutchinsonвҖҷs trace estimator, and black-box ODE solvers.Our model stands in contrast to other methods with similar properties which rely on restricted, hand-engineered neural network architectures.We have demonstrated that this additional flexibility allows our approach to achieve improved performance on density estimation and variational inference. We also demonstrate FFJORDвҖҷs ability to model distributions which comparable methods such as Glow and Real NVP cannot model.

We believe there is much room for further work exploring and improving this method. We are interested specifically in ways to reduce the number of function evaluations used by the ODE-solver without hurting predictive performance. Advancements like these will be crucial in scaling this method to even higher-dimensional datasets.
