Twitter bots, also known as Twitter accounts controlled by automatic programs, have become a widely known phenomenon for social media users (Cresci, 2020; Ferrara, 2022). Twitter bots are posing threats and challenges to online communities by spreading misinformation (Cresci et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2021; Zannettou et al., 2019; Starbird, 2019), deepening societal divides (Uyheng and Carley, 2020; Flores-Saviaga et al., 2022), promoting conspiracy theories (Ferrara, 2020; Greve et al., 2022), and influencing major elections (Ferrara, 2017; Rossi et al., 2020). As a result, extensive research efforts have been made to counter the malicious impact of Twitter bots through automatic Twitter bot detection systems (Davoudi et al., 2020; Chavoshi et al., 2016), context-specific case studies (Pastor-Galindo et al., 2020; Brachten et al., 2017), and more.

Existing automatic Twitter bot detection models could be characterized by their information sources (feature-based, text-based, and graph-based (Feng et al., 2022b)), reflecting the arms race between bot operators and bot detection research (Cresci, 2020): Early Twitter bots are mostly simple spam bots posting repetitive and malicious content (Cresci et al., 2017), thus they could be easily identified by feature-based models through feature engineering and traditional classifiers (Yang et al., 2020; Kudugunta and Ferrara, 2018). However, in part due to the awareness of these feature-based detectors, bot operators began to tamper with user metadata (Cresci, 2020; Cresci et al., 2017), leaving feature-based approaches less effective. As a result, text-based methods were proposed to leverage natural language processing(NLP) techniques (Wei and Nguyen, 2019; Dukić et al., 2020; Heidari and Jones, 2020) to analyze the textual content in tweets and user descriptions, aiming to identify Twitter bots through their malicious content. However, text-based methods have become less effective as bots began to post tweets stolen from genuine users (Cresci, 2020), diluting the malicious content in the timeline of Twitter bots. These advanced bots are better captured by graph-based models (Li et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2020; Feng et al., 2022a; Lei et al., 2022), which leverage graph neural networks to analyze Twitter bot clusters and reveal their collective actions.

Though Twitter bot detection research has come a long way to counter increasingly advanced and evasive Twitter bots, the complexity of bot disguises and the diversified nature of Twitter communities as shown in Figure 1 remain underexplored in automatic bot detection systems. As a result, existing Twitter bot detection approaches fall short of addressing these two key challenges:•Manipulated Features. In addition to promoting malicious content, advanced Twitter bots manipulate their information in one or more modalities to evade detection. (Kumarage et al., 2023)Deliberately engineering user metadata (Cresci, 2020; Yan et al., 2021), copying user descriptions from genuine users (Cresci, 2020), and exploiting follow relationship with the help of botnets (Ferrara, 2022; Yang et al., 2023) are among the numerous attempts to manipulate user features in metadata, textual content, graph structure, and more. These multi-modal feature manipulation strategies in turn incapacitate the ability of existing bot detectors that merely take one or few information sources into account.•Diverse Communities. The distribution of Twitter bots is heterogeneous across the whole Twitter network (Peng et al., 2022; tan2023botpercent), while bot behavior and characteristics greatly vary from one user community to another (Sayyadiharikandeh et al., 2020). However, existing bot detectors often assume the uniformity of Twitter bots, presenting seemingly general-purpose approaches that neglect the heterogeneity of user communities and the difference in bot behavior across different bot clusters. This limitation harms the domain generalization ability of existing bot detectors, creates model fairness issues for employing them for content moderation, and more.

In light of these challenges, we propose BotMoE, a novel Twitter bot detection framework that employs a community-aware mixture-of-experts architecture with different experts modeling multi-modal user information sources. Specifically, we first employ modal-specific encoders to jointly leverage the metadata, textual content, and network structure of Twitter users, alleviating the feature manipulation challenge caused by merely analyzing one user information modality. Each modality is then processed by a mixture of modal-specific experts with each expert responsible for the analysis of one specific Twitter community, resulting in community-aware representation learning. These learned representations are further aggregated by assigning Twitter users to one community and activating the corresponding expert. Finally, user representations from each mixture of experts are further synthesized with a novel Expert Fusion layer, aiming to fuse input modalities and assess the consistency across the three modalities to detect feature manipulations. The multi-modal user representations and feature consistency matrices are then linearized and used for the binary classification of Twitter bots and genuine users.

Extensive experiments demonstrate that BotMoE achieves state-of-the-art performance on three Twitter bot detection benchmarks, significantly outperforming 10 representative baseline models from different categories. In addition, modality fusion study and case study testifies to BotMoE’s ability to tackle manipulated features through jointly leveraging and enabling the interaction across multiple user information modalities. In the user community study and generalization study, we demonstrate that BotMoE better generalizes to new and emerging Twitter communities through the community-aware mixture of experts.

Twitter bots are Twitter accounts controlled by automated programs. Bots with malicious goals often harm the integrity of online discourse and reduce search quality. Existing Twitter bot detection methods can be often categorized into three types: feature-based, text-based, and graph-based approaches (Feng et al., 2021c).

These methods conduct feature engineering based on handcrafted user features derived from metadata (Yang et al., 2020; Hays et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023a) and user textual information (Kudugunta and Ferrara, 2018). These features are then combined with traditional classification algorithms (Morstatter et al., 2016) to identify Twitter bots. Kudugunta and Ferrara (2018) exploits user metadata and proposes SMOTENN to combat imbalanced datasets. Miller et al. (2014) leverages the feature of tweet content and performs anomaly detection to identify bots. Hayawi et al. (2022) use hybrid types of numerical, binary, and textural features as high dimensional tensors for classification. However, as noted by Cresci (2020), evolving bots can evade the detection of feature-based approaches by creating deceptive accounts with manipulated metadata and stolen tweets.

The methods use NLP techniques to detect Twitter bots with their tweets and descriptions. LSTMs (Kudugunta and Ferrara, 2018; Luo et al., 2020), the attention mechanism (Feng et al., 2021b), word embeddings (Wei and Nguyen, 2019; Wu et al., 2023b), and pretrained language models (Dukić et al., 2020; Silva et al., 2019) are leveraged to process tweets for detection. (Wei and Nguyen, 2019) use word vectors and bidirectional LSTMs to process user textual information for bot detection and Dukić et al. (2020) present a BERT-based bot detection model to analyze user tweets. However, text-based methods are easily deceived when advanced bots post stolen tweets and descriptions from genuine users (Cresci, 2020).

These methods attempt to identify bots based on the graph structure on the Twittersphere. Dehghan et al. (2022) use node centrality, while Magelinski et al. (2020) makes use of a graph’s latent local features. Methods leveraging heterogeneous graph neural networks (GNNs) (Ali Alhosseini et al., 2019; Feng et al., 2021a; Lei et al., 2022; Feng et al., 2022a; Shi et al., 2023; Alothali et al., 2023) construct a heterogeneous graph based on following, follower, and other user relationships, and learn user representations through graph neural network and achieve state-of-the-art bot detection performance. However, with bot operators creating botnets to manipulate the user networks, methods leveraging only graph information fall short. (Cresci, 2020).

Among these models, there are preliminary attempts to partially address the manipulated feature and diverse community challenges in Twitter bot detection. For example, BotRGCN (Feng et al., 2021a) considers metadata and text features as the initial features for graph convolution. However, shallow concatenation of information sources falls short of identifying advanced bots through compare and contrast while identifying the inconsistency across different modalities. In addition, BotBuster (Ng and Carley, 2022) proposes to train different experts for different features in various modalities and use the combined results of expert outputs. However, this approach assumes the uniformity of bot behavior across communities and platforms, neglecting the heterogeneous nature of Twitter bot distributions.To this end, we propose BotMoE, a Twitter bot detection framework with community-aware mixtures of modal-specific experts, aiming to improve robustness towards feature manipulation and generalization towards diversified Twitter communities.

Based on the Divide-and-Conquer principle, the Mixture of Experts (MoE) method is proposed to divide an input sample into sub-tasks and train experts specialized in each sub-task.MoE is adopted in NLP to enhance models’ capacity (Shazeer et al., 2017), reasoning (Madaan et al., 2021), and generalization (Ma et al., 2018). Shazeer et al. (2017) propose a sparsely-gated Mixture-of-Experts layer for conditional computing in large language models. Besides, MoE is also well explored in graph mining: Hu et al. (2021) use MoE to combat imbalance in graph classification tasks. Fedus et al. (2021) create simplified routing algorithms for MoE to achieve high training stability and less computational costs. Moreover, MoE can be seen as a natural fit for multi-task learning with different modalities. Ma et al. (2018) come up with a multi-gate Mixture-of-Experts with shared experts as sub-models and different gates for each task, aiming to capture inter-task relationships while optimizing task-specific objectives. Bao et al. (2021) use a mixture-of-modality-experts transformer to unify vision and language in training.

The most relevant work among all MoE-related literature would be BotBuster (Ng and Carley, 2022), which proposes a multi-platform bot detection framework based on MoE, where each expert is assigned a specific type of user feature to deal with incomplete data. However, BotBuster falls short of leveraging different experts as representatives of different Twitter communities while still assuming the uniformity of Twitter bots across different bot clusters. Therefore, we propose BotMoE, which, in each modality, trains specialized experts for different sub-communities, achieving a community-aware mixture of experts to tackle the diverse community challenge.

Figure 2 presents an overview of our proposed BotMoE framework with multi-modal user information and community-aware MoE layers. Specifically, we first encode users’ metadata, text, and graph information to obtain more comprehensive user representations from three different perspectives. To deal with diverse user communities, we then leverage the Mixture-of-Expert architecture to assign users to experts based on their communities for community-aware bot detection. After that, to enable information interaction, we fuse the user representations from metadata, text, and graph modality with an extractor fusion layer while measuring their inconsistency. Finally, we classify Twitter users into bots or genuine users and learn model parameters.

We evaluate BotMoE on three widely adopted Twitter bot detection benchmarks: Cresci-15 (Cresci et al., 2015), TwiBot-20 (Feng et al., 2021c), and TwiBot-22 (Feng et al., 2022b). Cresci-15 (Cresci et al., 2015) is proposed in 2015 and includes 5,301 Twitter users and their network structure.TwiBot-20 (Feng et al., 2021c) contains 229,580 users from the sports, economy, entertainment, and politics domains. TwiBot-22 (Feng et al., 2022b) is the most extensive dataset to date for Twitter bot detection, featuring a diverse set of entities and relationships on Twitter with 1,000,000 users. We follow the original train, valid, and test splits of the datasets for a fair comparison with previous works.

We compare BotMoE with feature-, text-, and graph-based Twitter bot detection models:•SGBot (Yang et al., 2020) extracts features from users’ metadata and feeds them into random forest classifiers for scalable and generalizable bot identification.•Wei et al. (Wei and Nguyen, 2019) use word embeddings and bidirectional LSTMs to process user textual information for bot detection.•Varol et al. (Varol et al., 2017) mainly use metadata, network, and other derived statistics to perform bot detection with random forest, aiming to detect different types of Twitter bots.•SATAR (Feng et al., 2021b) is a self-supervised approach for Twitter user representation learning, which use semantic, property, and neighborhood information and is further fine-tuned on Twitter bot detection datasets.•Botometer (Yang et al., 2022) use more than 1,000 features from user metadata, content, and interaction. They use an ensemble of existing datasets for training.•GraphHist (Magelinski et al., 2020) is a graph-based approach based on the latent feature histograms of users, which detects Twitter bots by users’ ego graphs.•BotRGCN (Feng et al., 2021a) builds a heterogeneous graph from the Twitter network and employs relational graph convolutional networks for user representation learning and Twitter bot detection.•RGT (Feng et al., 2022a) is short for relational graph transformers, which model the inherent heterogeneity in the Twittersphere to improve Twitter bot detection.•BotBuster (Ng and Carley, 2022) is a social bot detection system that processes user metadata and textual information with mixture-of-experts to enhance cross-platform bot detection.

Existing Twitter bot detection models over-rely on training data, requiring large quantities of labeled users for training and becoming insufficiently trained if otherwise. However, due to the obscure nature of advanced Twitter bots, obtaining accurate and reliable labels for Twitter bot detection is difficult, expensive, and noisy, creating challenges for the creation of Twitter bot detection datasets (Hays et al., 2023). Therefore, it is essential to investigate BotMoE and existing baselines’ robustness against limited labels, user interactions, and noisy user features, which are common in existing bot detection datasets.Specifically, we create ablated settings with partial training sets, randomly removed user interaction edges, and masked user features to re-evaluate bot detection models and present performance in Figure 6.It demonstrates that even with only 60% of user interactions and annotations, BotMoE outperforms the full version of the state-of-the-art model RGT (Feng et al., 2022a), proving that BotMoE is more robust towards fewer training data.We also find that when the number of user features is reduced, BotMoE exhibits performance drops. We suspect this limitation could be in part alleviated by employing fewer experts in the community-aware MoE layers, which we further discuss in Section 4.8.

An important parameter in the BotMoE framework is the number of experts in each modality, which governs the number of communities and the inductive bias of BotMoE. To investigate the optimal setup of experts, we conduct grid search and present the model’s performance on Twibot-20 in Figure 7.Overall, model performance is worst for the non-MoE setting (1 expert only). However, more experts don’t necessarily guarantee better performance: the highest performance is achieved at 222 text experts, 222 graph experts, and 222 metadata experts. This is because compared to the other large language models trained on colossal datasets, BotMoE is rather a lightweight model trained on a small part of social information. The total parameters of our model are less than 1 million, contrasting to large language models with millions of parameters in Shazeer et al. (2017). Therefore, the increase in the capacity may lead the model to overfit the training data. Besides, another finding is that models with more experts tend to be unstable during training perhaps because of the special nature of MoE.

From the attention weights of the expert fusion layer, we find metadata and graph features carry higher weights and provide more trustworthy information for bot detection. Therefore, relying solely on one modality of features for prediction may not be effective, as the model would not be able to utilize all the information that bots produce in order to identify them. However, BotMoE’s multi-modal joint detection framework enables the model to focus on the more informative features and provide more reliable results.

BotMoE has several limitations:First, more modalities could be introduced in BotMoE. Twitter content is not limited to textual tweets, as many accounts also post pictures and videos. Therefore, more user information and modalities could be introduced for Twitter bot detection.Second, our study is limited to Twitter users due to the abundant resources on Twitter bot detection, while other social media platforms are underexplored and whether BotMoE could generalize to Reddit or Facebook remains unclear.Finally, the interpretability of BotMoE is limited, as it lacks direct evidence of which modality and what information in that modality contributes most to the model’s performance and a detailed explanation needs to be explored for how the communities are formed and identified. We leave it to future work on how to incorporate more user information modalities, evaluate BotMoE on social media other than Twitter, and improve the interpretability of BotMoE and Twitter bot detection in general.

BotMoE should serve as an aid rather than the sole decision maker in detecting Twitter bots, and human oversight is necessary for making final judgments when deployed on real-world social networks. First, BotMoE can have false positive predictions, where legitimate accounts are classified as bots by mistake, which can have serious consequences for businesses or individuals who spread important information (Thieltges et al., 2016). Second, as a combination of graph neural networks and language models, BotMoE inherits the bias presented in these models. For example, language models could introduce stereotypes and social bias (Nadeem et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2021) and graph neural networks could lead to discrimination towards certain demographic groups (Dong et al., 2022).

We propose BotMoE, a novel Twitter bot detection framework with community-aware mixtures of modal-specific experts, aiming to tackle the challenges of malicious feature manipulation and diverse user communities. Extensive experiments demonstrate that BotMoE significantly advances the state-of-the-art on three Twitter bot detection benchmarks. Further studies demonstrate the effectiveness of our original technical contributions, highlighting BotMoE’s ability to tackle malicious feature manipulation and diverse Twitter communities, and proving the superior generalization ability and data efficiency of BotMoE.
