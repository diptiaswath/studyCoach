We summarise our conclusions as follows.

1.Examining word embeddings — including LSA, word2vec, GloVe — through the relationship with low-rank matrix factorisations with respect to a criterion f𝑓f makes it clear that the solution V𝑉V is non-identifiable: for a particular solution V∗superscript𝑉V^{*}, C​V∗𝐶superscript𝑉CV^{*} for any C∈𝖦𝖫​(d)𝐶𝖦𝖫𝑑C\in\mathsf{GL}(d) is also a solution. Different elements of the d2superscript𝑑2d^{2}-dimensional solution set perform differently in evaluations, g𝑔g, of word task performance.2.An important implication is that the disparity in performance between word embeddings on tasks g𝑔g maybe due to the particular elements selected from the solution sets. In word embeddings for which the f𝑓f is optimized numerically with some randomness, for example in the initializations, the optimisation may converge to different elements of the solution set. An embedding chosen based on the best performance in g𝑔g over repeated runs of the optimisation can essentially be viewed as a Monte Carlo optimisation over the solution set.3.The evaluation function g𝑔g is usually only invariant to orthogonal (𝖮​(d)𝖮𝑑\mathsf{O}(d)) and scale-type (c​ℐ𝑐ℐc\mathcal{I}) transformations. Thus for an embedding dimension d𝑑d, the effective dimension of the solution set after accounting for the orthogonal transformations, and scaled versions of the identity, is d​(d+1)/2−1𝑑𝑑121d(d+1)/2-1. Conclusions from evaluations with large d𝑑d must hence be interpreted with some care, especially if the V𝑉V is optimized with respect to the incompatible transformations ℱdsubscriptℱ𝑑\mathcal{F}_{d} directly or indirectly, for example as in point 2 above.4.These considerations have a bearing on the interpretation of the performance of the popular embedding approach of taking V=Λα​V∗𝑉superscriptΛ𝛼superscript𝑉V=\Lambda^{\alpha}V^{*} where α𝛼\alpha is a tuning parameter and ΛΛ\Lambda is a diagonal matrix taken, for example, to contain the singular values of X𝑋X. This amounts to providing a way to perform a search over a one-dimensional subset of the (d​(d+1)/2−1)𝑑𝑑121(d(d+1)/2-1)-dimensional solution set. Our numerical results suggest there is nothing special about this particular choice of ΛΛ\Lambda (or the corresponding one-dimensional subset being searched over), nor is there a clear rationale for restricting to a one-dimensional subset.
