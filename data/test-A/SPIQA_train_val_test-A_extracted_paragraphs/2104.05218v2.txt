Recent advances in large pretrained language modelsallow us to generate increasingly realistic text by modeling a distribution P‚Äč(X)ūĚĎÉūĚĎčP(X) over natural language sequences XūĚĎčX. The distribution P‚Äč(X)ūĚĎÉūĚĎčP(X) may be truly unconditional, as is common in language modeling, or it may model P‚Äč(X|I)ūĚĎÉconditionalūĚĎčūĚźľP(X|I) conditioned on some input IūĚźľI, as in machine translation or summarization.

We are frequently interested in controlled text generation, the task of generating text conditioned on an additional desirable attribute aūĚĎéa which is not already built into P‚Äč(X)ūĚĎÉūĚĎčP(X). That is, we would like to model P‚Äč(X|a)ūĚĎÉconditionalūĚĎčūĚĎéP(X|a) (or possibly P‚Äč(X|I,a)ūĚĎÉconditionalūĚĎčūĚźľūĚĎéP(X|I,a); henceforth we will drop IūĚźľI from the notation for simplicity). For example, P‚Äč(X)ūĚĎÉūĚĎčP(X) may be a pretrained translation model for Spanish inputs IūĚźľI to English outputs XūĚĎčX, but we may wish to additionally constrain the outputs to possess a new attribute aūĚĎéa, e.g., formality, which we did not optimize for during training.

Unfortunately, once we have already obtained an unconditioned P‚Äč(X)ūĚĎÉūĚĎčP(X) defined as the output distribution of some large generative model ūĚíĘūĚíĘ\mathcal{G}, it is nontrivial to add conditioning on a new attribute aūĚĎéa without either training a new model from scratch or fine-tuning with additional data. Although in principle we can trivially sample from P‚Äč(X|a)ūĚĎÉconditionalūĚĎčūĚĎéP(X|a) via rejection sampling from P‚Äč(X)ūĚĎÉūĚĎčP(X), rejection sampling may be highly inefficient in practice. On the other hand, while generating according to attribute aūĚĎéa, P‚Äč(X)ūĚĎÉūĚĎčP(X) should be left otherwise intact: in the previous translation formality example, it is pointless to generate formal English outputs if they do not preserve the original Spanish meaning.

In light of these concerns, we proposeFuture Discriminators for Generation (Fudge), a flexible and modular methodfor modeling P‚Äč(X|a)ūĚĎÉconditionalūĚĎčūĚĎéP(X|a) which accesses only the output probabilities of the generative model ūĚíĘūĚíĘ\mathcal{G} which defines P‚Äč(X)ūĚĎÉūĚĎčP(X).Fudge learns a binary predictor for whether attribute aūĚĎéa will become true in the complete future, based on an incomplete sequence prefix (Sec. 3). Multiplying the output probabilities of this predictor with ūĚíĘūĚíĘ\mathcal{G}‚Äôs original probabilities and then renormalizing yields a model for the desired P‚Äč(X|a)ūĚĎÉconditionalūĚĎčūĚĎéP(X|a) via Bayes‚Äô Rule.

We run experiments on three controlled text generation tasks ‚ÄĒ couplet completion in poetry, topic control in language generation, and formality change in machine translation ‚ÄĒ showing our method‚Äôs broad applicability. Additionally, we demonstrate the modularity of Fudge by composing multiple attribute constraints in both the couplet and topic control tasks. In our experiments, we find that Fudge is highly effective at attribute control, outperforming both a baseline which directly fine-tunes ūĚíĘūĚíĘ\mathcal{G} and also a strong gradient-based method (Pplm Dathathri et¬†al. (2019)). Our code is available athttps://github.com/yangkevin2/naacl-2021-fudge-controlled-generation.

Ideally, a controlled text generation method should efficiently control for aūĚĎéa while preserving P‚Äč(X)ūĚĎÉūĚĎčP(X) as much as possible. Recent work on controlled text generation has greatly advanced our ability to control for a required attribute aūĚĎéa flexibly and cheaply, with varying degrees of modification to the original model ūĚíĘūĚíĘ\mathcal{G} which defines P‚Äč(X)ūĚĎÉūĚĎčP(X).

One line of work fine-tunes a pretrained model for a desired attribute Ficler and Goldberg (2017); Yu et¬†al. (2017); Ziegler et¬†al. (2019). The result is a class-conditional language model (CCLM).However, it is difficult to isolate the desired attribute from the distribution shift between ūĚíĘūĚíĘ\mathcal{G} and the fine-tuning dataset Hu et¬†al. (2017); John et¬†al. (2018); Lazaridou et¬†al. (2020), i.e., it is nontrivial to preserve the desirable qualities of the P‚Äč(X)ūĚĎÉūĚĎčP(X) modeled by ūĚíĘūĚíĘ\mathcal{G}.One may also need to fine-tune separately for each attribute of interest.Ctrl Keskar et¬†al. (2019) partially addresses these issues by providing 55 attribute control codes for a large language model trained from scratch, although this is expensive.Very recently, GeDi Krause et¬†al. (2020) achieves strong performance by using CCLM generators as discriminators, though it relies on several heuristics. More broadly, text generation models for style transfer Hu et¬†al. (2017); Lample et¬†al. (2018b); Dai et¬†al. (2019a), summarization See et¬†al. (2017); Gehrmann et¬†al. (2018); Zaheer et¬†al. (2020), and machine translation Lample et¬†al. (2018a); Ng et¬†al. A second type of approach instead conditions on a desired attribute by backpropagating gradients, either to directly modify model activations Dathathri et¬†al. (2019); Liu et¬†al. In fact, Subramani et¬†al. (2019) show that by carefully modifying the latent state, one can cause the base ūĚíĘūĚíĘ\mathcal{G} to produce arbitrary outputs.

A third class of methods, referred to as weighted decoding (WD), assumes access only to P‚Äč(X)ūĚĎÉūĚĎčP(X) (i.e., ūĚíĘūĚíĘ\mathcal{G}‚Äôs output logits), and operates directly on these logits Ghazvininejad et¬†al. Compared to other approaches, WD methods are relatively interpretable in how they obtain P‚Äč(X|a)ūĚĎÉconditionalūĚĎčūĚĎéP(X|a) from P‚Äč(X)ūĚĎÉūĚĎčP(X), but prior WD implementations have been observed to perform poorly in controlled text generation See et¬†al. (2019); Dathathri et¬†al. (2019).While Fudge shares a Bayesian motivation with other WD methods, Fudge follows the Bayesian factorization more closely in implementation (Sec. 3).The key distinguishing feature of Fudge is that it models whether attribute aūĚĎéa will be true in the future, rather than in the present. We find that Fudge substantially outperforms previous WD approaches in our experiments (Sec. 4.2).

Even though our GPT2-Medium-generated training dataset is completely different from the test domain, and contains essentially zero examples of correct couplets, Fudge is able to learn the desired attribute. As shown in Table 2, Fudge greatly outperforms all automated baselines in success rate.

Surprisingly, the Pplm baseline achieves zero success. Funnily enough, Fudge even matches Shakespeare according to ‚ĄĪ‚ĄĪ\mathcal{F}, although this is largely due to the narrowness of ‚ĄĪ‚ĄĪ\mathcal{F} and should not be taken seriously.666 We define ‚ĄĪ‚ĄĪ\mathcal{F} using somewhat narrow criteria (Appendix A), which capture only a subset of what Shakespeare considered to be well-written couplets. Of course, Shakespeare is only included as a whimsical point of reference; our generations obviously do not hold a candle to Shakespeare‚Äôs originals.Similarly, the grammaticality and perplexity metrics are designed for our automated baselines, and thus assign poor scores to Shakespeare‚Äôs antiquated and flowery style.

Metrics. Unfortunately, we cannot easily construct a rule-based ‚ĄĪ‚ĄĪ\mathcal{F} for being ‚Äúon-topic.‚ÄĚ Additionally, use rate of words in ūĚí≤ūĚí≤\mathcal{W} is a poor metric, because a model can score highly by e.g., simply returning the words in ūĚí≤ūĚí≤\mathcal{W}, without generalizing to the full topic that ūĚí≤ūĚí≤\mathcal{W} approximates.Instead, we adopt a notion of success which requires the model to generalize the bag ūĚí≤ūĚí≤\mathcal{W} to the full topic. The remaining metrics are measures of quality and diversity.1.Success, the average number of distinct words in a heldout bag ūĚí≤‚Ä≤superscriptūĚí≤‚Ä≤\mathcal{W}^{\prime} which appear in the model output.Specifically, for each word in ūĚí≤ūĚí≤\mathcal{W}, we add to ūĚí≤‚Ä≤superscriptūĚí≤‚Ä≤\mathcal{W}^{\prime} the closest GloVe Pennington et¬†al. Additionally, following the evaluation procedure of prior work such as Dathathri et¬†al. (2019), we run human evaluations via Amazon Mechanical Turk for Fudge against each baseline, comparing topic control and fluency. For each pairwise comparison, we ask 3 workers to evaluate each of 420 paired outputs. Workers were asked to mark which generation is more on topic (first, second, both, or neither), and to rate each generation‚Äôs fluency on a Likert scale from 1 to 5. Finally, we turn to a somewhat more challenging task, changing formality in machine translation ‚ÄĒ specifically, from informal to formal. Given a source sentence written in an informal and conversational style, the goal is to output a translation which is also more formal. We test on the Fisher and CALLHOME Spanish‚ÄďEnglish Speech Translation Corpus Post et¬†al. (2013), a collection of transcribed Spanish conversations with English translations.Both the source Spanish and target English are highly informal and disfluent. Salesky et¬†al. (2019) augment the Fisher dataset with additional parallel English translations, rewritten to be more fluent (and hence more formal); see Table 7 for an example. Our task is to translate the original informal Spanish to into more formal English.However, we assume that Salesky et¬†al. (2019)‚Äôs fluent references are unavailable during training.

In contrast, the ūĚíĘūĚíĘ\mathcal{G} + st baseline achieves near-perfect formality but less than half the BLEU of ūĚíĘūĚíĘ\mathcal{G}, due to the style transfer model overfitting to the GYAFC Entertainment/Music dataset. This is similar to the distribution shift issue that we observed in topic control for Finetune, an issue which Fudge largely avoids. Nevertheless, there remains substantial room for improvement on this difficult task.

Fudge is a principled approach to controlled text generation which models P‚Äč(X|a)ūĚĎÉconditionalūĚĎčūĚĎéP(X|a) by closely following a Bayesian factorization, thus preserving the base P‚Äč(X)ūĚĎÉūĚĎčP(X) as much as possible.Fudge achieves strong performance on a wide range of different tasks: poetry couplet completion, topic control, and informal-to-formal machine translation. Additionally, Fudge can easily compose different attributes in a modular fashion: the meter, rhyme, and end-of-sentence constraints for couplet completion, and the individual words within each topic bag for topic control. In principle, Fudge is applicable to any controlled generation task where we can train discriminators for the desired attribute or attributes.

We recognize that strong controlled generation methods have the potential to produce harmful outputs and/or misinformation when used adversarially Wallace et¬†al. (2019, 2020). However, such methods can also be a powerful tool for mitigating harmful biases learned by large pretrained language models Radford et¬†al. (2019); Brown et¬†al. (2020), for example by detoxifying language Dathathri et¬†al. (2019); Krause et¬†al. (2020). Overall, we believe it is still beneficial to continue research into general controlled text generation methods such as Fudge.
