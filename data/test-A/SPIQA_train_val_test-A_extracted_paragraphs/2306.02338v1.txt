Dense subgraph discovery (DSD) is a fundamental graph-mining primitive, routinely used to mine social, financial, and biological networks among others (Gionis and Tsourakakis, 2015). Applications include team formation (Gajewar and Das Sarma, 2012; Rangapuram et al., 2013), detecting correlated genes (Tsourakakis et al., 2013), community and spam link farm detection in the Web graph (Gibson et al., 2005; Dourisboure et al., 2007), finding experts in crowdsourcing systems (Kawase et al., 2019), spotting money laundering in financial networks (Li et al., 2020; Starnini et al., 2021; Chen and Tsourakakis, 2022), assessing the statistical significance of motifs (Chen et al., 2022), and modeling real-world networks (Chanpuriya et al., 2021). See the tutorial (Gionis and Tsourakakis, 2015) for an extensive list of related applications.

Among various DSD formulations, the densest subgraph problem (DSP) stands out for various reasons (Lanciano et al., 2023). For a given undirected graph G=(V,E)𝐺𝑉𝐸G=(V,E) with n=|V|𝑛𝑉n=|V| nodes and m=|E|𝑚𝐸m=|E| edges, DSP is solvable in polynomial time using maximum flow (Goldberg, 1984; Picard and Queyranne, 1982) or linear programming (LP) (Charikar, 2000) and can be approximated within a factor of 2 using a greedy algorithm (Kortsarz and Peleg, 1994; Charikar, 2000),and more recently solved near-optimally by an iterative greedy algorithm over the input (Chekuri et al., 2022; Boob et al., 2020).Furthermore, DSP is solvable on massive graphs using distributed and streaming implementations (Bahmani et al., 2012)and admits useful variants, e.g., (Kawase and Miyauchi, 2018; Kuroki et al., 2020; Tsourakakis, 2015; Veldt et al., 2021).

In numerous real-world settings, we have extra information about the nodes. For example, in a graph database we may know each individual’s gender and race. On the Twitter follow network, there exist methods to infer from tweets whether a node is positive, neutral, or negative towards a controversial topic (De et al., 2016). On brain networks neurons can play different functional roles (Adami et al., 2011; Matejek et al., 2022).Consider also the problem of organizing a cocktail party (Sozio and Gionis, 2010) with diversity constraints as follows:

A number of computer scientists aim to organize a cocktail party to celebrate Turing’s legacy. They believe that the success of the event will be higher if they invite computer scientists who have collaborated in the past but also who span different research areas. Whom should they invite?

We will refer to the set of different attribute values/types of nodes as colors. The aforementioned settings motivate the problem of finding a densest diverse subgraph, namely a subset of nodes that induce many edges, but also are diverse in terms of colors. We will be referring to the concept of diversity as fairness when the attribute concerns sensitive information such as gender, race, or religion. Applying a DSD method does not guarantee that the extracted densest subgraph will be diverse. Actually on a variety of real data, we observe that this is the typical case, i.e., the densest subgraph often exhibits strong homophily. Suppose the output of such a method is used to select a group of individuals in a social network. In that case, it will not be representative of the different races/religions/opinions that may co-exist in the network. This can be especially harmful in the context of selecting teams using dense subgraphs (Rangapuram et al., 2013), recommending material to social media users that is not balanced in terms of opinions and hence increasing polarization (Musco et al., 2018) or even leading to lawsuits (Graber, 2020). In such cases, it becomes of paramount importance to have algorithms that can extract a cluster with diversity guarantees.

With the exception of a recent paper by Anagnostopoulos et al. (Anagnostopoulos et al., 2020), very little attention has been given to dense diverse subgraph extraction, despite the extensive research on the DSP and its applications (Lanciano et al., 2023). Although Anagnostopoulos et al. (Anagnostopoulos et al., 2020) have made progress, there are still many unanswered questions. For example, the methods they propose exclusively focus on scenarios involving two colors and strive to achieve the complete fairness in the output. Furthermore, their spectral approach offers theoretical guarantees, but these guarantees are contingent upon restrictive conditions for the spectrum of the input graph that are computationally burdensome to verify. Additionally, they offer heuristics for scenarios involving more than two colors, but without any assurances regarding their quality.

We introduce two novel formulations for finding a densest diverse subgraph. The first one is called the densest diverse subgraph problem (DDSP), and the second one is called the densest at-least-k→→𝑘\vec{k}-subgraph problem (Dalk→→𝑘\vec{k}S). Informally, the first problem aims to offer diversity guarantees that concern the relative sizes of the color classes, while the second guarantees in terms of absolute counts.

Let C𝐶C be the set of colors. Our first formulation guarantees the diversity of the output in the sense that it is not dominated by any single color. To this end, the formulation introduces a parameter α∈[1/|C|,1]𝛼1𝐶1\alpha\in[1/|C|,1] that determines the maximum portion of any color in the output solution. It should be noted that our formulation is a substantial generalization of the fair densest subgraph problem introduced by Anagnostopoulos et al. (Anagnostopoulos et al., 2020), enabling us to deal with non-binary attributes and to freely adjust the degree of diversity.Interestingly, from a theory perspective, our formulation contains two important variants of DSP, the densest at-least-k𝑘k-subgraph problem (Dalk𝑘kS) and the densest at-most-k𝑘k-subgraph problem (Damk𝑘kS), as special cases, cf. Sections 2 and 3.1 for details.

On the other hand, our second formulation guarantees the diversity of the output in the sense that it does not overlook any specified color. In particular, consider a graph where |C|𝐶|C| is a very small constant and the minority colors appear only in a handful of nodes. Instead of imposing relative constraints on the sizes through ratios, we impose absolute constraints on the cardinalities of the nodes from each color. Specifically, the formulation requires the output to contain at least a given number of representatives from each color. The formulation is a novel generalization of Dalk𝑘kS, where instead of just demanding k𝑘k nodes in the output, we have a vector 𝒌𝒌\bm{k} of demands from each color, i.e., they are lower bounds on how many nodes we have to include from each possible color.

We evaluate our algorithms on real-life attributed datasets, including social networks with gender and profession attributes. We compare against Anagnostopoulos et al. (Anagnostopoulos et al., 2020), but we also develop a novel baseline that uses node embeddings (Perozzi et al., 2014; Qiu et al., 2018; Chanpuriya et al., 2020) combined with advances in scalable fair clustering of points (Backurs et al., 2019). The algorithms we propose have the capability to extract dense and diverse subgraphs. We demonstrate that real-world networks contain dense subgraphs that exhibit significant homophily, emphasizing the importance of employing our tools in scenarios where diversity or fairness is essential.

DSP and its variations. Given an undirected graph G=(V,E)𝐺𝑉𝐸G=(V,E), we define for any non-empty subset of nodes S⊆V𝑆𝑉S\subseteq V the (degree) density d​(S)=|E​(S)|/|S|𝑑𝑆𝐸𝑆𝑆d(S)=|E(S)|/|S|, where E​(S)={{u,v}∈E∣u,v∈S}𝐸𝑆conditional-set𝑢𝑣𝐸𝑢𝑣𝑆E(S)=\{\{u,v\}\in E\mid u,v\in S\}. The DSP aims to maximize the degree density over all possible subsets. Notice that the degree density is just half of the average degree of an induced subgraph. The DSP is polynomial-time solvable using O​(log⁡n)𝑂𝑛O(\log n) max-flow computations (Goldberg, 1984; Picard and Queyranne, 1982), O​(1)𝑂1O(1) number of flows using parametric maximum flow (Gallo et al., 1989), or LP (Charikar, 2000). There also exists a linear-time 1/2121/2-approximation algorithm that greedily removes a node of the smallest degree, and reports the maximum degree density seen among these subsets (Kortsarz and Nutov, 2005; Charikar, 2000).This kind of algorithm is often called the greedy peeling algorithm.Recently, Boob et al. (Boob et al., 2020) proposed Greedy++, an iterative peeling algorithm that generalizes the above and converges to a near-optimal solution extremely fast without the use of flows. Very recently, Chekuri et al. (Chekuri et al., 2022) analyzed the performance of Greedy++, and showed that its algorithmic idea could be generalized to general fractional supermodular maximization.

DSP has a lot of problem variants (Lanciano et al., 2023).Unlike the original DSP, its size-restricted variants are known to be NP-hard. Indeed, the densest k𝑘k-subgraph problem (Dk𝑘kS) that asks for the densest subgraph with exactly k𝑘k nodes, is not only NP-hard, but also hard to approximate, with the best-known approximation ratio beingΩ​(1/n1/4+ϵ)Ω1superscript𝑛14italic-ϵ\Omega(1/n^{1/4+\epsilon}) for any ϵ>0italic-ϵ0\epsilon>0 (Bhaskara et al., 2010). This approximability result is far off from the best-known hardness result that assumes the Exponential Time Hypothesis (ETH). If ETH holds, then Dk𝑘kS cannot be approximated within a ratio of n1/(log⁡log⁡n)csuperscript𝑛1superscript𝑛𝑐n^{1/(\log\log n)^{c}} for some c>0𝑐0c>0.Another size-restricted variant, Damk𝑘kS, aims to maximize the degree density over all subsets of nodes S𝑆S such that |S|≤k𝑆𝑘|S|\leq k (Andersen and Chellapilla, 2009).It is known that α𝛼\alpha-approximation to Damk𝑘kS leads to α/4𝛼4\alpha/4-approximation to Dk𝑘kS (Khuller and Saha, 2009).

Closest to this work lies Dalk𝑘kS, which imposes the cardinality constraint |S|≥k𝑆𝑘|S|\geq k (Andersen and Chellapilla, 2009). The problem is also known to be NP-hard (Khuller and Saha, 2009). Andersen and Chellapilla (Andersen and Chellapilla, 2009) designed a 1/3131/3-approximation algorithm that runs in linear time, using greedy peeling.Khuller and Saha (Khuller and Saha, 2009) designed two different approximation algorithms, that both achieve 1/2121/2-approximation using either a small number of flows, or by solving an LP (Khuller and Saha, 2009).

Fair densest subgraph problem. Despite the large amount of research on DSD, the problem of finding a densest diverse subgraph has not received attention with the single exception of Anagnostopoulos et al. (Anagnostopoulos et al., 2020) who introduced the fair densest subgraph problem for two colors.Assuming the graph is fair to begin with, i.e., the two colors are equally represented in V𝑉V, they demand equal representation of each category in the output.For this case, they proposed a greedy 1/2121/2-approximation algorithm and a spectral approach. The spectral approach comes with guarantees only in limited cases (e.g., all degrees being almost equal), which are not typical on real data that tend to have a skewed degree distribution. This algorithm can be extended to the case of |C|>2𝐶2|C|>2, but without quality guarantees.Finally, the authors studied the hardness of the problem and showed that their formulation is at least as hard as Damk𝑘kS: Any α𝛼\alpha-approximation to their formulation leads to α𝛼\alpha-approximation to Damk𝑘kS.

Fairness and algorithms. While DSD with diversity is not yet well studied, fair clustering of clouds of points has received much attention from the data mining community. Chierichetti et al. (Chierichetti et al., 2017) initiated the problem of finding balanced clusters in a cloud of points, namely clusters where two groups are equally represented. They proposed a method called fairlet decomposition, that decomposes a dataset into minimal sets that satisfy fair representation, called fairlets. Afterwards, typical machine learning algorithms, like k𝑘k-median, can be used to obtain fair clusters. Backurs et al.  (Backurs et al., 2019) provided a scalable algorithm for the fairlet decomposition. Later work has extended the problem of finding fair clusters to the case of correlation clustering (Ahmadian et al., 2020b) and hierarchical clustering  (Ahmadian et al., 2020a).

Preliminary experiments. Although we have created a reliable algorithmic toolbox for dense diverse clusters, we must assess their practical effectiveness. Specifically, when examining the densest subgraph or the top-k𝑘k densest subgraphs with sensitive attributes, do we observe diversity in the subgraphs? The answer is generally no, as observed in the vast majority of cases. This trend can result in unfair solutions, particularly in the context of sensitive attributes.

In Figure 1 we present the statistics of the entire graph and the top-4 densest subgraphs of the Amazon Product and Facebook100 datasets from Table 1, using standard box plots.As can be seen, the densest subgraphs tend to be not diverse.Especially for the Amazon Product dataset (leftmost figure), the densest subgraph most of the times is nearly monochromatic. This phenomenon is not restricted to the densest subgraph but also found for the top-4 densest subgraphs, implying that strong homophily is a key factor behind dense clusters. We observe a similar trend for the Facebook100 dataset (the latter three figures). Even if we restrict our attention to the specific attribute we consider, profession or gender, we see that again they are not represented equally in the densest subgraph. These findings motivate the study of Problem 1, where we can control the extent to which a specific color dominates the subgraph returned by an algorithm.

In this work, we have focused on the problem of finding a densest diverse subgraph.We proposed novel formulations and approximation algorithms for two different notions of diversity.We performed various experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets, verifying that the densest subgraphs tend to be driven by homophily and that our tools provide the state-of-the-art methods.

Our work makes significant progress towards DSD with diversity and opens up several interesting problems.Can we improve the Ω​(1/n)Ω1𝑛\Omega(1/\sqrt{n})-approximation for Problem 1 (in the case of V𝑉V being diverse)? Can we design a better algorithm for Problem 2, in terms of both the approximation ratio and the runtime?Investigating the hardness of approximation is also an interesting direction.
