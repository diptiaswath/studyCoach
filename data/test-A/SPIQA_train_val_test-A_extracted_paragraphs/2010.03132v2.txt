Conditional generative models provide a natural mechanism to jointly learn a data distribution and optimize predictions. In contrast, discriminative models improve predictions by modeling the label distribution. Learning to model the data distribution allows generating novel samples and is considered a preferred way to understand the real world. Existing conditional generative models have generally been explored in single-modal settings, where a one-to-one mapping between input and output domains exists (Nalisnick etВ al., 2019; Fetaya etВ al., 2020). Here, we investigate continuous multimodal (CMM) spaces for generative modeling, where one-to-many mappings exist between input and output domains. This is critical since many real world situations are inherently multi-modal, e.g., humans can imagine several outcomes for a given occluded image.In a discrete setting, this problem becomes relatively easy to tackle using techniques such as maximum-likelihood-estimation, since the output can be predicted as a vector (Zhang etВ al., 2016), which is not possible in continuous domains. Consequently, generative modeling in CMM spaces remains a challenging task.

To model CMM spaces, a prominent approach in the literature is to use a combination of reconstruction and adversarial losses (Isola etВ al., 2017; Zhang etВ al., 2016; Pathak etВ al., 2016). However, this entails key shortcomings. 1) The goals of adversarial and reconstruction losses are contradictory (Sec.В 4), hence model engineering and numerous regularizers are required to support convergence(Lee etВ al., 2019; Mao etВ al., 2019), thereby resulting in less-generic models tailored for specific applications(Zeng etВ al., 2019; Vitoria etВ al., 2020). 2) The adversarial loss based models are notorious for difficult convergence due to the challenge of finding Nash equilibrium of a non-convex min-max game in high-dimensions (Barnett, 2018; Chu etВ al., 2020; Kodali etВ al., 2017). 3) The convergence is heavily dependent on the architecture, hence such models show lack of scalability (Thanh-Tung etВ al., 2019; Arora & Zhang, 2017). 4) The promise of assisting downstream tasks remains challenging, with a large gap in performance between the generative modelling approaches and their discriminative counterparts (Grathwohl etВ al., 2020; Jing & Tian, 2020).

In this work, we propose a general-purpose framework for modeling CMM spaces using a set of domain-agnostic regression cost functions instead of the adversarial loss. This improves both the stability and eliminates the incompatibility between the adversarial and reconstruction losses, allowing more precise outputs while maintaining diversity. The underlying notion is to learn the вҖҳbehaviour of the latent variablesвҖҷ in minimizing these cost functions while converging to an optimum mode during the training phase, and mimicking the same at inference.Despite being a novel direction, the proposed framework showcases promising attributes by: (a) achieving state-of-the-art results on a diverse set of tasks using a generic model, implyinggeneralizability, (b) rapid convergence to optimal modes despite architectural changes, (c) learning useful features for downstream tasks, and (d) producing diverse outputs via traversal through multiple output modes at inference.

Generalizability: The incompatibility of above mentioned loss functions demands domain specific design choices from models that target high realism in CMM settings. This hinders the generalizability across different tasks (Vitoria etВ al., 2020; Zeng etВ al., 2019). We further argue that due to this discrepancy, cGANs learn sub-optimal features which are less useful for downstream tasks (Sec.В 5.3).

Convergence and the sensitivity to the architecture: The difficulty of converging GANs to the Nash equilibrium of a non-convex min-max game in high-dimensional spaces is well explored (Barnett, 2018; Chu etВ al., 2020; Kodali etВ al., 2017). Goodfellow etВ al. (2014b) underlines if the discriminator has enough capacity, and is optimal at every step of the GAN algorithm, then the generated distribution converges to the real distribution; that cannot be guaranteed in a practical scenario. In fact, Arora etВ al. (2018) confirmed that the adversarial objective can easily approach to an equilibrium even if the generated distribution has very low support, and further, the number of training samples required to avoid mode collapse can be in order of expвҒЎ(d)рқ‘‘\exp(d) (dрқ‘‘d is the data dimension).

Multimodality:The ability to generate diverse outputs, i.e., convergence to multiple modes in the output space, is an important requirement. Despite the typical noise input, cGANs generally lack the ability to generate diverse outputs (Lee etВ al., 2019). Pathak etВ al. (2016) and Iizuka etВ al. (2016) even state that better results are obtained when the noise is completely removed. Further, variants of cGAN that target diversity often face a trafe-off between the realism and diversity (He etВ al., 2018), as they have to compromise between the reconstruction and adversarial losses.

Spectral moments of 3D objects provide a compact representation, and help building light-weight networks (Ramasinghe etВ al., 2020, 2019b; Cohen etВ al., 2018; Esteves etВ al., 2018).However, spectral information of 3D objects has not been used before for self-supervised learning, a key reason being the difficulty of learning representations in the spectral domain due to the complex structure and unbounded spectral coefficients. Here, we present an efficient pretext task that is conducted in the spectral domain: denoising 3D spectral maps. We use two types of spectral spaces: spherical harmonics and Zernike polynomials (App. 4). We first convert the 3D point clouds to spherical harmonic coefficients, arrange the values as a 2D map, and mask or add noise to a map portion (App.В 3.12). The goal is to recover the original spectral map. Fig.В 18 and Table 6 depicts our qualitative and quantitative results. We perform favorably well against other methods. To evaluate the learned features, we use Zernike polynomials, as they are more discriminative compared to spherical harmonics (Ramasinghe etВ al., 2019a). We first train the network on the 55k ShapeNet objects by denoising spectral maps, and then apply the trained network on the ModelNet10 & 40. The features are then extracted from the bottleneck (similar to Sec.В 5.3), and fed to a FC classifier (Table 4). We achieve the state-of-the-art results in ModelNet40 with a simple pretext task.

Conditional generation in multimodal domains is a challenging task due to its ill-posed nature. In this paper, we propose a novel generative framework that minimize a family of cost functions during training. Further, it observes the convergence patterns of latent variables and applies this knowledge during inference to traverse to multiple output modes during inference. Despite using a simple and generic architecture, we show impressive results on a diverse set of tasks. The proposed approach demonstrates faster convergence, scalability, generalizability, diversity and superior representation learning capability for downstream tasks.

Although heavily used in the literature, per pixel metrics such as PSNR does not effectively capture the perceptual quality of an image. To overcome this shortcoming, more perceptually motivated metrics havebeen proposed such as SSIM Wang etВ al. (2004), MSSIM Wang etВ al. (2003), and FSIM Zhang etВ al. (2011). However the similarity of two images islargely context dependant, and may not be captured by the aforementioned metrics. As a solution, recently, two deep feature based perceptual metricsвҖ“LPIP Zhang etВ al. (2018) and PieAPP Prashnani etВ al. (2018)вҖ“were proposed, which coincide well with the human judgement. The color distribution of a natural dataset in aрқ‘Һa and bрқ‘Ҹb planes (LAB space) are strongly biased towards low values. To tackle this problem in the continuous domain, we push the output color distribution towards a uniform distribution as explained in Sec.В 5.2 in the main paper.

Evaluation of synthesized images is an open problem (Salimans etВ al., 2016). In the Turing test, we show the users a series of paired images, ground truth and our predictions, and ask the users to pick the most realistic image. Here, following Zhang etВ al. In this experiment, we use two types of spectral moments: spherical harmonics and Zernike polynomials (see App. 4). The minimum number of sample points required to accurately represent a finite energy function in a particular function space depends on the used sampling theorem. According to Driscoll and HealyвҖҷs theorem Driscoll & Healy (1994), 4вҖӢN24superscriptрқ‘Ғ24N^{2} equiangular sampled points are needed to represent a function on рқ•Ҡ2superscriptрқ•Ҡ2\mathbb{S}^{2} using spherical moments at a maximum degree Nрқ‘ҒN. Therefore, we compute the first 163841638416384 spherical moments of 3D objects where lвүӨ128рқ‘ҷ128l\leq 128 by sampling 256Г—256256256256\times 256 equiangular points in Оёрқңғ\theta and П•italic-П•\phi directions, where 0вүӨОёвүӨПҖ0рқңғрқңӢ0\leq\theta\leq\pi and 0вүӨП•вүӨ2вҖӢПҖ0italic-П•2рқңӢ0\leq\phi\leq 2\pi. Afterwards, we arrange the spherical moments as a 128Г—128128128128\times 128 feature map, and convolve with a 2Г—2222\times 2 kernel with stride size 222 to downsample the feature map to 64Г—64646464\times 64 size. The output is then fed to 646464-size architecture. We add Gaussian noise and mask portions of the spectral map to corrupt it. Afterwards, the model is trained to de-noise the input.

For Zernike polynomials, we compute the first 100100100 moments for each 3D object where nвүӨ9рқ‘ӣ9n\leq 9, and arrange the moments as a 10Г—10101010\times 10 feature map. Then, the feature map is upsampled using transposed convolution by using a 5Г—5555\times 5 kernel and with a stride size 333. The upsamapled feature map is fed to a 323232-size network and trained end-to-end to denoise. We first train the network on 55k objects in ShapeNet, and then apply the trained network on the Modelnet10 and Modelnet40 to extract the bottleneck features. These features are then fed to a single fully connected layer for classification.
