Variational inequalities have a rather long history of research. Their first applications were the equilibrium problems in economics and game theory [50, 25, 22]. Even then it was clear that variational inequalities were not just a separate broad class of interesting problems, but a universal paradigm involving other problems that are no less attractive than variational inequalities. Here one can distinguish minimization, saddle point, fixed point problems and others. Whereas research for minimization was actively developed separately, research for saddle point problems was very often coupled with the development of science around variational inequalities. This trend continues even now. This is partly elated to the fact that the methods for minimization problems are not always suitable for saddles and variational inequalities. Moreover, from the theoretical point of view these methods give rather poor or even zero guarantees of convergence. Therefore a separate branch of algorithms has developed for variational inequalities and saddle problems. Such methods include, e.g. Extra Gradient [36, 49, 31], Tseng’s method [60] and many others [13].

Over time, variational inequalities found new applications, e.g. in signal processing [54]. In the last decade, they also attracted the interest of the machine learning community. Nowadays, variational inequalities and saddle point problems are used in various aspects of machine learning. For example, there are many applications for the already classic learning tasksin supervised learning (with non-separable loss [29]; with non-separable regularizer [6]), unsupervised learning (discriminative clustering [64]; matrix factorization [7]), image denoising [21, 16], robust optimization [9]. But there are also new links with modern areas of learning science. Here we can highlight applications in reinforcement learning [51, 28], adversarial training [41], and GANs [24, 19, 23, 45, 18, 39, 52].

The rapid development of machine learning opens up more and more possibilities for consumers. But it poses a huge number of new challenges for engineers. To meet the demands of modern society, the performance and generalization capacity of the model need to be improved. At the moment, this issue is addressed by increasing the size of both the training data and the models [5]. It is difficult to imagine effective handling of these kinds of workloads without the various advances in hardware acceleration, but above all without distributed algorithm design [61]. Often, the distributed nature of the problem occurs not only due to a desire for speeding up the learning process via parallelization, but because of the severe external constraints, e.g. as in federated learning [35, 38]. Federated learning is a pioneering area of distributed learning in which the computing machines are the personal devices of the users. The primary issue here is privacy in the form of prohibiting the data transfer from the local devices, it means that any approach other than distributed is not suitable.

Meanwhile, for most distributed algorithms, the bottleneck is communications. They are a waste of time, which slows down the training/optimization process and prevents effective parallelization. There are different approaches for both reducing the number of communication rounds and the cost per round.The first popular approach is compression of transmitted information [2]; it helps to increase the speed of the communication round without much loss in the quality of learning. The second approach is local steps [42]; by increasing the number of local computations it is sometimes possible to significantly reduce the number of communication rounds. Finally, the third basic approach is to use data similarity [55]. This approach often employes the help of local steps. With the similarity of the data, we can assume that all local loss functions on the devices are similar, it means that the local computations are in some sense close and replaceable. Each approach and even combinations of the two approaches have been quite extensively researched, especially for minimizations. But synergy of all three pillars of distributed algorithms are not presented neither for minimization problems, nor for more general variational inequalities. Therefore, the purpose of this paper is to answer the following questions:

Is it possible to combine the three main techniques for dealing with the communication bottleneck into one algorithm? Does the new method have better convergence in both theory and practice?

Techniques for effective communications in distributed algorithms is a hot direction in recent years. Therefore, the literature contains many results on this topic. Most of the works are devoted to minimization problems. For saddles and variational inequalities, there are also methods that attempt to solve the communication bottleneck problem. We summarize these approaches in Table 1.

∙∙\bullet Local methods. The use of local steps is probably the most popular approach to the communication bottleneck. Methods with local updates are also widely used in general distributed and federated learning. The idea of distributed minimization using methods similar to Local SGD is not an achievement of recent years and comes back to [42, 43, 66]. The development of theoretical analysis and new modifications for the original methods can be traced in the following works: [40, 56, 33, 32, 63, 62, 34]. [47]With the appearance of [47], local methods for distributed minimization problems get a new boost.

∙∙\bullet Compression. Methods with compression also have a good background. Here we can highlight the basic methods that add compression to GD or SGD [2], modifications of this approach using memory [46]. Also of interest are works investigating biased compression operators [12] and error-compaction techniques for them [3, 57, 53]. For variational inequalities and saddle problems, methods with different compressions were proposed in [14]. These algorithms are based on the variance reduction method. See also [53] and [14] for a more detailed survey of compressed methods for distributed minimization problems.

∙∙\bullet Similarity. In the literature one can find different assumptions about the similarity of local functions. In this paper, we focus on the similarity of hessians (See Assumption 4.3). This assumption is the most natural one and is confirmed by both theory and practice. The first method for distributed minimization problems with data similarity was DANE [55]. After one year the lower bounds were obtained in [4]. For quadratic problems, the optimal method was obtained using a modification of the DANE approach [65]. In the general case, however, the optimal method was obtained much later [37]. For details and a complete picture of the methods for the distributed minimization problems with similarity, see [37] and [26]. The saddle point problems were also considered under similarity assumption. In paper [15], both the lower bounds and the optimal method were obtained. In the current work, we "break down" these lower estimates by using compression (see Table 1 with footnote 1). Note that most methods for the distributed problem with similarity also use the technique of local steps.

∙∙\bullet Local methods with compression. The combination of local updates and compression for minimization problems has been presented for example in the following papers: [8, 48]. However, the theoretical guarantees obtained for these methods are far from optimal, moreover, they use rather restrictive assumptions. For variational inequalities and saddle point problems there are no methods with both local steps and compression. The first local methods for variational inequalities and saddle point problems were simply adaptations of local algorithms for the minimization problem. In particular, the Local SGD/FedAvg method was adapted in [20, 27]. [27] also analysed the Scaffold method [32] for saddle point problems. In the paper [10], the Extra Gradient method, a key method for saddles and variational inequalities, was modified by the us of local steps.

∙∙\bullet Compression and similarity. The first attempt to use compression for the distributed minimization problem under the data similarity assumption has only recently appeared [58]. The authors introduced a new class of compressions, namely permutation compressors. But due to the fact that the authors did not use local steps technique, the gain for the combination of the two approaches turned out to be slight. For variational inequalities the close results was presented in [11], but with the use new basic methoddifferent from [58].

We answer in the affirmative to the questions posed at the end of Section 1. In particular, our contributions are as follows.

∙∙\bullet Three Pillars Algorithm. We present a new method Three Pillars Algorithm (Algorithm 1) that combines three approaches: compression, similarity and local steps for the efficient solution of distributed variational inequalities and saddle point problems.

∙∙\bullet The best communication complexity. We analyze the convergence of the new distributed algorithm for smooth strongly monotone variational inequalities and strongly convex-strongly concave saddle point problems under similarity assumption. As can be seen in Table 1, our algorithm has better communication complexity than all available competitors.

∙∙\bullet Main calculations on server. Many distributed approaches, such as one of the most popular from [44], use the power of all computing devices evenly. But in terms of federated learning, we prefer to have the main computational workload on the server, rather than on the local devices of the users. Our algorithm has this feature.

∙∙\bullet Extension with partial participation. We also present a modification of our first method – Algorithm 2, where instead of compression, partial participation of devices is used. The essence of the replacement is that now not all devices send compressed information, but one specifically selected node sends an uncompressed package. In this case, the local complexity on the devices is even lower than in Algorithm 1, while the overall communication complexity remains the same as for Algorithm 1.

∙∙\bullet Experimental confirmation. Experimental results on adversarial training of regression models confirm the theoretical conclusion that both of our algorithms outperform their competitors.

The essence of this approach is that all compressors are related to each other. If each device independently selects a random set of coordinates from its gradient for forwarding, it could very likely happen that the final averaged "gradient" on the server would have zero coordinates. This is due to the fact that because of their independence nodes could send the same coordinate many times, and some coordinate could not be sent at all. The permutation compressors fix this issue by ensuring that all devices send different coordinates. Then the final averaged package on the server does not have any zero coordinates. Additionally, since all the local gradients on the devices are in some sense similar, then "the gradient" on the server is very close to the real gradient if no compression are applied. It is this type of compressors that allows us to take into account the similarity property.The authors of the original paper [58] noted that their proposed compressor helps in the case of data similarity, but did not derive maximum benefit from it, since they considered the method without local steps and in the non-convex setting.

Despite the fact that the estimates from Corollary 5.3 and 5.5 are the same. Algorithm 2 is better than Algorithm 1 in terms of local devices (but not the server) complexity. In this paper we presented two new algorithms for the efficient solving of distributed variational inequalities and saddle point problems. Algorithm 1 combines compression, similarity and local steps techniques. Algorithm 2 uses partial participation instead of compression. Both Algorithms have the best communication complexity, outperforms existing methods in theory as well as in practice.
