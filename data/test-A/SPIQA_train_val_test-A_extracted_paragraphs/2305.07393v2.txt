Existing powerful dialogue models (Zhang et al., 2020; Adiwardana et al., 2020; Roller et al., 2021; Thoppilan et al., 2022) have been typically pre-trained on a significant number of English dialogue sessions extracted from either social media (e.g. Reddit and Twitter) or web documents. However, dialogue systems for many other languages have long been under-explored. An intuitive approach to building dialogue systems in other languages is to train from scratch on large-scale dialogue data of a specific language (e.g. Chinese (Bao et al., 2020, 2021a, 2021b; Wang et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2021; Gu et al., 2023) and Japanese (Sugiyama et al., 2021)), but it consumes considerable computing resources for each language, which is not eco-friendly. In addition, it is unlikely to collect massive dialogue sessions of some languages due to the data scarcity and/or scatteredness problem (Arora et al., 2022). Another line of investigation has focused on adaptation from pre-trained English models (Adewumi et al., 2021, 2022). Such adaptation, however, is far from satisfactory given significant vocabulary mismatch.

To address the problem of data unavailability, some prior work has focused on training a model on the dialogue sessions of another language translated from English (Naous et al., 2020), but it involves much human efforts in post-editing. Others have utilized pre-trained language model (PLM) for the English language with the help of machine translation (MT), but MT pipeline is somewhat limited by translation quality, system turnaround, budget for APIs, and others (Lin et al., 2021). Another approach is to initialize a dialogue model with the learned parameters from off-the-shelf PLM for the same language (e.g. Arabic PLM (Antoun et al., 2020)) and then fine-tuning on a small-scale dialogue data (Naous et al., 2021). Unfortunately, PLM is not available for most languages due to the shortage of data and computing resources. Recent work has explored multilingual dialogue generation by fine-tuning mPLM (Conneau et al., 2020; Chi et al., 2020; Xue et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2020) on dialogue data of multiple languages simultaneously, but the performance varies among different languages (Zhang et al., 2022).

While mPLM has shown promise in enabling cross-lingual transfer for generation tasks, zero-shot cross-lingual transfer (ZS-XLT) with mPLM suffers much from catastrophic forgetting, where mPLM that has been fine-tuned on the source language is unable to generate fluent sentences in the target language when being evaluated on it (Lauscher et al., 2020; Xue et al., 2021; Maurya et al., 2021; Vu et al., 2022). In this work, we would like to investigate if catastrophic forgetting occurs in two other cross-lingual scenarios namely FS-XLT and MTL in the context of dialogue generation given the availability of just a few data examples in target language. We observed catastrophic forgetting in our preliminary experiments of both scenarios. To mitigate the issue, we propose a prompt learning method that preserves the multilinguality of mPLM during fine-tuning with the help of hand-crafted prompts where we cast dialogue generation into the pre-training tasks of mPLM (e.g. span-corruption in mT5 (Xue et al., 2021)). Experimental results on all 6 languages demonstrate our approach effectively mitigates the issue in both scenarios, thereby improving the overall generation quality.

Our main contributions are summarized in the following.

•To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to investigate catastrophic forgetting in the context of FS-XLT and MTL for open-domain dialogue generation, which sheds light on the study of non-English dialogue generation in the future.•We propose a simple yet effective prompt learning method that preserves multilinguality of mPLM by bridging the gap between pre-training and fine-tuning to mitigate the issue, providing insights into cross-lingual transfer for generation.•Experimentation with the Fixed-prompt LM Tuning approach and our hand-crafted task-specific prompts on 6 languages.

Existing work on English dialogue generation has mostly been done by training from scratch on massive English dialogues with the above objective (Zhang et al., 2020; Adiwardana et al., 2020; Roller et al., 2021; Thoppilan et al., 2022), but that is not the case for most non-English languages due to a few factors introduced in prior sections. In this work, we approach to dialogue generation for non-English languages by exploring two cross-lingual transfer scenarios including FS-XLT and MTL with the help of mPLM (e.g. mT5 (Xue et al., 2021)) where we assume the availability of just a few data examples of those non-English languages.

FS-XLT with the help of mPLM (Lauscher et al., 2020; Hedderich et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2021) is a promising approach to many natural language processing (NLP) tasks for resource-lean languages where we assume the access to sufficient training data of source language but just a few data examples (i.e. few-shot) of target language. Generally, we have two stages in FS-XLT:

•Source-training: mPLM is fine-tuned on the full training data of the source language (e.g. English);•Target-adapting: the source-trained model is further fine-tuned on the few-shot examples of the target language.

MTL is an effective inductive transfer approach that improves generalization by jointly learning one or more auxiliary tasks together with target task (Caruana, 1997; Luong et al., 2016; Changpinyo et al., 2018). In this work, we focus on pairwise MTL where there is only one auxiliary task trained together with target task as it works better when target dataset is smaller than auxiliary dataset (Weller et al., 2022). When it comes to dialogue generation, we have auxiliary language(s) and target language as auxiliary task(s) and target task, respectively. Specifically, we have three stages here:

•Data interleaving: we evenly interleave the full training data of auxiliary language with few-shot examples of target language, with one data example of target language inserted for every Na​u​x/Nt​g​tsubscript𝑁𝑎𝑢𝑥subscript𝑁𝑡𝑔𝑡N_{aux}/N_{tgt} data examples of auxiliary language where Na​u​xsubscript𝑁𝑎𝑢𝑥N_{aux} and Nt​g​tsubscript𝑁𝑡𝑔𝑡N_{tgt} denote the number of data examples in the training set of auxiliary language and the number of few-shot data examples of the target language, respectively;•Multitask training: mPLM is fine-tuned on the interleaved data that contains context-response pairs of both languages;•Target evaluation: the fine-tuned mPLM is evaluated on the test set of target language.

As is discussed in prior sections, zero-shot cross-lingual generation with mPLM (Lauscher et al., 2020; Maurya et al., 2021) suffers much from catastrophic forgetting (Xue et al., 2021; Vu et al., 2022). Prior work assumes it is because mPLM has never been exposed to sentences of any other languages especially the target language during its fine-tuning on supervised data of source language (Xue et al., 2021). To mitigate the issue, they mix a small amount of unsupervised data that covers either all the languages used in pre-training (Xue et al., 2021) or the target language only (Vu et al., 2022) into its fine-tuning on source language.

Different from previous work, we explore whether catastrophic forgetting occurs in two other cross-lingual transfer scenarios (i.e. FS-XLT and MTL) in dialogue generation task given the availability of just a few data examples of the target language. In our preliminary experiments, we observed the issue in both FS-XLT and MTL, which is not surprising as we assume the availability of only 10 supervised data examples of each target language in both target-adapting of FS-XLT and multitask training of MTL. Our intuition here is that the unsupervised pre-training tasks of multiple languages which essentially bring multilinguality to mPLM during the pre-training stage are not presented in the fine-tuning stage (i.e. FS-XLT or MTL), making it difficult for mPLM to maintain their multilinguality.

Inspired by the prompt learning approaches with PLM for the English language (Liu et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2021; Schick and Schütze, 2021c, a; Yuan et al., 2021; Madotto et al., 2021; Kasahara et al., 2022), we propose to preserve the multilinguality of mPLM by bridging the gap between the unsupervised pre-training tasks and downstream tasks (e.g. dialogue generation) in the fine-tuning stage (i.e. FS-XLT and MTL). Specifically, we cast dialogue generation into the pre-training tasks of mPLM to bridge the gap, thereby maintaining the multilinguality of mPLM during fine-tuning and mitigating catastrophic forgetting. While there are a few mPLMs available in the literature (Xue et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2020; Chi et al., 2021), in this work we experiment with mT5 (Xue et al., 2021) only as we aim to shed light on the study of non-English dialogue generation, instead of establishing strong baselines or state-of-the-art performances.

Automatic evaluation. We use SacreBLEU222https://github.com/mjpost/sacreBLEU (Post, 2018) to measure the word overlap between predicted responses and reference responses as it produces shareable, comparable, and reproducible BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) scores. In addition, we use Distinct N-grams (Li et al., 2016) to measure the diversity of predicted responses. Human Evaluation. We conduct human evaluations on Prolific333https://www.prolific.co/ to further verify the effectiveness of our proposed method. We recruit one annotator for each language given the limited number of native speakers of some languages available. Systems are paired with each other based on whether they are under the same setting (e.g. FS-XLT is paired with FS-XLTpmpt). We randomly pick 100 samples from the test set of each language. For each sample, given the context, a native speaker of the corresponding language is asked to indicate the response they believe is better than the other in terms of Fluency which pertains to the overall grammatical correctness and fluidity of a response. In cases where it is difficult to determine which response is better, annotators can indicate “Neutral”.

In this work, we investigated the problem of catastrophic forgetting occurred in FS-XLT and MTL in the context of dialogue generation for non-English languages. We proposed a prompt learning method that preserves the multilinguality of mPLM during fine-tuning to mitigate the issue. Results on all 6 languages demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed approach. We will explore whether the proposed approach mitigates catastrophic forgetting in FS-XLT and MTL for other languages in our future work. In addition, we would like to investigate how much the model performance varies when using different task prompts in the future. It is also interesting to explore soft/trainable prompts for dialogue generation.
