Measuring the centrality of nodes is one of the fundamental operations in network analysis and has various applications (Das et al., 2018; Saxena and Iyengar, 2020).The H-index was originally proposed by J. E. Hirsch (Hirsch, 2005) for measuring the productivity and impact of scientists.It is defined as the maximum value hℎh such that at least hℎh of the scientist’s publications have been cited at least hℎh times.The corresponding centrality measure for static networks defines the H-index of a node as the maximum value hℎh, such that at least hℎh of the node’s neighbors have a degree of at least hℎh.Recent work has shown that the index yields a successful heuristic for finding super-spreaders in static networks (Lü et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2018; Gao et al., 2019).Recently, Lü et al. (2016) formally linked the established concepts of degree centrality, H-index, and the coreness of a node by introducing the n𝑛n-th order H-index.The n𝑛n-th order H-index is defined recursively using the ℋℋ\mathcal{H} operator, which, given a multiset S𝑆S of integers, returns the maximum integer i𝑖i such that at least i𝑖i elements in S𝑆S are not smaller than i𝑖i.So far, the (n𝑛n-th order) H-index has been applied primarily to static networks.

However, recently there has been an increasing focus on dynamically changing networks (Holme, 2015; Michail, 2016; Latapy et al., 2018).This trend is motivated by the fact that real-world processes are often inherently dynamic, implying possible causalities that must be taken into account to obtain valid interpretations (Holme, 2015).Figure 1(a) shows an example of a small temporal network representing a communication network. The nodes represent persons, and each edge is a bidirectional communication at a specific time shown at the edge.Figure 1(c) shows the evolution of the network over time. There is a static graph containing the edges with the corresponding time stamp for each time point.Note how temporal evolution restricts the possible flow of information.For example, person a𝑎a communicates with b𝑏b at time two and b𝑏b with c𝑐c at time four, there is a possibility that information flows from a𝑎a to c𝑐c via b𝑏b but not in the reverse direction due to the chronological order of communications—all communications between b𝑏b and c𝑐c are after the communications between a𝑎a and b𝑏b.If we only consider the underlying aggregated graph, in which the time stamps are removed, and resulting multi-edges are merged (see Figure 1(b)), there are no restrictions on the possible flow of information.Therefore, in this work, we focus on analyzing temporal networks consisting of a set of nodes and a set of temporal edges endowed with time stamps indicating their time of existence.Such temporal networks are prime models of real-world interactions like human contact, communication, and social networks (Candia et al., 2008; Eckmann et al., 2004; Holme et al., 2004; Ciaperoni et al., 2020; Chaintreau et al., 2007).

Current work: We generalize the n𝑛n-th order H-index for temporal networks.First, we introduce the time-dependent in- and outdegrees of a node v𝑣v at time t𝑡t, counting the number of incoming or outgoing edges before or after t𝑡t.On this basis, we define the n𝑛n-th order temporal H-index, respecting temporal reachability and possible implied causality.We obtain a family of (1) temporal centrality measures and (2) corresponding core-like temporal decompositions.

The centrality measures based on the n𝑛n-th order temporal H-index capture the importance of a node by considering its spatial and temporal position in a neighborhood of depth n𝑛n in the network.We propose two variants—the first measures a node’s ability to influence or distribute information, and the second measures how well a node can be influenced or obtain information.The 1-st order temporal H-index corresponds to the classic static variant with the critical difference that the temporal restrictions in terms of possible incoming or outgoing information flows are respected.For example, in Figure 1(a), node c𝑐c has four outgoing edges at time t=1𝑡1t=1. Each of the four neighbors also has four outgoing edges at times t>1𝑡1t>1. Hence, any information leaving node c𝑐c over one of the four edges at the time one can be further distributed via four edges. Therefore, the 111-st order outward temporal H-index is four.With similar arguments, we see that node b,c𝑏𝑐b,c, and e𝑒e have a 111-st order outward temporal H-index of two (e.g., b𝑏b has two outgoing edges to e𝑒e at times four and five, and e𝑒e has two outgoing edges later than five). Finally, node d𝑑d has a value of one.However, suppose we ignore the temporal information and calculate the conventional static H-index in the underlying aggregated static graph shown in Figure 1(b). In that case, all nodes obtain the (traditional) H-index value of three, which is uninformative.Furthermore, if we compute the 111-st order inward temporal H-index Figure 1(a), we obtain a different ranking of the nodes inwhich a𝑎a has the smallest index-value and d𝑑d the highest. Note that for the static H-index, there is no such distinction in undirected networks.Going from the 111-st order to higher orders, we can include a deeper neighborhood into the index computation and can identify nodes thathave a strong influence in large parts of the network (or can be influenced by large parts of the network in the inward case).

Moreover, a recursive application of the temporal H-index leads to a core-like decomposition of the network.Core decompositions are an essential concept in the study of graph properties with many important applications, e.g., (social) network analysis, community detection, or network visualization (Kong et al., 2019; Malliaros et al., 2020).We define the (n,k)𝑛𝑘(n,k)-pseudocore in a temporal network 𝒢𝒢\mathcal{G} as the maximal induced temporal subgraph such that each node has an n𝑛n-th order temporal H-index of at least k𝑘k with respect to 𝒢𝒢\mathcal{G}.We again propose two variants based on the in- and outward temporal H-index.Our evaluation shows that our n𝑛n-th order temporal H-index leads to pseudocores characterized by high temporal reachability.

Our contributions are:(1)We introduce the n𝑛n-th order temporal H-index measuring the importance of nodes based on the structure of their neighborhood respecting temporal reachability.We propose two variants—the first measures a node’s ability to influence or distribute information, and the second measures how well a node can be influenced or obtain information.(2)Based on the n𝑛n-th order temporal H-index, we introduce a corresponding decomposition of the network into (n,k)𝑛𝑘(n,k)-pseudocores, describing components with high communication capabilities.(3)The n𝑛n-th order temporal H-index can be straightforwardly computed by directly implementing its recursive formulation.However, this approach is not scalable, and we propose a highly scalable streaming algorithm operating on the chronologically ordered edge stream using only a single pass over the edges.(4)Our evaluation on real-world temporal networks shows that our algorithms are highly efficient even for networks with millions of nodes and tens of millions of temporal edges.The n𝑛n-th order temporal H-index effectively identifies central nodes and temporally cohesive subgraphs.We demonstrate this in the use case of identifying super-spreaders in epidemic processes in real-world networks.

There are several thorough surveys and introductions to temporal networks, e.g., (Holme, 2015; Latapy et al., 2018; Santoro et al., 2011; Michail, 2016), centrality measures, e.g., (Das et al., 2018; Landherr et al., 2010; Rodrigues, 2019; Saxena and Iyengar, 2020), and core decompositions (Kong et al., 2019; Malliaros et al., 2020).We focus our discussion on techniques for temporal networks closely related to our work.

Several works (Nicosia et al., 2013; Kim and Anderson, 2012; Tang et al., 2013, 2010) examine properties of temporal networks, including various temporal centrality measures, and discuss the necessity of considering temporal dynamics.Variants of temporal closeness have been introduced and discussed in (Pan and Saramäki, 2011; Magnien and Tarissan, 2015; Santoro et al., 2011; Oettershagen and Mutzel, 2020; Crescenzi et al., 2020).Similar to static closeness, the temporal closeness is often defined as the inverse of the sum of optimal temporal distances.Another path-based centrality measure is betweenness.Buß et al. (2020) evaluate the theoretical complexity and practical hardness of computing several variants of temporal betweenness centrality.Santoro and Sarpe (2022) discuss the estimation of temporal betweenness.Tsalouchidou et al. (2019) extend Brandes’ algorithm (Brandes, 2001) for distributed computation of betweenness centrality in temporal networks.Temporal closeness and betweenness only consider optimal temporal paths. Our new centralities, in contrast, consider the general temporal reachability of the node itself and its neighborhood, which is more meaningful in settings where information does not necessarily spread along the shortest paths.The Katz centrality (Katz, 1953) measures node importance in terms of the weighted random walks starting (or arriving) at a node.The authors of (Béres et al., 2018; Grindrod et al., 2011) adapt the walk-based Katz centrality to temporal networks.Rozenshtein and Gionis (2016) adapt PageRank for temporal networks by using temporal walks.Oettershagen et al. (2022) generalized the random walk betweenness for temporal networks.These centrality measures are based on counting temporal walks, while our n𝑛n-th order temporal H-index is based oncounting neighbors with high temporal reachability through n𝑛n recursion steps.Recent works try to identify influential nodes in spreading processes under the influence maximization model for temporal networks (Erkol et al., 2020, 2022).

Wu et al. (2015) proposed (k,h)𝑘ℎ(k,h)-core decompositions for temporal networks, where each node in a (k,h)𝑘ℎ(k,h)-core has at least k𝑘k neighbors and at least hℎh temporal edges to each neighbor. The concept can be interpreted as a weighted static core decomposition and does not take temporal dynamics such as reachability into account.A recent work by Galimberti et al. (2020) introduced a notion of temporal span-cores where each core is associated with a time span, i.e., a time interval, for which the coreness property holds.Let T𝑇T be the length of the time interval spanned by a temporal graph 𝒢𝒢\mathcal{G} with edge set ℰℰ\mathcal{E}, then there is a quadratic number of time intervals for which a temporal span-core can exist, and the asymptotic running times of the proposed algorithms are in 𝒪​(T2⋅|ℰ|)𝒪⋅superscript𝑇2ℰ\mathcal{O}(T^{2}\cdot|\mathcal{E}|) which is prohibitive for large networks.A notable difference to our new approach is that the span-cores decompose a temporal network in its temporal domain such that each vertex may have several distinct span-core values in different time intervals. Whereas our decomposition, similar to the conventional k𝑘k-core, leads to a single core number for each vertex.

Core-like concepts are also used in temporal community mining.Hung and Tseng (2021) define a temporal community as a (l,k)𝑙𝑘(l,k)-lasting core, which is a k𝑘k-core existing for at least l𝑙l consecutive time steps and discuss the problem of finding the maximum (l,k)𝑙𝑘(l,k)-lasting core.Qin et al. (2019) consider the densest subgraph problem and define a temporal community as an (l,δ)𝑙𝛿(l,\delta)-maximal dense core which has an average degree of at least δ𝛿\delta in a time interval of length at least l𝑙l.Qin et al. (2020) mine stable communities in temporal networks using the concept of (μ,τ,ϵ)𝜇𝜏italic-ϵ(\mu,\tau,\epsilon)-stable cores. Here, a node is in a (μ,τ,ϵ)𝜇𝜏italic-ϵ(\mu,\tau,\epsilon)-stable core if it has no less than μ𝜇\mu neighbors that have a similarity of at least ϵitalic-ϵ\epsilon to the node in at least τ𝜏\tau snapshots of the temporal network.Li et al. (2018) propose as community model the (θ,τ)𝜃𝜏(\theta,\tau)-persistent k𝑘k-core variant. It is defined for a time interval I𝐼I such that a k𝑘k-core of size τ𝜏\tau exists in any subinterval of I𝐼I with length θ𝜃\theta. Computing maximum (θ,τ)𝜃𝜏(\theta,\tau)-persistent k𝑘k-cores is 𝖭𝖯𝖭𝖯\mathsf{NP}-hard.

Our n𝑛n-th order temporal H-index leads to a decomposition that has only a single parameter and allows efficient computation (see Table 2).Core decompositions are valuable tools, e.g., in social network analysis (Shin et al., 2016), community detection (Rossetti and Cazabet, 2018), and network visualization (Alvarez-Hamelin et al., 2006).The previously mentioned approaches do not take temporal reachability explicitly into account, whereas ours supports this naturally via the n𝑛n-th order temporal H-index.

In static networks, the static n𝑛n-th order H-index of a node v∈V𝑣𝑉v\in V converges to the k𝑘k-core value of the node v𝑣v for sufficiently large n𝑛n (Lü et al., 2016). This does not hold for the n𝑛n-th order temporal H-index.

Therefore, neither the outward nor the inward n𝑛n-th order temporal H-index converges to the k𝑘k-core composition of the network.

The two variants of the n𝑛n-th order temporal H-index provide two natural ways of decomposing the temporal network.

We distinguish in- and out-pseudocores for ⋆⁣=⁣−⋆\star=- and ⋆⁣=⁣+⋆\star=+, resp.The following observations and results hold for both variants.For a node v𝑣v in a (n,k)𝑛𝑘(n,k)-pseudocore 𝒢(n,k)subscript𝒢𝑛𝑘\mathcal{G}_{(n,k)}, the inequality hv,⋆(n)≥ksuperscriptsubscriptℎ𝑣⋆𝑛𝑘h_{v,\star}^{(n)}\geq k does not hold necessarily with respect to 𝒢(n,k)subscript𝒢𝑛𝑘\mathcal{G}_{(n,k)}.However, each (n,k)𝑛𝑘(n,k)-pseudocore is a subset of nodes that implies a temporal subgraph containing nodes with similar temporal activity and importance in the network 𝒢𝒢\mathcal{G}.In Section 5.4, we show that the pseudocore can be used to identify temporally well-connected subgraphs.
