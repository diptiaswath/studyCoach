To solve real-world problems, we often formulate problems as mathematical models and then develop algorithms working on these mathematical models.In this context, algorithms are usually designed to find a single (near) optimal solution by optimizing an objective function formulated in a mathematical model.However, such a solution may be inadequate for original real-world problems since mathematical models “approximately” formulate them and some tacit rules and ambiguous constraints inherent in real-world problems are usually ignored for taming mathematical models.One possible (and straightforward) solution to this issue is to find multiple solutions rather than a single solution.The concept of k𝑘k-best enumeration [13] is a promising approach along this line.In this approach, given a parameter k𝑘k, we compute a set of k𝑘k distinct solutions, such that every solution in the set is better than any solutions not in the set.This is a natural extension of usual optimization problems, and many algorithms for finding k𝑘k-best solutions have been developed for classical combinatorial optimization problems [25, 23, 15] and other problems arise in several fields [19, 24].

However, solutions obtained by k𝑘k-best enumeration algorithms might be similar to each other since those solutions are essentially made by some local modifications.The basic strategy of k𝑘k-best enumeration algorithms is that we first find a single optimal solution S={x1,…,xt}𝑆subscript𝑥1…subscript𝑥𝑡S=\{x_{1},\ldots,x_{t}\} and then, for each 1≤i≤t1𝑖𝑡1\leq i\leq t, compute an optimal solution that includes {x1,…,xi−1}subscript𝑥1…subscript𝑥𝑖1\{x_{1},\ldots,x_{i-1}\} but excludes xisubscript𝑥𝑖x_{i}.The entire algorithm recursively computes k𝑘k solutions in this way.Since the subsequent solution must contain {x1,…​xi−1}subscript𝑥1…subscript𝑥𝑖1\{x_{1},\ldots x_{i-1}\} and also tends to contain {xi+1,…,xt}subscript𝑥𝑖1…subscript𝑥𝑡\{x_{i+1},\ldots,x_{t}\}, these solutions would be similar, which would not fit for our original purpose.
