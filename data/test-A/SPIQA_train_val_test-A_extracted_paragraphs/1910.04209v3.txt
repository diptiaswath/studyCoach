Stochastic gradient-based optimization serves as the workhorse training approach for many classes of parametric models, including neural networks. Stochastic gradient descent and its various first-order cousinsВ (Polyak 1964; Nesterov 1983) have enabled numerous advances in deep learning across domainsВ (Krizhevsky, Sutskever, and Hinton 2012; He etВ al. 2016; Gehring etВ al. 2017). More recently, adaptive optimization algorithms have become prevalent in training the largest deep learning models. These adaptive methods, which include AdagradВ (Duchi, Hazan, and Singer 2010), RMSPropВ (Hinton, Srivastava, and Swersky 2012), and AdamВ (Kingma and Ba 2014), scale the step size for each individual parameter based on various gradient moments.

Many practitioners have adopted the Adam algorithm for general-purpose use; notably, the preponderance of recent state-of-the-art results in natural language processingВ (Devlin etВ al. 2018; Radford etВ al. 2019; Liu etВ al. 2019b; Brown etВ al. 2020) have employed Adam, demonstrating the algorithmвҖҷs ability to effectively train neural networks with parameter counts from 100 million to several billion.

In these large-scale settings, AdamвҖҷs global learning rate is usually annealed with a вҖңwarmup scheduleвҖқ which promotes early-stage training stability by regulating the size of the parameter updates. The prevalent warmup schedule is a simple linear warmup, in which the global learning rate starts at zero and increases by a constant at each iteration until reaching its intended value.В 111Linear warmup has also been deployed for first-order optimization вҖ“ see, for example, Goyal etВ al. (2017). The parameters of these warmup schedules are typically tuned for each problem setting and model.

Liu etВ al. (2020) performed an analysis of Adam with warmup, concluding that Adam requires a warmup schedule to mitigate the large or divergent variance of the per-parameter scale term. They then propose the rectified Adam (вҖңRAdamвҖқ) algorithm, which automatically corrects for this high variance. Liu etВ al. highlight the robustness of RAdam, noting in particular that RAdam reduces or eliminates the need for tuning warmup schedules when using Adam. RAdam has been applied to domains including generative modelingВ (Yamamoto, Song, and Kim 2020), natural language processingВ (Nguyen and Salazar 2019), and video retrievalВ (Liu etВ al. 2019a).

Our contributions in this work are as follows:

Reexamining RAdam and the variance-based motivation for warmupвҖғWe dive into the inner operation of RAdam and find that it is precisely Adam with a fixed warmup schedule, with the only deviation being to perform four iterations of heavy-ball momentumВ (Polyak 1964) at the outset. We then argue that the variance-based motivation for warmup is impaired as it overlooks the correlation between the first and second moment estimators, which is crucial for understanding the actual parameter updates applied by Adam.

Analyzing AdamвҖҷs early-stage update magnitudesвҖғShifting focus from gradients to parameter updates, we then perform a simulation-based analysis on the magnitudes of AdamвҖҷs parameter updates. We find that even at a simulated local minimum of the objective, Adam exhibits considerable non-regularity in its early-stage parameter updates, shedding light on why Adam may require learning rate warmup to a greater extent than first-order optimization methods.

Demonstrating the sufficiency of untuned warmupвҖғWe provide some simple and intuitive вҖңrule-of-thumbвҖқ warmup schedules for Adam, all of which require no tuning. As our main empirical result, we demonstrate that these schedules result in substantively identical performance and training dynamics to those of RAdam across a wide range of models, problem settings, and hyperparameters, indicating that any claimed benefits can be achieved with lower complexity using off-the-shelf optimization tools. As a sensible untuned default, we recommend linear warmup over 2вӢ…(1вҲ’ОІ2)вҲ’1вӢ…2superscript1subscriptрқӣҪ212\cdot\left(1-\beta_{2}\right)^{-1} iterations.

We provide an alternative view of the frequent necessity of learning rate warmup when using Adam. We do so by directly investigating the magnitudes of the update steps, perhaps the most proximate determinant of training stability.

In stochastic gradient descent, parameter updates are simply the gradients multiplied by the learning rate. Warmup for SGD can thus be motivated as mitigating the large expected magnitudes of the gradients (directly proportional to update magnitudes) and rapid change in gradients at the beginning of trainingВ (Goyal etВ al. 2017; Gotmare etВ al. 2019). Similar logic can be employed for adaptive methods.

On the other hand, if a modelвҖҷs gradients have near-zero means and low gradient variances, the update steps are similarly well-regulated and optimization via SGD can be stable without any learning rate warmup. For example, a nearly-converged model (thus having near-zero expected gradients and low gradient magnitudes) trained via SGD can have its optimization be stably restarted without learning rate warmup.

This is not the case with Adam. We proceed to computationally analyze the magnitude of AdamвҖҷs update step over the course of training. Specifically, we demonstrate via simulation that even when the model parameters Оёtsubscriptрқңғрқ‘Ў\theta_{t} are initialized at an idealized local minimum of в„’вҖӢ(Оё)в„’рқңғ{\mathcal{L}}(\theta) (i.e. вҲҮв„’^tвҖӢ(Оёt)вҲҮsubscript^в„’рқ‘Ўsubscriptрқңғрқ‘Ў{\nabla\hat{\mathcal{L}}}_{t}(\theta_{t}) has zero mean and is i.i.d.В across time), the magnitude of AdamвҖҷs update steps will still be quite high at the start of training, only gradually decaying toward a stationary distribution.

We discuss various consequences of our findings, along with directions for future work.

The analysis of the update step magnitudes in SectionВ 3.3 suggests shorter warmup periods than those typically used in practice. For example, using the setting of ОІ2=0.999subscriptрқӣҪ20.999\beta_{2}=0.999, AdamвҖҷs update magnitudes in the theoretical model converge to a stationary distribution in roughly 40 iterations. If update magnitudes were the only relevant consideration, then a warmup schedule over a few hundred iterations would suffice to stabilize training. In contrast, the effective warmup periods of both RAdam and our rule-of-thumb schedules are roughly 1000 iterations for ОІ2=0.999subscriptрқӣҪ20.999\beta_{2}=0.999. State-of-the-art methods with hand-tuned warmup schedules often go well beyond, using up to 10000 iterations of linear warmup in some casesВ (Liu etВ al. 2019b; Baevski and Auli 2018; Ott etВ al. 2019).

Accordingly, we surmise that the precise channel by which Adam necessitates an extended period of warmup is still an unresolved question, likely related to the properties of the gradients at random initialization. Future work could rigorously investigate the effect of extended warmup periods on the training dynamics of Adam, beyond simple per-iteration statistics.

One ancillary finding of SectionВ 3.3 is that the magnitudes of AdamвҖҷs update steps during later stages of training are largely invariant to the properties or dynamics of the gradient distribution вҖ“ both the simulated local optimum and real-world random initialization settings result in convergence to similar stationary distributions of update magnitudes. This suggests that learning rate decay at later stages of training could be the only way to improve late-stage convergence, as AdamвҖҷs late-stage update magnitudes do not appear to be very sensitive to the variance or stationarity of gradients. In particular, we suspect that variance-based methods of improving the late-stage convergence of SGD, such as increasing the batch sizeВ (Smith etВ al. 2018), will not yield comparable benefits when applied to Adam, as the stationary distribution of the update magnitudes will remain largely the same. Partially adaptable methodsВ (Chen and Gu 2018; Keskar and Socher 2017; Luo etВ al. 2019), which interpolate between the full adaptivity of Adam and the non-adaptivity of SGD, may hold more promise for improving late-stage convergence.

All methods considered by this work use fixed warmup schedules, computed only as a function of the training iteration tрқ‘Ўt and various hyperparameters. Such schedules will inevitably be brittle to some combination of problem setting, model, and optimizer configuration. Another direction for future work could be to devise truly dynamic mechanisms for scheduling warmup in Adam. Such a mechanism could (among other things) track and utilize auxiliary statistics, such as the running moments of the applied updates, in order to determine the stability of training at each iteration.

This direction comes dangerously close to seeking the вҖңholy grailвҖқ of an automatic learning rate tuner; existing attempts to devise such a method have achieved limited adoption as of yetВ (Li, Tai, and E 2017; Zhang, Mitliagkas, and RГ© 2017; Baydin etВ al. 2018). What makes this potentially more tractable is that a maximum learning rate is still tuned and given a priori to the optimizer; the task is then restricted to dynamic scheduling of the learning rate from zero to this known constant, instead of an arbitrary range (0,вҲһ)0(0,\infty).

We show that the Rectified Adam (RAdam) algorithm can be characterized as four steps of momentum SGD, followed by Adam with a fixed warmup schedule. We also examine the shortcomings of a variance-based approach to analyzing the learning rate warmup heuristic, and we illustrate that AdamвҖҷs frequent need for learning rate warmup can be partially explained by inspecting AdamвҖҷs early-stage update step magnitudes when applied to an already-converged model.

RAdamвҖҷs claimed benefits are its superior performance to Adam and its elimination of costly warmup schedule tuning. We obviate RAdam by providing two simple вҖңrule-of-thumbвҖқ warmup schedules for Adam, both of which require no tuning.
