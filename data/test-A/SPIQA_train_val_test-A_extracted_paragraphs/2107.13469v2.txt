We extensively evaluate our method on both popular UDA classification and semantic segmentation benchmarks. It can be naturally generalized to partial UDA.

Unsupervised domain adaptation is aimed at transferring knowledge learned from a labeled source domain to an unlabeled target domain [23]. The solutions can be mainly classified into statistic moment matching (e.g., maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) [35]), domain style transfer [50], self-training [65, 29, 32], and feature-level adversarial learning [13, 18, 19, 34]. As one of the mainstreams, adversarial learning based UDA proposes to encourage the features fвЂ‹(x)рќ‘“рќ‘Ґf(x) from different domains indistinguishable. Conditional and label shifts, however, are ubiquitous in real-world tasks. At present, the moment matching and self-training usually dominate the top place in classification and segmentation tasks, respectively.

Domain shifts [23] can be categorized into four classes based on the shift content, as summarized in Fig. 3. Conventionally, each shift is studied independently, by assuming that the other shift conditions are invariant across domains [23].

The conditional shift [35] is more realistic than the covariate shift assumption [23]. However, without ptвЂ‹(y)subscriptрќ‘ќрќ‘Ўрќ‘¦p_{t}(y), estimation of ptвЂ‹(x|y)subscriptрќ‘ќрќ‘Ўconditionalрќ‘Ґрќ‘¦p_{t}(x|y) is, in general, ill-posed [63].

The label shift [5] (a.k.a. the target shift) occurs when the proportion of each class is different between source and target domains. [26] propose a test distribution estimator to detect the label shift. [1] introduce a regularization approach to correct the label shift. [7] consider the label shift in an optimal transportation-based UDA task, which is also related to the class imbalance problem in the MMD framework. [56] propose an asymmetrically-relaxed alignment in adversarial UDA. These methods, however, assume that there is no conditional shift.

Moreover, in partial UDA [4], classes in a target domain are a subset of those in a source domain. It can be regarded as a special case of the label shift in that some classes in the target domain are zero. Instead of calculating the transferability of each source example, which can be each pixel in the segmentation task [4], we directly estimate the probability of each class and demonstrate its generality and scalability for a novel segmentation partial UDA task. In a related development, the open set adaptation [40] also has new target categories and treats them as unknown, which is different from the above settings.

The concept shift [23] can arise, when classifying a tomato as a vegetable or fruit in different countries. It is usually not a common problem in popular object classification or semantic segmentation tasks.

[63, 15, 9] consider the conditional and target shifts in a casual system (yв†’xв†’рќ‘¦рќ‘Ґy\rightarrow{x}) with a somewhat unrealistic linearity assumption. Unfortunately, they are not aligned with the modern UDA methods and sensitive to the scale of the dataset. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first attempt at considering both the conditional and label shifts in adversarial UDA.

In this paper, we tackle the problem of both conditional and label shifts in the context of adversarial UDA. We carry out a thoroughly theoretical analysis to demonstrate that it gives a more realistic and even minimal assumption in many tasks. Then, we propose to align these two shifts in an alternative optimization with a fade-in ptвЂ‹(y)subscriptрќ‘ќрќ‘Ўрќ‘¦p_{t}(y) framework which consists of three to-be learned players, i.e., feature extractor, joint parameterized discriminator and classifier, and target label distribution estimator. The merits of joint parameterization are also investigated. After the training, the posterior alignment is applied for real-world deployment. Extensive experiments on popular (partial) UDA benchmarks verify our analysis and revoke the adversarial learning, which can still be effective as the recent fast developed self-training, dropout or moment matching methods.
