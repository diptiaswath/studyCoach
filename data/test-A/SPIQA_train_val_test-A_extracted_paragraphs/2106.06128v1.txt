It has been heavily studied in the community of social sciences how opinions evolve and shape through social interactions among individuals with potentially different opinions (Degroot, 1974; Friedkin andJohnsen, 1990). In the current digital age, social media and online social networks provide unprecedented access to social interactions and individuals’ opinions, leading to a fundamental change of the way people propagate, share, and shape opinions (Ledford, 2020). As such, in the past years the problem of opinion dynamics has received considerable attention from scientists in various disciplines (Jiaet al., 2015; Donget al., 2018; Anderson and Ye, 2019), including the AI community (Daset al., 2014; Fotakis et al., 2016; Aulettaet al., 2018). In order to understand the evolution and shaping of opinions, numerous models have been introduced (Noorazaret al., 2020), among which the DeGroot model (Degroot, 1974) is a popular one. In the DeGroot model, the final opinions of individuals reach consensus. Since its establishment, the DeGroot model has been extended or generalized by incorporating different factors affecting opinion formation or agreement (Jiaet al., 2015; Donget al., 2018).

Within the area of opinion dynamics, in addition to the development of models explaining the spread and formation of opinions, the problem of effectively shifting opinions in a social network has also become an active direction of study in recent years (Gioniset al., 2013; Yildiz et al., 2013; Lucaet al., 2014; Abebe et al., 2018; Xu et al., 2020), since it is closely related to various practical application settings, such as public health campaigns, political candidates, and product marketing. Most of previous work focus on the operations on individuals in a social network so as to optimize different opinions, for example, to maximize or minimize the overall opinion of the network. However, link operations for optimizing the overall opinion has not been well studied and understood. In this paper we consider the problem of maximizing the overall opinion by adding edges, namely, making friends in a social network.

We consider a variant of the DeGroot model (Yildiz et al., 2013; Lucaet al., 2014; Mackin andPatterson, 2019) for opinion dynamics on a social network with n𝑛n nodes and m𝑚m edges, where nodes represent individuals, and edges denote interactions among individuals. In this extended DeGroot model, the n𝑛n individuals are classified two classes S𝑆S and F𝐹F: S𝑆S includes a small number s≪nmuch-less-than𝑠𝑛s\ll n of leader nodes, while the remaining n−s𝑛𝑠n-s nodes in F𝐹F are followers. In a real social network, a leader could be a paid promoter of a certain product or political position. The leader set S𝑆S is further categorized into to two subsets S0subscript𝑆0S_{0} and S1subscript𝑆1S_{1}, which represent firm supporters of the opposing parties, containing nodes with opposing opinions 0 and 1, respectively. In the model, each i𝑖i node at time t𝑡t has a nonnegative scalar-value opinion 𝒙i​(t)subscript𝒙𝑖𝑡\bm{\mathit{x}}_{i}(t) in the interval [0,1]01[0,1]. When i𝑖i is a follower, 𝒙i​(t)subscript𝒙𝑖𝑡\bm{\mathit{x}}_{i}(t) evolves as a weighted average of the opinions 𝒙j​(t−1)subscript𝒙𝑗𝑡1\bm{\mathit{x}}_{j}(t-1) of i𝑖i’s neighbors j𝑗j. When i𝑖i is a leader, 𝒙i​(t)subscript𝒙𝑖𝑡\bm{\mathit{x}}_{i}(t) never changes over time. Finally, the opinion of each follower converges to a value between 0 and 1.

We address the following problem based on the aforementioned DeGroot model: Given a social network and a positive integer k𝑘k, how to create k𝑘k edges, each connecting a 1-valued leader and a follower, so that the overall stead-state opinion is maximized. This problem is at variance with existing work in terms of both formulation and solution. The main contributions of our work are as follows. First, we show that the objective function is monotone and submodular, which are established using a technique completely different from existing ones. Then, we propose two approximation algorithms solving the combinatorial optimization problem based on a greedy strategy of adding edges. The two greedy algorithms are guaranteed to have, respectively, approximation ratios (1−1e)11𝑒\left(1-\frac{1}{e}\right) and (1−1e−ϵ)11𝑒italic-ϵ\left(1-\frac{1}{e}-\epsilon\right), where ϵ>0italic-ϵ0\epsilon>0 is the error parameter. The first algorithm has time complexity O​(n3)𝑂superscript𝑛3O(n^{3}), while the second algorithm has complexity O~​(m​k​ϵ−2)~𝑂𝑚𝑘superscriptitalic-ϵ2\widetilde{O}(mk\epsilon^{-2}), where the notation O~​(⋅)~𝑂⋅\widetilde{O}(\cdot) hides poly​(log⁡n)poly𝑛{\rm poly}(\log n) factors. Finally, comprehensive experiments on various real networks are performed to demonstrate the efficiency and effectiveness of our algorithms, as well as their performance advantages, compared with several baseline strategies of edge addition.

In this section, we briefly review the literature related to our work.

The model under consideration is based on a variant of the popular DeGroot model for opinion dynamics (Degroot, 1974). Sincethe time the DeGroot model was established, numerous extensions or variants have been proposed by considering different aspects or processes affecting the limiting opinions. For example, the Friedkin-Johnson (FJ) model (Friedkin andJohnsen, 1990) is a generalization of the DeGroot model, where each individual has two opinions, internal opinion and expressed opinion. After a long time evolution, the expressed opinions of individuals in the FJ model converge but often do not reach agreement. Another extension of the DeGroot model is the Altafini model (Altafini, 2013), where it is supposed thatthe interactions between individuals are not always cooperative, but sometimes antagonistic.

Many existing studies also pay attention to variants of the DeGroot model by selecting 1-leaders in the presence of competing 0-leaders, which are formulated for optimizing different objectives such as minimizing disagreement and polarization (Yi and Patterson, 2020), maximizing the diversity (Mackin andPatterson, 2019) and the total opinion (Yildiz et al., 2013; Lucaet al., 2014; Mai and Abed, 2019). Similar opinion maximization problem was also studied for the FJ model by using different strategies, including identifying a given number of 1-leaders (Gioniset al., 2013), as well as modifying individual’s internal opinions (Xu et al., 2020) or susceptibility to persuasion (Abebe et al., 2018; Chanet al., 2019). Although the problem we address is also the maximization of the overall opinion, our strategy is optimally selecting edges to add, instead of leader selection.

Admittedly, as a practical approach of graph edit, edge addition operation has been extensively used for different application purposes, such as improving the centrality of a node (Crescenzi et al., 2016; Shanet al., 2018; D’Angeloet al., 2019) and maximizing the number of spanning trees (Liet al., 2020). For a social network, creating edges corresponds to making friends.In the field of opinion dynamics, the problem of adding edges has also been studied in order to optimize different objectives.For example, the edge addition strategy was exploited in (Bindelet al., 2011, 2015), aiming at minimizing the social cost at equilibrium in the FJ model. Again for instance, in (Garimella et al., 2017) and (Chenet al., 2018), creating edges was adopted to reduce, respectively, controversy and risk of conflict. Finally, edge recommendation was used in (Amelkin and Singh, 2019) with an aim to strategically fight opinion control in a social network. Motivated in part by these work, we exploit the manner of adding edges to maximize the overall opinion. Departing from the literature in the area of opinion dynamics, we present a nearly linear algorithm for evaluating the overall opinion, which is proved to have a guaranteed approximation ratio.

Problem 1 is inherently a combinatorial problem. It can be solved by the following naïve brute-force approach. For each set T𝑇T of the (|Q|k)binomial𝑄𝑘\tbinom{|Q|}{k} possible subsets of edges, compute the overall equilibrium opinion of follower nodes in the resultant graph when all edges in this set are added. Although this method is simple, it is computationally impossible even for small networks, since its has an exponential complexity O​((|Q|k)⋅(n−s)3)𝑂⋅binomial𝑄𝑘superscript𝑛𝑠3O\big{(}\tbinom{|Q|}{k}\cdot(n-s)^{3}\big{)}.

In this section, we will study the performance of our two heuristic algorithms Exact and Approx in terms of the effectiveness and the efficiency by implementing experiments on various real-life networks with different scales. The selected data sets of real networks are publicly available in the KONECT (Kunegis, 2013) and SNAP at website https://snap.stanford.edu, detailed information for whose largest components is presented in the first three columns of Table 1. For the convenience of using the linear solver Solve (Kyng and Sachdeva, 2016), which can be found at https://github.com/danspielman/Laplacians.jl, all our experiments are programmed in Julia using a single thread, and are run on a machine equipped with 32G RAM and 4.2 GHz Intel i7-7700 CPU. In our experiments, the candidate edge set Q𝑄Q contains all nonexistent edges satisfying constraints in Section 4.2, for each of which, one end is in set S1subscript𝑆1S_{1} and the other end is in set F𝐹F.

We first compare the effectiveness of our algorithms with the optimum solution and the solution of a random scheme that randomly choose k𝑘k edges from Q𝑄Q to add. To this end, we execute experiments on four small real networks: Karate club with 34 nodes and 78 edges, Dolphins with 62 nodes and 159 edges, Tribes with 16 nodes and 58 edges, and FirmHiTech with 33 nodes and 147 edges, which allow us to compute the optimal set of added edges. We randomly select three 0-leaders and three 1-leaders. In this paper, we examined the problem of maximizing the influence of opinion for leaders in a social network with n𝑛n nodes and m𝑚m edges by adding k𝑘k new edges based on the discrete leader-follower DeGroot model for opinion dynamics with s≪nmuch-less-than𝑠𝑛s\ll n leaders. The problem is inherently a combinatorial optimization problem that can be applied to various domains. We established the monotonicity and submodularity of the objective function. We put forward two heuristic algorithms. The former returns a (1−1e)11𝑒(1-\frac{1}{e}) approximation of the optimal solution in time O​(n3)𝑂superscript𝑛3O(n^{3}), while the latter has a (1−1e−ϵ)11𝑒italic-ϵ(1-\frac{1}{e}-\epsilon) approximation ratio and O~​(k​m​ϵ−2)~𝑂𝑘𝑚superscriptitalic-ϵ2\tilde{O}(km\epsilon^{-2}) complexity. Finally, we performed experiments on real networks of different scales, demonstrating the efficiency and effectiveness of our fast algorithm that is scalable to large-scale networks with more than one million nodes. In future work, we plan to extend or modify our algorithm to other optimization problems for opinion dynamics, such as minimizing risk of conflict, disagreement, and so on.
