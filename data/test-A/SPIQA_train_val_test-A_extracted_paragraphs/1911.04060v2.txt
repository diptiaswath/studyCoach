Supervised machine learning models learn to associate a target yūĚĎ¶y with underlying factors of data xūĚĎ•x that are informative of yūĚĎ¶y. However, trained models often learn to associate irrelevant factors of xūĚĎ•x with yūĚĎ¶y¬†(?), e.g., stroke-width of text in optical character recognition. Learning incorrect associations between yūĚĎ¶y and nuisance factors leads to overfitting. Models might also learn associations between the target yūĚĎ¶y and factors that are correlated with yūĚĎ¶y in collected training data due to external (sometimes historical) reasons, e.g., age, gender, and race in socio-economical prediction tasks. These biasing factors, which are sometimes known a priori, make trained models unfair to under-represented groups, posing ethical and legal challenges to the usage of such models. It is, therefore, imperative to develop models that are invariant to both nuisance and biasing factors.

Popular approaches for achieving invariance to undesired factors sūĚĎ†s have involved training with data augmentation through small perturbations to real xūĚĎ•x with respect to sūĚĎ†s¬†(?). While these methods have been utilized for training deep neural networks (DNNs)¬†(?;¬†?), in recent years methods have emerged that remove sūĚĎ†s from the latent representations z~~ūĚĎß\tilde{z}, penalizing the model for the presence of sūĚĎ†s¬†(?;¬†?;¬†?;¬†?;¬†?;¬†?;¬†?;¬†?). These methods perform better than data augmentation, due to their approach of invariance by exclusion rather than inclusion¬†(?;¬†?;¬†?).

We propose a novel framework for invariance in DNNs that promotes removal of information about sūĚĎ†s from z~~ūĚĎß\tilde{z} through an adversarial forgetting mechanism. The working principle of the model is to map data xūĚĎ•x to an embedding zūĚĎßz that encodes everything about xūĚĎ•x and use a forget-mask to filter out sūĚĎ†s information from zūĚĎßz while retaining information about yūĚĎ¶y to produce the invariant z~~ūĚĎß\tilde{z}. Specifically, an encoder network generates a latent code zūĚĎßz from xūĚĎ•x, which is used to reconstruct xūĚĎ•x through a decoder. At the same time, a forget-gate network generates a mask mūĚĎöm from xūĚĎ•x, which is multiplied elementwise with zūĚĎßz to produce z~~ūĚĎß\tilde{z}. The encoding z~~ūĚĎß\tilde{z} is used by a predictor to infer the target yūĚĎ¶y. These components of the framework are trained adversarially with a discriminator that aims to predict sūĚĎ†s from z~~ūĚĎß\tilde{z}. However, during training, gradients from the discriminator are allowed to only affect the training of the forget-gate. Finally, the framework is augmented with a regularizer that pushes the components misubscriptūĚĎöūĚĎĖm_{i} of the forget-mask to be close to either 00 or 111, inducing disentanglement within the components of zūĚĎßz and effective masking of sūĚĎ†s to produce an invariant representation z~~ūĚĎß\tilde{z}.

We show that the forgetting mechanism is equivalent to a bound on the mutual information I(z~:z)I(\tilde{z}:z) and that coupled with the yūĚĎ¶y-prediction task, can be interpreted as an information bottleneck. Further, by the data processing inequality, the forgetting mechanism bounds I(z~:s)I(\tilde{z}:s). The generated mask can be manipulated through adversarial training to remove information about sūĚĎ†s from zūĚĎßz. Empirical results show that the proposed framework exhibits state-of-the-art performance at inducing invariance to both nuisance and biasing factors across a diverse collection of datasets and tasks.

Recent work¬†(?;¬†?;¬†?) has modeled invariance in supervised DNNs through information bottleneck¬†(?), wherein representations minimize the mutual information I(x:z~)I(x:\tilde{z}) while maximizing I(z~:y)I(\tilde{z}:y). For nuisance variables (s‚üāyperpendicular-toūĚĎ†ūĚĎ¶s\perp y), these methods bring about compression in the latent space, which removes information about sūĚĎ†s and indirectly minimizes I(z~:s)I(\tilde{z}:s). Under optimality, the information bottleneck objective will remove all such nuisance sūĚĎ†s completely from z~~ūĚĎß\tilde{z}¬†(?). The bottleneck objective is, however, difficult to optimize and has, hence, been approximated using variational inference in prior work¬†(?) or implemented as Information Dropout¬†(?).

The bottleneck objective cannot remove sūĚĎ†s from z~~ūĚĎß\tilde{z} that are correlated with yūĚĎ¶y (biasing factors). In such cases, it is necessary to force the exclusion of these biasing sūĚĎ†s from z~~ūĚĎß\tilde{z}. Methods that employ such mechanisms additionally benefit the removal of nuisance sūĚĎ†s for which annotations are available. Moyer et al.¬†(?) achieve this by augmenting the bottleneck objective with I(z~:s)I(\tilde{z}:s) and optimizing its variational bound, which we call the Conditional Variational Information Bottleneck (CVIB). The Variational Fair Autoencoder (VFAE)¬†(?) optimizes this objective indirectly as a Variational Autoencoder (VAE)¬†(?) and uses Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD)¬†(?) to boost the removal of undesired sūĚĎ†s from the latent embedding. The Hilbert-Schmidt Independence Criterion (HSIC)¬†(?) constrained VAE (HCV)¬†(?) uses HSIC to enforce independence between the latent embedding and sūĚĎ†s. NN+MMD¬†(?) directly minimizes MMD as a regularizer for DNNs.

Invariance to nuisance variables has also been modeled as an implicit feature selection method within DNNs in the Unsupervised Adversarial Invariance framework (UAI)¬†(?;¬†?) that splits data representation into an invariant embedding, which is used for predicting yūĚĎ¶y, and a nuisance embedding through competitive training between prediction and reconstruction tasks combined with disentanglement between the two embeddings.

The Domain-Adversarial Neural Network (DANN)¬†(?) and the Controllable Adversarial Invariance (CAI) model¬†(?) indirectly optimize an invariance objective using the gradient-reversal trick¬†(?) that penalizes a DNN if it encodes sūĚĎ†s. In the case of invariant representation learning for a supervised prediction task (which is the subject of this work), CAI, DANN and Fader Networks¬†(?) reduce to the same model. In contrast, we propose a new forgetting mechanism that more explicitly ‚Äúmasks‚ÄĚ sūĚĎ†s out of zūĚĎßz, which encodes everything about xūĚĎ•x, to generate a new representation z~~ūĚĎß\tilde{z} that is maximally informative of yūĚĎ¶y but invariant to sūĚĎ†s. Inspired by (1) the discovery of richer features in DNN classifiers upon augmentation with reconstruction objectives¬†(?;¬†?), and (2) the forgetting operation in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)¬†(?) cells, the proposed framework adopts the idea of ‚Äúdiscovery and separation of information‚ÄĚ for invariance, which is foundationally different from the direct removal of undesired factors as in DANN and CAI.

Invariant representation learning aims to produce a mapping of data (xūĚĎ•x) to code (z~~ūĚĎß\tilde{z}) that is minimally informative of undesired factors of data (sūĚĎ†s) but maximally discriminative for the prediction task (yūĚĎ¶y). There are two cases that arise from this formulation ‚Äď (1) sūĚĎ†s is nuisance, i.e., there is little or no information shared between sūĚĎ†s and yūĚĎ¶y asymptotically (e.g., pose in face recognition), and (2) sūĚĎ†s contains biasing factors, i.e., there is correlation between sūĚĎ†s and yūĚĎ¶y, but for outside reasons, it is necessary to exclude these biases from the prediction process (e.g., gender, race, etc. in socio-economical prediction tasks using historical data). Invariance to sūĚĎ†s leads to robust models that generalize better on test data for case (1)¬†(?), and produces fair models that do not incorporate sūĚĎ†s while making predictions for case (2).

We induce invariance to sūĚĎ†s within a DNN using a novel approach of adversarial forgetting, which is inspired by forget-gates in Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) cells in recurrent neural networks¬†(?). Within the proposed framework, the model learns to embed everything about a data sample into an intermediate representation, which is transformed into an invariant representation through multiplication with a mask generated by an adversarial forgetting mechanism. We have presented a novel framework for invariance induction in neural networks through ‚Äúforgetting‚ÄĚ of information related to unwanted factors. We showed that the forget-gate used in the proposed framework acts as an information bottleneck and that adversarial training encourages generation of forget-masks that remove unwanted factors. Empirical results show that the proposed model exhibits state-of-the-art performance in both nuisance and bias settings.
