While statisticians are trained to be aware of multiple testing issues, temporal multiplicity is often easy to miss. Let us examine the following simplified situation alluded to in the abstract. Consider a team of statisticians at a pharmaceutical company who test a new drug every week of the year. In week tūĚĎ°t, a new drug is under consideration, and to assess its treatment effect őłtsubscriptūĚúÉūĚĎ°\theta_{t}, the team conducts a new randomized clinical trial with new participants. Suppose that the data, such as the normalized empirical difference in means between the treatment and control groups, can be summarized by the observation Xt‚ąľN‚Äč(őłt,1)similar-tosubscriptūĚĎčūĚĎ°ūĚĎĀsubscriptūĚúÉūĚĎ°1X_{t}\sim N(\theta_{t},1), independent of all the previous XisubscriptūĚĎčūĚĎĖX_{i}.

Now consider the following selection rule: if Xt<3subscriptūĚĎčūĚĎ°3X_{t}<3, then the statisticians simply ignore drug tūĚĎ°t, and if Xt>3subscriptūĚĎčūĚĎ°3X_{t}>3, then the team reports the two-sided 99%percent9999\% marginal CI for őłtsubscriptūĚúÉūĚĎ°\theta_{t} to the management (who may then decide to run a much larger second phase clinical trial since the CI does not contain 0). This may initially seem like an innocuous situation: each drug is different and has a different treatment effect őłtsubscriptūĚúÉūĚĎ°\theta_{t}, the data XtsubscriptūĚĎčūĚĎ°X_{t} is always fresh and independent, the decision for whether or not to construct the CI for őłtsubscriptūĚúÉūĚĎ°\theta_{t} is dependent only on XtsubscriptūĚĎčūĚĎ°X_{t} and independent of all other XisubscriptūĚĎčūĚĎĖX_{i}, and so is the interval if constructed.

Nonetheless, the combination of multiplicity and selection is a cause for concern already in the offline setting, as was insightfully pointed out by Benjamini and¬†Yekutieli (2005).In the online case, when there is an infinite sequence of parameters, it is even easier to construct an example where ignoring selection has undesirable consequences.Indeed, consider the special case where őłt=0subscriptūĚúÉūĚĎ°0\theta_{t}=0 for all tūĚĎ°t, in other words, every tested drug is equivalent to a placebo.In this situation, every single CI that is reported to the management is incorrect, since it does not contain zero.Because a selection will eventually occur, among constructed CIs the proportion of non-covering CIs‚ÄĒthis is later formally defined as the false coverage proportion, FCP‚ÄĒwill equal one from this point on.Thus, the FCR‚ÄĒexpectation of FDP‚ÄĒis not controlled.Of course, the second phase of the trial will rectify this error, but at a huge cost of time and money, and loss of faith in the team of statisticians.

One natural solution for this is provided by conditional post-selection inference: instead of a 99%percent9999\% marginal CI, we may construct a conditional 99%percent9999\% interval, where we condition on the event that Xt>3subscriptūĚĎčūĚĎ°3X_{t}>3, leading to inference based on a truncated gaussian likelihood in the above setting.Confidence intervals based on a truncated normal observation were proposed by Zhong and¬†Prentice (2008) and Weinstein et¬†al. (2013) to counteract the selection effect when providing inference after hypothesis testing.While these works consider the batch (offline) setting, in our simple example constructing such conditional CIs (as well as the selection rule) is a legitimate online CI procedure.Furthermore, this controls the FCR‚ÄĒin fact, as will be discussed in Section¬†3 and demonstrated in our simulations, constructing conditional intervals provides unnecessarily strong guarantees, that come at a price.

In this paper, we will propose a new approach for online FCR control that is very different from the aforementioned conditional approach.Informally, in order to achieve FCR control at level őĪūĚõľ\alpha, instead of constructing (1‚ąíőĪ)1ūĚõľ(1-\alpha) conditional CIs, we construct (1‚ąíőĪi)1subscriptūĚõľūĚĎĖ(1-\alpha_{i}) marginal CIs for some őĪi<őĪsubscriptūĚõľūĚĎĖūĚõľ\alpha_{i}<\alpha.The algorithm to set the őĪisubscriptūĚõľūĚĎĖ\alpha_{i}s is inspired by recent advances in the online false discovery rate (FDR) control literature, specifically recent work by the first author (Ramdas et¬†al., 2017).The new CI procedure works in much more generality than the simple example described above, that is when őłisubscriptūĚúÉūĚĎĖ\theta_{i} are multi-dimensional, the data is not necessarily gaussian, and so on‚ÄĒcases in which constructing a conditional CI may be substantially harder if at all possible.

Even more importantly, by constructing marginal instead of conditional CIs, we leave open the possibility to use as a criterion for selection the candidate CI itself.For example, the rule may entail constructing the candidate marginal CI only if it does not include values of opposite signs.Thus, returning to the motivating example, this allows the team of statisticians to ensure that each reported CI is conclusive about the direction of the treatment effect, while the FCR is controlled.With such situations in mind, we instantiate our marginal CI procedure to propose a confidence interval-driven procedure, that constructs sign-determining CIs and can be seen as an online adaptation of the ideas of Weinstein and¬†Yekutieli (2019).Every such sign-determining CI procedure corresponds to an online sign-classification procedure that controls the false sign rate (FSR).As a special case we show that for some recently proposed online testing procedures, supplementing rejections based on two-sided pūĚĎĚp-values with directional decisions suffices to control the FSR.
