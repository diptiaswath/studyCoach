Clustering is a family of problems that aims to group a given set of objects in a meaningful way—the exact “meaning” may vary based on the application. These are fundamental problems in Computer Science with applications ranging across multiple fields like pattern recognition, machine learning, computational biology, bioinformatics and social science. Thus, these problems have been a subject of extensive studies in the field of Algorithm Design (and its sub-fields), see for instance, the surveys on this topic (and references therein) [24, 22, 5].

Two of the central clustering problems are k𝑘k-Median and k𝑘k-Means. In the standard k𝑘k-Median problem, we are given a set X𝑋X of n𝑛n points, and an integer k𝑘k, and the goal is to find a set C∗⊆Xsuperscript𝐶𝑋C^{*}\subseteq X of at most k𝑘k centers, such that the following cost function is minimized over all subsets C𝐶C of size at most k𝑘k.𝖼𝗈𝗌𝗍​(X,C)≔∑p∈Xminc∈C⁡𝖽​(p,c)≔𝖼𝗈𝗌𝗍𝑋𝐶subscript𝑝𝑋subscript𝑐𝐶𝖽𝑝𝑐\mathsf{cost}(X,C)\coloneqq\sum_{p\in X}\min_{c\in C}\mathsf{d}(p,c)In k𝑘k-Means, the objective function instead contains the sum of squares of distances.

Often real world data are contaminated with a small amount of noise and these noises can substantially change the clusters that we obtain using the underlying algorithm. To circumvent the issue created by such noises, there are several studies of clustering problems with outliers, see for instance, [8, 21, 15, 12, 13, 1].

In outlier extension of the k𝑘k-Median problem, which we call k𝑘k-MedianOut, we are also given an additional integer m≥0𝑚0m\geq 0 that denotes the number of outliers that we are allowed to drop. We want to find a set C𝐶C of at most k𝑘k centers, and a set Y⊆X𝑌𝑋Y\subseteq X of at most m𝑚m outliers, such that 𝖼𝗈𝗌𝗍​(X∖Y,C)≔∑p∈X∖Yminc∈C⁡𝖽​(p,c)≔𝖼𝗈𝗌𝗍𝑋𝑌𝐶subscript𝑝𝑋𝑌subscript𝑐𝐶𝖽𝑝𝑐\mathsf{cost}(X\setminus Y,C)\coloneqq\sum_{p\in X\setminus Y}\min_{c\in C}\mathsf{d}(p,c) is minimized over all (Y,C)𝑌𝐶(Y,C) satisfying the requirements. In this paper, we will focus on approximation algorithms. An algorithm is said to have an approximation ratio of α𝛼\alpha, if it is guaranteed to return a solution of cost no greater than α𝛼\alpha times the optimal cost, while satisfying all other conditions. That is, the solution must contain at most k𝑘k centers, and drop m𝑚m outliers. If the algorithm is randomized, then it must return such a solution with high probability, i.e., probability at least 1−n−c1superscript𝑛𝑐1-n^{-c} for some c≥1𝑐1c\geq 1.

For a fixed set C𝐶C of centers, the set of m𝑚m outliers is automatically defined, namely the set of m𝑚m points that are farthest from C𝐶C (breaking ties arbitrarily). Thus, an optimal clustering for k𝑘k-MedianOut, just like k𝑘k-Median, can be found in nO​(k)superscript𝑛𝑂𝑘n^{O(k)} time by enumerating all center sets. On the other hand, we can enumerate all nO​(m)superscript𝑛𝑂𝑚n^{O(m)} subsets of outliers, and reduce the problem directly to k𝑘k-Median. Other than these straightforward observations, there are several non-trivial approximations known for k𝑘k-MedianOut, which we discuss in a subsequent paragraph.

In this work, we describe a general framework that reduces a clustering with outliers problem (such as k𝑘k-MedianOut or k𝑘k-MeansOut) to its outlier-free counterpart in an approximation-preserving fashion. More specifically, given an instance ℐℐ\mathcal{I} of k𝑘k-MedianOut, our reduction runs in time f​(k,m,ϵ)⋅nO​(1)⋅𝑓𝑘𝑚italic-ϵsuperscript𝑛𝑂1f(k,m,\epsilon)\cdot n^{O(1)}, and produces multiple instances of k𝑘k-Median, such that a β𝛽\beta-approximation for at least one of the produced instances of k𝑘k-Median implies a (β+ϵ)𝛽italic-ϵ(\beta+\epsilon)-approximation for the original instance ℐℐ\mathcal{I} of k𝑘k-MedianOut. This is the main result of our paper.

Our framework does not rely on the specific properties of the underlying metric space. Thus, for special metrics, such as Euclidean spaces, or shortest-path metrics induced by sparse graph classes, for which FPT (1+ϵ)1italic-ϵ(1+\epsilon)-approximations are known for k𝑘k-Median, our framework implies matching approximation for k𝑘k-MedianOut. Our reduction is inspired from the following seemingly simple observation that relates k𝑘k-MedianOut and k𝑘k-Median. Let ℐℐ\mathcal{I} be an instance of k𝑘k-MedianOut, where we want to find a set C𝐶C of k𝑘k centers, such that the sum of distances of all except at most m𝑚m points to the nearest center in C𝐶C is minimized. By treating the outliers in an optimal solution for ℐℐ\mathcal{I} as virtual centers, one obtains a solution for (k+m)𝑘𝑚(k+m)-Median without outliers whose cost is at most the optimal cost of ℐℐ\mathcal{I}. In other words, the optimal cost of an appropriately defined instance ℐ~~ℐ\widetilde{\mathcal{I}} of (k+m)𝑘𝑚(k+m)-Median is a lower bound on the optimal cost of ℐℐ\mathcal{I}. Since k𝑘k-Median is a well-studied problem, at this point, one would hope that it is sufficient to restrict the attention to ℐ~~ℐ\widetilde{\mathcal{I}}. That is, if we obtain a solution (i.e., a set of k+m𝑘𝑚k+m centers) for ℐ~~ℐ\widetilde{\mathcal{I}}, can then be modified to obtain a solution (i.e., a set of k𝑘k centers and m𝑚m outliers) for ℐℐ\mathcal{I}. However, it is unclear whether one can do such a modification without blowing up the cost for ℐℐ\mathcal{I}. Nevertheless, this connection between ℐ~~ℐ\widetilde{\mathcal{I}} and ℐℐ\mathcal{I} turns out to be useful, but we need several new ideas to exploit it.

As in before, we start with a constant approximation for ℐ~~ℐ\widetilde{\mathcal{I}}, and perform a sampling similar to [9] to obtain a weighted set of points. This set is obtained by dividing the set of points connected to each center in the approximate solution into concentric rings, such that the “error” introduced in the cost by treating all points in the ring as identical is negligible. Then, we sample O​((k+m)​log⁡n/ϵ)𝑂𝑘𝑚𝑛italic-ϵO((k+m)\log n/\epsilon) points from each ring, and give each point an appropriate weight. We then prove a crucial concentration bound (cf. Lemma 1), which informally speaking relates the connection cost of original set of points in a ring, and the corresponding weighted sample. In particular, for any set of k𝑘k centers, with good probability, the difference between the original and the weighted costs is “small”, even after excluding at most m𝑚m outliers from both sets. Intuitively speaking, this concentration bound holds because the sample size is large enough compared to both k𝑘k and m𝑚m. Then, by taking the union of all such samples, we obtain a weighted set S𝑆S of O​(((k+m)​log⁡n/ϵ)2)𝑂superscript𝑘𝑚𝑛italic-ϵ2O(((k+m)\log n/\epsilon)^{2}) points that preserves the connection cost to any set of k𝑘k centers, even after excluding m𝑚m outliers with at least a constant probability. Then, we enumerate all sets Y𝑌Y of size m𝑚m from S𝑆S, and solve the resulting k𝑘k-Median instance induced on S∖Y𝑆𝑌S\setminus Y. Finally, we argue that at least one of the resulting instances ℐ′superscriptℐ′\mathcal{I}^{\prime} will have the property that, a β𝛽\beta-approximation for ℐ′superscriptℐ′\mathcal{I}^{\prime} implies a (β+ϵ)𝛽italic-ϵ(\beta+\epsilon)-approximation for ℐℐ\mathcal{I}.

In this paper, we give a reduction from k𝑘k-MedianOut to k𝑘k-Median that runs in time FPT in k,m𝑘𝑚k,m, and ϵitalic-ϵ\epsilon, and preserves the approximation ratio up to an additive ϵitalic-ϵ\epsilon factor. As a consequence, we obtain improved FPT approximations for k𝑘k-MedianOut in general as well as special kinds of metrics, and these approximation guarantees are known to be tight in general. Furthermore, our technique is versatile in that it also gives improved approximations for related clustering problems, such as k𝑘k-MeansOut, Matroid Median with Outliers, and Colorful k𝑘k-Median, among others.

The most natural direction is to improve the FPT running time while obtaining the tight approximation ratios. More fundamentally, perhaps, is the question whether we need an FPT dependence on the number of outliers, m𝑚m; or whether it is possible to obtain approximation guarantees for k𝑘k-MedianOut matching that for k𝑘k-Median, with a running time that is FPT in k𝑘k and ϵitalic-ϵ\epsilon alone.
