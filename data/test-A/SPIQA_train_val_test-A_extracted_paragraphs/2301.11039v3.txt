Graph-structured data are prevalent across application domains ranging from chemo- and bioinformaticsВ [Barabasi and Oltvai, 2004, Jumper etВ al., 2021, Stokes etВ al., 2020] to imageВ [Simonovsky and Komodakis, 2017] and social-network analysisВ [Easley and Kleinberg, 2010], indicating the importance of machine learning methods for such data. Nowadays, there are numerous approaches for machine learning for graph-structured, most notably those based on graph kernelsВ [Borgwardt etВ al., 2020, Kriege etВ al., 2020] or graph neural networks (GNNs)В [Chami etВ al., 2020, Gilmer etВ al., 2017, Morris etВ al., 2021]. Here, graph kernelsВ [Shervashidze etВ al., 2011] based on the 111-dimensional WeisfeilerвҖ“Leman algorithm (111-WL)В [Weisfeiler and Leman, 1968], a well-studied heuristic for the graph isomorphism problem, and corresponding GNNsВ [Morris etВ al., 2019, Xu etВ al., 2019], have recently advanced the state-of-the-art in supervised vertex- and graph-level learningВ [Morris etВ al., 2021]. Further, based on the kрқ‘ҳk-dimensional WeisfeilerвҖ“Leman algorithm (kрқ‘ҳk-WL\xspace), 111-WLвҖҷs more powerful generalization, several works generalized GNNs to higher-order GNNs (kрқ‘ҳk-GNNs), resulting in provably more expressive architectures, e.g.,В Azizian and Lelarge [2021], Geerts and Reutter [2022], Maron etВ al. [2019], Morris etВ al. [2019, 2020b, 2021, 2022].

While devising provably expressive GNN-like architectures is a meaningful endeavor, it only partially addresses the challenges of machine learning with graphs. That is, expressiveness results reveal little about an architectureвҖҷs ability to generalize to graphs outside the training set. Surprisingly, only a few notable contributions study GNNsвҖҷ generalization behaviors, e.g.,В Garg etВ al. [2020], Kriege etВ al. [2018], Liao etВ al. [2021], Maskey etВ al. [2022], Scarselli etВ al. [2018]. However, these approaches express GNNвҖҷs generalization ability using only classical graph parameters, e.g., maximum degree, number of vertices, or edges, which cannot fully capture the complex structure of real-world graphs. Further, most approaches study generalization in the non-uniform regime, i.e., assuming that the GNNs operate on graphs of a pre-specified order. Further, they only investigate the case k=1рқ‘ҳ1k=1, i.e., standard GNNs, ignoring more expressive generalizations; see the previous paragraph.

This paper investigates the influence of graph structure and the parametersвҖҷ encoding lengths on GNNsвҖҷ generalization by tightly connecting 111-WLвҖҷs expressivity and GNNsвҖҷ VapnikвҖ“Chervonenkis (VC) dimension. Specifically, we show that:1.In the non-uniform regime, we prove tight bounds on GNNsвҖҷ VC dimension. We show that GNNsвҖҷ VC dimension depends tightly on the number of equivalence classes computed by the 111-WL over a set of graphs; seeВ PropositionsВ 1 andВ 2. Moreover, our results easily extend to the kрқ‘ҳk-WL\xspaceand many recent expressive GNN extensions.2.In the uniform regime, i.e., when graphs can have arbitrary order, we show that GNNsвҖҷ VC dimension is lower and upper bounded by the largest bitlength of its weights; seeВ PropositionВ 5.3.In both the uniform and non-uniform regimes, GNNsвҖҷ VC dimension depends logarithmically on the number of colors computed by the 111-WL and polynomially on the number of parameters; seeВ TheoremВ 6.4.Empirically, we show that our theoretical findings hold in practice.Overall, our results provide new insights into GNNsвҖҷ generalization behavior and how graph structure and parameters influence it. Specifically, our results imply that a complex graph structure, captured by 111-WL, results in worse generalization performance. The same holds for increasing the encoding length of the GNNвҖҷs parameters. Importantly, our theory provides the first link between expressivity results and generalization ability. Moreover, our results establish the first lower bounds for GNNsвҖҷ VC dimension. SeeВ Figure 1 for a high-level overview of our results.

The 111-WL or color refinement is a well-studied heuristic for the graph isomorphism problem, originally proposed byВ Weisfeiler and Leman [1968].111Strictly speaking, the 111-WL and color refinement are two different algorithms. That is, the 111-WL considers neighbors and non-neighbors to update the coloring, resulting in a slightly higher expressive power when distinguishing vertices in a given graph; seeВ Grohe [2021] for details. For brevity, we consider both algorithms to be equivalent.Intuitively, the algorithm determines if two graphs are non-isomorphic by iteratively coloring or labeling vertices. Given an initial coloring or labeling of the vertices of both graphs, e.g., their degree or application-specific information, ineach iteration, two vertices with the same label get different labels if the number of identically labeled neighbors is unequal. These labels induce a vertex partition, and the algorithm terminates when after some iteration, the algorithm does not refine the current partition, i.e., when a stable coloring or stable partition is obtained. Then, if the number of vertices annotated with a specific label is different in both graphs, we can conclude that the two graphs are not isomorphic. It is easy to see that the algorithm cannot distinguish all non-isomorphic graphsВ [Cai etВ al., 1992]. Nonetheless, it is a powerful heuristic that can successfully test isomorphism for a broad class of graphsВ [Babai and Kucera, 1979].

Our results include the first lower bounds of GNNsвҖҷ VC dimension and the first inherent connection between GNNsвҖҷ VC dimension and the expressive power of the 111-WL. In particular, we find that a larger bit precision leads to a higher VC dimension and sample complexity. Therefore, our findings provide an additional incentive for reducing the precision of GNNs besides the already-known benefits of reduced training and inference time. Furthermore, we show that the VC dimension bounds for GNNs can be tightened when considering graphs of low color complexity. This connection to the number of colors indicates that graphs with complex structures, captured by the 111-WL, may have a higher VC dimension. Moreover, if the 111-WL can distinguish a large set of graphs of a given dataset, our results imply that a sufficiently expressive GNN will require a large set of training samples to generalize well. Therefore, we can use the 111-WL to assess GNNsвҖҷ generalization ability on a given dataset quickly. The same relation holds for kрқ‘ҳk-GNNs and the kрқ‘ҳk-WL\xspace. Moreover, our results extend easily to recent, more expressive GNN enhancements, e.g., subgraph-basedВ [Bouritsas etВ al., 2020] or subgraph-enhanced GNNsВ [Bevilacqua etВ al., 2022, Qian etВ al., 2022]. Hence, our results lead to a better understanding of how expressivity influences the learning performance of modern GNN architectures; seeВ SectionВ C.1.

Although the results are the first ones explicitly drawing a tight connection between expressivity and generalization, there are still many open questions. First, excludingВ PropositionВ 1, the results investigate specific GNN classes using sum aggregation.Hence, the results should be extended to specific GNN layers commonly used in practice, such as that inВ Xu etВ al. [2019], and the effect of different aggregation functions, such as max or mean, should be studied in detail. Moreover, the results only give meaningful results for discretely labeled graphs. Hence, the results should be extended to attributed graphs. Secondly, although the experimental results inВ SectionВ 5 suggest that our VC dimension bounds hold in practice to some extent, it is well known that they do not explain the generalization behavior of deep neural networks in the over-parameterized regime, as shown inВ Bartlett etВ al. [2019b], trained with variants of stochastic gradient descent. Therefore, it is a future challenge to understand how graph structure influences the generalization properties of over-parameterized GNNs, trained with variants of stochastic gradient descent and what role the WeisfeilerвҖ“Leman algorithm plays in this context.

We investigated GNNsвҖҷ generalization capabilities through the lens of VC dimension theory in different settings. Specifically, when not assuming a bound on the graphsвҖҷ order, we showed that the VC dimension tightly depends on the bitlength of the GNNsвҖҷ weights. We further showed that the number of colors computed by the 111-WL, besides the number of parameters and layers, influences the VC dimension.When a bound on the graphsвҖҷ order is known, we upper and lower bounded GNNsвҖҷ VC dimension via the maximal number of graphs distinguishable by the 111-WL. Thus, our theory provides the first link between expressivity results and generalization. Further, our theory also applies to a large set of recently proposed GNN enhancements.
