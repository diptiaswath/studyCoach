Subspace clustering (Parsonset al., 2004; Von Luxburg, 2007; Vidal, 2011; Simet al., 2013) assumes that the data points lie in a union of low-dimensional subspaces and segments the data into different groups corresponding to different subspaces. Classical subspace clustering algorithms such as sparse subspace clustering (SSC) (Elhamifar andVidal, 2013), low-rank representation (LRR) (Liuet al., 2013), and their variants (Li and Vidal, 2015; Luet al., 2018; Zhanget al., 2019; Fanet al., 2021) are based on the self-expressive property (Elhamifar andVidal, 2013).These algorithms111This paper focuses on a relatively narrow definition of and approach to subspace clustering. More general problems and methods of subspace clustering can be found in (Simet al., 2013).  have two major steps. First, they learn an affinity matrix, of which the time complexity is O​(n2)𝑂superscript𝑛2O(n^{2}) or even O​(n3)𝑂superscript𝑛3O(n^{3}) in every iteration of the optimization. The second step is to perform spectral clustering on the affinity matrix, of which the eigenvalue decomposition of the Laplacian matrix has polynomial time complexity. As a result, these algorithms are not applicable to large-scale data (Chenet al., 2010) owing to their high time and space complexity.

Recently, a few fast subspace or spectral clustering algorithms were proposed for large-scale datasets (Fowlkeset al., 2004; Chen and Cai, 2011; Penget al., 2013; Wanget al., 2014; Youet al., 2016a, b; Li and Zhao, 2017; Liet al., 2020; Fanet al., 2018; Wuet al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020). For instance, (Chen and Cai, 2011) proposed a landmark-based spectral clustering algorithm: 1) produces a few landmark points using k-means; 2) computes the features of all data points via exploiting eigenvectors of the affinity matrix obtained from the landmark points; 3) performs k-means on the features to get the clusters. In (Penget al., 2013), the authors treated large-scale subspace clustering as an out-of-sample extension problem of SSC on a few selected landmark data points, in which the clustering problem on the remainders is solved via classification.In (Chenet al., 2018), the authors proposed an algorithm to directly optimize the normalized cut model and used an anchor-based strategy to extend the algorithm to large-scale data.In (Matsushima andBrbic, 2019), a method called S5C was proposed as a scalable variant of SSC. The method first selects a small subset of the data points by performing sparse representation iteratively; then it performs sparse representation again for all data points using the selected samples and constructs an affinity matrix for all data points; lastly, it uses orthogonal iteration to compute the required eigenvectors for clustering.

Although the aforementioned large-scale subspace clustering methods have achieved considerable success in numerous applications, they still have a few limitations. First, those methods often start with a few samples of the dataset and then expand the representation coefficients to all data. Thus the clustering cannot effectively exploit the whole information of the dataset, which may reduce the clustering accuracy. Second, those methods usually have to store the affinity matrix and compute eigenvectors, which prevent the application to extremely large datasets. Finally, those methods are not effective in handling sparse noise, outliers, missing data, and streaming data, which are pervasive in real applications.

To handle the aforementioned problems, this paper presents a method called k-Factorization Subspace Clustering (k-FSC), which directly factorizes the data matrix into k𝑘k groups corresponding to k𝑘k subspaces. The contributions of this work are as follows.

(1) The paper proposes a group-sparse factorization model for subspace clustering. The method k-FSC does not need to learn an affinity matrix and perform spectral clustering. The time and space complexity of the method are linear with the number of data points.

(2) The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the effectiveness of the k-FSC model.

(3) The paper provides an efficient algorithm with convergence guarantee for the nonconvex nonsmooth optimization of k-FSC.

(4) The paper provides online extension and out-of-sample extension for k-FSC to handle arbitrarily large datasets.

(5) The paper extends k-FSC to robust clustering that is able to handle sparse noise, outliers, and missing data.

Extensive experiments show that the proposed methods222The MATLAB codes of the proposed methods are available at https://github.com/jicongfan/K-Factorization-Subspace-Clustering. outperform the state-of-the-art methods of large-scale clustering.

Section 5 discusses the connection with previous work. Section 6 details the experiments. Section 7 presents the conclusion of this paper.

The proposed k-FSC has a connection with nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) (Lee and Seung, 2001).It is known that k-means clustering can be formulated as NMF (Dinget al., 2005). Therefore, both NMF and k-FSC factorize the data into k clusters directly. The difference is that NMF aims to find the cluster centers while k-FSC aims to find the subspaces.

K-FSC is also closely related to the k-plane clustering (k-PC) (Bradley andMangasarian, 2000; Heet al., 2016),which aims to minimize the sum of residuals of data points to their assigned subspace.An efficient method to solve k-PC is performing assignment and learn the subspace bases alternately: cluster the data points by their nearest subspaces and update the subspace bases by PCA on the data points in each cluster. K-PC is sensitive to initialization (Heet al., 2016), subspace dimension estimation, missing data, and outliers (Gitlinet al., 2018).

The model of k-FSC can be regarded as a variant of dictionary learning and sparse coding (DLSC) (Mairalet al., 2009). Similar to (Szabó et al., 2011; Ramirez et al., 2010; Suo et al., 2014)444Structured dictionary was also considered in compressed sensing (Eldaret al., 2010) but the dictionary is not unknown in that case., k-FSC also considers structured dictionary. It is worth pointing out that, in these previous work, the regularization on the coefficients matrix is ℓ1subscriptℓ1\ell_{1} norm. In contrast, k-FSC puts ℓ21subscriptℓ21\ell_{21} norm on the k𝑘k sub-matrices of the coefficients matrix to make it be group-sparse, which enables us to factorize the data matrix into k𝑘k groups directly. In (Sprechmann andSapiro, 2010), the authors proposed to perform DLSC and clustering alternately, which is-time consuming and not applicable to large datasets.

This paper has presented a linear-complexity method k-FSC for subspace clustering. K-FSC is able to handle arbitrarily large dataset, streaming data, sparse noise, outliers, and missing data. Extensive experiments showed that k-FSC and its extensions are more accurate and efficient than state-of-the-art methods of subspace clustering. This improvement stems from the following aspects. First, k-FSC, k-FSC-MB, and k-FSC-L can utilize much more data points in the learning step while most of the other methods require the subset be small enough to ensure the scalability. Second, in the proposed methods, the number of clusters, as an important information, is directly exploited. Other methods except k-PC do not use the information before the spectral clustering step. K-FSC-MB and k-FSC-L are very efficient in handling very large datasets and are as accurate as k-FSC is.Future study may focus on the sufficient conditions for k-FSC to succeed.
