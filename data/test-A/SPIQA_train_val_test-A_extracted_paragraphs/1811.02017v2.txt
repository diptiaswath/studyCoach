Through the use of convolution layers, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have a built-in understanding of locality and translational symmetry that is inherent in many learning problems.Because convolutions are translation equivariant (a shift of the input leads to a shift of the output), convolution layers preserve the translation symmetry.This is important, because it means that further layers of the network can also exploit the symmetry.вҖ вҖ *Qualcomm AI Research is an initiative of Qualcomm Technologies, Inc.

Motivated by the success of CNNs, many researchers have worked on generalizations, leading to a growing body of work on Group equivariant CNNs (G-CNNs) for signals on Euclidean space and the sphere [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7] as well as graphs [8, 9].With the proliferation of equivariant network layers, it has become difficult to see the relations between the various approaches. Furthermore, when faced with a new modality (diffusion tensor MRI, say), it may not be immediately obvious how to create an equivariant network for it, or whether a given kind of equivariant layer is the most general one.

In this paper we present a general theory of homogeneous G-CNNs.Feature spaces are modelled as spaces of fields on a homogeneous space.They are characterized by a group of symmetries GрқҗәG, a subgroup HвүӨGрқҗ»рқҗәH\leq G that together with GрқҗәG determines a homogeneous space BвүғG/Hsimilar-to-or-equalsрқҗөрқҗәрқҗ»B\simeq G/H, and a representation ПҒрқңҢ\rho of Hрқҗ»H that determines the type of field (vector, tensor, etc.).Related work is classified by (G,H,ПҒ)рқҗәрқҗ»рқңҢ(G,H,\rho).The main theorems say that equivariant linear maps between fields over BрқҗөB can be written as convolutions with an equivariant kernel, and that the space of equivariant kernels can be realized in three equivalent ways.We will assume some familiarity with groups, cosets, quotients, representations and related notions (see Appendix A).

This paper does not contain truly new mathematics (in the sense that a professional mathematician with expertise in the relevant subjects would not be surprised by our results), but instead provides a new formalism for the study of equivariant convolutional networks.This formalism turns out to be a remarkably good fit for describing real-world G-CNNs.Moreover, by describing G-CNNs in a language used throughout modern physics and mathematics (fields, fiber bundles, etc.), it becomes possible to apply knowledge gained over many decades in those domains to machine learning.

This paper has two main parts.First, in Sec. 2, we introduce a mathematical model for convolutional feature spaces.The basic idea is that feature maps represent fields over a homogeneous space.As it turns out, defining the notion of a field is quite a bit of work.So in order to motivate the introduction of each of the required concepts, we will in this section provide an overview of the relevant concepts and their relations, using the example of a Spherical CNN with vector field feature maps.

The second part of this paper (Section 3) is about maps between the feature spaces.We require these to be equivariant, and focus in particular on the linear layers.The main theorems (3.1вҖ“3.4)show that linear equivariant maps between the feature spaces are in one-to-one correspondence with equivariant convolution kernels (i.e. convolution is all you need), and that the space of equivariant kernels can be realized as a space of matrix-valued functions on a group, coset space, or double coset space, subject to linear constraints.

In order to specify a convolutional feature space, we need to specify two things: a homogeneous space BрқҗөB over which the field is defined, and the type of field (e.g. vector field, tensor field, etc.).A homogeneous space for a group GрқҗәG is a space BрқҗөB where for any two x,yвҲҲBрқ‘Ҙрқ‘Ұрқҗөx,y\in B there is a transformation gвҲҲGрқ‘”рқҗәg\in G that relates them via gвҖӢx=yрқ‘”рқ‘Ҙрқ‘Ұgx=y.Here we consider the example of a vector field on the sphere B=S2рқҗөsuperscriptрқ‘Ҷ2B=S^{2} with symmetry group G=SOвҒЎ(3)рқҗәSO3G=\operatorname{SO}(3), the group of 3D rotations.The sphere is a homogeneous space for SOвҒЎ(3)SO3\operatorname{SO}(3) because we can map any point on the sphere to any other via a rotation.

Formally, a field is defined as a section of a vector bundle associated to a principal bundle.In order to understand what this means, we must first know what a fiber bundle is (Sec. 2.1), and understand how the group GрқҗәG can be viewed as a principal bundle (Sec. 2.2).Briefly, a fiber bundle formalizes the idea of parameterizing a set of identical spaces called fibers by another space called the base space.

The first way in which fiber bundles play a role in the theory is that the action of GрқҗәG on BрқҗөB allows us to think of GрқҗәG as a вҖңbundle of groupsвҖқ or principal bundle.Roughly speaking, this works as follows:if we fix an origin oвҲҲBрқ‘ңрқҗөo\in B, we can consider the stabilizer subgroup HвүӨGрқҗ»рқҗәH\leq G of transformations that leave oрқ‘ңo unchanged: H={gвҲҲG|gвҖӢo=o}рқҗ»conditional-setрқ‘”рқҗәрқ‘”рқ‘ңрқ‘ңH=\{g\in G\;|\;go=o\}.For example, on the sphere the stabilizer is SOвҒЎ(2)SO2\operatorname{SO}(2), the group of rotations around the axis through oрқ‘ңo (e.g. the north pole).As we will see in Section 2.2, this allows us to view GрқҗәG as a bundle with base space BвүғG/Hsimilar-to-or-equalsрқҗөрқҗәрқҗ»B\simeq G/H and a fiber Hрқҗ»H.This is shown for the sphere in Fig. 1 (cartoon).In this case, we can think of SOвҒЎ(3)SO3\operatorname{SO}(3) as a bundle of circles (H=SOвҒЎ(2)рқҗ»SO2H=\operatorname{SO}(2)) over the sphere, which itself is the quotient S2вүғSOвҒЎ(3)/SOвҒЎ(2)similar-to-or-equalssuperscriptрқ‘Ҷ2SO3SO2S^{2}\simeq\operatorname{SO}(3)/\operatorname{SO}(2).

To define the associated bundle (Sec. 2.3) we take the principal bundle GрқҗәG and replace the fiber Hрқҗ»H by a vector space Vрқ‘үV on which Hрқҗ»H acts linearly via a group representation ПҒрқңҢ\rho.This yields a vector bundle with the same base space BрқҗөB and a new fiber Vрқ‘үV.For example, the tangent bundle of S2superscriptрқ‘Ҷ2S^{2} (Fig. 2) is obtained by replacing the circular SOвҒЎ(2)SO2\operatorname{SO}(2) fibers in Fig. 1 by 2D planes. Under the action of H=SOвҒЎ(2)рқҗ»SO2H=\operatorname{SO}(2), a tangent vector at the north pole is rotated (even though the north pole itself is fixed by SOвҒЎ(2)SO2\operatorname{SO}(2)), so we let ПҒвҖӢ(h)рқңҢв„Һ\rho(h) be a 2Г—2222\times 2 rotation matrix.In a general convolutional feature space with nрқ‘ӣn channels, Vрқ‘үV would be an nрқ‘ӣn-dimensional vector space.Finally, fields are defined as sections of this bundle, i.e. an assignment to each point xрқ‘Ҙx of an element in the fiber over xрқ‘Ҙx (see Fig. 3).

Having defined the feature space, we need to specify how it transforms (e.g. say how a vector field on S2superscriptрқ‘Ҷ2S^{2} is rotated).The natural way to transform a ПҒрқңҢ\rho-field is via the induced representation ПҖ=IndHGвҒЎПҒрқңӢsuperscriptsubscriptIndрқҗ»рқҗәрқңҢ\pi=\operatorname{Ind}_{H}^{G}\rho of GрқҗәG (Section 2.4),which combines the action of GрқҗәG on the base space BрқҗөB and the action of ПҒрқңҢ\rho on the fiber Vрқ‘үV to produce an action on sections of the associated bundle (See Figure 3).Finally, having defined the feature spaces and their transformation laws, we can study equivariant linear maps between them (Section 3).In Sec. 4вҖ“6 we cover implementation aspects, related work, and concrete examples, respectively.

Intuitively, a fiber bundle is a parameterization of a set of isomorphic spaces (the fibers) by another space (the base).For example, we can think of a feature space in a classical CNN as a set of vector spaces Vxвүғв„қnsimilar-to-or-equalssubscriptрқ‘үрқ‘Ҙsuperscriptв„қрқ‘ӣV_{x}\simeq\mathbb{R}^{n} (nрқ‘ӣn being the number of channels), one per position xрқ‘Ҙx in the plane [2].This is an example of a trivial bundle, because it is simply the Cartesian product of the plane and в„қnsuperscriptв„қрқ‘ӣ\mathbb{R}^{n}.General fiber bundles are only locally trivial, meaning that they locally look like a product while having a different global topological structure.

The simplest example of a non-trivial bundle is the Mobius strip, which locally looks like a product of the circle (the base) with a line segment (the fiber), but is globally distinct from a cylinder (see Fig. 4).A more practically relevant example is given by the tangent bundle of the sphere (Fig. 2), which has as base space S2superscriptрқ‘Ҷ2S^{2} and fibers that look like в„қ2superscriptв„қ2\mathbb{R}^{2}, but is topologically distinct from S2Г—в„қ2superscriptрқ‘Ҷ2superscriptв„қ2S^{2}\times\mathbb{R}^{2} as a bundle.

Various more refined notions of fiber bundle exist, each corresponding to a different kind of fiber.In this paper we will work with principal bundles (bundles of groups) and vector bundles (bundles of vector spaces).

A section sрқ‘ s of a fiber bundle is an assignment to each xвҲҲBрқ‘Ҙрқҗөx\in B of an element sвҖӢ(x)вҲҲFxрқ‘ рқ‘Ҙsubscriptрқҗ№рқ‘Ҙs(x)\in F_{x}.Formally, it is a map s:BвҶ’E:рқ‘ вҶ’рқҗөрқҗёs:B\rightarrow E that satisfies pвҲҳs=idBрқ‘қрқ‘ subscriptidрқҗөp\circ s=\mathrm{id}_{B}.If the bundle is trivial, a section is equivalent to a function f:BвҶ’F:рқ‘“вҶ’рқҗөрқҗ№f:B\rightarrow F, but for a non-trivial bundle we cannot continuously align all the fibers simultaneously, and so we must keep each sвҖӢ(x)рқ‘ рқ‘Ҙs(x) in its own fiber Fxsubscriptрқҗ№рқ‘ҘF_{x}.Nevertheless, on a trivializing neighbourhood UвҠҶBрқ‘ҲрқҗөU\subseteq B, we can describe the section as a function sU:UвҶ’F:subscriptрқ‘ рқ‘ҲвҶ’рқ‘Ҳрқҗ№s_{U}:U\rightarrow F, by setting ПҶвҖӢ(sвҖӢ(x))=(x,sUвҖӢ(x))рқң‘рқ‘ рқ‘Ҙрқ‘Ҙsubscriptрқ‘ рқ‘Ҳрқ‘Ҙ\varphi(s(x))=(x,s_{U}(x)).

Recall (Sec. 1.1) that with every feature space of a G-CNN is associated a homogeneous space BрқҗөB (e.g. the sphere, projective space, hyperbolic space, Grassmann & Stiefel manifolds, etc.), and recall further that such a space has a stabilizer subgroup H={gвҲҲG|gвҖӢo=o}рқҗ»conditional-setрқ‘”рқҗәрқ‘”рқ‘ңрқ‘ңH=\{g\in G\;|\;go=o\} (this group being independent of origin oрқ‘ңo up to isomorphism).As discussed in Appendix A, the cosets gвҖӢHрқ‘”рқҗ»gH of Hрқҗ»H (e.g. the circles in Fig. 1) partition GрқҗәG, and the set of cosets, denoted G/Hрқҗәрқҗ»G/H (e.g. the sphere in Fig. 1), can be identified with BрқҗөB (up to a choice of origin).

It is this partitioning of GрқҗәG into cosets that induces a special kind of bundle structure on GрқҗәG.The projection map that defines the bundle structure sends an element gвҲҲGрқ‘”рқҗәg\in G to the coset gвҖӢHрқ‘”рқҗ»gH it belongs to.Thus, it is a map p:GвҶ’G/H:рқ‘қвҶ’рқҗәрқҗәрқҗ»p:G\rightarrow G/H, and we have a bundle with total space GрқҗәG, base space G/Hрқҗәрқҗ»G/H and canonical fiber Hрқҗ»H.Intuitively, this allows us to think of GрқҗәG as a base space G/Hрқҗәрқҗ»G/H with a copy of Hрқҗ»H attached at each point xвҲҲG/Hрқ‘Ҙрқҗәрқҗ»x\in G/H.The copies of Hрқҗ»H are glued together in a potentially twisted manner.

This bundle is called a principal Hрқҗ»H-bundle, because we have a transitive and fixed-point free group action GГ—HвҶ’GвҶ’рқҗәрқҗ»рқҗәG\times H\rightarrow G that preserves the fibers.This action is given by right multiplication, gвҶҰgвҖӢhmaps-toрқ‘”рқ‘”в„Һg\mapsto gh, which preserves fibers because pвҖӢ(gвҖӢh)=gвҖӢhвҖӢH=gвҖӢH=pвҖӢ(g)рқ‘қрқ‘”в„Һрқ‘”в„Һрқҗ»рқ‘”рқҗ»рқ‘қрқ‘”p(gh)=ghH=gH=p(g).That is, by right-multiplying an element gвҲҲGрқ‘”рқҗәg\in G by hвҲҲHв„Һрқҗ»h\in H, we get an element gвҖӢhрқ‘”в„Һgh that is in general different from gрқ‘”g but is still within the same coset (i.e. fiber).That the action is transitive and free on cosets follows immediately from the group axioms.

One can think of a principal bundle as a bundle of generalized frames or gauges relative to which geometrical quantities can be expressed numerically.Under this interpretation the fiber at xрқ‘Ҙx is a space of generalized frames, and the action by Hрқҗ»H is a change of frame.For instance, each point on the circles in Fig. 1 can be identified with a right-handed orthogonal frame, and the action of SOвҒЎ(2)SO2\operatorname{SO}(2) corresponds to a rotation of this frame.The group Hрқҗ»H may also include internal symmetries, such as color space rotations, which do not relate in any way to the spatial dimensions of BрқҗөB.

In order to numerically represent a field on some neighbourhood UвҠҶG/Hрқ‘Ҳрқҗәрқҗ»U\subseteq G/H, we need to choose a frame for each xвҲҲUрқ‘Ҙрқ‘Ҳx\in U in a continuous manner.This is formalized as a section of the principal bundle.Recall that a section of p:GвҶ’G/H:рқ‘қвҶ’рқҗәрқҗәрқҗ»p:G\rightarrow G/H is a map s:G/HвҶ’G:рқ‘ вҶ’рқҗәрқҗ»рқҗәs:G/H\rightarrow G that satisfies pвҲҳs=idG/Hрқ‘қрқ‘ subscriptidрқҗәрқҗ»p\circ s=\mathrm{id}_{G/H}.Since pрқ‘қp projects gрқ‘”g to its coset gвҖӢHрқ‘”рқҗ»gH, the section chooses a representative sвҖӢ(gвҖӢH)вҲҲgвҖӢHрқ‘ рқ‘”рқҗ»рқ‘”рқҗ»s(gH)\in gH for each coset gвҖӢHрқ‘”рқҗ»gH.Non-trivial principal bundles do not have continuous global sections, but we can always use a local section on UвҠҶG/Hрқ‘Ҳрқҗәрқҗ»U\subseteq G/H, and represent a field on overlapping local patches covering G/Hрқҗәрқҗ»G/H.

Aside from the right action of Hрқҗ»H, which turns GрқҗәG into a principal Hрқҗ»H-bundle, we also have a left action of GрқҗәG on itself, as well as an action of GрқҗәG on the base space G/Hрқҗәрқҗ»G/H.In general, the action of GрқҗәG on G/Hрқҗәрқҗ»G/H does not agree with the action on GрқҗәG, in that gвҖӢsвҖӢ(x)вү sвҖӢ(gвҖӢx)рқ‘”рқ‘ рқ‘Ҙрқ‘ рқ‘”рқ‘Ҙgs(x)\neq s(gx), because the action on GрқҗәG includes a twist of the fiber.This twist is described by the function h:G/HГ—GвҶ’H:hвҶ’рқҗәрқҗ»рқҗәрқҗ»\mathrm{h}:G/H\times G\rightarrow H defined by gвҖӢsвҖӢ(x)=sвҖӢ(gвҖӢx)вҖӢhвҖӢ(x,g)рқ‘”рқ‘ рқ‘Ҙрқ‘ рқ‘”рқ‘Ҙhрқ‘Ҙрқ‘”gs(x)=s(gx)\mathrm{h}(x,g) (whenever both sвҖӢ(x)рқ‘ рқ‘Ҙs(x) and sвҖӢ(gвҖӢx)рқ‘ рқ‘”рқ‘Ҙs(gx) are defined).This function will be used in various calculations below.We note for the interested reader that hh\mathrm{h} satisfies the cocycle condition hвҖӢ(x,g1вҖӢg2)=hвҖӢ(g2вҖӢx,g1)вҖӢhвҖӢ(x,g2)hрқ‘Ҙsubscriptрқ‘”1subscriptрқ‘”2hsubscriptрқ‘”2рқ‘Ҙsubscriptрқ‘”1hрқ‘Ҙsubscriptрқ‘”2\mathrm{h}(x,g_{1}g_{2})=\mathrm{h}(g_{2}x,g_{1})\mathrm{h}(x,g_{2}).

Feature spaces are defined as spaces of sections of the associated vector bundle, which we will now define.In physics, a section of an associated bundle is simply called a field.

To define the associated vector bundle, we start with the principal Hрқҗ»H-bundle GвҶ’рқ‘қG/Hрқ‘қвҶ’рқҗәрқҗәрқҗ»G\xrightarrow{p}G/H, and essentially replace the fibers (cosets) by vector spaces Vрқ‘үV.The space Vвүғв„қnsimilar-to-or-equalsрқ‘үsuperscriptв„қрқ‘ӣV\simeq\mathbb{R}^{n} carries a group representation ПҒрқңҢ\rho of Hрқҗ»H that describes the transformation behaviour of the feature vectors in Vрқ‘үV under a change of frame.These features could for instance transform as a scalar, a vector, a tensor, or some other geometrical quantity [2, 6, 8].Figure 3 shows an example of a vector field (ПҒвҖӢ(h)рқңҢв„Һ\rho(h) being a 2Г—2222\times 2 rotation matrix in this case) and a scalar field (ПҒвҖӢ(h)=1рқңҢв„Һ1\rho(h)=1).

The network as a whole is equivariant if all of its layers are equivariant.So our theory would not be complete without a discussion of equivariant nonlinearities and other kinds of layers.In a regular G-CNN [1], ПҒрқңҢ\rho is the regular representation of Hрқҗ»H, which means that it can be realized by permutation matrices.Since permutations and pointwise nonlinearities commute, any such nonlinearity can be used.For other kinds of representations ПҒрқңҢ\rho, special equivariant nonlinearities must be used.Some choices include norm nonlinearities [3] for unitary representations, tensor product nonlinearities [8], or gated nonlinearities where a scalar field is normalized by a sigmoid and then multiplied by another field [6].Other constructions, such as batchnorm and ResNets, can also be made equivariant [1, 2].AВ comprehensive overview and comparison over equivariant nonlinearities can be found inВ [7].

Several different approaches to implementing group equivariant CNNs have been proposed in the literature.The implementation details thereby depend on the specific choice of symmetry group GрқҗәG, the homogeneous space G/Hрқҗәрқҗ»G/H, its discretization and the representation ПҒрқңҢ\rho.In any case, since the equivariance constraints on convolution kernels are linear, the space of Hрқҗ»H-equivariant kernels is a linear subspace of the unrestricted kernel space.This implies that it is sufficient to solve for a basis of Hрқҗ»H-equivariant kernels, in terms of which any equivariant kernel can be expanded using learned weights.

A case of high practical importance are equivariant CNNs on Euclidean spaces в„қdsuperscriptв„қрқ‘‘\mathbb{R}^{d}.Implementations mostly operate on discrete pixel grids.In this case, the steerable kernel basis is typically pre-sampled on a small grid, linearly combined during the forward pass, and then used in a standard convolution routine.The sampling procedure requires particular attention since it might introduce aliasing artifactsВ [4, 6].A more in depth discussion of an implementation of equivariant CNNs, operating on Euclidean pixel grids, is provided inВ [7].Alternatively to processing signals on a pixel grid, signals on Euclidean spaces might be sampled on an irregular point cloud.In this case the steerable kernel space is typically implemented as an analytical function, which is subsequently sampled on the cloudВ [5].

Implementations of spherical CNNs depend on the choice of signal representation as well.InВ [10], the authors choose a spectral approach to represent the signal and kernels in Fourier space.The equivariant convolution is performed by exploiting the Fourier theorem.Other approaches define the convolution spatially.In these cases, some grid on the sphere is chosen on which the signal is sampled.As in the Euclidean case, the convolution is performed by matching the signal with a Hрқҗ»H-equivariant kernel, which is being expanded in terms of a pre-computed basis.

In this paper we have developed a general theory of equivariant convolutional networks on homogeneous spaces using the formalism of fiber bundles and fields.Field theories are the de facto standard formalism for modern physical theories, and this paper shows that the same formalism can elegantly describe the de facto standard learning machine: the convolutional network and its generalizations.By connecting this very successful class of networks to modern theories in mathematics and physics, our theory provides many opportunities for the development of new theoretical insights about deep learning, and the development of new equivariant network architectures.
