Understanding the causal structure of a problem is important for areas such as economics, biology (Sachs etВ al., 2005) and healthcare (Sanchez etВ al., 2022), especially when reasoning about the effect of interventions. When interventional data from randomised trials are not available, causal discovery methods (Glymour etВ al., 2019) may be employed to discover the causal structure of a problem solely from observational data. Causal structure is typically modelled as a directed acyclic graph (DAG) рқ’ўрқ’ў{\mathcal{G}} in which each node is associated with a random variable and each edge represents a causal mechanism i.e.В how one variable influences another.

However, learning such a model from data is NP-hard (Chickering, 1996). Traditional methods search the DAG space by testing for conditional independence between variables (Spirtes etВ al., 1993) or by optimising some goodness of fit measure (Chickering, 2002). Unfortunately, solving the search problem with a greedy combinatorial optimisation method can be expensive and does not scale to high-dimensional problems.

In line with previous work (Teyssier & Koller, 2005; Park & Klabjan, 2017; BГјhlmann etВ al., 2014; Solus etВ al., 2021; Wang etВ al., 2021; Rolland etВ al., 2022), we can speed up the combinatorial search problem over the space of DAGs by rephrasing it as a topological ordering task, ordering from leaf nodes to root nodes. The search space over DAGs with dрқ‘‘d nodes and (d2вҲ’d)/2superscriptрқ‘‘2рқ‘‘2(d^{2}-d)/2 possible edges is much larger than the space of permutations over dрқ‘‘d variables. Once a topological ordering of the nodes is found, the potential causal relations between later (cause) and earlier (effect) nodes can be pruned with a feature selection algorithm (e.g. BГјhlmann etВ al. (2014)) to yield a graph which is naturally directed and acyclic without further optimisation.

Rolland etВ al. (2022) estimate the Hessian point-wise with a second-order Stein gradient estimator (Li & Turner, 2018) over a radial basis function (RBF) kernel. However, point-wise estimation with kernels scales poorly to datasets with large number of samples nрқ‘ӣn because it requires inverting a nГ—nрқ‘ӣрқ‘ӣn\times n kernel matrix.

Here, we enable scalable causal discovery by utilising neural networks (NNs) trained with denoising diffusion instead of Rolland etВ al.вҖҷs kernel-based estimation. We use the ordering procedure, based on Rolland etВ al. (2022), which requires re-computing the scoreвҖҷs Jacobian at each iteration. Training NNs at each iteration would not be feasible. Interestingly, our algorithm does not require architectural constraints on the neural network, as in previous causal discovery methods based on neural networks (Lachapelle etВ al., 2020; Zheng etВ al., 2020; Yu etВ al., 2019; Ng etВ al., 2022). Our training procedure does not learn the causal mechanism directly, but the score of the data distribution.

Ordering-based Causal Discovery.В The observation that a causal DAG can be partially represented with a topological ordering goes back to Verma & Pearl (1990). Searching the topological ordering space instead of searching over the space of DAGs has been done with greedy Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) (Friedman & Koller, 2003), greedy hill-climbing search (Teyssier & Koller, 2005), arc search (Park & Klabjan, 2017), restricted maximum likelihood estimators (BГјhlmann etВ al., 2014), sparsest permutation (Raskutti & Uhler, 2018; Lam etВ al., 2022; Solus etВ al., 2021), and reinforcement learning (Wang etВ al., 2021). In linear additive models, Ghoshal & Honorio (2018); Chen etВ al. (2019) propose an approach, under some assumptions on the noise variances, to discover the causal graph by sequentially identifying leaves based on an estimation of the precision matrix.

Hessian of the Log-likelihood.В Estimating рқ‘ҜвҖӢ(logвҒЎpвҖӢ(рқҗұ))рқ‘Ҝрқ‘қрқҗұ{\bm{H}}(\log p({\mathbf{x}})) is the most expensive task of the ordering algorithm. Our baseline (Rolland etВ al., 2022) propose an extension of Li & Turner (2018) which utilises the SteinвҖҷs identity over a RBF kernel (SchГ¶lkopf & Smola, 2002). Rolland etВ al.вҖҷs method cannot obtain gradient estimates at positions out of the training samples. Therefore, evaluating the Hessian over a subsample of the training dataset is not possible. Other promising kernel-based approaches rely on spectral decomposition (Shi etВ al., 2018) solve this issue and can be promising future directions. Most importantly, computing the kernel matrix is expensive for memory and computation on nрқ‘ӣn. There are, however, methods (Achlioptas etВ al., 2001; Halko etВ al., 2011; Si etВ al., 2017) that help scaling kernel techniques, which were not considered in the present work. Other approaches are also possible with deep likelihood methods such as normalizing flows (Durkan etВ al., 2019; Dinh etВ al., 2017) and further compute the Hessian via backpropagation. This would require two backpropagation passes giving OвҖӢ(d2)рқ‘Ӯsuperscriptрқ‘‘2O(d^{2}) complexity and be less scalable than denoising diffusion. Indeed, preliminary experiments proved impractical in our high-dimensional settings.

We use DPMs because they can efficiently approximate the Hessian with a single backpropagation pass and while allowing Hessian evaluation on a subsample of the training dataset. It has been shown (Song & Ermon, 2019) that denoising diffusion can better capture the score than simple denoising (Vincent, 2011) because noise at multiple scales explore regions of low data density.

We have presented  a scalable method using DPMs for causal discovery. Since DPMs approximate the score of the data distribution, they can be used to efficiently compute the log-likelihoodвҖҷs Hessian by backpropagating each element of the output with respect to each element of the input. The deciduous score allows adjusting the score to remove the contribution of the leaf most recently removed, avoiding re-training the NN. Our empirical results show that neural networks can be efficiently used for topological ordering in high-dimensional graphs (up to 500500500 nodes) and with large datasets (up to 105superscript10510^{5} samples).

Our deciduous score can be used with other Hessian estimation techniques as long as obtaining the score and its full Jacobian is possible from a trained model, e.g.В sliced score matching (Song etВ al., 2020) and approximate backpropagation (Kingma & Cun, 2010). Updating the score is more practical than re-training in most settings with neural networks. Therefore, our theoretical result enables the community to efficiently apply new score estimation methods to topological ordering. Moreover, DPMs have been previously used generative diffusion models in the context of causal estimation (Sanchez & Tsaftaris, 2022). In this work, we have not explored the generative aspect such as Geffner etВ al. (2022) does with normalising flows. Finally, another promising direction involves constraining the NN architecture as in Lachapelle etВ al. (2020) with constrained optimisation losses (Zheng etВ al., 2018).
