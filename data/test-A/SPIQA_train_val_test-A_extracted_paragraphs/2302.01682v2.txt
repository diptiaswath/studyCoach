In recent years, there has been considerable research interest in Terahertz (THz) band for sixth-generation (6G) wireless cellular networks because of the availability of extremely wide bandwidth in THz spectrum. In particular, THz wireless communications is envisioned to provide significant enhancements in data rate (>100​Gb/sabsent100Gb/s>100\text{Gb/s}), extremely low propagation latency (<1​msabsent1ms<1\text{ms}), and ultra-reliability (99.999%percent99.99999.999\%) [1]. To this end, several signal processing challenges in this band are yet to be addressed including severe path loss arising from fading and molecular absorption, extremely-sparse channel model, very short transmission ranges, and beam-split; see [2, 3] and references therein, for details. In general, the severe path loss is addressed by deploying ultra-massive MIMO architectures, wherein subcarrier-independent (SI) analog beamformers are employed. This is analogous to massive multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) arrays used in millimeter-wave (mm-Wave) systems [4, 5]. In a wideband ultra-massive MIMO array, the directions of the generated beams at different subcarriers may point to different directions, or beam-split, because the analog beamformers are designed with respect to a single subcarrier [6, 2].

Further, conventional wireless systems operating at sub-666 GHz and mm-Wave bands employ far-field plane-wave models. But the THz communications range is much shorter than far-field assumption may not always hold [3]. In particular, when the transmission range is shorter than the Fraunhofer distance, the wavefront is spherical in the near-field [7]. As a result, channel acquisition algorithms should take into account both direction and range information [2].

The existing THz channel estimation techniques mostly rely on far-field signal model [8, 9, 10, 11]. The near-field scenarios in [12, 13] are limited to mm-Wave and ignore the effect of beam-split. The narrowband processing suggested in [12] considers hybrid (near- and far-field) models. On the other hand, several methods have been proposed to compensate the far-field beam-split for both THz channel estimation [9, 10, 8, 11] and beamforming [14, 15, 16] applications. Nevertheless, THz channel estimation in the presence of near-field beam-split (NB) remains relatively unexamined.

In this work, we introduce an NB-aware orthogonal matching pursuit (NBA-OMP) approach for wideband THz channel estimation. First, we define the NB model in terms of both user direction-of-arrival (DoA) and range parameters. Then, we design an NBA dictionary whose columns are subcarrier-dependent (SD), which spans the whole angular spectrum and transmission range up to the Fraunhofer distance. While the degree of beam-split is proportionally known prior to the DoA/range estimation, it depends on the unknown user location. For example, consider fmsubscript𝑓𝑚f_{m} and fcsubscript𝑓𝑐f_{c} to be the frequencies for the, respectively, m𝑚m-th and center subcarriers. When θ𝜃\theta is the physical user direction, then the spatial direction corresponding to the m𝑚m-th-subcarrier is shifted by fcfm​θsubscript𝑓𝑐subscript𝑓𝑚𝜃\frac{f_{c}}{f_{m}}\theta. We then employ the OMP algorithm, which accounts for this deviation, thereby ipso facto compensating the effect of NB. Numerical experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach that we compare with the existing THz channel-estimation-based methods [12, 11].

The range-dependent beampattern is observed in certain far-field applications such as frequency diverse array (FDA) radars [17], which employ linear frequency offsets across the antennas to yield a range dependent beampattern. This enables a joint estimation [17] of target angle and range, including in the presence of clutter [18]. However, the FDA wavefront is not spherical. Rydberg synthetic apertures [19, 20] also exhibit similar complex beampatterns but do not employ large arrays. Contrary to these prior works, we focus on spherical wavefront near-field for a THz communications system that employs an extremely large array and transmits multiple subcarriers via orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing (OFDM) signaling.

We introduced an NBA-OMP approach to offset the impact of beam-split for near-field channel estimation. The proposed approach is especially beneficial at THz, wherein the signal wavefront becomes spherical for the close-proximity users and the far-field assumption is no longer valid. We evaluated the performance of our NBA-OMP channel estimation and demonstrated that it is very close to the MMSE performance.
