Background.Early Classification algorithms classify time series before observing every timestep (Xing et al., 2009).These powerful methods give users enough time to react in time-sensitive domains.For example, early warning systems help clinicians improve their patients’ outcomes (Yala et al., 2022; Zhaoet al., 2019).Still, the best time to classify depends on the task and a given time series because signals are often sporadic.Too early and it may be a false alarm, fatiguing the end-user (Graham and Cvach, 2010).Too late and the user cannot react.An ideal classification is both early and accurate, but these goals conflict: an early classification may ignore relevant future data, as illustrated in Figure 1.

State-of-the-art.Early classification of time series (ECTS) is advancing quickly.Recent approaches overcome the poor scaling and false alarms inherent to classic ECTS (Wu et al., 2021) via reinforcement learning, predicting whether to Stop or Wait at every step of an ongoing series (Martinez et al., 2019; Hartvigsen et al., 2019, 2020; Yala et al., 2022).This approach counteracts overconfident classifiers, resulting in better predictions.In addition, by using neural networks they classify multivariate time series seamlessly, a setting known to challenge similarity search methods (Ghalwash et al., 2013, 2014; Heet al., 2015; Ye and Keogh, 2009; Xinget al., 2011).However, the state-of-the-art methods, along with all prior ECTS algorithms, disregard uneven gaps between observations.

Knowledge Gap.Despite rapid improvements, there remain ample opportunities to broaden the reach of early classifiers.In particular, existing early classifiers require that their input time series have even spaces between observations: they decide whether to stop early whenever any new observation arrives.Meanwhile, irregular time series (ITS), which have uneven and often-large gaps between observations, are ubiquitous in impactful domains like healthcare (Liptonet al., 2016), climate science (Bahadori and Liu, 2012), and astronomy (Richardset al., 2011).In a hospital, for example, clinicians quantify their patient’s health over time by taking measurements (laboratory tests, vital signs, microbiology tests, etc.).Such ITS are often multivariate and irregular between variables: measuring one variable does not mean measuring all.

To model ITS data effectively, we must acknowledge and leverage asynchronous observations (Rubanovaet al., 2019).Specifically, successful ITS classifiers should consider patterns of irregularity; different classes often have unique signatures in their observations’ timing (Liptonet al., 2016).For example, diabetes-risk patients receive more blood sugar tests while in the hospital.The early classification community has yet to consider this common, rich, and general type of time series data.

Problem Definition.Our work is the first to consider early classification of irregular time series, which is an open, impactful, and challenging real-world problem.For a previously-unseen ITS X𝑋X, we seek one small (early) real-valued time τ𝜏\tau at which the entire series X𝑋X may be classified accurately without using any observations later than τ𝜏\tau.The goals of earliness and accuracy naturally conflict because early predictions are usually made at the expense of accuracy (fewer observations have been collected).This is a multi-objective problem, which we compare to other settings in Figure 2.A key application of this problem is in healthcare, where clinicians require early and accurate predictions, yet use almost entirely with irregular time series (Janeet al., 2016; Yala et al., 2022; Zhaoet al., 2019; Alavi et al., 2022).

Challenges.Our problem is challenging for three main reasons:•Conflicting Objectives: Earliness and accuracy contradict each other, so a balance must be struck, where the relative importance of each goal may change depending on the task.This trade-off may also change for each instance. •Unknown Halting Times: The optimal halting time τ𝜏\tau is unobserved and unavailable for supervision or evaluation.•Irregular, multivariate observations: ITS are often sparse and multivariate. This is notoriously challenging for most machine learning methods, which typically require fixed-length and regularly spaced inputs. Signals for earliness and accuracy can also be found in patterns of missingness.

Proposed Method.We overcome these challenges, proposing the first early classifier for irregular time series, which we refer to as Stop&Hop in reference to our key idea.Stop&Hop instantiates a general and modular solution, integrating three essential components.First, a continuous-time recurrent Prefix Encoder embeds ITS data up to a candidate halting time, expanding on recent successes in representation learning for ITS.This handles irregular, multivariate observations by producing dense vector representations of ongoing time series.Then, a reinforcement learning-based Halting Policy Network decides whether or not to Stop and predict or Wait for more data. If it chooses to Wait, it predicts for how long and Hops forward in time before reactivating.Thus we formulate the early classification problem as a Partially-Observable Markov Decision Process with actions that operate on varying time scales.This formulation lets us encourage early stopping, even though the true halting times are unknown.The policy can thus adapt to variations in the irregularity of each input instance, using the observations’ timing to inform the halting time.Once the Halting Policy Network stops, a Prefix Classifier Network classifies the series.All components are trained jointly so that the conflicting objectives, earliness and accuracy, are easily balanced.

Contributions.Our contributions are as follows:(1)We define the open problem Early Classification of Irregular Time Series, bridging a major gap between modern early classifiers and real time-sensitive decision making.(2)Our method, Stop&Hop, is the first solution to this problem, integrating a continuous-time representation learner with two cooperative reinforcement learning agents.(3)We show that Stop&Hop classifies ITS earlier and more-accurately than state-of-the-art alternatives. We also show that Stop&Hop learns to stop exactly when signals arrive using four synthetic datasets, leading to the earliest-possible classifications.

Early Classification of Time Series.Early Classification of Time Series (ECTS) is a machine learning problem: correctly predict the label of a streaming time series using as few observations as possible (Guptaet al., 2020).Solutions choose one early timestep per time series at which the whole instance is classified (without cheating and looking at future values).Classifying sequences early is classically targeted at time series(Heet al., 2015; Xinget al., 2011; Xing et al., 2012; Ghalwash et al., 2014, 2013; Moriet al., 2018; Schäfer andLeser, 2020).Most recent approaches (Hartvigsen et al., 2020, 2019; Martinez et al., 2019; Denniset al., 2018; Ebihara et al., 2020) have turned to deep learning, extending beyond traditional methods for univariate time series (Moriet al., 2017; Xing et al., 2012, 2009; Xinget al., 2011), which scale poorly by exhaustively searching for discriminative subsequences (Heet al., 2015).The current best solution is to frame this problem as a Partially-Observe Markov Decision Process, where at each regularly-spaced timestep, a policy decides whether or not to stop and predict the label. Some halt RNNs early (Hartvigsen et al., 2019, 2020) while others use Deep Q-Networks (Martinez et al., 2019).

A major limitation of existing ECTS methods is their reliance on inputs being regularly-spaced; they decide whether or not to halt at each possible timestep.This does not account for missing values or gaps between observations,features essential to classifying ITS (Cheet al., 2018).In ITS, the gaps between consecutive observations may even be large andunpredictable, so waiting until the next value arrives has consequences. Furthermore, many ITS are multivariate, and multiple variables are rarely observed concurrently.This compounds issues with existing ECTS methods.Further, the times at which observations arrive can itself provide valuable knowledge for accuracy (Liptonet al., 2016) and earliness.A successful solution to our problem should take advantage of this extra source of information.

Learning from Irregular Time Series.Standard machine learning techniques often fail for ITS as they assume fixed-length and regularly-spaced inputs (Shukla and Marlin, 2020), which are especially rare in important medical settings (Sunet al., 2020).To bridge this gap, myriad recent works learn from ITS directly, developing models that take irregular series as inputs.Some approaches augment RNNs by either including auxiliary information such as a missingness-indicator (Liptonet al., 2016) or time-since-last-observation (Cheet al., 2018) as extra features to preserve properties found in the irregularity.Others build more complex value estimators by either learning generative models (Li and Marlin, 2020), using gaussian kernel adapters (Shukla and Marlin, 2019; Li and Marlin, 2016), set functions (Hornet al., 2020), or including decay mechanisms in Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) to encode information-loss when variables go unobserved over long periods of time (Mozeret al., 2017; Cheet al., 2018).Some recent works have begun parameterizing ordinary differential equations to serve as time series models (Kidgeret al., 2020; Lechner andHasani, 2020; Rubanovaet al., 2019; Jia and Benson, 2019),Some very recent models have also begun to integrate attention mechanisms into this estimation process (Shukla and Marlin, 2021; Chen and Chien, 2021; Tan et al., 2020).

However, ITS model considers when to return predictions to end users in the ongoing timeline. A key constraint of the early classification of irregular time series problem is that when classifying a series at a particular point in its timeline, we cannot use any future values.This constraint hinders the use of methods that use all observations during interpolation (Shukla and Marlin, 2019, 2021) or ODE models that encode sequences backwards (Rubanovaet al., 2019).Such methods are quickly becoming pervasive (Kidgeret al., 2020), though creating online models for ITS remains a burgeoning area (Morrillet al., 2021; Cheng et al., 2020; Weerakodyet al., 2021).

We propose an intuitive first solution to the open Early Classification of Irregular Time Series (ECITS) problem, which we name Stop&Hop and illustrate in Figure 3.The ultimate goal of our proposed method is to predict the best halting time τ𝜏\tau for a given series so as to balance the cost of delaying a prediction with the benefits of accuracy, according to the requirements of the task at hand.Thus, one halting timestep τ𝜏\tau is predicted per series X𝑋X along with a prediction y^^𝑦\hat{y} made using only observations made before time τ𝜏\tau.

Our work facilitates decision making given partial temporal information.This lack of knowledge can naturally lead to misclassifications, which are more or less dangerous depending on the task. For example, incorrectly predicting Cancer early may cause undue stress and financial burden to a patient.Still, the cost of risking a false positive must be balanced with the cost of delaying predictions, which itself may have negative impacts.Early classifiers do not suffer from or introduce this trade-off, and instead embrace reality—the trade-off is real in practice, regardless of the algorithm.Standard classifiers, for instance, ignore prediction timing, so they always pick one side of the trade-off.Algorithms like Stop&Hop crucially allow end-users to balance their own risk aversion.

Our work introduces the open Early Classification of Irregular Time Series problem.This is an important, challenging, and interesting problem that has yet to be considered by the early classification community.We provide a general formulation for solving this problem, which we instantiate as a modular framework that serves as an effective first solution, named Stop&Hop.Stop&Hop is a novel reinforcement learning-based continuous-time recurrent network that leverages irregularity in the inputs to inform classifications that are both early and accurate.By using irregularity to inform when to classify ongoing series, Stop&Hop advances beyond the state-of-the-art for early classification.Our irregularity-aware halting policy network chooses when to stop, which allows more-flexible halting policies than recent alternatives and shortens the reinforcement learning trajectories, leading to more stable training.We find that Stop&Hop indeed halts at the earliest-possible times on all four synthetic data setsand consistently outperforms alternatives onall three real datasets by making earlier and more-accurate predictions.

With this work, we also advocate for broadening the evaluation of machine predictions.Our community often evaluates machine learning models using solely accuracy (or a similar measure).However, in time-sensitive domains, accuracy is irrelevant if a model is used too late.We pose that earliness, along with other highly-impactful directions, like fairness and explainability, should also be considered when developing machine learning systems.
