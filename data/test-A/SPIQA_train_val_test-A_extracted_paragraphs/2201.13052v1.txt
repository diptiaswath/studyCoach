In low rank matrix completion, a well-known problem that appears in various applications, the task is to recover a rank-r𝑟r matrix X∗∈ℝn1×n2superscript𝑋superscriptℝsubscript𝑛1subscript𝑛2X^{*}\in\mathbb{R}^{n_{1}\times n_{2}} given few of its entries, where r≪min⁡{n1,n2}much-less-than𝑟subscript𝑛1subscript𝑛2r\ll\min\{n_{1},n_{2}\}.In the problem of inductive matrix completion (IMC), beyond being low rank, X∗superscript𝑋X^{*} is assumed to have additional structure as follows: its columns belong to the range of a known matrix A∈ℝn1×d1𝐴superscriptℝsubscript𝑛1subscript𝑑1A\in\mathbb{R}^{n_{1}\times d_{1}} and its rows belong to the range of a known matrix B∈ℝn2×d2𝐵superscriptℝsubscript𝑛2subscript𝑑2B\in\mathbb{R}^{n_{2}\times d_{2}}, where r≤d1≤n1𝑟subscript𝑑1subscript𝑛1r\leq d_{1}\leq n_{1} and r≤d2≤n2𝑟subscript𝑑2subscript𝑛2r\leq d_{2}\leq n_{2}. Hence, X∗superscript𝑋X^{*} may be written as X∗=A​M∗​B⊤superscript𝑋𝐴superscript𝑀superscript𝐵topX^{*}=AM^{*}B^{\top}, and the task reduces to finding the smaller matrix M∗∈ℝd1×d2superscript𝑀superscriptℝsubscript𝑑1subscript𝑑2M^{*}\in\mathbb{R}^{d_{1}\times d_{2}}.In practice, the low rank and/or the additional structure assumptions may hold only approximately, and in addition, the observed entries may be corrupted by noise.

The side information matrices A,B𝐴𝐵A,B may be viewed as feature representations. For example, in movies recommender systems, the task is to complete a matrix X∗superscript𝑋X^{*} of the ratings given by n1subscript𝑛1n_{1} users to n2subscript𝑛2n_{2} movies.The columns of A,B𝐴𝐵A,B may correspond to viewers’ demographic details (age, gender) and movies’ properties (length, genre), respectively [ABEV09, MCG+11, CZL+12, YL19].The underlying assumption in IMC is that uncovering the relations between the viewers and the movies in the feature space, as encoded in M∗superscript𝑀M^{*}, suffices to deduce the ratings X∗=A​M∗​B⊤superscript𝑋𝐴superscript𝑀superscript𝐵topX^{*}=AM^{*}B^{\top}.Other examples of IMC include multi-label learning [XJZ13, SCH+16, ZDG18], disease prediction from gene/miRNA/lncRNA data [ND14, CWQ+18, LYL+18] and link prediction in networks [ME11, CDH18].

If the side information matrices allow for a significant dimensionality reduction, namely d≪nmuch-less-than𝑑𝑛d\ll n where d=max⁡{d1,d2}𝑑subscript𝑑1subscript𝑑2d=\max\{d_{1},d_{2}\} and n=max⁡{n1,n2}𝑛subscript𝑛1subscript𝑛2n=\max\{n_{1},n_{2}\}, recovering X∗superscript𝑋X^{*} is easier from both theoretical and computational perspectives.From the information limit aspect, the minimal number of observed entries required to complete a matrix of rank r𝑟r with side information scales as 𝒪​(d​r)𝒪𝑑𝑟\mathcal{O}(dr), compared to 𝒪​(n​r)𝒪𝑛𝑟\mathcal{O}(nr) without side information. Similarly, the number of variables scale as d𝑑d rather than as n𝑛n, enabling more efficient computation and less memory.Finally, features also allow completion of rows and columns of X∗superscript𝑋X^{*} that do not have even a single observed entry.Unlike standard matrix completion which requires at least r𝑟r observed entries in each row and column of X∗superscript𝑋X^{*}, in IMC the feature vector is sufficient to inductively predict the full corresponding row/column; hence the name ’Inductive Matrix Completion’.

Several IMC methods were devised in the past years. Perhaps the most popular ones are nuclear norm minimization [XJZ13, LYL+18] and alternating minimization [JD13, ND14, ZJD15, CWQ+18]. Another recent method is multi-phase Procrustes flow [ZDG18]. While nuclear norm minimization enjoys strong recovery guarantees, it is computationally slow. Other methods are faster, but the number of observed entries for their recovery guarantees to hold depends on the condition number of X∗superscript𝑋X^{*}.

In this work, we make three contributions to the IMC problem.First, by deriving an RIP (Restricted Isometry Property) guarantee for IMC, we prove that under certain conditions the optimization landscape of IMC is benign (Theorem 3.1).Compared to a similar result derived in [GSG18], our guarantee requires significantly milder conditions, and in addition, addresses the vanilla IMC problem rather than a suitably regularized one.

Second, we propose a simple scheme to estimate the rank of X∗superscript𝑋X^{*} from its observed entries and the side information matrices A,B𝐴𝐵A,B. We also provide a theoretical guarantee for the accuracy of the estimated rank (Theorem 4.1), which holds for either exactly or approximately low rank X∗superscript𝑋X^{*} and with noisy measurements.

Third, we propose a simple Gauss-Newton based method to solve the IMC problem, that is both fast and enjoys strong recovery guarantees. Our algorithm, named GNIMC (Gauss-Newton IMC), is an adaptation of the GNMR algorithm [ZN22] to IMC.At each iteration, GNIMC solves a least squares problem; yet, its per-iteration complexity is of the same order as gradient descent. As a result, empirically, our tuning-free GNIMC implementation is 2 to 17 times faster than competing algorithms in various settings, including ill-conditioned matrices and very few observations, close to the information limit.

On the theoretical front, we prove that given a standard incoherence assumption on A,B𝐴𝐵A,B and sufficiently many observed entries sampled uniformly at random, GNIMC recovers X∗superscript𝑋X^{*} at a quadratic convergence rate (Theorem 5.1). As far as we know, this is the only available quadratic convergence rate guarantee for any IMC algorithm.In addition, we prove that GNIMC is stable against small arbitrary additive error (Theorem 5.4), which may originate from (i) inaccurate measurements of X∗superscript𝑋X^{*}, (ii) inaccurate side information, and/or (iii) X∗superscript𝑋X^{*} being only approximately low rank.

Remarkably, our guarantees do not require X∗superscript𝑋X^{*} to be incoherent, and the required number of observations depends only on properties of A,B𝐴𝐵A,B and not on those of X∗superscript𝑋X^{*}.Other guarantees have similar dependence on A,B𝐴𝐵A,B, but in addition either depend on the condition number of X∗superscript𝑋X^{*} and/or require incoherence of X∗superscript𝑋X^{*}, see Table 1.Relaxing the incoherence assumption on X∗superscript𝑋X^{*} is important, since X∗superscript𝑋X^{*} is only partially observed and such an assumption cannot be verified.In contrast, the matrices A,B𝐴𝐵A,B are known and their incoherence can be verified.

In this work, we presented three contributions to the IMC problem: benign optimization landscape guarantee; provable rank estimation scheme; and a simple Gauss-Newton based method, GNIMC, to solve the IMC problem. We derived recovery guarantees for GNIMC, and showed empirically that it is faster than several competing algorithms.A key theoretical contribution is a proof that under relatively mild conditions, IMC satisfies an RIP, similar to the matrix sensing problem.

Interestingly, in our simulations GNIMC recovers the matrix significantly faster than first-order methods, including a very recent one due to [TMC21].A possible explanation is that GNIMC makes large non-local updates, yet with the same time complexity as a single local gradient descent step.This raises the following intriguing questions: are there other non-convex problems for which non-local methods are faster than first-order ones?In particular, can these ideas be extended to faster training of deep neural networks?

Another interesting direction is extending our method to generalized frameworks of IMC. Important examples include recovering an unknown low rank X∗superscript𝑋X^{*} which lies in some known linear subspace instead of property (1) [JM18], and non-linear IMC [ZSJD19].
