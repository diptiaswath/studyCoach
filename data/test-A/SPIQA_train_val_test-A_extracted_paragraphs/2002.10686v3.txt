By asynchronously detecting brightness changes, event cameras offer a fundamentally different way to detect and characterise physical motion. Currently, active research is being conducted to employ event cameras in many areas, such as robotics/UAVsВ [10, 23], autonomous driving[34, 24, 33], and spacecraft navigationВ [8, 7]. While the utility of event cameras extends beyond motion perception, e.g., object recognition and trackingВ [28, 30], the focus of our work is on estimating visual motion using event cameras.

Due to the different nature of the data, new approaches are required to extract motion from event streams. A recent successful framework is contrast maximisation (CM)В [14]. Given an event stream, CM aims to find the motion parameters that yield the sharpest motion-compensated event image; see Fig.В 1. Intuitively, the correct motion parameters will align corresponding events, thereby producing an image with high contrast. We formally define CM below.

Recent improvements to CM include modifying the objective function to better suit the targeted settingsВ [12, 31]. However, the optimisation work horse remains locally optimal methods. Other frameworks for event processingВ [6, 20, 21, 13, 19, 7] conduct filtering, Hough transform, or specialised optimisation schemes; these are generally less flexible than CMВ [14]. There is also active research in applying deep learning to event dataВ [37, 38, 35, 29], which require a separate training phase on large datasets.

We focus on estimating rotational motion from events, which is useful for several applications, e.g., video stabilisationВ [15] and attitude estimationВ [8].

Specifically, we propose a BnB method for globally optimal CM for rotation estimation. Unlike previous CM techniques, our algorithm does not require external initialisations рққҺ(0)superscriptрққҺ0\bm{\omega}^{(0)}, and can guarantee finding the global solution рққҺвҲ—superscriptрққҺ\bm{\omega}^{*} toВ (6). Our core contributions are novel bounding functions for CM, whose theoretical validity are established. As we will show in Sec.В 4, while local methods generally produce acceptable resultsВ [15, 14], they often fail during periods with fast rotational motions. On the other hand, our global method always returns accurate results.
