Algorithms for non-convex optimization are one of the most important tools in modern machine learning, as training neural networks requires optimizing a non-convex objective.Given the abundance of data in many domains, the time to train a neural network is the current bottleneck to having bigger and more powerful machine learning models. Motivated by this need, the past few years have seen an explosion of research focused on understanding non-convex optimizationВ (Ghadimi & Lan, 2013; Carmon etВ al., 2017; Arjevani etВ al., 2019; 2020; Carmon etВ al., 2019; Fang etВ al., 2018). Despite significant progress, key issues remain unaddressed.

In this paper, we work to minimize a potentially non-convex objective F:в„қdвҶ’в„қ:рқҗ№вҶ’superscriptв„қрқ‘‘в„қF:\mathbb{R}^{d}\to\mathbb{R} which we only accesss in some stochastic or вҖңnoisyвҖқ manner.As motivation, consider FвҖӢ(рқҗұ)вүңрқ”јрқҗі[fвҖӢ(рқҗұ,рқҗі)]вүңрқҗ№рқҗұsubscriptрқ”јрқҗіdelimited-[]рқ‘“рқҗұрқҗіF({\mathbf{x}})\triangleq\mathop{\mathbb{E}}_{{\mathbf{z}}}[f({\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{z}})], where рқҗұрқҗұ{\mathbf{x}} can represent the model weights, рқҗірқҗі{\mathbf{z}} a minibatch of i.i.d. examples, and fрқ‘“f the loss of a model with parameters рқҗұрқҗұ{\mathbf{x}} on the minibatch рқҗірқҗі{\mathbf{z}}. In keeping with standard empirical practice, we will restrict ourselves to first order algorithms (gradient-based optimization).

The vast majority of prior analyses of non-convex optimization algorithms impose various smoothness conditions on the objectiveВ (Ghadimi & Lan, 2013; Carmon etВ al., 2017; Allen-Zhu, 2018; Tripuraneni etВ al., 2018; Fang etВ al., 2018; Zhou etВ al., 2018; Fang etВ al., 2019; Cutkosky & Orabona, 2019; Li & Orabona, 2019; Cutkosky & Mehta, 2020; Zhang etВ al., 2020a; Karimireddy etВ al., 2020; Levy etВ al., 2021; Faw etВ al., 2022; Liu etВ al., 2022). One motivation for smoothness assumptions is that they allow for a convenient surrogate for global minimization: rather than finding a global minimum of a neural networkвҖҷs loss surface (which may be intractable), we can hope to find an Пөitalic-Пө\epsilon-stationary point, i.e., a point рқҗұрқҗұ{\mathbf{x}} such that вҖ–вҲҮFвҖӢ(рқҗұ)вҖ–вүӨПөnormвҲҮрқҗ№рқҗұitalic-Пө\|\nabla F({\mathbf{x}})\|\leq\epsilon. By now, the fundamental limits on first order smooth non-convex optimization are well understood: Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) will find an Пөitalic-Пө\epsilon-stationary point in OвҖӢ(ПөвҲ’4)рқ‘Ӯsuperscriptitalic-Пө4O(\epsilon^{-4}) iterations, which is the optimal rateВ (Arjevani etВ al., 2019). Moreover, if Fрқҗ№F happens to be second-order smooth, SGD requires only OвҖӢ(ПөвҲ’3.5)рқ‘Ӯsuperscriptitalic-Пө3.5O(\epsilon^{-3.5}) iterations, which is also optimalВ (Fang etВ al., 2019; Arjevani etВ al., 2020). These optimality results motivate the popularity of SGD and its variants in practiceВ (Kingma & Ba, 2014; Loshchilov & Hutter, 2016; 2018; Goyal etВ al., 2017; You etВ al., 2019).

Unfortunately, many standard neural network architectures are non-smooth (e.g., architectures incorporating ReLUs or max-pools cannot be smooth). As a result, these analyses can only provide intuition about what might occur when an algorithm is deployed in practice: the theorems themselves do not apply (see also the discussion in, e.g., Li etВ al. (2021)). Despite the obvious need for non-smooth analyses, recent results suggest that even approaching a neighborhood of a stationary point may be impossible for non-smooth objectivesВ (Kornowski & Shamir, 2022b). Nevertheless, optimization clearly is possible in practice, which suggests that we may need to rethink our assumptions and goals in order to understand non-smooth optimization.

Fortunately, Zhang etВ al. (2020b) recently considered an alternative definition of stationarity that is tractable even for non-smooth objectives and which has attracted much interest (Davis etВ al., 2021; Tian etВ al., 2022; Kornowski & Shamir, 2022a; Tian & So, 2022; Jordan etВ al., 2022). Roughly speaking, instead of вҖ–вҲҮFвҖӢ(рқҗұ)вҖ–вүӨПөnormвҲҮрқҗ№рқҗұitalic-Пө\|\nabla F({\mathbf{x}})\|\leq\epsilon, we ask that there is a random variable рқҗІрқҗІ\mathbf{y} supported in a ball of radius Оҙрқӣҝ\delta about рқҗұрқҗұ{\mathbf{x}} such that вҖ–рқ”ј[вҲҮFвҖӢ(рқҗІ)]вҖ–вүӨПөnormрқ”јdelimited-[]вҲҮрқҗ№рқҗІitalic-Пө\|\mathop{\mathbb{E}}[\nabla F(\mathbf{y})]\|\leq\epsilon. We call such an рқҗұрқҗұ{\mathbf{x}} an (Оҙ,Пө)рқӣҝitalic-Пө(\delta,\epsilon)-stationary point, so that the previous definition (вҖ–вҲҮFвҖӢ(рқҗұ)вҖ–вүӨПөnormвҲҮрқҗ№рқҗұitalic-Пө\|\nabla F({\mathbf{x}})\|\leq\epsilon) is a (0,Пө)0italic-Пө(0,\epsilon)-stationary point. The current best-known complexity for identifying an (Оҙ,Пө)рқӣҝitalic-Пө(\delta,\epsilon) stationary point is OвҖӢ(ПөвҲ’4вҖӢОҙвҲ’1)рқ‘Ӯsuperscriptitalic-Пө4superscriptрқӣҝ1O(\epsilon^{-4}\delta^{-1}) stochastic gradient evaluations.

In this paper, we significantly improve this result: we can identify an (Оҙ,Пө)рқӣҝitalic-Пө(\delta,\epsilon)-stationary point with only OвҖӢ(ПөвҲ’3вҖӢОҙвҲ’1)рқ‘Ӯsuperscriptitalic-Пө3superscriptрқӣҝ1O(\epsilon^{-3}\delta^{-1}) stochastic gradient evaluations. Moreover, we also show that this rate is optimal. Our primary technique is a novel online-to-non-convex conversion: a connection between non-convex stochastic optimization and online learning, which is a classical field of learning theory that already has a deep literatureВ (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006; Hazan, 2019; Orabona, 2019). In particular, we show that an online learning algorithm that satisfies a shifting regret can be used to decide the update step, when fed with linear losses constructed using the stochastic gradients of the function Fрқҗ№F. By establishing this connection, we hope to open new avenues for algorithm design in non-convex optimization and also motivate new research directions in online learning.

In addition to the papers discussed in the introduction, here we discuss further related work.

In this paper we build on top of the definition of (Оҙ,Пө)рқӣҝitalic-Пө(\delta,\epsilon)-stationary points proposed by Zhang etВ al. (2020b). There, they prove a complexity rate of OвҖӢ(ПөвҲ’4вҖӢОҙвҲ’1)рқ‘Ӯsuperscriptitalic-Пө4superscriptрқӣҝ1O(\epsilon^{-4}\delta^{-1}) for stochastic Lipschitz functions, which we improve to OвҖӢ(ПөвҲ’3вҖӢОҙвҲ’1)рқ‘Ӯsuperscriptitalic-Пө3superscriptрқӣҝ1O(\epsilon^{-3}\delta^{-1}) and prove the optimality of this result.

The idea to reduce machine learning to online learning was pioneered by Cesa-Bianchi etВ al. (2004) with the online-to-batch conversion. There are also few papers that explore the possibility to transform non-convex problems into online learning ones. Ghai etВ al. (2022) provides some conditions under which online gradient descent on non-convex losses is equivalent to a convex online mirror descent. Hazan etВ al. (2017) defines a notion of regret which can be used to find approximate stationary points of smooth objectives. Zhuang etВ al. (2019) transform the problem of tuning of learning rates in stochastic non-convex optimization into an online learning problem. Our proposed approach differs from all the ones above in applying to non-smooth objectives. Moreover, we employ online learning algorithms with shifting regretВ Herbster & Warmuth (1998) to generate the updates (i.e. the differences between successive iterates), rather than the iterates themselves.

We have presented a new online-to-non-convex conversion technique that applies online learning algorithms to non-convex and non-smooth stochastic optimization. When used with online gradient descent, this achieves the optimal ПөвҲ’3вҖӢОҙвҲ’1superscriptitalic-Пө3superscriptрқӣҝ1\epsilon^{-3}\delta^{-1} complexity for finding (Оҙ,Пө)рқӣҝitalic-Пө(\delta,\epsilon) stationary points.

These results suggest new directions for work in online learning. Much past work is motivated by the online-to-batch conversion relating static regret to convex optimization. We employ switching regret for non-convex optimization. More refined analysis may be possible via generalizations such as strongly adaptive or dynamic regret (Daniely etВ al., 2015; Jun etВ al., 2017; Zhang etВ al., 2018; Jacobsen & Cutkosky, 2022; Cutkosky, 2020; Lu etВ al., 2022; Luo etВ al., 2022; Zhang etВ al., 2021; Baby & Wang, 2022; Zhang etВ al., 2022). Moreover, our analysis assumes perfect tuning of constants (e.g., D,T,Kрқҗ·рқ‘ҮрқҗҫD,T,K) for simplicity. In practice, we would prefer to adapt to unknown parameters, motivating new applications and problems for adaptive online learning, which is already an area of active current investigation (see, e.g., Orabona & PГЎl, 2015; Hoeven etВ al., 2018; Cutkosky & Orabona, 2018; Cutkosky, 2019; Mhammedi & Koolen, 2020; Chen etВ al., 2021; Sachs etВ al., 2022; Wang etВ al., 2022). It is our hope that some of this expertise can be applied in the non-convex setting as well.

Finally, our results leave an important question unanswered: the current best-known algorithm for deterministic non-smooth optimization still requires OвҖӢ(ПөвҲ’3вҖӢОҙвҲ’1)рқ‘Ӯsuperscriptitalic-Пө3superscriptрқӣҝ1O(\epsilon^{-3}\delta^{-1}) iterations to find a (Оҙ,Пө)рқӣҝitalic-Пө(\delta,\epsilon)-stationary pointВ (Zhang etВ al., 2020b). We achieve this same result even in the stochastic case. Thus it is natural to wonder if one can improve in the deterministic setting.For example, is the OвҖӢ(ПөвҲ’3/2вҖӢОҙвҲ’1/2)рқ‘Ӯsuperscriptitalic-Пө32superscriptрқӣҝ12O(\epsilon^{-3/2}\delta^{-1/2}) complexity we achieve in the smooth setting also achievable in the non-smooth setting?
