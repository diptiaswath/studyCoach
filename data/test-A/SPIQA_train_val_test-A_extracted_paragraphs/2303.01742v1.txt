The growing amount of computational power has led to the widespread use of deep neural networks, such as sentiment analysis, face recognition, autonomous driving, etc.In the meantime, large-scale data is required more than ever.Model trainers need to use crowd-sourced data, publicly available data, or third-party datasets to train a more effective model.

In this case, the adversary has the opportunity to perform backdoor attacks by polluting the training dataset with a small amount of well-designed poisoned data. When innocent trainers use poisoned datasets for training, a backdoor is then placed into the model.The backdoored model performs normal output if the input is clean, and performs malicious behavior specified by the adversary such as being misleaded to the target label in classification tasks, when input contains a specific pattern, i.e., a backdoor trigger.

Backdoor attacks have attracted widespread attention in the field of computer vision[1, 2],natural language processi-ng[3, 4, 5, 6],graph neural networks[7], etc. In a real-world scenario, textual backdoor attacks would result in spam and offensive contents escaping detection. As far as we know, existing textual backdoor attacks can be divided into three types,namely word-level[4, 8, 9], sentence-level[3, 9] and feature-level[5, 6, 10],which utilize specific words, sentences and high-level feature as triggers and embed them into training data, respectively.Existing backdoor defense methods are studied insufficiently. BKI[11] and ONION[12] rely on traversing the dataset and remove possible triggers. BFclass[13] forms a candidate trigger set by leveraging a discriminator and sanitize dataset.However, these methods can only defend against word-level attacks well since they utilize trigger detection that regard trigger as one or several words.

To fill this gap, it is necessary to enhance the defense against backdoor attacks without trigger detection. We derive the intuition that backdoor attacks always need to establish a mapping between the backdoor trigger and the target label (classification task) during model training.Inspired by that, we could mitigate backdoor effectiveness by cutting this mapping.In this paper,as depicted in Fig. 1,we propose a novel backdoor defense method, Noise-augmented Contrastive Learning (NCL) framework that trains a clean model from untrustworthy data and protects model from backdoor threats.NCL mainly consists of two stages:(1) We add appropriate noise and augment each training sample with triplicates diversely, with the aim of perturbing text triggers while preserving the semantics. And then we fix some of the toxic labels in label-correction process.(2) We propose NCL loss for fine-tuning, which can pull training samples and their noise-augmented samples close in feature space while training. This stage is intended to mitigate the mapping between triggers and the target label during training, and highlight effective semantic feature.

The contributions can be summarized as follows:

(1)To the best of our knowledge, NCL framework is the first textual defense by model cleansing instead of trigger detection. Trigger-detection methods mainly work against word-level attacks, whereas our framework works can work against more types of backdoor attack.

(2)We firstly utilize contrastive learning in backdoor defense. We propose noise-augmented method (Sec. 2.2) and a novel NCL objective (Sec. 2.3) to mitigate the mapping between backdoor triggers and the target label.

(3)Experiments demonstrate that the effectiveness of our defense outperforms prior works. For example, it achieves an average decline of 40% and 50% on ASR on SST-2 and Agnews dataset, respectively. And it is even more effective in high poisoning rate setting.

Threat model.Our attack scenario is derived from a common real-world threat of usinguntrustworthy third-party dataset to train a model[12]. In this scenario, a malicious data-provider has a good chance to perform backdoor attack bypolluting the dataset.We assume the adversary is able to manipulate the dataset, but cannot control the training process.The normal backdoor attack flow in a defenseless situation is as follows:(1) The adversary selects samples with a fixed poisoning rate from the original dataset. And the adversary generates a batch of poisoned samples by inserting backdoor triggers into text, and sets those labels as the target label.(2) The adversary adds these well-crafted poisoned samples to original dataset, and then publishes the entire poisoned dataset pretending to be clean.(3) When an innocent victim trains and deploys a model using these poisoned dataset, the model is stealthily backdoored.Any sentences of test dataset with trigger will be classified to target label by that backdoored model.

Defense overview.We assume the both training dataset and validation dataset have been injected with poisoned examples.We assume that the defender has full control over the training stage, but does not have any trusted dataset and prior knowledge about the backdoor attack method.Our defense pipeline consists of the following steps (Fig. 1):(1) We utilize adding noiseand augment each sample into three noise-added samples to generate a new training dataset. Then we fix its toxic labels.(2) We train the new dataset using NCL objective to mitigate backdoor attacks during training.

Our goal of this part is to break possible triggers in the text while preserving core semantics, and then fix toxic labels utilizing the mapping of the backdoor model to the triggers.Noise-augmentation.Without the knowledge of the type and position of triggers in a sentence, we augment the training dataset samples using global noise.We choose Paraphrase-Generation model[14] as a method of adding global noise, because it can normalizesentences while maintaining original semantics.To perturb triggers and generate positive instances for contrastive learning,we change top-p sampling value[14] to make n𝑛n different augmented samples for each training sample.Then we obtain noise-augmented training datasets{𝒟1,𝒟2​…​𝒟n}subscript𝒟1subscript𝒟2…subscript𝒟𝑛\{\mathcal{D}_{1},\mathcal{D}_{2}...\mathcal{D}_{n}\}. n𝑛n is the number of augmentations per sample in original training dataset 𝒟0subscript𝒟0\mathcal{D}_{0}.We experimentally prove the noise effect of perturbing triggers.Fig. 2 shows thedata distribution of two poisoned datasets.The samples embedded with the trigger are easily distinguished, while they are hard to distinguish after adding noise.

Datasets and Models. We evaluate our NCL framework on SST-2[16] and AGnews[17] dataset. Due to space constraints, we uniformly show the experiments using the popular pre-trained language model BERT[18].Same experiment results can be obtained on other models like RoBERTa[19], DistilBERT[20], etc.

Attack methods and Defense baselines.As mentioned in Sec.1, for word-level, sentence-level and feature-level backdoor attacks, we choose BN[4], InSent[3], StyleBkd[5] alone with SynBkd[6], respectively.We select two feature-level backdoor attacks because they are more stealthy and difficult to defend against by existing defenses[6].We choose ONION[12] as our baseline, because of its general workability for different attack scenarios and victim models, which is based on calculating perplexity (ppl) and eliminating outlier words.We also utilize Bt-defense[6] and Syn-Defense[6], which use Back-translationandSyntactic Structure Alteration to change original sentences before feeding them into models.We choose 0.1, 0.1, 0.2 and 0.2 poisoning rates for BN, InSent, BibleBkd and SynBkd, respectively, following their default settings.

In ℒN​C​Lsubscriptℒ𝑁𝐶𝐿\mathcal{L}_{NCL}, we set β𝛽\mathcal{\beta}, γ𝛾\mathcal{\gamma} and τ0subscript𝜏0\tau_{0} to 0.1, 0.9 and 0.3 following [15], and set τ1subscript𝜏1\tau_{1} to 0.05 following [21]. Metrics.We adopt two metrics to evaluate defense methods performance: CACC (Clean Accuracy), that is the model’s accuracy on benign test samples; ASR (Attack Success Rate), the probability that backdoor samples are misclassified into target label.The lower ASR (higher ΔΔ\DeltaASR) and the higher CACC, the better defense performance.

In this part, we evaluate our experiments thatfocus on the performance under different types of attack methods and different poisoning rates.

For the varying backdoor attacks,in Tab. 1,we observe that NCL framework effectively mitigates three types of backdoor attacks: the average ΔΔ\DeltaASR is up to 40.90% and 51.59% for SST-2 and AG News dataset respectively, outperforming other defenses (eg. 20.42% and 9.14% of ONION). And the decline of CACC is negligible within 2%.We find that ℒu​N​C​Lsubscriptℒ𝑢𝑁𝐶𝐿\mathcal{L}_{uNCL} objective achieves greater ΔΔ\DeltaASR, but the decrease of CACC is also larger. It confirms that in Eq. 1 ℒU​C​Lsubscriptℒ𝑈𝐶𝐿\mathcal{L}_{UCL} mitigates backdoor attacks, and ℒS​C​Lsubscriptℒ𝑆𝐶𝐿\mathcal{L}_{SCL} prevents the utility decay from noise.Our experiments also show that trigger-detection method like ONION works well against word-level backdoor (BN), but is insufficient against other backdoor types.SynDefense effectively defenses against SynBkd since the word order in sentences is broken. However, it reduces the CACC largely.BtDefense is not observed good defense performance in experiments.

Existing backdoor defense works are effective against basic backdoor attacks such as word-level and sentence-level attacks in low poisoning rate.But we found that, When the poisoning rate increases, trigger-detection defense methods do not work well even against basic backdoor attacks because of the missing detection.Fig. 3 shows the NCL defense performance of at different poisoning rates. We also use ONION as a comparison method. When the poisoning rate increases, the effect of ONION decreases, and the CACC of ONION defense decreases as well in Fig. 3 (b) probably because deleting the trigger in sentences but not handling the wrong labels introduces noise labels.At the meantime, the effect of NCL does not decrease obviously.Even with a high poisoning rate of 80%, NCL still reduces the ASR value to 55.26% and 36.29%, respectively. It proves the NCL’s defense effectivenessin high poisoning rates.

In Tab. 2 we show the ablation studies of the NCL framework. We find the effect decreases when we remove any part of NCL, which proves their usefulness. For basic backdoor attacks (BN), label-correction is more useful, while for the stealthy feature-level backdoor attacks (BibleBkd), NCL objective is more useful.

Hyper-parameter α𝛼\alpha analysis.In Eq. 1, the term ℒU​C​Lsubscriptℒ𝑈𝐶𝐿\mathcal{L}_{UCL} is the key to mitigating trigger influence. Fig. 4 shows the defense result of different α𝛼\alpha as a weight parameters in Eq. 1. We find as α𝛼\alpha increases, the ASR value of the model decreases, while the CACC also decreases. It means that the larger weight of ℒU​C​Lsubscriptℒ𝑈𝐶𝐿\mathcal{L}_{UCL} term in training, the greater the defense capability of the model against backdoor attacks, meanwhile, the larger the utility decline of the model on benign data.

The NCL loss is the most critical part of the framework, which makes the model resistance against backdoor attacks. We attach additional experiments to prove the effect of NCL loss. In Fig. 5, we trained two BERT models with BN attack using NCL loss without Label-correction (Green line) and cross-entropy loss (Blue line). We obtain sentence embeddings ([cls] output from BERT model) for randomly selected 300 benign examples and their trigger-embedded samples. The we calculated Pearson Correlation Coefficient between benign embeddings and trigger-embedded embeddings for two models. We find that for the model trained with cross-entropy loss, when inserting a trigger word, there is a large swing of sentence embedding. And for the model trained with NCL loss, we observe almost no change.It means the model trained with cross-entropy loss, i.e. the backdoored model, is affected by backdoor triggers, while NCL loss avoids this effect.

In this paper, we propose NCL framework, an effective backdoor defense method,which mitigates the impact of backdoor triggers during training utilizing noise-augment method and NCL loss, without the requirement of trigger detection. Experiments show NCL framework achieves outperforming defense performance against mainstream backdoor attacks, while maintaining model utility on benign samples.
