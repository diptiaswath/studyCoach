Sequential recommendation (SR) is essential to online platforms such as movie and music streaming services (Chen et al., 2018; Schedl et al., 2018; Kang and McAuley, 2018; Bontempelli et al., 2022; Quadrana et al., 2018; Sun et al., 2019).By modeling temporal sequences of user actions (Fang et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021; Quadrana et al., 2018), SR systems predict the best content to recommend at a given time based on past interactions, e.g., the best songs to extend a listening session (Pereira et al., 2019).Over the past years, researchers have devoted significant efforts to improving SR (Hidasi et al., 2016; Trinh and Tu, 2017; Hu et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2018; You et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020; Fang et al., 2020; Ren et al., 2019). In particular, Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), the most recent class of neural architectures for sequence modeling, emerged as powerful SR methods (Kang and McAuley, 2018; Sun et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2019a; Wu et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2022; Jing et al., 2020; Du et al., 2022).

However, most existing Transformer systems for SR omit or insufficiently exploit the temporal context associated with user actions (Hansen et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Cho et al., 2020; Xian et al., 2021; Ye et al., 2020). While they process the relative position of an interaction within a sequence, these systems often overlook time-related information such as the hour or the day of each interaction. Yet, such factors can be crucial in real-world SR problems. For instance, in the music domain, Hansen et al. (Hansen et al., 2020) observed that capturing temporal elements such as the hour of the day helps predict the following songs users listen to on a music streaming service. In another study focusing on various domains, from music to e-commerce, Ye et al. (Ye et al., 2020) identified several time-dependent patterns in interaction sequences. This includes seasonal behaviors, e.g., Christmas songs being more consumed in December, and periodic behaviors, e.g., buying a new toothbrush every three months.

This paper introduces  Mixture Of Joint Item and Temporal cOntext (MOJITO), an improved Transformer SR system overcoming this limitation.MOJITO relies on the assumption that users tend to consume similar items, content-wise, for a given temporal context. Specifically, it jointly considers user-item interactions and the temporal context to learn two different attention matrices subsequently combined within Gaussian mixtures of temporal context and item embedding representations. This allows for an effective time-aware sequential modeling of user actions. We provide comprehensiveexperiments on various real-world datasets covering application domains ranging from movie to music recommendation.Our results emphasize that MOJITO empirically outperforms existing Transformersfor SR, including models previously proposed to capture the dependencies between user preferences and the temporal context (Li et al., 2020; Cho et al., 2020; Xian et al., 2021; Rashed et al., 2022). Consequently, we believe MOJITO will be helpful for researchers and practitioners eager to thoroughly leverage time-related contextual aspects when tackling SR problems. We publicly release our code on GitHub to facilitate the usage of our system.

This paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we formally present the SR problem and review related work.In Section 3, we introduce our proposed system for time-aware SR.We discuss our experimental analysis in Section 4, and conclude in Section 5.

Addressing an SR problem usually requires to jointly account for:(1)“Long-term” preferences: this term designates intrinsic user preferences, independent of the current context (Wang et al., 2021).(2)“Short-term” preferences: on the contrary, this term refers to how the context affects preferences, including how recent interactions alter the perception of a recommendation (Fang et al., 2020; Adomavicius and Tuzhilin, 2011).In recent years, several SR systems have been proposed to model these two aspects, often building upon advances in deep learning for sequence modeling (Hidasi et al., 2016; Trinh and Tu, 2017; Hu et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2019a; You et al., 2019; Kang and McAuley, 2018; Li et al., 2020; Fang et al., 2020; Ren et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022).This paper focuses on Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) for SR. Using attention mechanisms (Vaswani et al., 2017) for sequence modeling, they have recently shown competitive results on SR problems (Kang and McAuley, 2018; Sun et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2019a; Wu et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2022; Jing et al., 2020; Du et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022; Xia et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021).

SASRec (Kang and McAuley, 2018) was the first system to leverage self-attention to identify relevantitems among temporal sequences for SR. BERT4Rec (Sun et al., 2019) subsequently implemented bidirectional self-attention techniques.To combine long-term and short-term preferences more carefully, AttRec  (Zhang et al., 2019a) adopted a multi-task metric learning framework, while SSE-PT (Wu et al., 2020) concatenated user “embedding” vector representations with each item in the sequence. Lately, FISSA (Jing et al., 2020) incorporated an attentive version of the  Factored Item Similarity Model (FISM) (Kabbur et al., 2013) for long-term user preference, and used an item similarity-based gating mechanism to merge short-term and long-term representations, with promising performances. Other recent work also combined attention mechanisms for SR with graph neural networks (Qiu et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2019) or models based on contrastive learning (Du et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022), studied fairness-aware attention-based SR (Li et al., 2022), and presented more effective training strategies (Petrov and MacDonald, 2022).

Despite their success, these Transformers were also criticized for not fully exploiting temporal factors (Hansen et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Cho et al., 2020; Xian et al., 2021; Ye et al., 2020). While their attention mechanisms exploit relative positions in interaction sequences for preference modeling,they neglect the temporal context of each interaction, captured by Cu​isubscript𝐶𝑢𝑖C_{ui} sequences in Section 2.1.Yet, as illustrated in the introduction, time-related factors such as the current month, day, or hour can significantly modify user needs and preferences (Bontempelli et al., 2022; Hansen et al., 2020; Ye et al., 2020).

Attempting to tackle this problem, TiSASRec (Li et al., 2020) enhanced SASRec by analyzing time intervals between interactions using self-attention operations. TASER (Ye et al., 2020) combined pairwise relative and pointwise absolute time patterns in the same model. MEANTIME (Cho et al., 2020) leveraged multiple self-attention heads to learn several embedding representations, encoding different temporal patterns. TimelyRec (Xian et al., 2021) modeled heterogeneous temporal patterns in user behaviors, by processing hierarchical time granularities. Recently, CARCA (Rashed et al., 2022) relied on contextual correlations computed via cross-attention to provide time-aware recommendations.

Nonetheless, we believe that time-aware SR remains incompletely addressed. While existing time-aware Transformers gather item and temporal representations using simple concatenations or additions, such an approach oversimplifies the complex relationship between user preferences and the temporal context.Moreover, except MEANTIME (Cho et al., 2020), the aforementioned methods suffer from redundant attention heads problems (Michel et al., 2019; Voita et al., 2019; Bhojanapalli et al., 2021). The remainder of this paper will emphasize that overcoming these limitations permits to better model preferences and empirically improve time-aware SR.

We now turn to long-term representation learning. At this stage, we found it crucial to capture the heterogeneity of user preferences.

In real-world applications, users often have diverse preferences (Tay et al., 2018; Park et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2019b). For instance, one can simultaneously like metal and classical music.Hence, the practice of representing long-term user tastes using a single embedding vector (as in AttRec (Zhang et al., 2019a) or SSE-PT (Wu et al., 2020)) has been shown to be limiting when preferences are dispersed in the embedding space (Tay et al., 2018; Tran et al., 2021).

To overcome this issue, we use an attentive version of FISM (Kabbur et al., 2013) to represent users in MOJITO. This model, also incorporated in the FISSA system for SR (Jing et al., 2020), allows user representations to flexibly translate depending on the current target item representation (Tran et al., 2021).Contrary to FISSA, we learn user representations using N∈ℕ∗𝑁superscriptℕN\in\mathbb{N}^{*} randomly selected items from the user’s past history, instead of using only items from the currently observed sequence. This Section 4 presents our experimental evaluation of MOJITO. For reproducibility and future usage, we release our code on GitHub333https://github.com/deezer/sigir23-mojito.

We consider three real-world datasets covering different domains, namely movie, book, and music recommendation:

(1)MovieLens (Harper and Konstan, 2015): a movie recommendation dataset gathering 1 million ratings of 3 883 movies by 6 040 users. Each rating acts as a binary “interaction” in the following.(2)Amazon Book (McAuley et al., 2015): a collection of more than 1 million interactions with 96 421 books by 109 730 Amazon users.(3)LFM-1b (Schedl, 2016): more than a billion listening events by 120 322 Last.fm users, associated with 3 190 371 distinct music items.Like Sun et al. (Sun et al., 2020), we implement a kitemsuperscript𝑘itemk^{\text{item}}-core (respectively, a kusersuperscript𝑘userk^{\text{user}}-core) pre-processing step on each dataset,consisting in recursively filtering data until all users (resp., all items) have at least kitemsuperscript𝑘itemk^{\text{item}} (resp., kusersuperscript𝑘userk^{\text{user}}) interactions. Values of kitemsuperscript𝑘itemk^{\text{item}} and kusersuperscript𝑘userk^{\text{user}} are reported in Table 1.

For each interaction sequence, the last item is held out for test and the penultimate one for validation. We evaluate the ability of SR models to retrieve these items in evaluation sets containing the ground truth missing item and 1K other “negative” items that the user has not interacted with. SR models must recommend a list of 10 items, ordered by relevance scores. We evaluate recommendations using the Hit Rate (HR@10) and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG@10) metrics. The former metric reports the percentage of top-10 lists containing missing items, whereas the latter also takes into account the missing item’s ranking in the list.

We compare MOJITO to eight of the best Transformer models mentioned in Section 2.2:SASRec (Kang and McAuley, 2018), BERT4Rec (Sun et al., 2019),SSE-PT (Wu et al., 2020), FISSA (Jing et al., 2020), AttRec (Zhang et al., 2019a), TiSASRec (Li et al., 2020), MeanTime (Cho et al., 2020), and CARCA (Rashed et al., 2022). We recall that, like MOJITO, the three last baselines explicitly aim to capture temporal aspects for SR.

We train all models for a maximum of 100 epochs using the Adam optimizer (Kingma and Ba, 2015).We set d=𝑑absentd= 64, L=𝐿absentL= 50, batch sizes of 512, and B=𝐵absentB= 2, H=𝐻absentH= 2 for MOJITO.We selected all other hyperparameters via a grid search on validation items. For brevity, we report optimal values of all models in our GitHub repository3. Most notably, we tested learning rates values in {0.0002, 0.0005, 0.00075, 0.001}, N𝑁N in {20, 50, 100}, and λ𝜆\lambda in {0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 0.8, 1.0}.

Table 1 reports all results on test sets, along with standard deviations computed over five model trainings. Overall, MOJITO reaches competitive results on all three datasets, which we discuss below.

Our analysis reveals that time-aware systems tend to outperform non time-aware baselines, especially on MovieLens and Amazon Book. This highlights the relevance of modeling dependencies between preferences and the temporal context for SR in various domains. Yet, these performance differences are less pronounced on LFM-1b, likely due to the complexities of the temporal context in music consumption. Music consumption has lower levels of engagement than book or movie consumption, resulting in increased frequency of interactions and shorter time intervals between them (Schedl, 2019). This makes modeling the temporal context for music recommendation more challenging, especially given the higher number of music tracks for the same context. Nonetheless, MOJITO, explicitly accounting for day-of-the-week periodic information, has the best performance on LFM-1b (60.14% NDCG, 75.72% HR), showing its capacity to model the granularity of music consumption that is prone to periodic patterns (Bontempelli et al., 2022).

While accounting for the temporal context is crucial, the approach used to achieve this goal is equally important.In our experiments, MOJITO outperforms time-aware baselines according to 5 out of 6 scores (e.g., with a top 83.26% HR and a top 59.94% NDCG on Amazon Book) and reaches the second-best result for the 6th score (i.e., 59.82% NDCG on MovieLens, close to the top 59.97% NDCG obtained with MEANTIME on this dataset).These positive results emphasize the effectiveness of our flexible mixture-based probabilistic approach to automatically learn complex relationships between the temporal context and user preferences from various application domains.

To conclude our discussion, we formulate two additional conjectures, that we plan to investigate in future research.Firstly, we believe that the inferior performance of CARCA with respect to time-aware alternatives on MovieLens and LFM-1B in Table 1 could also be explained by the relative complexity of this system. Indeed, CARCA uses an entire Transformer encoder-decoder architecture while others only employ the decoder part (Rashed et al., 2022).Secondly, we recall that, by design, MOJITO aims to reduce head redundancies, which our experiments confirm. For instance, we report a 0.85 ±plus-or-minus\pm 0.43 mean pairwise L2subscript𝐿2L_{2} distance between head distances at the output layer for MOJITO on Amazon Book, vs. 0.57 ±plus-or-minus\pm 0.13 for TiSASRec and 0.76 ±plus-or-minus\pm 0.59 for CARCA (large values indicate less head redundancy). This head diversity seems to contribute to performance gains, as in the work of Nguyen et al. (Nguyen et al., 2022), although more ablation studies will be required in future work for confirmation.

In this paper, we proposed an effective Transformer for time-aware SR using mixtures of attention-based temporal context and item embedding representations. Backed by conclusive experiments on real-world datasets from various domains, we demonstrated its ability to thoroughly capture the impact of the temporal context on user preferences, enabling a more careful sequential modeling of user actions for SR.We also identified areas of analysis that, in the future, would help us better understand and improve our system.
