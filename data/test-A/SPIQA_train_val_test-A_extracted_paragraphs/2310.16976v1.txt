The Nash equilibrium (NE)В [Nash, 1950] formalizes the notion of a stable outcome in a multiagent strategic interaction, and has arguably served as the most influential solution concept in the development of game theory. Indeed, algorithms designed to approximate Nash equilibria in two-player zero-sum games have recently resolved major challenges in AIВ [Bowling etВ al., 2015, Brown and Sandholm, 2018, Perolat etВ al., 2022]. Its prescriptive power, however, has been severely undermined in multi-player general-sum games by intrinsic computational barriersВ [Daskalakis etВ al., 2006, Chen etВ al., 2009, Rubinstein, 2018, Etessami and Yannakakis, 2007, Conitzer and Sandholm, 2008, Gilboa and Zemel, 1989], limitations which also manifest in the inability of natural learning algorithms to convergeВ [Milionis etВ al., 2022, Vlatakis-Gkaragkounis etВ al., 2020, Kim etВ al., 2022, Mertikopoulos etВ al., 2018]. Another well-established but orthogonal critique is the equilibrium selection problemВ [Harsanyi etВ al., 1988]: a general-sum game may have multiple Nash equilibria with widely different welfare. As a result, a modern research agenda in computational game theory and multiagent systems has been to identify and characterize natural classes of games that circumvent those fundamental limitations.

In this paper, we uncover a new natural class of games for which the aforementioned caveats of Nash equilibria can be effectively addressed. In particular, our investigation originates from a natural question: when are efficientвҖ”in terms of social welfareвҖ”Nash equilibria easy to compute? The answer to this question is, at first glance, unsatisfactory: even if Nash equilibria are fully efficient, computational hardness still persists given that constant-sum multi-player games are hard. Indeed, efficiency and computational tractability are, in general, two orthogonal considerations. We show, however, an interesting twist, an unexpected interplay between efficiencyвҖ”when viewed from a specific lensвҖ”and the behavior of a family of no-regret learning algorithms.

To elucidate the alluded connection that drives much of our results, we first have to recall that the canonical paradigm for establishing the efficiency of equilibria is RoughgardenвҖҷs celebrated smoothness frameworkВ [Roughgarden, 2015a] (exposed thoroughly inВ SectionВ 2). In this context, we observe that if full efficiency of equilibria can be guaranteed via a smoothness argument (with bounded parameters), then Nash equilibria are approachable under a family of no-regret learning algorithms. In other words, full efficiency via smoothness implies computational tractability of NE. This is surprising in that RoughgardenвҖҷs smoothness framework was developed primarily in order to automatically extend price of anarchy (PoA) bounds to more permissive equilibrium concepts, such as coarse correlated equilibria (CCE); tractability of NE appears at first glance entirely unrelated. In fact, while applying the smoothness framework to (multi-player) constant-sum games appears to make little sense, given that PoA considerations are trivial in such games (all outcomes attain the same social welfare), it turns out that a certain regime of smoothness in (multi-player) constant-sum games is equivalent to a well-known condition in the optimization literature called the Minty propertyВ [Facchinei and Pang, 2003] (3.4).

The condition described above already capturesвҖ”somewhat unexpectedlyвҖ”well-studied settings, such as games that admit a minimax theoremВ [Cai etВ al., 2016], but we have found that the most fertile and novel ground to apply this theory revolves around large games, that is, games with a large number of players nвү«1much-greater-thanрқ‘ӣ1n\gg 1.111We use the terminology large games hereвҖ”in accordance with much of the economics literatureвҖ”to refer to games with a large number of players; it should not be confused with another common usage referring to games with a вҖңlargeвҖқ action space. The reason we focus on large games is a well-documented economic phenomenon: equilibria in large games approachвҖ”under natural conditionsвҖ”full efficiency as nвҶ’+вҲһвҶ’рқ‘ӣn\to+\infty, a property often established via smoothness argumentsВ [Feldman etВ al., 2016, Cole and Tao, 2016]вҖ”a crucial ingredient in our framework. There are ample compelling aspects in connecting the convergence of no-regret learning algorithms with RoughgardenвҖҷs smoothness framework. First, there has been a considerable interest in understanding smoothness, insights that can now be inherited to a seemingly entirely different but equally fundamental problem. For example, smoothness naturally lends itself to composition when multiple mechanisms are executed, either simultaneously or sequentially: smoothness at each separate mechanism implies smoothness globally В [Syrgkanis and Tardos, 2013, Theorems 5.1 and 5.2]. Smoothness also naturally extends to Bayesian gamesВ [Syrgkanis, 2012, Roughgarden, 2015b, Hartline etВ al., 2015] (see alsoВ [Sessa etВ al., 2020] for the related class of contextual games), a property that we leverage in TheoremВ 3.8 to expand our scope to mechanisms of incomplete information. Additional extensions based on local smoothnessВ [Roughgarden and Schoppmann, 2015, Nguyen, 2019] and the refined primal-dual framework ofВ Nadav and Roughgarden [2010] are also described in SectionsВ A.4 andВ A.3. Finally, our criterion has a clear and natural economic interpretation, and is part of an ongoing research effort to identify tractable classes of variational inequalities (VIs) beyond the Minty propertyВ [Diakonikolas etВ al., 2021].

As a concrete example, our framework subsumes games wherein each playerвҖҷs effect on the outcome vanishes as nвҶ’вҲһвҶ’рқ‘ӣn\to\infty; this captures, for example, simple voting settingsВ [Kearns and Mansour, 2002], as well as general auction design problems where establishing that property turns out to be highly non-trivial and quite delicateВ [Feldman etВ al., 2016]. More concretely, for games with vanishing sensitivity ПөnвҶ’0вҶ’subscriptitalic-Пөрқ‘ӣ0\epsilon_{n}\to 0, in that unilateral deviations can only affect a playerвҖҷs utility by an additive Пөnsubscriptitalic-Пөрқ‘ӣ\epsilon_{n} (DefinitionВ 3.5), we show that the conclusion ofВ TheoremВ 1.1 applies as long as ПөnвүӨonвҖӢ(1/n)subscriptitalic-Пөрқ‘ӣsubscriptрқ‘ңрқ‘ӣ1рқ‘ӣ\epsilon_{n}\leq o_{n}(1/\sqrt{n}) (TheoremВ 3.6). At the same time, the condition that ПҒnвҶ’1вҶ’subscriptрқңҢрқ‘ӣ1\rho_{n}\to 1 goes much deeper than games with vanishing sensitivity, and, as we explained earlier, surprisingly applies to two-player zero-sum games as well. We find it conceptually appealing that a unifying framework can establish tractability of Nash equilibria in two seemingly disparate classes of problems such as two-player zero-sum games and games with vanishing strategic sensitivity.

Remaining on smooth games, but relaxing the assumption ПҒвүҲ1рқңҢ1\rho\approx 1, we next study conditions under which the efficiency guaranteed by the smoothness framework can be improved, while at the same time ensuring the no-regret property, thereby implying convergence to the set of CCE. The smoothness bound is known to be applicable to any outcome of no-regret dynamics, but here we are instead interested in more refined guarantees when specific learning algorithms are in place. Building on a recent resultВ [Anagnostides etВ al., 2022], we show in TheoremВ 4.1 that the clairvoyant variant of gradient descent, introduced byВ Piliouras etВ al. [2022], enjoys an improved welfare bound and ensures fast convergence to the set of CCE for the average correlated distribution of play. Crucially, compared to an earlier resultВ [Anagnostides etВ al., 2022], the clairvoyant algorithm manages to satisfy an appealing notion of per-player incentive compatibility, in the form of convergence to CCE. In other words, improving the welfare predicted by smoothness is not at odds with incentive compatibility (CorollaryВ 4.3). It is worth noting that this new result interacts interestingly with the hardness ofВ Barman and Ligett [2015] pertaining computing non-trivial CCE, which is discussed inВ SectionВ A.10.

Finally, we suggest an approach for characterizing convergence to Nash equilibria through the lens of computational complexity. Specifically, Anagnostides etВ al. [2022] showed that Nash equilibria can be computed efficiently as long as the sum of the playersвҖҷ regrets is nonnegative. Interestingly, we show that determining whether the sum of the playersвҖҷ regrets can be negative (with respect to a given action profile) is \NP-hard in succinct multi-player games (TheoremВ A.28). Now the upshot is that in classes of games where making the sum of the playersвҖҷ regrets negativeвҖ”or relatedly strictly improving the social welfare predicted by smoothness (TheoremВ 6.2)вҖ”is \NP-hard, identifying and characterizing the hard instances would readily provide insights into the convergence properties of learning algorithms in instances that are otherwise analytically elusive.

The study of non-cooperative games with many players (i.e., large games) has been a classical topic in economic theoryВ [Schmeidler, 1973, Fudenberg and Levine, 1988, Mailath and Postlewaite, 1990, Rustichini etВ al., 1994, Pesendorfer and Swinkels, 1997, Feddersen and Pesendorfer, 1997, Gradwohl and Reingold, 2014], most recently revived in the context of mean-field games (e.g., [Lasry and Lions, 2007, Gomes and SaГәde, 2014, Muller etВ al., 2022, Guan etВ al., 2023, Campi and Fischer, 2022, Perrin etВ al., 2022, Subramanian etВ al., 2022, PГ©rolat etВ al., 2022, Cui and Koeppl, 2022, LauriГЁre etВ al., 2022]). Indeed, many traditional motivating scenarios in algorithmic game theory, including markets and Internet routing, often feature a large number of players in practice. In particular, it has emerged that, under certain conditions, equilibria in large games exhibit certain remarkable robustness and stability properties; see, for example, the recent survey ofВ Gradwohl and Kalai [2021], as well as the older treatment ofВ Kalai [2004] on the subject. Furthermore, mechanism design in large games, along with privacy guarantees, is explored in the work ofВ Kearns etВ al. [2014] (see alsoВ [Kearns and Mansour, 2002, Kearns etВ al., 2015]).

Of particular importance to our work, and specifically the precondition of TheoremВ 3.1, is the line of work uncovering the by now well-documented phenomenon in economics that large games exhibit, under certain relatively mild assumptions, fully efficient equilibria. Our framework additionally requires that the efficiency of equilibria can be derived via a smoothness argument, in the sense ofВ Roughgarden [2015a]; we stress again that efficiency alone is of little use when it comes to equilibrium computation (PropositionВ 3.7). Fortunately, smoothness has emerged as the canonical paradigm for bounding the price of anarchy (e.g., see the survey ofВ Roughgarden etВ al. In conclusion, we have furnished a new sufficient condition under which a family of no-regret learning algorithms, including optimistic gradient descent (рқҷҫрқҷ¶рқҷірқҷҫрқҷ¶рқҷі\mathtt{OGD}), approaches (weak) Nash equilibria. Our criterion has a natural economic interpretation, being intricately connected with RoughgardenвҖҷs smoothness framework, and captures other well-studied conditions such as the Minty property. We have also shown that clairvoyant gradient descent attains an improved welfare bound compared to that predicted by the smoothness framework, while ensuring at the same time fast convergence to the set of CCE.
