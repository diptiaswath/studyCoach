Theories of belief revision and update have been an important field in AI community, especially in knowledge representation and database systems [26]. An agent’s beliefs about the world may be incorrect or incomplete and she wants to change the beliefs. Such a process is known as belief revision [3].Belief revision is intended to capture changes in belief state reflecting new informationabout a static world. In contrast, belief update is intended to capture changes of belief in response to a changing world. An agent’s beliefs may be correct at one time. But as the world changes, for example, other agents take acts and disrupt their environment, certain facts become true and others false. The agent must accommodate these changes to update its state of beliefs. Such a process is called belief update [17]. Besides the traditional symbolic formalism, probability theory can be used to represent an agent’s belief state. In the probabilistic setting, an agent’s cognitive state is represented by a probability function p𝑝p overa set ΩΩ\Omega of possible worlds. Conditioning and imaging are two probabilistic versions of belief change that correspond to belief revision and belief update, respectively. Upon learning a sure fact C𝐶C, on the one hand, conditioning works by suppressing the possible worlds which are inconsistent with C𝐶C and normalizing the probabilities of the remaining possible worlds. It is a fundamental approach in probabilistic reasoning and statistical inference [23]. On the other hand, imaging (or updating) performs by transferring the probabilities of worlds outside C𝐶C to the closest worlds in C𝐶C. It is a common method to study intervention and causality [24].

In this paper, we take the two views of belief revision and update to study constrained differential privacy. Differentialprivacy (DP) is a mathematically rigorous definition of privacy which addresses the paradox of learning nothing about an individual while learning useful information about a population [7; 8].Differentially private data releases are often required to satisfy aset of external constraints that reflect the legal, ethical, and logicalmandates to which the data curator is obligated [2; 15]. For example, in US Census 2020, the so-called touchstone of DP by Dwork, the CensusBureau is constitutionally mandated to report the total population of each state as exactly enumerated without subjecting them to any perturbation protection; in data queries, constraints are often used to improve the accuracy while maintaining the quality of privacy protection of the unconstrained DP mechanisms.The central question in designing DP mechanisms with those constraints (called constrained DP) is how to integrate randomized DP privacy mechanisms with deterministic constraints while maintaining the standard trade-off between privacy protection and data utility. Our main contribution is to study this integration from the perspectives of belief revision and update.

In this paper, we mainly focus on those constraints that are known to hold also for the original datasets, which are hence called invariants. We first give a definition of data-independent invariants C𝐶C (Definition 3.1), which is a subset of the output space ℝnsuperscriptℝ𝑛\mathbb{R}^{n} of the privacy mechanism M𝑀M. In this paper, Laplace and Gaussian mechanisms are considered. For a given dataset D𝐷D, M​(D)𝑀𝐷M(D) is a (continuous) random vector over ℝnsuperscriptℝ𝑛\mathbb{R}^{n}. Let PM​(D)subscript𝑃𝑀𝐷P_{M(D)} and pM​(D)subscript𝑝𝑀𝐷p_{M(D)} denote the corresponding probability distribution and (density) function, which is regarded as an agent’s belief state. We then design constrained DP mechanisms by performing belief change on pM​(D)subscript𝑝𝑀𝐷p_{M(D)} (or PM​(D)subscript𝑃𝑀𝐷P_{M(D)}). In order to revise pM​(D)subscript𝑝𝑀𝐷p_{M(D)} by conditioning, we have to consider two cases: PM​(D)​(C)>0subscript𝑃𝑀𝐷𝐶0P_{M(D)}(C)>0 and PM​(D)​(C)=0subscript𝑃𝑀𝐷𝐶0P_{M(D)}(C)=0. When PM​(D)​(C)>0subscript𝑃𝑀𝐷𝐶0P_{M(D)}(C)>0, conditioning works as usual. One of our technical contributions is to deal with the challenge when PM​(D)​(C)=0subscript𝑃𝑀𝐷𝐶0P_{M(D)}(C)=0. We use the techniques of changing variables in multivariate calculus to compute the conditional density. Even though the invariant C𝐶C is data-independent, conditioning is data-dependent because the denominator in conditional density function depends on the original data D𝐷D. So conditioning may add more privacy loss as shown in [12]. In this paper, we show that, if M𝑀M is additive and C𝐶C is represented by a group of linear equalities, conditioning does not incur any extra privacy loss (Lemma 3.3). In addition to the standard postprocessing and composition properties (Lemmas 3.4,3.6), we obtain from the characterizing property of conditioning an interesting form of composition: conditional privacy mechanism on the disjoint union of two invariants is a convex combination of the mechanisms conditioned on individual invariants (Theorem 3.5). From the perspective of belief update, we perform imaging on pM​(D)subscript𝑝𝑀𝐷p_{M(D)} and show the standard postprocessing and composition properties (Lemmas 3.8,3.10). In contrast, imaging is a privacy-preserving postprocessing and does not incur any privacy loss (Lemma 3.7). Moreover, we obtain a characteristic proposition about composition and show that imaging on mixture of privacy mechanisms is the mixture of imaging privacy mechanisms (Theorem 3.9). In addition to the above analysis of privacy, we also perform analysis of utility and perform some experiments to show the differences between the two perspectives as belief revision and update. The theory of belief revision and update can guide us in choosing the appropriate constraining approach, thereby clarifying many confusions in the literature regarding these two constrained DPs.
