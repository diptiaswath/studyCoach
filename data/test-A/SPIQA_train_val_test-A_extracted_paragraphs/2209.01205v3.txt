Knowledge graphs (KGs) represent a large amount of entities and their relationships via a collection of factual triplets, (h,r,t)ℎ𝑟𝑡(h,r,t), where each triplet expresses the relationship r𝑟r between a head entity hℎh and a tail entity t𝑡t. Large-scale KGs (Vrandečić and Krötzsch, 2014; Mitchell et al., 2018; Suchaneket al., 2007; Bollacker et al., 2008) can provide powerful knowledge inference capabilities for many intelligent applications, including question answering (Yao and Van Durme, 2014), web search (Eder, 2012) and recommendation systems (Wang et al., 2019). As KGs are often built semi-automatically from unstructured data, it has become a well-known fact that most existing KGs are far from complete (Dong et al., 2014). This has expedited research on knowledge graph completion, which aims to add new factual triplets to a knowledge graph by predicting missing relations. Following the seminal work of TransE (Bordes et al., 2013) and its extraordinary performance on WN18 (Bordes et al., 2013) and FB15k (Bordes et al., 2013), KG embedding methods and their variants have dominated the task of KG completion. Despite their promising results, conventional KG completion methods are only suitable for predicting relations with large amounts of available triplets.However, real-world KGs suffer from the notorious long-tail problem – a considerable proportion of relations are associated with only very few triplets. For example, about 10% of relations in Wikidata have no more than 10 triplets (Xionget al., 2018). As a result, the performance of current KG completion methods significantly drops when predicting for relations with a limited number (few-shot) of training triplets. This issue has prompted a series of research works on few-shot KG completion that focuses on predicting the missing tail entity t𝑡t for query triplets by learning from only 𝒦𝒦\mathcal{K} reference triplets about a target relation r𝑟r.

Given a target relation r𝑟r and 𝒦𝒦\mathcal{K} reference triplets, the goal of 𝒦𝒦\mathcal{K}-shot KG completion is to correctly predict the tail entity t𝑡t for each query triplet (h,r,?)ℎ𝑟?(h,r,?) using the generalizable knowledge learned from the given reference triplets.Thus, the crucial aspect of few-shot KG completion is to learn meta representation of each few-shot relation from the limited amount of reference triplets that can generalize to novel relations. In essence, we identity three levels of relational information that contribute to learning meta representation of the target relation (see Figure 1). Firstly, each reference triplet is closely related to its wider contexts, providing abundant relevant information. Such context-level information is crucial for enriching entity and relation embeddings. Secondly, as the quantity of reference triplets is limited for each few-shot relation, it is crucial to learn meta relation information that can inherently capture triplet-level commonality among reference triplets. Thirdly, the learned meta representation of the given relation should well generalize to unseen triplets (i.e. query triplets), which calls for entity-level relational learning.

To exploit relational information for few-shot KG completion, a series of research works have been proposed. The pioneering work GMatching (Xionget al., 2018) designs a neighbor encoder to leverage local neighborhood structure for learning better representation of each entity in the given reference triplet. Subsequent works (e.g., MetaR (Chenet al., 2019), FSRL (Zhang et al., 2020), GANA (Niu et al., 2021), and FAAN (Sheng et al., 2020)) extend one-shot setting of GMatching to more shots, with key challenges to extract relation-specific meta information from 𝒦𝒦\mathcal{K} reference triplets. MetaR simply average the embe3ddings of all the 𝒦𝒦\mathcal{K} reference triplets, while FSRL (Zhang et al., 2020), GANA (Niu et al., 2021), and FAAN (Sheng et al., 2020)) utilize recurrent processors composed of LSTMs (Hochreiter andSchmidhuber, 1996) to learn meta relation representations.Although these methods are found to achieve empirical improvements, they fundamentallyneglect the fact that reference triplets are order-invariant, thus violating the property of permutation-invariance. As a result, the unrealistic assumption of sequential dependency jeopardizes the model’s ability to capture complex interactions among the reference triplets.Table 1 lists the relational information utilized by existing KG completion methods.In summary, existing works have primarily focused on designing local neighbor aggregators to learn entity-level information and/or imposing sequential assumption at the triplet level to learn meta relation information. However, the potential of leveraging pairwise triplet-level interactions and context-level relational information has been largely unexplored.

In this paper, we propose a Hierarchical Relational learning framework (HiRe) for few-shot KG completion. HiRe jointly models three levels of relations (entity-level, triplet-level, and context-level relations) within each few-shot task as mutually reinforcing sources of information to generalize to few-shot relations. Firstly, we propose a contrastive learning based context-level relational learning method to learn expressive entity/relation embeddings by modeling correlations between the target triplet and its true/false contexts. We argue that the triplet itself has a close relation with its wider contexts, especially the semantic structures among neighboring triplets anchored at the target triplet. Taking Figure 3 as an example, jointly considering the wider context shared by head/tail entity would reveal crucial information – both “Lionel Messi” and “Sergio Agüero” playFor “Argentina’s National Team” – for determining whether the relation workWith holds between the given entity pair (“Lionel Messi”, “Sergio Agüero”). Motivated by this, HiRe employs a contrastive loss to learn better entity embeddings, based on the idea that a given triplet should be pulled close to its true context, but pushed apart from its false contexts in the embedding space. In doing so, we could imbue entity embeddings with the contextual knowledge learned from the wider contexts of the target triplet.Secondly, we propose a transformer-based meta relation learner (MRL) to perform triplet-level relation learning for the meta representation of the given relation r𝑟r. Taking advantage of the set transformer (Leeet al., 2019), our proposed MRL is capable of capturing pairwise interactions among reference triplets, while preserving the permutation-invariance property and being insensitive to the size of the reference set.Thirdly, we propose a meta representation based embedding learner named MTransD to refine the learned meta relation representation at the entity level following the paradigm of KG translational models (Bordes et al., 2013; Wanget al., 2014; Jiet al., 2015). By forcing the learned meta representation of the target relation holds between unseen query triplets, entity-level relations can be captured and generalize to novel relations.Lastly, we adopt a MAML-based training strategy (Finnet al., 2017) to optimize HiRe on each meta task within a unified framework. By performing relational learning at three granular levels, our proposed HiRe framework offers significant advantages for extracting expressive meta relation representations and improving model generalizability for few-shot KG completion.

We summarize our contributions as follows:•We identify three levels of relational information that are essential for learning meta representation of a given relation for few-shot KG completion.•We propose a hierarchical relational learning framework (HiRe) to learn a more stereoscopic and generalizable representation space for few-shot KG completion.•Extensive experiments on two benchmark datasets validate the efficacy of HiRe, demonstrating its superiority over state-of-the-art methods.

In this section, we review related works from three main streams: (1) relational learning in knowledge graphs; (2) few-shot learning on knowledge graphs; and (3) contrastive learning on graphs.

Recent advances in graph deep learning has accelerated the development of embedding-based methods for KG completion. This line of research focuses on utilizing relational information available in KGs to learn a unified low-dimensional embedding space for the input triplets, where each entity is projected as a k𝑘k-dimensional vector and the relation r𝑟r between the entity pair (h,t)ℎ𝑡(h,t) serves as the translation from hℎh to t𝑡t. A scoring function is then used to measure the quality of the translation and to learn a unified embedding space for all triplets.

The pioneering work TransE (Bordes et al., 2013) is the first to propose the aforementioned idea of using relation r𝑟r as translation in the embedding space, i.e. h+r≈thrt\textbf{h}+\textbf{r}\approx\textbf{t} when (h,r,t)∈𝒯​𝒫ℎ𝑟𝑡𝒯𝒫(h,r,t)\in\mathcal{TP}.Inspired by TransE, TransH (Wanget al., 2014) moves forward to project entities into different relation-specific hyperplanes, enabling the learning of distinct entity representations when connected by different relations.TransR (Linet al., 2015) builds distinct embedding spaces for entities and relations in order to allow different relations to focus on different aspects of entities.ComplEx (Trouillon et al., 2016) uses a bilinear scoring function to model asymmetric relations in a complex vector space.RotatE (Sunet al., 2019a) further improves the modeling of relation patterns by defining each relation as a rotation in the complex space.ConvE (Dettmers et al., 2018) and ConvKB (Nguyenet al., 2018) take advantage of convolution operators to enhance the learning entity and relation embeddings. However, the aforementioned KG embedding methods require a large number of triplets for each relation to find the unified embedding space during training. The performance of these methods can significantly degrade at few-shot settings, where only very few triplets are available for each relation.

Existing few-shot KG completion works can be grouped into two main categories: (1) Metric learning based methods:GMatching (Xionget al., 2018) is the first work to formulate few-shot (one-shot) KG completion. GMatching is comprised of two main parts: a neighbor encoder and a matching processor. The neighbor encoder aggregates the one-hop neighbors of any given entity, and the matching processor compares similarity between query and reference entity pairs. FSRL (Zhang et al., 2020) relaxes the setting to more shots and explores how to integrate the information learned from multiple reference triplets.FAAN (Sheng et al., 2020) is the first to propose a dynamic attention mechanism for one-hop neighbors adapting to the different relations which connect them.(2) Meta learning based methods:MetaR (Chenet al., 2019) is a meta learning approach that transfers relation-specific meta information to enhance the model learning capacity. However, it generates meta relation representations by simply averaging the representations of all reference triplets.GANA (Niu et al., 2021) puts more emphasis on neighboring information and accordingly proposes a gated and attentive neighbor aggregator. FSRL (Zhang et al., 2020), GANA (Niu et al., 2021), and FAAN (Sheng et al., 2020)) utilize recurrent processors composed of LSTMs (Hochreiter andSchmidhuber, 1996) to learn meta relation representations, but they impose an unrealistic sequential dependency assumption when modeling reference triplets.

Although these methods have achieved state-of-the-art performance on benchmark datasets, they fail to capture valuable pairwise triplet-level interactions and context-level relational information. Our work is proposed to fill this important research gap in the literature.

Contrastive learning is a self-supervised learning scheme that measures similarity and difference between instances. The general goal of contrastive learning is to contrast positive pairs against negative pairs, i.e. pulling similar (positive) instances closer and pushing different (negative) instances apart (Hadsellet al., 2006; Dosovitskiy et al., 2014). Successful contrastive methods follow the instance discrimination principle that pairs instances based on whether they are derived from the same instance (i.e. positive pairs) or not (i.e. negative pairs).

Inspired by the success in computer vision and natural language processing (Oordet al., 2018; Hjelm et al., 2019; Wuet al., 2018; Sohn, 2016; Tianet al., 2020; Doerschet al., 2015; Mikolov et al., 2013), contrastive methods have been recently proposed to learn expressive node embeddings on graphs. In general, these methods train a graph encoder that produces node embeddings and a discriminator which distinguishes node embedding pairs that are similar from those that are dissimilar.For instance, DGI (Velickovic et al., 2019) trains a node encoder to maximize mutual information between patch representations and corresponding high-level summaries of graphs. InfoGraph (Sunet al., 2019b) demonstrates that it is essential to maximize the mutual information between the representation of the entire graph and the representations of substructures. The work of (Hassani andKhasahmadi, 2020) proposes to use contrast encodings from one-hop neighbors and a graph diffusion, achieving state-of-the-art performance on both node and graph classification tasks. GCC (Qiu et al., 2020) adopts InfoNCE loss (Oordet al., 2018) and presents a graph-based contrastive learning framework for downstream tasks.

In KGs, we note that positive and negative pairs naturally exist in the problem of few-shot KG completion. However, the potential of contrastive learning in this task is under-explored. In our work, we adopt the idea of contrastive learning at the context level to capture correlations between a target triplet and its wider context. This enables to enrich the expressiveness of entity embeddings in KGs and improving model generalisation for few-shot KG completion. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to integrate contrastive learning with KG embedding learning for few-shot KG completion.

In this section, we introduce the overall structure of our proposed learning framework in details. We target at the 𝒦𝒦\mathcal{K}-shot KG completion task, where each meta task consists of 𝒦𝒦\mathcal{K} reference triplets for one given relation r𝑟r as the reference set.

Based on our observation that three levels of relational information inherently exist in KGs, we break down the few-shot KG completion task into three sub-tasks: (1) Augmenting the entity representation via aggregating neighboring information, (2) Learning meta representation of a given relation r𝑟r via modeling interactions among the reference set, and (3) Measuring the similarity between query and reference set via matching functions.As discussed earlier, previous works have mostly focused on designing local neighbor aggregators to learn entity-level relational information and building sequential dependencies to capture triplet-level relationships, while crucial pairwise interactions among reference triplets and context-level relational information have been largely overlooked.

To fill this research gap, we propose to perform three levels of relational learning within each meta task (context-level, triplet-level and entity-level), to learn a more stereoscopic and generalizable representation space for few-shot KG completion. Fig. 2 shows an overview of our proposed hierarchical relational learning (HiRe) framework. HiRe is composed of three major components: (1) Contrastive learning based context-level relational learning; (2) Transformer-based triplet-level relational learning; (3) Meta representation based entity-level relational learning.

Given a reference set 𝒮rsubscript𝒮𝑟\mathcal{S}_{r} with only 𝒦𝒦\mathcal{K} training triplets, existing works (e.g., (Xionget al., 2018; Niu et al., 2021)) seek to learn better head/tail entity embedding by aggregating information from its respective local neighbors, and then concatenate the embeddings of head entity and tail entity to represent each triplet. Although treating the neighborhoods of head/tail entity separately is a common practice in homogeneous graphs, we argue that it is not suitable for few-shot KG completion.Consider the following example in the context of interactions among professional football players, as illustrated in Fig. 3. Here, we would like to infer the potential relation between an entity pair (“Lionel Messi”, “Sergio Agüero”). Previous works typically treat the neighborhoods of head entity “Lionel Messi” and tail entity “Sergio Agüero” separately, as shown in Figure 3(a).Each neighboring pair, such as (“Paris”, liveIn), partially contributes to the embedding of the anchor entity “Lionel Messi”.However, if we jointly look into the wider contexts of both head entity and tail entity (see Figure 3(b)), the crucial fact that both “Lionel Messi” and “Sergio Agüero” playFor “Argentina’s National Team” would reveal critical information when deciding if the relation workWith holds between the given entity pair. Clearly, treating neighborhoods of head/tail entity separately would lead to loss of critical information. This motivates us to jointly consider neighborhoods of head/tail entity.The idea of joining neighborhoods also inspires us to leverage relational knowledge in the context of a given triplet. Intuitively, a given triplet should be close to its wider context and be apart from the context of other triplets or its corrupted contexts.

Based on the above insights, we propose a contrastive learning based context-level relational learning method for learning expressive entity embeddings. The idea is to enrich entity embeddings by capturing correlations between a target triplet and its wider context. Towards this goal, for each triplet (h,r,t)ℎ𝑟𝑡(h,r,t) from the reference set, we construct its true context as the union of the neighborhoods of head entity hℎh and tail entity t𝑡t, and synthesize a group of false contexts by randomly corrupting the entity/relation within the true context.For the target triplet, we propose a context encoder to learn the embeddings of its true/false contexts based on self-attention mechanism so that important neighbors within the context would be given a larger weight. Furthermore, a contrastive loss is employed to pull close the triplet towards its true context and push apart from its false contexts. As such, we can imbue the embedding of the target triplet with its context-level relational information, thus enriching the expressiveness of its head and tail entity embeddings.

After obtaining the embeddings of all reference triplet, the next focus is to learn meta relation representation for the target relation r𝑟r. For this purpose, the state-of-the-art models (e.g. FSRL (Zhang et al., 2020), GANA (Niu et al., 2021)) utilize recurrent processors comprised of LSTMs, based on a sequential dependency assumption on reference triplets. Despite their excellent empirical results, these models violate two important properties here.First, the model should be insensitive to the size of reference set (i.e. few-shot size 𝒦𝒦\mathcal{K}). Second, the triplets in the reference set should be permutation-invariant since they do not have sequential dependencies.

To address these issues, our goal is to better model triplet-level relational information, so that complex interactions among reference triplets can be captured to learn generic meta relational knowledge.

To this end, we propose a transformer-based meta relation learner (MRL) that effectively models pairwise interactions among reference triplets and learn generic meta representation of the given relation r𝑟r that generalizes well to new unseen relations.Inspired by set transformer (Leeet al., 2019), we design our MRL based on the idea of set attention block (SAB) to leverage triplet-triplet interactions while conforming to the two aforementioned properties. Generally, an SAB is designed to take a set of objects as input and perform self-attention mechanism between the elements. Our proposed MRL takes advantage of the SAB to model pairwise triplet-triplet interactions within 𝒮rsubscript𝒮𝑟\mathcal{S}_{r} so as to cultivate the ability to learn generalizable meta representation of the target relation.

In this section, we validate the performance of our proposed method through comparisons against the state-of-the-arts on KG benchmark datasets, followed by detailed ablation study and parameter analysis.

We conduct experiments on two widely-used few-shot KG completion datasets, NELL-One and Wiki-One, for the evaluation of the proposed method against other baselines. We follow the experimental protocol proposed by GMatching (Xionget al., 2018), where relations associated with more than 50 but less than 500 triplets are chosen for few-shot completion tasks. For each target relation, the candidate entity set also follows the setting provided by GMatching (Xionget al., 2018).The statistics of both datasets are provided in Table 3. We use 51/5/11 and 133/16/34 tasks for training/validation/test on NELL-One andWiki-One, respectively.

We report both MRR (mean reciprocal rank) and Hits@n (n=1,5,10𝑛1510n=1,5,10) on both datasets for the evaluation of performance. MRR is the mean reciprocal rank of the correct entities, and Hits@n is the ratio of correct entities that rank in top n𝑛n. We compare the proposed method against other baseline methods in 1-shot and 5-shot settings, which are the most common settings in the literature.

For evaluation, we compare our proposed method against state-of-the-art baselines, which are grouped into two categories below:

Conventional KG completion methods:This category of methods focus on modeling relational structures in KG to learn expressive entity/relation embeddings. We adopt four widely used methods as baselines, including TransE (Bordes et al., 2013), TransH (Wanget al., 2014), DistMult (Yanget al., 2015) and ComplEx (Trouillon et al., 2016). We use OpenKE (Hanet al., 2018) to reproduce the experimental results of these conventional models with the hyper-parameters reported in the original papers.

Few-shot KG completion methods:This category of baselines are designed specifically for few-shot KG completion and have achieved state-of-the-art performance on NELL-One and Wiki-One datasets. For MetaR (Chenet al., 2019) (both In-Train and Pre-Train) and FAAN (Sheng et al., 2020), we directly report results obtained from the original papers. For GMatching (Xionget al., 2018), we report the results provided by MetaR (Chenet al., 2019) for both 1-shot and 5-shot. Because FSRL (Zhang et al., 2020) was initially reported in different experimental settings, where the candidate set is much smaller, we report the results re-implemented by FAAN (Sheng et al., 2020) under the same setting with other methods. Due to the fact that the reproduced results of GANA (Niu et al., 2021) is less competitive, we leave GANA out in our comparison.

For fair comparison, we use the entity and relation embeddings pretrained by TransE (Bordes et al., 2013) on both datasets, released by GMatching (Xionget al., 2018), for the initialization of HiRe. Following the literature, the embedding dimension is set to 100100100 and 505050 for NELL-One and Wiki-One, respectively. On both datasets, we set the number of SAB to 111 and each SAB contains 111 self-attention head. We apply drop path to avoid overfitting with a drop rate set to 0.20.20.2. Our proposed HiRe framework is composed of three key components: (1) Contrastive learning based context-level relational learning; (2) Transformer-based triplet-level relational learning; (3) Meta representation based entity-level relational learning. To investigate the contributions of each component to the overall performance, we conduct an ablation study on Wiki-One under 3-shot and 5-shot settings. The results are reported in Table. 5:•w/o MTransD: In order to study the effectiveness of meta representation based entity-level relational learning, we substitute MTransD with TransE, retaining the MAML-based training strategy. Substituting MTransD leads to performance drops at a significant level under both settings, indicating the necessity of constraining entity and relation embeddings while considering the diversity of entities and relations simultaneously.•w/o MRL: To study the impact of transformer-based triplet-level relational learning, we remove the proposed MRL and represent meta relation by simply averaging the embeddings of all reference triplets. The removal of MRL has a profound negative effect on the performance, resulting in a performance drop of 4% in terms of MRR under 333-shot setting and 5.4% under 555-shot setting. In addition, the performance under 333-shot and 555-shot settings are similar, indicating that the simple strategy of averaging the embeddings fails to take advantage of more training triplets. This ablation study validates the importance of MRL to capture the underlying pairwise interactions among reference triplets in learning meta relation representation.•w/o Context: By ablating ℒcsubscriptℒ𝑐\mathcal{L}_{c}, we remove contrastive learning based context-level relational learning but retain triplet-level and entity-level relational information. As compared with jointly considering the context of the target triplet, the performance drop verifies our assumption that the semantic contextual information plays a crucial role in few-shot KG completion.

HiRe uses N𝑁N false contexts for a given triplet for the contrastive loss. To study the impact of N𝑁N, we conduct a sensitivity test on Wiki-One under 111-shot, 333-shot, and 555-shot settings. We set N𝑁N as 111, 222, 444, and 666. As shown in Fig. 5, HiRe achieves the best result when N𝑁N=111 on all settings, and its performance slightly drops when N𝑁N=222. As the number of false contexts continues to increase, the performance of HiRe drops accordingly. One main reason is that, when only one true context is available for the given triplet, increasing the number of false contexts would accelerate the separation of true and false contexts, leading to the result that the model converges too quickly on the contrastive loss.

As discussed in Section 4.4, HiRe employs a hyper-parameter λ𝜆\lambda to balance the contribution of the margin loss and the contrastive loss in the overall loss function (Eq. 24). To identify the best value of λ𝜆\lambda, we conduct a sensitivity test on Wiki-One under 111-shot, 333-shot and 555-shot settings. Due to the fact that the value of contrastive loss is significantly larger than the value of margin loss, the trade-off hyper-parameter should be small to retain the supervision from the margin loss effective. Therefore, we searched the best λ𝜆\lambda between 0 and 0.5 with an increment of 0.1. Finally, we set λ𝜆\lambda to 0.05 for the best performance. As shown in Fig. 4, the contrastive loss effectively improves the performance in a wide range, demonstrating the efficacy of the proposed contrastive learning based context-level relational learning.

This paper presents a hierarchical relational learning framework (HiRe) for few-shot KG completion. We investigate the limitations of current few-shot KG completion methods and identify that jointly capturing three levels of relational information is crucial for enriching entity and relation embeddings, which ultimately leads to better meta representation of the target relation and model generalizability. To be specific, HiRe learns context-level relational information by modeling the contrastive correlation between a target triplet and its true/false contexts and captures pairwise triplet-level relational information via a transformer-based meta relation learner. The learned entity/relation embeddings are further refined via MTransD at the entity level. A MAML-based training strategy is adopted to optimize HiRe on each meta task within a unified framework. Experimental results on two commonly used benchmark datasets show that HiRe outperforms current state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating its superiority and efficacy for few-shot KG completion. The ablation study and hyper-parameter sensitivity study illustrate the significance of the key components of HiRe.
