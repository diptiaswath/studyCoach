\author{%

\begin{abstract}

Reinforcement Learning typically assumes a controller to be directly interacting with an environment, such that observations describe the state at the current time point and actions are applied instantaneously.
In reality, these assumptions are generally violated due to physical or algorithmic constraints: a remote robot controller, for instance, experiences transmission delays between the actual and the processed observations, emits an action only after evaluating its policy, followed by delayed transmission to the actuator and mechanically induced delays in the actuator itself.
In short, nothing really happens instantaneously, and the performance of algorithms based on the standard Markov Decision Process (MDP) setting degrades significantly when their instantaneity assumptions are violated.
We define Randomly Delayed Markov Decision Processes (RDMDPs) ans propose a novel algorithm, Delay-Correcting Actor Critic (DC/AC), which can handle arbitrary and variable delays for both actions and observations.
We demonstrate superior performance of our method on continuous control tasks with realistic delays, outperforming Soft-Actor Critic and Real-Time Actor-Critic in such settings.
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}


\begin{wrapfigure}{r}{0.4\textwidth}

 \caption{\small General setting: an undelayed environment (the remote actor and its surroundings) is augmented into a Delayed MDP with a decision maker and delayed communication dynamics.}
 \label{fig:overview}
\vspace{0.1cm}
\end{wrapfigure}

Training real-world systems such as robots through classic Reinforcement Learning (RL) algorithms is often difficult, in part because some of the basic assumptions made in the idealized mathematical framework break in reality. In particular, in real-time control, the world can hardly be considered a general turn-based Markov environment. Contrary to the classic environments used in the RL literature, reality cannot be paused during computations and information transfers, and actions are never instantaneous.

In traditional control theory, real-time controllers typically operate at a fixed frequency that is tuned to the time constant of the controlled system.
In reality, however, delays occur, and the decision making process is not necessarily synchronized with the controlled system due to computational, sensing, and transmission constraints.
Many factors contributing to such delays are not necessarily under the control of the designer, and are best modeled as random delays \cite{ge2013modeling}.
\citet{nilsson1998stochastic} show that neglecting delays or just considering average delays leads to suboptimal control in the best case, and unstable systems in the worst.

Most current RL approaches treat time delays as negligible
\cite{cruz2017using} or design their systems to be effectively paused between 
time-steps \cite{kalashnikov2018qt}. However, this is not suitable for systems that are not 
inherently stable and require high-frequency control inputs.
In this work, we integrate stochastic observation and action delays into the theoretical framework of RL by augmenting the underlying environment with explicit delays, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:overview}. We then propose a family of algorithms for this setting.

Our main contributions are as follows. \TODO{Update}
We define Randomly-Delayed MDPs (RDMDPs) as a generalization of Constantly-Delayed MDPs (CDMDPs) \cite{Walsh2008LearningAP}. 
We introduce state-value estimators for both settings and prove that they are valid (Thm. \ref{th:off_policy_thm}), efficient (Cor. \ref{cor:bias}), and unbiased (Cor. \ref{cor:unbiased}) in off-policy settings.
We validate our estimators by deriving a novel off-policy algorithm (DC/AC) and providing empirical results for both the constantly and the randomly-delayed case.




\section{Delayed Environments}

\label{section_environments_delays}

\begin{wrapfigure}{r}{0.35\textwidth}
  \begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.35\textwidth, trim=0 2em 0 2em]{img/histogram.pdf}
  \end{center}
 \caption{\small 10000 real communication delays between a computer and a drone through a fast WiFi network}
 \label{fig:histogram}
\vspace{0.1cm}
\end{wrapfigure}

As a motivating illustration of real-world delayed setting, we have collected data on the round-trip time of 10000 UDP exchanges between a decision-making computer and a flying robot, summarized in Figure~\ref{fig:histogram}. For reference, the high-level control frequency of the robot is 50Hz, and the flight controller runs at 8kHz.

We frame the general setting of real-world reinforcement learning in terms of a 
\textit{remote actor}, a \textit{decision maker}, random \textit{observation-delays}, and random \textit{action-delays}.
We consider the delays to be integers: for a real-valued delay of $d_d$ seconds and a real-valued time-step of $d_{s}$ seconds, we redefine the delay as $\lceil \frac{d_d}{d_{s}} \rceil$.
Let us consider a given observation $s$.
The \textit{observation delay} $\alpha(s)$ refers to the duration between the moment $s$ finishes being captured and the moment a new action is computed from it.
The \textit{action delay} $\beta(s)$ refers to the duration between the moment the last action influencing $s$ starts being computed and the time-step before the remote actor finishes capturing $s$.
We call \textit{time-step} a fixed, atomic duration which can typically be an upper bound of the decision maker's inference time. Yet this is not a theoretical constraint: the time-step can be made arbitrarily small.
At the beginning of each time-step, the decision maker starts computing a new action from the last available observation. Meanwhile, the remote actor starts capturing a new observation and starts applying the last action it has received. Note that in both cases, by "last", we mean "most recently produced".



In the presence of delays, the naive approach is to simply use the last received observation.
In this case, any delay longer than 1 time-step violates the Markov assumption, since the last sent action becomes an unobserved part of the current state of the environment. To overcome this issue, we define a framework that extends MDPs by taking into account the communication dynamics.





\subsection{Randomly Delayed Markov Decision Processes}

We propose Randomly Delayed Markov Decision Processes ($\mathit{RDMDPs}$) to generally describe real-world environments with variable delays:

\begin{definition}
A Randomly Delayed Markov Decision Process $\mathit{RDMDP}(E, p_\alpha, p_{\beta}) = (X, A, \cd\mu, \cd p)$, augments a Markov Decision Process $E = (S,A,\mu,p)$ with:

(1) state space $X = S \times A^{\max\alpha+\max\beta} \times \mathbb R^2$, \ (2) action space $A$, \\
(3) initial state distribution $\cd\mu(x_0) = \cd\mu(\itup{s, u, \alpha, \beta}) = \mu(s) \ \delta (u - c) \ p_\alpha(\alpha) \ p_{\beta}(\beta)$, \\
(4) transition distribution $\small\medmuskip=1mu\thinmuskip=1mu\thickmuskip=1mu \cd p(\itup{s', u', \alpha', \beta'}, r' | \itup{s, u, \alpha, \beta}, a) = 
f_{\alpha-\alpha'}(s', \beta', r' | \itup{s, u, \alpha, \beta}, a) \ p_\alpha(\alpha'| \alpha) \ p_u(u'|u, a)$.

The transition distribution itself is composed of three parts: \\
(a) the observation delay distribution $p_\alpha$, \\
(b) the transition distribution for the action buffer $p_u(u'|u,a) = \delta(u'-(a, \bisn u1{-1}))$, \\
(c) the distribution $f_\Delta$ describing the evolution of observations and action delays.

We recursively define $f_\Delta$ for each change in observation delays ($\small\medmuskip=1mu\thinmuskip=1mu\thickmuskip=1mu \Delta = \alpha-\alpha'$) as

\begin{multline}
\small
    f_\Delta(s', \beta', r' \mid \itup{s, u, \alpha, \beta}, a) = \\
    \int_{ S\times \mathbb R^2} \!\!\!\!\!\!\! p(s', r'-r^* |s^* , \bi{u}{\underbrace{\alpha-\Delta}_{\alpha'}+\beta'}) \ p_{\beta}(\beta' | \beta^*) \ f_{\Delta-1}(s^*, \beta^*, r^* | \itup{s, u, \alpha, \beta}, a) \ d (s^*, \beta^*, r^*).
\end{multline}
The base case of the recursion is
\begin{equation}
    f_{-1}(s', \beta', r' \mid \itup{s, u, \alpha, \beta}, a) = \delta(s'-s) \ \delta(\beta'-\beta) \ \delta(r') .
\end{equation}

The first case of the recursion is
\begin{equation}
    f_{0}(s', \beta', r' \mid \itup{s, u, \alpha, \beta}, a) = p(s', r'|s, \bi{u}{\underbrace{\alpha}_{\alpha'}+\beta'}) \ p_{\beta}(\beta' | \beta).
\end{equation}



$u$ is a buffer of the last $\small \max \alpha + \max \beta$ sent actions. We index its elements using square brackets with $\bi u1$ being the most recent and $\bi u{\max \alpha + \max \beta}$ being the oldest action in the buffer. We denote slices by $\bis uij = (\bi ui, ..., \bi uj)$ and $\bisn ui{-j} = (\bi ui, ..., \bi u{\max\alpha+\max\beta-j})$. We slightly override this notation and additionally define $\bi{u}{0} = a$.
Finally, $\delta$ is the Dirac delta distribution.

\end{definition}

Note that the observation delay cannot increment by more than $1$ because the transition represents one time-step and we systematically discard older information. E.g. $f_{-1}$ is used when $\alpha$ is increasing, whereas $f_0$ is used when there is no change in observation delay.

More precisely, observation-delays are sampled in $p_\alpha$ and action-delays are sampled in $p_\beta$ where $p_\cdot(\cdot > \max \cdot) = p_\cdot(\cdot' > \cdot + 1|\cdot) = 0$.

A simple special case is $p_\alpha(\alpha') = \delta(\alpha' - \max\alpha)$ and $p_\beta(\beta') = \delta(\beta' - \max\beta)$, i.e. constant observation and action delays. The transition distribution then becomes $
\cd p(\itup{s', u', \alpha', \beta'}, r' | \itup{s, u, \alpha, \beta}, a)
 = p(s', r' | s, \bi{u'}{\alpha'+\beta'}) \ \delta(u' - (a, \ \bisn u1{-1})) \delta(\alpha - \max\alpha) \delta(\beta - \max\beta)$.
This reduces to Constantly Delayed Markov Decision Processes ($\mathit{CDMDPs}$), introduced by \citet{Walsh2008LearningAP}.
Note that, in $\mathit{CDMDPs}$, $\alpha$ and $\beta$ can be removed from the state-space since they are constant, and only the knowledge of their sum (i.e. the total delay) is required to define the size of the action buffer.

Similar to $\mathit{CDMDPs}$, the state-space of $\mathit{RDMDPs}$ is made Markov by augmenting the delayed observation $s$ with the last $\max\alpha+\max\beta$ actions sent by the decision maker. Indeed, in the worst-case scenario, the observation-delay is repeatedly $\max \alpha$ and the action-delay is repeatedly $\max \beta$, which is equivalent to a $\mathit{CDMDP}$. In such case, the $\alpha$ oldest actions along with $s$ describe the current state of the remote actor, whereas the $\beta$ most recent actions are yet to be applied. Visual examples of $\mathit{CDMDPs}$ are provided in Section \ref{section:visu_cdmdps}.

Contrary to $\mathit{CDMDPs}$, it is not possible to remove $\alpha$ and $\beta$ from the state-space of $\mathit{RDMDPs}$ in general. Indeed, this would forbid conditional distributions such as $p_\alpha(\alpha'|\alpha)$ and $p_\beta(\beta'|\beta)$. Also, note that in $\mathit{RDMDPs}$ the knowledge of the total delay is not as informative as the knowledge of separate delays. Indeed, the total delay ($\alpha+\beta$) alone does not tell the age of the observation ($\alpha$), but only the age of the last action that influenced it.
Visual examples are provided in Section \ref{section:visu_rdmdps}.


\section{Reinforcement Learning in Delayed MDPs}
\label{section:rl_in_delayed_mdps}


Delayed environments described in Section~\ref{section_environments_delays} are specific types of MDPs with augmented observation spaces and delayed dynamics. As such, usual RL approaches wrongly assuming Markov observations in the presence of delays are however completely valid with $\mathit{RDMDP}$ observations. In other words, augmenting the state space as described in Section~\ref{section_environments_delays} is sufficient to adapt traditional algorithms to the delayed setting. However their performance might still deteriorate because of the more difficult credit assignment due to the delayed observations and rewards. In addition they will not be able to make use of the fact that the delayed dynamics of delayed environments are partially known.





\subsection{State-Value in Delayed MDPs}


We can make use of the fact that part of the delayed dynamics are known to design algorithms better-suited for randomly or constantly delayed environments. We now introduce a new value estimator that makes use of the special properties of delayed MDPs.
\begin{definition} \label{def:cdmdp_state_value_estimator}
The multi-step delayed value estimator is a family of single-sample estimators defined recursively as %
\begin{multline}\small\medmuskip=0mu\thinmuskip=0mu\thickmuskip=0mu
    \hat v_{n}(\itup{s_0, u_0, \alpha_0, \beta_0};
    \itup{s_1, u_1, \alpha_1, \beta_1}, r_1, x_2, r_2, ..., x_n, r_n)
    \small\medmuskip=0mu\thinmuskip=0mu\thickmuskip=0mu
    = \\
        r_1 + \gamma \E_{a\sim\pi(\cdot|\itup{s_0, u_0, \alpha_0, \beta_0})} \hat v_{n-1}(\itup{s_1, (a, \bisn{u_0} 1 {-1}), \alpha_1, \beta_1}; x_2, r_2, ..., x_n, r_n)
\end{multline}
\end{definition}

In the on-policy case (i.e. with trajectories sampled according to $\pi$) the estimator $v_\infty$ reduces to the standard, unbiased Monte-Carlo return estimator. Generally, in the on-policy case the bias of our estimator $v_n$ depends on the bias of an auxiliary estimator $v_0$ (e.g. a neural network) via $\small \E_s \operatorname{bias}(\hat v_n(s))= \gamma^n \ \E_s \operatorname{bias}(\hat v_0(s))$. We say the expected bias reduction is $\gamma^n$. Proposition~\ref{prop:n-step-bias} in the Appendix describes this in more detail.

\subsection{N-Step Off-Policy State-Value Estimation in Delayed Environments}
For policy search, on-policy algorithms are known to be data-inefficient, since they are not able to reuse training trajectories that have been generated under old policies. To reuse past experience, we need to be able to estimate the value off-policy instead. Off-policy algorithms generally use a 1-step action-value estimator because it is amenable to off-policy estimation in the general case. Compared with the on-policy n-step estimators, 1-step action-value estimators only have an expected bias reduction of $\gamma$ (see Lemma~\ref{lemma:q-estimator-bias} in the Appendix).

By contrast, the $\mathit{RDMDP}$ setting allows for off-policy multi-step state-value estimation.
We define a \textit{valid sub-trajectory} as follows:

\begin{definition}
Let $p^\mu_n$ be the n-step state-reward distribution under policy $\mu$ (see Definition~\ref{def:p-nstep} in the Appendix).
Let $\tau_n \sim p^\mu_n(\cdot | x_0)$ be a n-step trajectory sampled in $p^\mu_n$ from an initial state $x_0$. A valid sub-trajectory of $\tau_n = (x_0,r_1,x_1, \dots, r_n, x_n)$ is 
a slice $\tau^\nu_n = (x_0,r_1,x_1, \dots, r_\nu, x_\nu)$ of $\tau_n$ such that $\forall x_i=\itup{s_i, u_i, \alpha_i, \beta_i} \in \tau^\nu_n, \alpha_i+\beta_i > i$ \TODO{check condition}
\end{definition}

In a $\mathit{CDMDP}(E, \alpha, \beta)$, the entire trajectory $\tau_n \sim p^\mu_n(\cdot | x_0)$ is always valid as long as $n<\alpha+\beta$ \TODO{check condition}.
In a
$\mathit{RDMDP}(E, p_\alpha, p_{\beta})$,
the first transition $(x_o, r_1, x_1)$ of
$\tau_n$
is always valid because
$\forall i, \beta_i \geq 2$,
so we can always find a valid sub-trajectory in $\tau_n $.
If $n \geq \alpha_{\text{max}}+\beta_{\text{max}}$, $\tau_n$ is always invalid because $\alpha_n+\beta_n \leq n$.


\begin{thmrep} \footnote{All mathematical results are proven in the appendix.}
\label{th:off_policy_thm}
The $\nu$-step delayed value estimator produces the same expected estimates in the off-policy case as in the on-policy case as long as a valid sub-trajectory is used,
i.e.
\begin{align}
    & \E_{\tau_n \sim p^\mu_n(\cdot|x)}[\ \hat v_{\nu}(x; \ \tau^\nu_n)] = \E_{\tau_n \sim p^\pi_n(\cdot|x)}[\ \hat v_{\nu}(x; \ \tau^\nu_n)]
\end{align}
\TODO{write this properly by sampling $\tau^\nu_n$ instead of $\tau_n$, or constraining n directly?}
\end{thmrep}
\begin{proof}
Direct corollary of Proposition~\ref{prop:offpolicy}, with $\overline{u} \leftarrow u$. In such case, the last $n$ elements of $u$ and $\overline{u}$ are trivially identical as long as $n \leq \alpha+\beta$.

(NB: in a $\mathit{CDMDP}$, $\alpha+\beta$ is the size of $u$)
\end{proof}

Using the n-step state-value estimators instead of the usual 1-step value estimators has a fundamental importance in $\gamma$-discounted deep RL. Indeed, they reduce the bias of the function approximator by a factor of $\gamma^n$.


\begin{corollaryrep} \label{cor:bias} %
Even if a different policy $\mu$ is used to sample the trajectories, as long as $\small n \le \alpha+\beta$ the multi-step delayed value estimators $\hat v_n$ have a bias of
\begin{equation}
    \operatorname{bias}^\mu( \hat v_{n}(x_0; \ \cdot \ )) = \gamma^n \ \E_{..., x_n, r_n \sim p^\pi_n(\cdot|x_0)}[ \operatorname{bias}( \hat v_0(x_n))].
\end{equation}
\end{corollaryrep}
\begin{proof}
We define
\begin{equation}
    \bias^\rho(\hat v_n(x_0)) =
    \E_{\tau_n \sim p^\rho_n(\cdot|x_0)}[
    \hat v_n(x_0; \ \tau_n)] - v(x_0).
\end{equation}
From Theorem~\ref{th:off_policy_thm} it follows that
\begin{equation}
    \bias^\mu(\hat v_n(x_0)) = \bias^\pi(\hat v_n(x_0)).
\end{equation}
Using Proposition~\ref{prop:n-step-bias} we then obtain
\begin{equation}
    \bias^\mu(\hat v_n(x_0)) = \gamma^n \E_{(\dots, x_n, r_n) \sim p^\pi_n(x_0)}[
    \bias(\hat v_{0}(x_n))].
\end{equation}
\end{proof}
In other words
the longer the trajectory,
the less we get influenced by the bias of the function approximator $\hat v_0$.
A direct conclusion of Corollary \ref{cor:bias} is that the off-policy multi-step state-value estimators are unbiased if the auxiliary estimator $\hat{v}_0$ is unbiased.
\begin{corollaryrep} \label{cor:unbiased}
The multi-step delayed value estimators $\hat v_n$ are unbiased if $\operatorname{bias}(\hat v_0(x)) = 0$, i.e.
\begin{equation}
    \E_{\tau_n \sim p^\mu_n(\cdot|x)}[\ \hat v_{n}(x; \ \tau_n)] = v(x).
\end{equation}
\end{corollaryrep}
\begin{proof}
Directly follows from Corollary~\ref{cor:bias}.
\end{proof}

\section{Delay-Correcting Actor-Critic} \label{Section_DCAC_CD}

The estimators introduced in Section \ref{section:rl_in_delayed_mdps} are general-purpose estimators which can easily be translated to improved version of existing RL algorithms for delayed settings. As an illustration, we derive Delay-Correcting Actor-Critic (DC/AC), which is an improved version of Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) \cite{haarnoja2018soft, haarnoja2018soft2} for real-time delayed settings.

\subsection{Value Approximation}
Like SAC, DC/AC makes use of the entropy-augmented soft value function \cite{haarnoja2018soft}:
\begin{lemmarep}
\label{lem:recursive_soft_value}
In a $\text{CDMDP}(E, \alpha, \beta)$ the soft value function is:
\begin{equation}\medmuskip=0mu\thinmuskip=0mu\thickmuskip=0mu
    v^\text{soft}(\itup{s, u})
    = r(s, \bi u{\alpha+\beta})
    + \mathbb{E}_{
    s' \sim p(\cdot|s, \bi u{\alpha+\beta})}[
    \E_{
    a \sim \pi(\cdot |\itup{s, u})
    }
    [\gamma v^\text{soft}(\itup{s', (a, \bisn u1{-1})})
    - \log \pi(a | \itup{s, u})]]
\end{equation}
\end{lemmarep}
\begin{proof}

The soft value function for an environment $(X, A, \tilde \mu, \tilde p, \tilde r)$ is defined as
\begin{equation}\medmuskip=0mu\thinmuskip=0mu\thickmuskip=0mu
\label{eq_soft_v_cd}
v^\text{soft}(x) =
\E_{a \sim \pi(\cdot \mid x)}[
q^\text{soft}(x, a) 
- \log \pi(a \mid x)
]
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
 q^\text{soft}(x, a) = \tilde r(s,a) + \gamma \E_{x' \sim \tilde p(x, a)}[v^\text{soft}(x')]
\end{equation}
If $(X, A, \tilde \mu, \tilde p, \tilde r) = \text{CDMDP}(E, \alpha, \beta)$ with $E = (S, A, \mu, p, r)$ this can be simplified to
\begin{equation}
\medmuskip=0mu\thinmuskip=0mu\thickmuskip=0mu
\label{eq_soft_q_cd}
q^\text{soft}(\itup{s,u}, a) = 
r(s, \bi u{\alpha+\beta})
+\gamma \E_{s' \sim p(\cdot \mid s, \bi u{\alpha+\beta})}[
v^\text{soft}(\itup{s',(a, \bisn u1{-1})})
]
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
v^\text{soft} (\itup{s,u}) = 
\E_{a \sim \pi(\cdot | \itup{s,u})}[
r(s, \bi u{\alpha+\beta})
+ \E_{s' \sim p(\cdot | s, \bi u{\alpha+\beta})}[
\gamma v^\text{soft}(\itup{s',(a, \bisn u1{-1})})]
- \log \pi(a|\itup{s,u})
]
\end{equation}
respectively.



\end{proof}

 Differently from SAC, we augment the delayed rewards with the entropies of the newly sampled actions, which are not directly applied.
 Indeed, we don't have access to the entropies of the applied actions, since these are old actions.
 Note that $s'$ only depends on actions from the action buffer, and not on $\pi$, which remains true when unrolling the equation for the first $\alpha+\beta$ time-steps. Therefore we can estimate $v$ using sub-trajectories $\tau_{\alpha+\beta}$ generated by arbitrary policies.

By augmenting the reward function in Definition \ref{def:cdmdp_state_value_estimator} we can adapt state-value estimator for the soft value function.
\begin{lemmarep}The delayed soft state-value estimator, i.e. the state-value estimator with entropy augmented rewards, is
\label{lem:dcac_value_target_estimator}
\begin{multline}\small\medmuskip=0mu\thinmuskip=0mu\thickmuskip=0mu
    \hat v^\text{soft}_n(\itup{s_0, u_0}; \ \itup{s_1, u_1}, x_2, ..., x_n)
    = \\
    r(s_0, \ \bi{u_0}{\alpha+\beta})
    + \E_{a\sim\pi(\cdot|\itup{s_0, u_0})}[
    \gamma
    \hat v^\text{soft}_{n-1}(\itup{s_1, (a, \bisn{u_0}1{-1})}; \ x_2, ..., x_n)
    - \log \pi (a|\itup{s_0,u_0})].
\end{multline}
\end{lemmarep}
\begin{proof}
By replacing the rewards in the standard multistep delayed value estimator (Definition \ref{def:cdmdp_state_value_estimator}) with the augmented rewards in Lemma~\ref{lemma:soft_rewards} we obtain the soft state-value estimator.
\end{proof}

Lemma \ref{lem:dcac_value_target_estimator} gives DC/AC's soft state-value target.
Note that DC/AC's critic estimates state-values rather than action-values.
The initial augmented state $\itup{s_{0}, u_0}$, the augmented state trajectory $\tau_n$
and the corresponding delayed rewards are sampled from a replay memory whereas the unapplied actions
are sampled from the current parameterized policy $\pi$. Indeed, given
the initial augmented state, and the sampled augmented state trajectory $\tau_n$, the state trajectory
$(s_i)_{i=0..n}$ from the augmented states $\itup{s_i,u_i} \in \tau_n$
is always valid (in expectation) as long as $n \leq \alpha+\beta$, regardless of the policy it has been sampled under. The same applies to the corresponding delayed rewards. This valid trajectory of states along with the corresponding action buffer can be used to unroll a new trajectory of actions from the current policy $\pi$. This new trajectory of actions yields a new action buffer which, along with the
last state of the trajectory, is the valid augmented state $x_{n}=\itup{s_{n}, u_{n}}$ as defined by the current policy $\pi$.

As shown in Corollary \ref{cor:bias}, unrolling the estimator for the maximum number of steps, i.e. for $\alpha + \beta$ steps, yields the best bias reduction of our function approximator. Denoting $v_\theta$ as the minimum between twin value approximators \cite{hasselt2015deep, fujimoto2018addressing}, we define the DC/AC state-value loss as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq_loss_critic_dcac}
    L_v^{\textrm{DC/AC}}(v) = \mathbb{E}_{(x_0, \tau_{\alpha+\beta}) \sim \mathcal{D}}[(v_\theta(x_0) - \hat v_{\alpha+\beta}(x_0; \ \tau_{\alpha+\beta}))^2]]
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{D}$ is a replay memory from which we can sample sub-trajectories of $\alpha+\beta$ steps, and a new action buffer is unrolled from this sub-trajectory with the current policy $\pi$.







\subsection{Policy Improvement}





In addition to using our n-step value estimator as target for our parametric value estimator we also use it for policy improvement. As in SAC we use the reparameterization trick \cite{kingma2013auto} to obtain the policy gradient from the value estimator. However, with our value estimator this involves backpropagation through time in the action buffer (see Proof~\ref{proof:actor-loss} of Proposition~\ref{prop:actor-loss} in the Appendix). 

\begin{definition}
The DC/AC actor loss is defined as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq_loss_actor_dcac}
    L^{\textrm{DC/AC}}_\pi(\pi) = - \E_{x, \tau_{n} \sim \mathcal D} [\hat v_n^\text{soft}(x; \ \tau_n)]
\end{equation}
with $n = \alpha+\beta$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proprep}\label{prop:actor-loss}
The DC/AC actor loss has the same expected policy gradient as SAC in a $\mathit{CDMDP}(E)$ assuming that an unbiased action-value estimate is used for the SAC loss and an unbiased $\hat v_0$ is used in the DC/AC loss.




\end{proprep}
\begin{proof}
 \label{proof:actor-loss}
 
We have defined the DC/AC actor loss as:
\begin{equation}
L^{\textrm{DC/AC}}_\pi(\pi) = - \E_{x, \tau_{\alpha+\beta} \sim \mathcal D} [\hat v_{\alpha+\beta}^\text{soft}(x; \ \tau_{\alpha+\beta})]
\end{equation}

To derive this formulation, we start from the SAC policy loss
\begin{equation}
    L^\text{SAC}_\pi(\pi) = \E_{x \sim \mathcal{D}} \KL(\pi(\cdot \mid x) \| \exp (q(x, \cdot)) / Z(x))
\end{equation}


Since we are ultimately interested in $\nabla_{\pi} L^\text{SAC}_{\mathit{CDMDP}(E)}(\pi)$, we can ignore the $Z$ partition, similarly to Haarnoja et al., because it only depends on the action-value, and not on the parameters of the policy. Thus
\begin{equation}
\label{eq_kl_1}
    L^\text{SAC}_\pi(\pi) = 
    \E_{x \sim \mathcal{D}} [
    \KL\left( \pi (\cdot \mid x) \|
    \exp (q (x, \cdot)) \right) ].
\end{equation}



Which is
\begin{equation}
\medmuskip=0mu
\thinmuskip=0mu
\thickmuskip=0mu
L^\text{SAC}_\pi(\pi) = 
\E_{x \sim \mathcal{D}} [
\E_{a \sim \pi(\cdot | x)} [
\log\pi(a|x) - q (x, a)
]].
\end{equation}

Using the definition of the soft action-value function (Equation \ref{eq_soft_v_cd}) we get
\begin{equation}
\medmuskip=0mu
\thinmuskip=0mu
\thickmuskip=0mu
L^\text{SAC}_{\mathit{CDMDP}(E)}(\pi) = 
\E_{x \sim \mathcal{D}} [
- v(x)
].
\end{equation}
Since $\E_{\tau_{\alpha+\beta}}[\hat{v}^\text{soft}_{\alpha+\beta}(x; \ \tau_{\alpha+\beta})] = v(x)$ that is
\begin{equation}
    = - \E_{x, \tau_{\alpha+\beta} \sim \mathcal D} [\hat v_{\alpha+\beta}^\text{soft}(x; \ \tau_{\alpha+\beta})] = L^{\textrm{DC/AC}}_\pi(\pi)
\end{equation}
\end{proof}


\subsection{DC/AC with Random Delays} \label{Section_DCAC_RD}

Although we derived the DC/AC algorithm in the $\mathit{CDMDP}$ setting, from definition \ref{def:rdmdp_state_value_estimator} it is fairly straightforward to generalize the algorithm to the more general $\mathit{RDMDP}$ setting.


Indeed, the main difference is that actions and observations may now be dropped. Namely, an action/observation may be superseded by another and thus never be applied/retrieved. However, each new action becomes part of the action buffer and influences all actions computed afterwards. Therefore, in the $\mathit{RDMDP}$ setting, the n-step Bellman backup corresponding to the 'round-trip' of a given action is not valid up to the end of the round-trip anymore. Instead, it is only valid up to the first delayed observation captured right before the application of the first action of a sub-trajectory, or of any action that followed.

As described in Definition \ref{def:rdmdp_state_value_estimator}, retrieving this information requires access to the value of $\alpha_i + \beta_i$ for each delayed observation of the sub-trajectory. In practice, these $\alpha_i + \beta_i$ can be retrieved by augmenting each action sent by the decision maker with an identifier of this action (for example, the timestamp corresponding to the beginning of its computation), and augmenting each observation sent by the remote actor with the identifier of the action that the remote actor is going to apply at the beginning of the time-step. Then, when the decision maker receives a delayed observation $i$, $\alpha_i + \beta_i$ is the index of the identified action in the action buffer.
In a nutshell, we simply adapt DC/AC to the $\mathit{RDMDP}$ setting by performing these ad-hoc multi-step backups for each sampled sub-trajectory.

\section{Experiments} \label{SectionExperiments}


To evaluate our approach and make future work in this direction easy for the RL community, we release as open-source, along with our code, a Gym wrapper that introduces constant or random multi-step delays in any classical turn-based Gym environment. In particular, this enables us to introduce random delays to the Gym MuJoCo continuous control suite \cite{brockman2016openai, todorovmujoco}, which is otherwise turn-based.

\subsection{Compared Algorithms}

As previously pointed out, the naive version of SAC wrongly assumes Markov observations, i.e. only uses the delayed observations, which violates the Markov assumption in delayed settings. Consequently, naive SAC exhibits near-random results in delayed environments. A few such experiments are provided in the appendix for illustration.
Our other experiments compare DC/AC against SAC in the same $\mathit{RDMDP}$ setting, i.e. in the presence of random delays with augmented observations fed to the model.
Since SAC is the algorithm that we chose to improve for delayed scenarios in order to demonstrate our approach, comparing DC/AC against it in the same setting provides a like-for-like comparison. We also found interesting to compare against RTAC \cite{ramstedt2019rtrl}, since DC/AC reduces to this algorithm in the special constant delay case where $\alpha=0, \beta=1$.
For the hyperparameters and other implementation details, we refer the reader to the appendix.

More implementation details are provided in the appendix. For each experiment, DC/AC performs backups of $n = \alpha+\beta$ time-steps, 3 seeds are run, and the 90\% confidence intervals are shaded.

\section{Related work}
Back in 2008, \citet{Walsh2008LearningAP} introduced the notion of ``Constantly Delayed Markov Decision Process'' ($\mathit{CDMDP}$) which we study in further details throughout this work. Whereas the recent advances in deep learning allow us to successfully implement what the authors called the ``augmented approach'', this was considered intractable at the time because the size of the state space is exponential with respect to the considered delays. Instead, they studied the case where observations are retrieved with a constant delay and developed a model-based algorithm to predict the current state of the environment.
Similarly, in 2010, \citet{Schuitema2010control} developed ``memory-less'' approaches based on SARSA and vanilla Q-learning, taking advantage of prior knowledge about the duration of a constant control delay.
More recently, \citet{Firoiu2018AtHS} introduced constant action delays to a video game to train agents whose reaction time compares to humans. Similar to previous work, the authors used a state-predictive model, but based on a recurrent neural network architecture.
\citet{ramstedt2019rtrl} formalized the framework of Real-Time Reinforcement Learning (RTRL) that we generalize here to all forms of real-time delays. Initially designed to cope with the fact that inference is not instantaneous in real-world control, the RTRL setting is equivalent to a 1-step action delay $\mathit{CDMDP}$.
Finally, \citet{Xiao2020Thinking} adopted a alternative approach by considering the influence of the action selection time when action selection is performed within the duration of a larger time-step. However their framework only allows delays smaller than one time-step.

\section{Conclusion and future work}

To make all tested algorithms work in $\mathit{RDMDPs}$, we used a few tricks requiring stronger assumptions than in $\mathit{CDMDPs}$. Indeed, while our framework is straightforwardly compatible with any constantly delayed task as long as the constant delays are measured beforehand, making it work optimally in the randomly delayed setting requires a few mild assumptions on the remote actor. Namely, the remote actor has to be able to compute its instantaneous reward (ideally), or at least it needs to be able to communicate the identifier of the action that it is applying. Using e.g. a Lamport clock \cite{lamport2019time}, the actor can also timestamp its observations, which allows the decision maker to deduce both observation and action delays. These options are further discussed in the appendix.

To the best of our knowledge, DC/AC is the first deep actor-critic approach to exhibit such performance on both constantly and randomly delayed settings. We believe that our model can be further improved by making use of the fact that our critic estimates the state-value instead of the action-value function. Indeed, in this setting, \cite{ramstedt2019rtrl} hinted that it is possible to simplify the model by merging the actor and the critic networks using the PopArt output normalization, which we did not try yet and leave for future work.

Delay-Correcting Reinforcement Learning (DCRL) does not make any assumption on the duration of the time-step, although in practice time-steps smaller than the upper bound of the inference time will require a few tricks. We believe that DCRL is actually close to time-step agnostic, and will further investigate this direction in future work.











\section{Broader Impacts}

Although this paper is relatively theoretical and the setting in which we have been testing our algorithm so far is artificial, the motivation behind our work is deeply related to real-world autonomous control. We believe that our contribution can have a direct impact on the transfer of deep Reinforcement Learning successes to real-world situations, which could accelerate both positive and negative outcomes. Real-world autonomous systems such as intelligent robots have a long record of dystopian literature and cinematography, which can inspire engineers and decision makers in certain cases. Moreover, the arms industry currently shows a growing interest for our field. Nevertheless, we believe that the contribution of this paper will be mostly beneficial to the community, as it should help to the development of many types of helpful real-world systems such as autonomous vehicles and household intelligent systems.

\section{Notes}

\subsection{Note on RDMDPs}

The way we chose to define $\mathit{RDMDPs}$, the decision maker only keeps the delayed observation that was most recently captured by the remote actor, and the remote actor only applies the action that most recently started being computed by the decision maker.

\subsection{Note on the reward function}
\label{section_note_rew}

We have implicitly made a choice when defining the reward function as:
$$\cd r(x, a) = \cd r(\itup{s, u}, a) = r(s, \bi u{\alpha+\beta})$$

$s$ is the last delayed observation received by the decision maker, and has therefore been captured $\alpha$ time-steps ago. On the other hand, $\bi u{\alpha+\beta}$ is the action that was computed $\alpha + \beta$ time-steps ago, has travelled from the decision maker to the remote actor for $\beta$ time-steps, and got applied right after $s$ was captured. In other words, the reward as we defined it is the `vanilla delayed reward', corresponding to the transition that happened at the remote actor $\alpha$ time-steps ago.

However, in the $\mathit{RDMDP}$ setting, observations can be dropped (superseded) and in such cases this quantity gets repeated, which creates a reward signal with a higher variance than in $\mathit{CDMDPs}$.

This can be alleviated by making the additional assumption that the remote actor can observe its own instantaneous reward. This allows the remote actor to compute its cumulative reward and send it to the decision maker along with the observation. The decision maker can then compute the difference between the last cumulative reward it received from the remote actor and the new one for each incoming observation (NB: outdated observations are discarded so the decision maker only sees cumulative rewards with time-increasing timestamps). This allows the decision maker to compute a more robust delayed reward signal instead of vanilla delayed rewards in the randomly delayed setting.


\section{Implementation Details}

\subsection{Model Architecture}
The model we use in all our experiments is composed of two separate multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs): a critic network, and an actor network. Both MLPs are built with the same simple architecture of 2 hidden layers, 256 units each. The critic outputs a single value, whereas the actor outputs an action distribution with the dimension of the action space, from which actions are sampled with the reparameterization trick. This architecture is compatible with the second version of SAC described in \cite{haarnoja2018soft2}. The only difference from the DC/AC model is that the SAC critic tracks $q(x)$, and not $v(x)$. Indeed, differently from usual actor-critic algorithms, the output of DC/AC's critic approximates the state-value $v(x)$ (instead of the action-value $q(x)$), as it is sufficient to optimize the actor loss described in equation \ref{eq_loss_actor_dcac}. Weights and biases are initialized with the default Pytorch initializer. Both the actor and the critic are optimized by gradient descent with the Adam optimizer, on losses $L^{\text{DC/AC}}(\pi)$ (equation \ref{eq_loss_actor_dcac}) and $L^{\text{DC/AC}}(v)$ (equation \ref{eq_loss_critic_dcac}), respectively. Classically, we use twin critic networks \cite{hasselt2015deep, fujimoto2018addressing} with target weight tracking \cite{mnih2015human} to stabilize training.

\subsection{CDMDPs}

In a $\mathit{CDMDP}(E, \alpha, \beta)$, the delays are fixed, and can be implicitly considered as a constant property of the environment. In this setting, we simply concatenate the members of the augmented observation $x=\itup{s,u}$, and feed the concatenation to the model.
During training, sub-trajectories of $\alpha + \beta$ steps are sampled from the replay memory, and we execute fixed $(\alpha + \beta)$-steps backups to compute the losses, as described in section \ref{Section_DCAC_CD}.


\subsection{RDMDPs}

Handling $\mathit{RDMDP}(E, p_{\beta}, p_{\alpha})$ is more involved.
As highlighted in section \ref{Section_DCAC_RD}, we need the $\alpha_i + \beta_i$ information for each delayed observation $s_i$ during training. This is achieved by appending an identifier to the action computed by the decision maker, and sending this identifier back to the decision maker along with the observation captured by the remote actor. When the decision maker receives the identifier back, it can deduce which action was applied by the remote actor right after it sent the observation. Indeed, the identifier corresponds to this action. Looking this action up in the action buffer, the decision maker finds it at the $(\alpha_i + \beta_i)^{th}$ position, as it was sent $\alpha_i + \beta_i$ time-steps ago. This information is appended to the augmented observation and stored in the replay memory.

The action identifier can always be the timestamp at which the decision maker started computing the action.
Making the additional assumption that the remote actor and the decision maker have synchronized clocks, the remote actor can directly deduce $\beta_i$ when it sends an observation, and append $\beta_i$ along the aforementioned identifier. Therefore, the decision maker can deduce $\alpha_i$ when $s_{i}$ is received, and append $\alpha_i$ and $\beta_i$ separately to $x_i$, instead of appending $\alpha_i + \beta_i$. Although this is not useful for the training algorithm, this is interesting as inputs of the model for inference, because this adds the information `how old is the delayed observation' ($\alpha_i$) to the information `how old is the subsequent action' ($\alpha_i + \beta_i$).

We implement the latter trick, which allows us to feed the concatenation of $x_i$, $\alpha_i$ and $\beta_i$ as input of our model. More exactly, we feed $\alpha_i$ and $\beta_i$ as one-hot encoded inputs, since this maps to positions in the action buffer (i.e. to past moments in time) in a more natural way than feeding their raw integer values.

To further improve performance in the randomly delayed setting, we use differences in cumulative rewards instead of the vanilla delayed reward signal, as described in \ref{section_note_rew}.

Note that all these `tricks' improve the performance of all algorithms in the RDMDP setting, and are implemented as part of our Gym wrapper.

Additionally for DC/AC, one may note that, when unrolling our multi-step delayed value estimator, the rewards are not affected by the $\alpha+\beta$ first computed actions and therefore they don't contribute do the policy gradient. It is possible to remove them from the actor loss, which we do in our implementation.

\section{Definitions}

\begin{definition} \label{def:p-nstep}
The $n$-step state-reward distribution for an environment $E=(S, A, \mu, p)$ and a policy $\pi$ is defined as
\begin{equation}
    p^\pi_{n+1}(\underbrace{s',r', \tau_n}_{\tau_{n+1}} | s)
    = \E_{a \sim \pi(\cdot|s)}[p^\pi_{n}(\tau_{n}|s') p(s', r'|s, a)] 
    = \int_A p^\pi_{n}(\tau_{n}|s') p(s', r'|s, a)\pi(a|s) da 
\end{equation}
with the base case $p^\pi_0(s) = 1$ and the first iterate $p^\pi_1(s', r'|s) = \int_A p(s', r'|s, a)\pi(a|s) da $.
\end{definition}


\begin{definition}
A 1-step action-value estimator is defined as
\begin{equation}\medmuskip=0mu\thinmuskip=0mu\thickmuskip=0mu
     \hat q_1(s, a; \ s', r')
     = r' + \gamma \ \E_{a'\sim\pi(\cdot|s')}[\hat q_0(s', a')].
\end{equation}
Part of this estimator is usually another parametric estimator $\hat q_0$ (e.g. a neural network trained with stochastic gradient descent).
\end{definition}


\section{Other Mathematical Results}






\begin{lemma} \label{lemma:q-estimator-bias}
The bias of a one-step action value estimator is
\begin{equation}
    \bias(\hat q_1(s,a; \cdot)) =   \gamma \ \E_{s' \sim p(\cdot|s,a)}[\E_{a' \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s')}[\bias(\hat q_0(s',a'))]
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}


\begin{align}
    & \bias(\hat q_1(s,a; \cdot)) =
    \E_{s' \sim p(\cdot|s,a)}[
    \hat q_1(s, a; s')
    ] - q(s,a) \\
    &= 
    \E_{s' \sim p(\cdot|s,a)}[
    r(s,a) + \gamma \E_{a' \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s')}[\hat{q}_0(s',a')]
    - r(s,a) - \gamma \E_{a' \sim \pi(\cdot | s')}[q(s',a')]
    ] \\
    &=
    \gamma \ \E_{s' \sim p(\cdot|s,a)}[
     \E_{a' \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s')}[\hat{q}_0(s',a') - q(s',a')]
    ] \\
    &= 
    \gamma \ \E_{s' \sim p(\cdot|s,a)}[
     \E_{a' \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s')}[\bias(\hat q_0(s',a'))]
    ]
\end{align}
\end{proof}








\begin{prop} \label{prop:n-step-bias}
In the on-policy case, the n-step delayed value estimators have the following bias:
\begin{equation}
    \bias^\pi(\hat v_n(\itup{s,u})) = \gamma^n \E_{(\dots, \itup{s^*,u^*}) \sim p^\pi_n(\itup{s,u})}[
    \bias(\hat v_{0}(\itup{s^*,u^*}))]
\end{equation}
\end{prop}
\begin{proof}

\begin{align}
    & \bias^\pi(\hat v_n(\itup{s,u})) =
    \E_{\tau_n \sim p^\pi_n(\cdot|\itup{s,u})}[
    \hat v_n(\itup{s,u}; \ \tau_n) - v(\itup{s,u})] \\
    &= \E_{(\itup{s',u'},\tau_{n-1}) \sim p^\pi_n(\cdot|\itup{s,u})}[
    r(s, \bi u{\alpha+\beta})
    + \gamma \hat v_{n-1}(\itup{s',u'}; \tau_{n-1})
    - r(s, \bi u{\alpha+\beta})
    - \gamma  v(\itup{s',u'})] \\
    &= \gamma \E_{(\itup{s',u'},\tau_{n-1}) \sim p^\pi_n(\cdot|\itup{s,u})}[
     \hat v_{n-1}(\itup{s',u'}; \tau_{n-1})
    - v(\itup{s',u'})] \\
    &= \gamma \E_{\itup{s',u'} \sim p^\pi_1(\itup{s,u})}[
    \E_{\tau_{n-1} \sim p^\pi_{n-1}(\cdot|\itup{s',u'})}[
     \hat v_{n-1}(\itup{s',u'}; \tau_{n-1})
    - v(\itup{s',u'})]] \\
    &= \gamma \E_{\itup{s',u'} \sim p^\pi_1(\itup{s,u})}[
    \bias^\pi(\hat v_{n-1}(\itup{s',u'}))] \\
    &= \dots \\
    &= \gamma^n \E_{(\dots, \itup{s^*,u^*}) \sim p^\pi_n(\itup{s,u})}[
    \bias(\hat v_{0}(\itup{s^*,u^*}))]
\end{align}

\end{proof}


\begin{prop} \label{prop:offpolicy}
The expected estimate of the multistep delayed value estimator is the same for trajectories sampled using different policies and initial states. More precisely,
\begin{align}
    & \E_{\tau_n \sim p^\mu_n(\cdot|\itup{s, \bar u_n})}[\ \hat v_{n}(\itup{s, u}; \ \tau_n)] = \E_{\tau_n \sim p^\pi_n(\cdot|\itup{s, u})}[\ \hat v_{n}(\itup{s, u}; \ \tau_n)]
\end{align}
on the condition that the last $n$ elements in $\bar u$ are the same as in $u$, i.e.
\begin{equation}
    \bis{\bar u}{\alpha+\beta-n+1}{\alpha+\beta} =  \bis{u}{\alpha+\beta-n+1}{\alpha+\beta}.
\end{equation}

\end{prop}
\begin{proof}
We are going to prove by induction. The base case
\begin{equation}
    \E_{\tau \sim p_0^\mu(\cdot|\itup{s, \bar u})}[\ \hat v_{0}(\itup{s, u})] = E_{\tau \sim p^\pi_0(\cdot|\itup{s, u})}[\ \hat v_{0}(\itup{s, u})]
\end{equation}
is trivial since $\hat v_{0}$ does not depend on $\tau$. For the induction step we have
\small{\begin{multline}\medmuskip=0mu\thinmuskip=0mu\thickmuskip=0mu
    \E_{\tau_{i} \sim p^\mu_{i}(\cdot|\itup{s, \bar u})}[\ \hat v_{i}(\itup{s, u}; \ \tau_{i})] \\
    = r(s, \bi{ u}{\alpha+\beta}) + \gamma \E_{(\itup{s', \bar u'}, \tau_{i-1}) \sim p^\mu_{i}(\cdot|\itup{s, \bar u})}[ \E_{a^\pi\sim\pi(\cdot|\itup{s, u})} [\hat v_{i-1}(\itup{s', (a^\pi, \bisn{u}1{-1})}; \ \tau_{n-1})]]
\end{multline}}
splitting up the expectation over $(\itup{s', \bar u'}, \tau_{i-1})$ we get
{\small\begin{multline}\medmuskip=0mu\thinmuskip=0mu\thickmuskip=0mu \label{eq:substitute-next}
    = r(s, \bi{u}{\alpha+\beta}) + \gamma \E_{a^\mu \sim \mu(\cdot| \itup{s, \bar u})}[\E_{s' \sim p(s |  \bi{\bar u}{\alpha+\beta})} [  \E_{a^\pi\sim\pi(\cdot|\itup{s, u})} [ \\
    \E_{\tau_{i-1} \sim p^\mu_{i-1}(\cdot|\itup{s', \underbrace{(a^\mu, \bisn{\bar u}1{-1})}_{\bar u'}}) }[\hat v_{i-1}(\itup{s', \underbrace{(a^\pi, \bisn{ u}1{-1})}_{u'}}; \ \tau_{i-1})]]]
\end{multline}}
Next, we present the induction hypothesis for $n=i-1$ in which
\begin{equation}
    \E_{\tau_{i-1} \sim p^\mu_{i-1}(\cdot|\itup{s', {\bar u'}}) }[\hat v_{i-1}(\itup{s', {u'}}; \ \tau_{i-1})] = \E_{\tau_{i-1} \sim p^\pi_{i-1}(\cdot|\itup{s', u'})}[\hat v_{i-1}(\itup{s', {u'}}; \ \tau_{i-1})]
\end{equation}
on the condition that the last $i-1$ elements in $\bar u'$ are the same as in $u'$.

This condition is fulfilled in Equation~\ref{eq:substitute-next}. The action buffers $\bar u'$ and $u'$ will have the same last $i-1$ elements since the last $i$ elements of $\bar u$ and $u$ are the same. Having verified this condition we can substitute our induction hypothesis into Equation~\ref{eq:substitute-next} to obtain
{\small\begin{multline}\medmuskip=0mu\thinmuskip=0mu\thickmuskip=0mu
    = r(s, \bi{ u}{\alpha+\beta}) + \gamma \E_{a^\mu \sim \mu(\cdot| \itup{s, \bar u})}[\E_{s' \sim p(s |  \bi{\bar u}{\alpha+\beta})} \E_{a^\pi\sim\pi(\cdot|\itup{s,  u})} [ \\
    \E_{\tau_{i-1} \sim p^\pi_{i-1}(\cdot|\itup{s', (a^\pi, \bisn{ u}1{-1})})}[\ \hat v_{i-1}(\itup{s', (a^\pi, \bisn{ u}1{-1})}; \ \tau_{i-1})]]].
\end{multline}}
Since $\bi{\bar u}{\alpha+\beta} = \bi{u}{\alpha+\beta}$ we can combine the expectations over $s'$ and $\tau_{i-1}$ into an expectation over $\tau_{i}$. Furthermore, we drop the expecation over $a^\mu$ since it is not used. We obtain
{\small\begin{equation}\medmuskip=0mu\thinmuskip=0mu\thickmuskip=0mu
    = r(s, \bi{ u}{\alpha+\beta}) + \gamma \E_{(\itup{s', u'}, \tau_{i-1}) \sim p^\pi_{i}(\cdot|\itup{s, u})}[\E_{a^\pi\sim\pi(\cdot|\itup{s, u})} [ \hat v_{i-1}(\itup{s', (a^\pi, \bisn{ u}1{-1})}; \ \tau_{i-1})]]]
\end{equation}}
reordering terms we get
{\small\begin{equation}\medmuskip=0mu\thinmuskip=0mu\thickmuskip=0mu
    = \E_{(\itup{s', u'}, \tau_{i-1}) \sim p^\pi_{i}(\cdot|\itup{s, u})}[r(s, \bi{u}{\alpha+\beta}) + \gamma \E_{a^\pi\sim\pi(\cdot|\itup{s, u})} [ \hat v_{i-1}(\itup{s', (a^\pi, \bisn{ u}1{-1})}; \ \tau_{i-1})]]]
\end{equation}}
which is
{\small\begin{equation}\medmuskip=0mu\thinmuskip=0mu\thickmuskip=0mu
    = \E_{\tau_{i} \sim p^\pi_{i}(\cdot|\itup{s, u})}[\hat v_{i}(\itup{s, u}; \ \tau_{i})]]]
\end{equation}}
\end{proof}





\begin{lemma} \label{lemma:soft_rewards}
The soft~value~function~(Lemma \ref{lem:recursive_soft_value}) is equivalent to the traditional value function in a $\mathit{CDMDP}$ with an augmented state-dependent reward function
\begin{equation}
    r^\text{soft}(\itup{s,u}) = r(s, \bi u{\alpha+\beta}) - \E_{a \sim \pi(\cdot | \itup{s,u})}[\log \pi(a|\itup{s,u})]
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
The traditional value function in a $\mathit{CDMDP}$ with the augmented reward function is
\begin{equation}
    v(\itup{s, u}) = r^\text{soft}(\itup{s,u}) + \E_{s' \sim p(\cdot|s, \bi u{\alpha+\beta})}[\E_{a \sim \pi(\cdot |\itup{s, u})}[v(\itup{s', (a, \bisn u1{-1})}]].
\end{equation}
After substituting $r^\text{soft}$ and combining the expectations over $a$ we obtain
\begin{equation}\medmuskip=0mu\thinmuskip=0mu\thickmuskip=0mu
    = r(s, \bi u{\alpha+\beta})
    + \mathbb{E}_{
    s' \sim p(\cdot|s, \bi u{\alpha+\beta})}[
    \E_{
    a \sim \pi(\cdot |\itup{s, u})
    }
    [\gamma v^\text{soft}(\itup{s', (a, \bisn u1{-1})})
    - \log \pi(a | \itup{s, u})]] = v^\text{soft}(\itup{s, u}).
\end{equation}
\end{proof}
